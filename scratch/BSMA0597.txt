 ABSTRACT  
The Rise of the Customer Success Function as Catalyst for Team Innovation:  
The Importance of Boundary Spanning, Knowledge Integration , and Shared Vision  
André Wagner  
Drexel University, February 2021  
Chairpersons: Daan van Knippenberg, Lauren D’Innocenzo  
In this study, I assess the causal impact of boundary spanning on team innovation. 
I explore conditions and processes in 144 engagement teams in an enterprise software 
company driving innovation with customers in North America across industries. In this 
context, innovation is defined as the development and implementation of technology -
based ideas in organizations. Using an integrated conditional process analysis, I find that 
boundary -spanning team practices combined  with a shared vision and functional 
background diversity lead to a significant team innovation increase . I further assess the 
team process of information elaboration through which functional diversity and boundary 
spanning foster solution use. In particular, I argue that boundary spanning amplifies the 
effects of shared vision and information elaboration , leading to significantly increased 
team innovation. Consistent with this argument, I find that function al diversity's positive 
impact  on team innovation through information elaboration is conditional on a shared 
vision , whereas  the impact is more significant  when team members share  a commitment 
to common goals  for their work. Therefore, my findings contribu te actionable insight into 
how to  enable cross -functional engagement teams to successfully drive customer 
innovation with enterprise software in the technology industry.

 CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION  
Context  
New digital technologies and business models  are radically transforming our 
economies  and reshap ing industry sectors  around the world . Digital offerings from 
dominant technology companies  including Amazon, Apple, Facebook, Google,  and 
Microsoft  are indivisible from most people’s lives in Western societ ies. Such d igital 
platform businesses – even relatively smaller c ompanies like Airbnb  with about 6,000 
employees and a market cap of $74 billion  – challenge incumbent enterprises , such as 
Hilton with 173,000 employees and $30B market cap italization  (Yahoo! finance, 2020) , 
in unprecedented ways and achieve  higher  market valuations with significantly fewer  
resources than traditional businesses (Cusumano, Gawer, & Yoffie , 2019 ). Therefore, for 
many businesses , the question is no longer whether they need to innovate  but how . 
One of the strategic imperatives for this digital business transformation is cloud 
computing ( Brynjolfsson, Hofmann, & Jordan , 2010). In the second decade of the cloud, 
the role of software has moved beyond  support ing the business . Digital transformation is 
the business  (Siebel, 2017) . Disney, for example , created Disney  Plus, a completely new 
direct -to-consumer digital business model.  While the total 2020 Disney revenue was 
down 6 percent because of the COVID -19 pandemic  with theme park closures and the 
shift of many movies to 2021 , Disney Plus counted 86+ million global subscribers in its 
first year , driving 41 percent revenue growth in its segment . Disney is now restructuring 
its media  and entertainment  divisions to accelerate its digital strategy by centraliz ing its 
delivery units with Disney Plus . However, Disney also acknowledg es that it will not be 
profitable until 2024  because of additional investment requirements  (Zeitchik, 2020 ).   2 
 In this context,  it is no surprise that lack of resources and knowledge ha ve been 
identified as a critical  challenge for businesses  innovating with cloud infrastructure, 
platform, and application services ( Retana, Forman, Narasimhan, & Niculescu , 2018) . 
Specifically, Lucas , Swanson, and Zmud (2007) ask ed for studies that help us understand 
better how innovat ion with digital technologies  can lead to  capabilities that provide 
sustainable competitive advantage s. 
Problem  Statement  
With the rise of subscription business models for technology companies, it is no 
longer good enough to sell enterprise application software packages ; instead,  technology 
providers need to ensure that customers adopt their solutions and that the expected va lue 
is delivered. In the traditional software license model, the software and what was 
accomplished with it was the customer’s responsibility. After an up -front payment, most 
revenue and profit were  guaranteed for the provider regardless of whether the cus tomer 
received value or not. Today , technology provider s realize that sustainable business 
success requires an organizational culture, determination, and strategic vision that 
prioritizes enduring customer value (Kumar & Reinartz, 2016). In other words, the 
provider needs to ensure value realization for the  customer  and orch estrate a simplified 
customer journey that ensures ongoing satisfaction and an optimal experience as 
recurring revenue streams could be stopped at any point if the service does not meet the 
needs. In response to this challenge, technology  providers are developing customer 
success functions  to drive adoption and usage  (Mehta & Pickens, 2020 ). Lah, Nanus, and 
Stegman (2017) define customer success as a technology provider's function  responsible 
for ensuring t hat customers receiv e the full value and are on track to achiev e their desired   3 
 business outcomes  with the acquired technolog ies. As shown in Figure 1, the customer 
success function  complements the sales function to ensure  full life  cycle engagement  and 
protect renewal revenues . Renewals are critical for long -term profitability. In fact, studies 
by Reichheld and Schefter  (2000)  found that a “5% improvement in  retention increases 
profits by up to 95%.”

Nevertheless , as an emerging  function , there has not been a reliable and proven 
model for technology providers to efficiently drive  customer success at scale ( Mehta & 
Pickens, 2020 ). Despite resource investments , technology providers still struggle with 
subscription renewals and customer busi ness innovation.  The complementary deployment 
of customer success resource s, for example,  could even lead to decreasing customer 
lifetime value . Despite the importance of churn management and the extensive  academic 
work on customer churn and retention in the field of marketing, information systems, and 
operations ( e.g., Hadden, Tiwari, Roy, & Ruta , 2007; Neslin , Gupta, Kamakura, Lu, & 
Mason, 2006), there is a gap whether it is efficient for businesses to target customers at 
risk without knowing if or to what intervention efforts they are responsive (Ascarza, 
2018). Ascar za, Iyengar, and Schleicher  (2016), for example, found that proactive 
  4 
 retentio n campaigns can increase rather than decrease customer churn. Digital 
transformation  studies from Forth, Reichert, de Laubier, and Chakraborty  (2020 ) and 
McKinsey (2018 ), furthermore , found that less than one-third of organizational 
transformation s succeed at improving performance and sustain ing it over time .  
The research of Anderson, Potocnik, and Zhou (2014) , van Knippenberg (2017) , 
and West (2002)  highlight s that understanding factors that enhance  implementation 
success deserves more focus since it is more challenging  than idea generation . Bring ing 
creative ideas to li fe in organizations requires  executive buy -in and the mobilization of 
resources and support, change management, diligence, persistence, and commitment 
(Drucker , 2002 ). Anderson  and colleagues  (2014) summarize  in their state -of-the-science 
review that innovation is more critical  than ever before for organizational survival and 
corporate growth . Moreover,  it is even more relevant  in today’s knowledge -intensive and 
fast-paced business environment with digital competit ors and changing customer needs  
and regulations (van Knippenberg, 2017) . However, the conditions that foster team 
innovation  are not very well understood (Hoever, van Knippenberg, van Ginkel, & 
Barkema, 2012). While the creativity aspect of innovat ion has received much attention in 
research, the contingencies of the relationship between ideation and implementation ha ve 
been identified as a  critical  understudied issue (van Knippenberg, 2017).  
Small and large  businesses  across industries facing the need to innovate rely on 
teams as a predominant way of organizing work (Mathieu, Hollenbeck, van Knippenberg, 
& Ilgen, 2017 ). Although teams play a central  role in the contemporary business 
environment  (Barczak , Griffin, & Kahn , 2009 ; Resick, Murase, Randall, & DeChurch , 
2014 ), innovation research has mainly focused on individual or organizational level of   5 
 analysis and has provided inconclusive results regarding crucial  factors that  give a team 
informational resources , and what motivates  a team to use those resources to drive 
innovati on (Hülsheger , Ander son, & Salgado , 2009; Lucas et al., 2003; Shams, Vrontis, 
Weber, & Tsoukatos, 2018 ; Tzabbar & Vestal, 2018 ; van Knippenberg, 2017 ). 
Furthermore, the extant learning literature has shown the role of organizational learning 
in overcoming knowledge barriers but much less is known about how technology 
provider s' customer success strategies affect the consumption of cloud services (Retana e t 
al., 2018). The pressing  question arising from these insights  puts the spotlight on the 
mechanisms and conditions that facilitate knowledge integration . This leads to my 
research questions . 
Research Questions  
Firstly, I address the question, how and under what conditions are customer 
engagement teams in technology companies likely to benefit from divers e informational 
resources in pursuing innovation challenges?  The central thesis is that team innovation 
benefits from informational resources through its relationship with information 
elaboration and engenders team innovation success only if teams operate in a positive 
climate for innovation. Factors contributing t o a team’s informational resources, such as 
functional background diversity and social networks, are beneficial for team innovation. 
Furthermore, Hoever and colleagues (2012) and Drach -Zahavy and Somech (2001) show  
that knowledge exchange and integration a re conducive to team innovation. Teams 
should be more innovative the more team members bring different perspectives and can 
create novel insight by engaging in information elaboration to address innovation 
challenges. However, team innovation benefits from  informational resources only if the   6 
 team engages in information elaboration . Even if a team possesse s informational 
resources, it is not automatically given that the team uses them. Edmondson and Harvey 
(2018) also found positive and negative effects of d iversity. The equivocal results of team 
diversity point to the presence of moderators , which I study by determining when 
functional diversity is beneficial to team innovation. In that regard , van Ginkel and van 
Knippenberg (2008) suggested that the team climate for innovation positively moderates  
the relationship between team informational resources and information elaboration.  This 
leads me to my next question.  
Secondly, I address  the question , what is the role of boundary spanning , and how 
can it stimulate team innovation success for customer engagement teams?  Building on 
the work of many i nnovation  researchers who highlight the need for organizations to 
manage innovation within and outside the firm to sustain their competitive advantage 
(Alexander & van Knippenberg, 2014; Gawer & Cusumano, 2014; Leifer, McDermott, 
O’Connor, Peters, Rice, & Veryzer, 2000) , the notion of bounda ry spanning is straight 
forward: Teams with members who promote cross -boundary work, in the sense of  
interface collaboration spanning functions or domains, significantly outperform teams 
without such people  (Casciaro, Edmondson, & Jang, 2019 ). This effect is specifically 
relevant for teams working complex service engagements and pursuing  innovation s that 
require tight business and information technology (IT) alignment  (Henderson & 
Venkatraman, 1993 ; Sabherwal, Hirsch heim, & Goles, 2001 ). Kleinbaum and Tushman 
(2007) further  argue that collaboration across organizational units is necessary for 
innovations to be successful .   7 
 Tambe, Hitt, and Brynjolfsson (2012)  found that external focus, decentraliz ation , 
and IT investments are corre lated and demonstrated that firms that combine all three 
practices derive substantially greater benefits from their IT investments. However, on the 
team level , the empirical evidence of boundary spanning success is equivocal , some 
research indicating a facilitating  effect and other s finding an inhibiting effect to 
knowledge exchange  (Tortoriello, Reagans, & McEvily , 2012 ). Ancona and Caldwell 
(1992) , for example,  found that cross -functional teams that engage in external 
communication drive long -term team innovation but also pointed out that specific types 
of boundary spanning such as too much scouting or pure ambassadorial activities without 
task-coordination are related to low er performance.  Boundary -spanning practices  in the 
IT industry , generally speaking,  help to break down silos (Casciaro et al. , 2019) and 
reduce the risk of being “too creative ,” in the sense of solely focusing on idea generation 
without or little thought about planning, prioritization , feasibility, resource mobilization,  
solution adoption , and change management . The integration of information across silos 
helps team s drive business and technology alignment necessary for sustainable 
innovation success (Reynolds & Yetton, 2015).  
Purpose  
My research aim s to understand better  the conditions that foster team innovati on. 
Specifically, developing a deeper level of understanding under what circumstances 
customer engagement teams successfully drive customer innovation with software 
solutions. Team innovation research will benefit from integrating  the knowledge and 
team climate perspectives ( van Knippenberg, 2017).  In this study , I conceptually apply 
van Knippenberg’s (2017) model of team innovation shown in  Figure 2 that flows from   8 
 integrating  these perspectives in the combined field of business / IT alignment and 
customer retention.  
 
Figure 2   Knowledge Integration / Team Climate Model of Team Innovation  
By building on van Knippenberg’s state-of-the-science integrated process  model 
and in cluding the notion of boundary spanning, I seek to further shed light on idea 
implementation and study tea m innovation not only from the perspective of knowledge 
integration and team climate but also from the perspective of the external context in 
which engagement teams operate to bring creative ideas to life  and drive business 
outcomes for the customer . Specifically , I study the impact of functional background 
diversity on team innovation through the team process of information elaboration  
motivated by a shared vision and boundary spanning.  
The causal impact of boundary -spanning practices  on team innovation and the 
contingenc ies inherent in the knowledge integration and team climate model is 
empirically assessed in an applied research approach with a cross -sectional company 
survey that is combined with field experiment data in the context of complex technology 
services engagements of a global enterprise software company, hereinafter referred to as 
the company, and its customers . In doing so, I seek to advance information elaboration  
theory by integrating perspectives and developing  practical guidance for innovation 
  9 
 management , addressing both theoretical and practical  calls to action from Anderson and 
colleagues  (2014) and van Knippenberg (2017)  and f urther a dding rigor to business 
issues around the emerging customer success function s of technology providers and 
developing a deeper level of understanding under what processes and conditions  
customer engagement teams drive customer innovation  with enterprise software  
technology solutions . 
In summary, t he analysis of mechanisms that facilitate team innovation will 
provide insights for three groups of stakeholders. First, the study  contributes to the 
organizational behavior research community by addressing the research gap regarding the 
integration of kno wledge and team climate perspectives. Second, since the deployment of 
cross -functional customer engagement teams is a strategic decision with implications on 
cost structures and profitab ility, the findings are relevant for technology  firms that engage 
collaboratively with their customers to develop effective go-to-market strategies. Third, 
these insights are of interest to organizations work ing with co -innovation partners on 
their digital strategies and seek to optimize their engageme nt model.  Therefore, research 
of the circumstances conducive to team innovation is imperative for business and IT 
practitioners a s well as  organizational behavior , leadership , and organizational 
psychology  academia. In the following section , I summarize an example engagement to 
illustrate  the work of customer engagement teams in an actual , real-life situation . 
An Example from the Construction Industry  
After the mining downturn in 2012, an equipment company faced a changing market 
environment res ulting in business contraction that required cost -cutting measures and 
improvements in efficiency across the business. The human resources (HR) organization   10 
 decided they needed to improve  the way they worked in order to create a sustainable 
business. As a trusted partner in the HR transformation, the technology provider 
company's customer engagement team  helped this organization successfully migrate 
largely paper -based HR processes into an  integrated HR platform giving the business 
increased transparency and enabling insight -driven decisions. Such an HR transformation 
requires executive support, business process adjustments, user training, organizational 
change management, measurement of im provements, and ongoing governance from the 
business perspective and  data quality management , system integration,  security 
management, and support  on the technical side . All such activities were  planned , 
coordinated , and executed  by the engagement team in collaboration with this company . 
As a result, the company has significantly improved HR processes' efficiency , reduced 
manual, time -consuming administrative work, increased employee retention, reduced 
lead times to fill open positions through automated HR processes, and repositioned HR 
from an administrative to a strategic business function. The chief human resou rce officer 
explains that “we see a shift now in how the HR team is seen by the business and by the 
board. We are no longer being seen a s a cost to the business or back -office support. 
There is being a shift because of the efficiencies we gain now with the  new operating 
model. Now we are much more seen as a partner for the generation  of revenue and the 
generation of profit as opposed to just the administrative cost center.”  
Assumptions  
There are several assumptions and restrictions  that influence my research  
objectives, the literature review , and the methodological research design.  First and 
foremost, t he findings will only represent teams from one technology provider company    11 
 operating with customers in the U.S.  and Canada.  Unlike prior team research studies, 
which often analyzed team s in educational or health care settings  with perceptual 
outcome measures  (Drach -Zahavy & Somech, 2001 ; Fay, Borrill, Amir , Haward, &  
West, 2006 ), I analyze cross -functional customer engagement teams  working with 
customer s across industries with an objective measure ment method  for team innovation.  
Regarding  the experimental part of the research design , it must be noted that despite the 
company's best efforts  and an initial random  assignment  between the treatment and 
control group , not all selected customer s did agree to program participation . Therefore,  
the company experiment's design, which in part I build my data analysis on, is considered 
a quasi -experimental study design with minor imper fections that often come with real -life 
customer engagement situations . And then , of course,  there is the global COVID -19 
crisis , which impacted the second half of the company experiment . I included these 
aspects in the customer control conditions  to ensure that it does not bias results.  
Furthermore,  the company experiment focus ed on existing customers  only and did not 
include engagement situations with prospects . For a detailed description , please see the 
section data and sample  in the methods chapter . 
Finally, while I am focusing on the two main themes of knowledge integration 
and team climate in management and organizational behavior research , the broad field of 
team creativity and innovation cannot be reduced entirely  to these two perspective s only. 
Other perspectives  and topics , such as team leadership , team composition , cross -cultural 
differences , and team dynamics , are outlined in the future research section.     12 
 CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW  
Theoretical Background  
This study builds on the social categorization theory that takes similarities and 
differences as a basis for categorizing individuals into groups and focuses on relational 
aspects of group processes (Turner et al., 1987 c ited in van Knippenberg , De Dreu, & 
Homan , 2004). Van Knippenberg  and colleagues  (2004) , building on this theory, 
developed  the categorization -elaboration model (CEM) that integrates social 
categorization perspectives with decision making in team elaboration and highlights the 
importance of diversity  implications for  team performance . Diversity is defined as 
“differences between individuals on any attribute that may lea d to the perception that 
another person is different from self”  (van Knippenberg et al., 2004: 1008).  I am build ing 
on the belief  in the value of diversity for team innovation  (van Knippenberg, Nishii, & 
Dwertmann, 2020) . However, an objective analysis of the available evidence in the 
diversity literature suggests that it does not consistently engender team creativity and 
innovation (e.g., Edmondson & Harvey, 201 8). Van Knippenberg and Schippers (2007) 
support this conclusion and outline that diversity can not only enhance but also impede  
team performance , primari ly through the advers e effects associated with social 
categorization processes for demographic diversity .  
Building  on diversity theory, I explore the effects of moderators by determining 
under which conditions diversity is beneficial for team innovation. The CEM has been 
proven as a useful framework  to study such moderating influences in an integrate d model 
of diversity and team innovation ( van Knippenberg & Hoever , 201 8). In this study, I 
focus specifically on functional background diversity  since cross -functional teams are   13 
 favorable  for digital business innovation  because of their capacity to engage in complex 
problem solving  (Resick et al., 2014 ). Functional diversity refers to differences between 
group members in their functional background.  
Customer engagement teams are defined as virtual work groups of people with 
different functional expertise sharing the responsib ility for group -level tasks to explore 
new business opportunities and apply enterprise software and technology solutions by 
engaging with their customers (Kozlowski & Bell, 2003). Exploration includes problem 
identification focused on customer needs, ideation of solutions that address the needs and 
problems, and eventually the implementation  of those solutions to address the identified 
customer need s (March, 1991) . Within technology providers , such teams of knowledge 
workers are typically referred to as account teams and comprise people with cross -
functional backgrounds. Now increasingly , account teams include team members 
performing customer success functions, such as deployment planning, feature adoption, 
user training, guidance for optimal solution usage , support , ongoing system health 
monitoring, and results tracking  (Lah et al., 2017; Mehta & Pickens, 2020) . These cross -
functional teams include members with different functional backgrounds, such as 
marketing, HR, logistic , and IT.  
The following  literature review will discuss the most recent and relevant 
published research and is grounded  on the literature review by v an Knippenberg (2017)  
and van Knippenberg and Hoever (in press ) that are arguabl y the most eminent  review s 
of team creativity and innovation literature in the recent history.  Van Knippenberg (2017)

Team Innovation  
Innovation is defined as the development and implementation of ideas in an 
organization (Edmondson, 2003). Within an organization, teamwork  bring s together 
different resources , expertise,  and perspectives to solve complex business problem s 
(Thayer, Petruzz elli, & McClurg, 2018). Following West and Farr (1990), I further define 
team innovation as a team’s capability to generate technology -based ideas new for the 
adopting customer and put these ideas into practice such that they achieve the desired 
business outcomes. It is essential  not to  confuse innovation with invention. For team 
innovation , it is not required to generally create something new but rather the intentional 
introduction and application of enterprise software and technology solutions that are new 
for the adopting custo mer and are designed to improve processes, products, or procedures 
within that organization (West & Farr, 1990: 9). Such ideas, processes, or products could 
already exist outside the adopting organization (van Knippenberg, 2017). In fact, since 
the unit of  analysis in this study is the customer engagement team, most innovations of 
such teams do exist and have been already implemented in similar forms in other   15 
 organizations.  That is w hy many practitioners in the field see k engagement with 
technology provider s to benefit from  such insight that is often regarded as best practice s. 
The need and importance of best practices are also evident in the role of communities, 
such as the Technology Services Industry Association, and analyst firms, such as Gartner 
or International Data Corporation , that pro vide IT advice and research for leaders across 
industries  to achieve their goals and optimize their businesses . The development , 
documentation,  and sharing of such best prac tices help drive progress in the entire 
technology industry and specifically speak to research calls for action addressing the 
importan ce of idea implementation (Anderson et al., 2014 ; van Knippenberg , 2017).  
Ultimately, it is only through the successful implementation of ideas and concepts that 
the desired business outcomes are achieved  (West , 2002) . 
In team creativity and innovation research , idea implementation is often  implied 
and combined with idea development. Apart from  brainstorming,  which is geared 
towards idea generation  specifically , most research assumes that creative and innovative 
ideas somehow automatically lead to innovation , conceiving both creativity and 
innovation as  integral parts of the same process . In that respect, Alexander and van 
Knippenberg (2014) highlight that teams need to mobilize resources and collaborate with 
stakeholders outside the team to drive innovation success. They specifically identi fy the 
roles of ambidextrous leadership and reflexive team processes as crucial  success factors 
for radical innovation. All of which take dedicated team efforts and by no means happen 
by themselves . From that perspective , the study of Somech and Drach -Zahavy (2013) is a 
good example that differentiates phases of creativity and implementation  by studying 
team innovation in primary care teams .   16 
 For IT-based innovations to become successfully adopted by an organization , it is 
essential  to realize that critical  challenges are often  beyond the team and typically require 
executive support  and collaboration across divisions , such as HR and IT (Leifer et al., 
2000 ). To rise to the challenge of adopting IT innovations, Swanson and Ramiller (1997) 
proposed the  conceptual  framework  of an “organizing vision ” that captures what a 
specific innovation is suitable  for, how it works , and how  it should be implemented. 
Through such a framework  and the related community discourse , teams can better 
translate IT-related buzzwords, such as artificial intelligence , into a real understanding of 
new IT , including its requi rements, design, applicability , and use. Charting a successful 
IT implementation requires not only new organizational designs but also the continuous 
reflection of own experiences and the evolving application in the industry  (Swanson & 
Ramiller, 1997) . All of which creates an intensive need for knowledge integration.  
Therefore, cross -boundary teamwork became a prevalent  practice to achieve innovation 
success (Edmondson & Harvey, 2018).  Moser, Dawson, and West (2019) , for example, 
found that information sharing and helping were positively related to innovation . 
In information systems research , it is well -documented that besides a firms’ 
internal capabilities, executive support, empowerme nt, operational excellence , and 
culture , it is the technical know -how that affects the timing of the adoption of a new 
information system and the extent of usage and diffusion after the initial stages (Seddon, 
Calvert, & Yang, 2010). Therefore, beginning w ith the knowledge integration and 
management perspective works well with the vast organizational behavior literature and  
aligns well with research in management information systems (Alegre, Sengupta, & 
Lapiedra, 2011; Andreeva & Kianto, 2012).    17 
 Knowledge Integration Perspective  
By applying th e knowledge integration  lens, team innovation is seen to follow 
from integrating  diverse information, ideas, perspectives, and knowledge. Bell,  Villado,  
Lukasik, and Briggs  (2011) and van Knippenberg, van Ginkel, and Homan (2013) argue 
that having a greater variety of functional  backgrounds in a team is beneficial for team 
innovation because such teams can utilize different pools of information, knowledge, and 
experiences. Different per spectives can then lead to discussion  and better integration of 
information in a collaborative team process resulting in a deeper under -standing of the 
problem, finding a broader range of potential solutions , and alternative implementation 
approaches , ultimately resulting in increased team innovation.  This logic is supported by 
meta -analyses  of van Dijk, van Engen, and van Knippenberg  (2012 ). 
Team Diversity  
Diversity research has a long history , and already over three decades ago , Bantel 
and Jackson (1989)  found that functional diversity in top management teams predict ed 
organizational innovation.  In 1992 , Ancona and Caldwell (1992) presented their research 
of boundary spanning practices in research and development (R&D) teams demonstrating 
that functional diversity enhances team innovation  through  team external communication . 
Ancona and Caldwell (1992) defined boundary spanning as external communication , 
including ambassadorial, scouting , and task-coordinat or activities. Ambassadorial 
activities include accessing and mobilizing critical resources and persuading others to 
support the team’s goals. Scouting involves scanning for ideas and gathering information 
about technologies , competition, and the industry . Coordination work embraces activities 
to create alignment with the customer and establis h a feedback loop.    18 
 Later , more supporting evidence for diversity 's main effects w as found confirming 
their research . Tzabbar and Vestal (2015), also studying R&D teams, found a curvilinear 
relationship between geographic dispersion and novelty of innovation that was moderated 
by relational strength and status asymmetry. However, research on boundary spanning  
was long evolving  in tw o parallel , almost independent research streams  (Schotter, 
Mudambi, Doz, & Gaur , 2017) . One stream studies boundary spanning as an 
organizational function (Zhao & Anand, 2013) , and the other focusing on individuals 
with specific characteristics  and tasks . In my research , I am focused on the individual and 
team -related aspects of boundary spanning and build  on Kleinbaum  and Tushman’s 
(2007) definition , who defined boundary spanners  as people who act as knowledge 
intermediaries among individuals within and outside the organization.  Casciaro  and 
colleagues  (2019) found that diverse teams with people who promote cross -boundary 
work significantly outperformed teams without such people. However, Zhao and Anand 
(2013)  argue that boundary spanning is only efficient for tangible , discrete knowledge 
and that more complex forms of collective knowledge require  a set of inter -unit ties.  
Nevertheless, the organizational behavior literature on boundary spanning has 
long recognized the informational advantages associated with access to external sources 
of knowledge (Tortoriello & Krackhardt, 2010) , which should help  teams to drive 
business and IT alignment that is necessary for sustainable team innovation success 
(Reynolds & Yetton, 2015).  Such an information advantage resulting in better access to 
knowledge, experiences, and resources shou ld amplify functional diversity's effects  on 
team innovation.  Drach -Zahavy and Somech (2001) support this conclusion and argue 
that f actors contributing to informational resources are conducive to innovation.    19 
 Cummings (2004) argues that team performance increases when structurally 
diverse teams engage in external knowledge sharing, including but not limited to 
exchang ing feedback with customers . Furthermore, Moser and colleagues (2019)  
presented evidence that teams were more innovative with greater occupational diversity 
at higher levels of information sharing and helping  behavior in the team . This leads me to 
my first hypothesis : 
Hypothesis 1. Functional diversity is positively related to team innovation, and this 
relationship is stronger for teams that practice boundary spanning.  
Information Integration  
As part of the knowledge integration theme , van Knippenberg (2017) put primary 
focus on information elaboration as the main process driving team innovation  and making 
the case that the integration of diverse information and perspectives enables teams to be 
innovative ; speaking to the very core reason of the team’s existence  and how it can be 
more innovative than any of the team members by themself . Van Knippenberg (2017) 
proposed the mediating role of information elaboration to create synergies from 
informational resources and defined it as the team process of “sharing, discussion, a nd 
integration of task -relevant information and perspectives .” From a conceptual 
perspective , as shown in Figure 2, constructs that provide the team informational 
resources can be seen as input s for the elaboration process , which is  then driving team 
innovation. This is also evident in diversity research on team networks (e.g., Baer , 2010; 
Balkundi & Harrison, 2006 ) and team composition. Mathieu, Tannenbaum, Donsbach, 
and Alliger  (2014 ) show that distributions of team member profiles facilitate team 
processes and effectiveness. This is not only co nsistent with findings from the diversity   20 
 literature but also points out that having access to informational resources does not mean 
that the team is naturally using them in the right way.  Specifically, the task -coordination 
aspect of boundary spanning cre ates a focus on better alignment with customer needs 
through feedback loops and thus helps teams thrive with information elaboration.  
Research has shown that accessing knowledge from across boundaries is an imperative  
driver of team innovati on (Ancona & Caldwell, 1992 ; Reagans & Zuckerman, 2001 ). 
Further, in the context of my research, boundary spanning is explicitly 
operationalized as boundary spanning between the account team and the customer , which 
is intended to bring  in information about the  client and create a focus on the client ’s 
perspective  to not only better understand the core issues at hand but also increase 
software us age to address these issues . This speaks to the broader issue in the software 
and technology industry where innovation is not just about idea generation or bringing 
products to market  but to ensure implementation (West, 2002) and customer usage . Many 
companies  directly work with clients to develop solutions  specifically for the client . The 
business interest is that the client uses the solution  and th at this  result s in a continued 
relationship. In that sense , boundary -spanning can help to collaboratively develop a 
mutual  understanding and address all aspects of the business  relationship , including the 
strategic direction , business model and process alignment, solution functionality, IT 
organization and implementation partner alignment, risk mitigation, as well as services 
and support  for ongoing operations . For fast value rea lization, boundary spanning is 
critical to bring in the customer's perspective to understand what users want and  include 
the perspective of implementation partner (s) who typically have accountability for the 
initial adoption .   21 
 With a different  research focus but similar context, Harvey , Johnson, Roloff, and 
Edmondson (2019)  studied sales teams of a large financial services firm and explored 
how they coordinate their activities, share information about customers’ needs and issues . 
They  found that the relationship between team learning orientation and team learning was 
mediated by psychological safety , but only for teams with low open -mindedness. In that 
regard , it is also important to note that a common and widely used practice of innovation -
focused account teams is design thinking , which enables the team to prioritize the user 
experience  (Plattner , Meinel, & Leifer , 2011 ). For example, Seidel & Fixson’s (2013) 
research  outlines the advantages and limitation s of design methods , explicit ly 
demonstrating how high team reflexivity  in the form of debating ideas, methods, and 
concept changes was associated with more successful outcomes. Although design 
thinking is essentially a form of boundary spanning  including communicat ion and 
collaboration across functions and with other companies , it is currently not linked the 
boundary spanning literature beyond foundational concepts, such as focus groups , 
prototyping,  or brainstorming to generate ideas  (Micheli, Wilner, Bhatti, Mura, & 
Beverland, 2019 ). 
Design thinking is a  design -led approach to human -centered innovation based on 
the notion that value can only be created if the needs of the people who use the 
innovation are understood clearly.  It relates to the principles of experiential learning and 
is practice d as an iter ative process of learning by doing, often including prototyp es and 
early market tests of a minimum viable product  (Cousins, 2018 ). Design thinking helps 
obtain a multi -perspective comprehension of complex and ambiguous problem s and 
develop feasible, desirable, and economically viable solutions (Plattner et al., 2012).   22 
 Venkatesh , Digerfeldt -Mansson, Brunel, and Chen (2012) argue that design thinking is a 
recipe for innovation success.  Gruber, de Leon, George, and Thompson (2015) f urther 
discuss using  a design -process  to innovate and focus on customer needs as a source of 
information. I argue that this is especially critical for complex services engagement 
where IT and business alignment is critical  over time (Sabherwal et al., 2001 ). Therefore, 
by drawing on the se notion s, I propose:  
Hypothesis 2. Functional diversity interacts with boundary spanning such that it is 
positively related to information elaboration for teams that practice boundary 
spanning . 
Building on the learning literature, Tzabbar and Vestal (2015) elaborate that 
knowl edge integration in a team requires not only access to unique information but also 
requires the team’s ability to transform individual information into valuable innovation. 
Bunderson and Sutcliffe (2003) provided consistent evidence and  found that an 
appro priate emphasis on learning improves team effectiveness . The main argument  here, 
further building on van Knippenberg’s (2017) propositions,  is that knowledge exchange 
and integration are conducive to team innovation. This is consistent with the finding of 
Ancona and Caldwell (1992) that external communication plays a mediating role  and 
demonstrates that the diversity of a  team’s external network can also be understood as an 
informational resource for the information elaboration process . 
Somech and Khalaili (2014) found a positive relationship between inter-team goal 
interdependence , functional diversity, and team innovation that was mediated by scouting 
and coordinating boundary -spanning activities. Teams should be more innovative the 
more team members bring different perspectives and can create novel insight by engaging   23 
 in informat ion elaboration to address innovation challenges. Drach -Zahavy and Somech 
(2001 ) presented supporting evidence for a positive relationship between functional 
diversity and team innovation mediated by team interaction processes that reflected the 
degree to which team members engaged in information exchange, learning, motivating, 
and negotiating . To enable team innovation success, customer engagement teams need to 
engage in boundary -spanning activities, such as exchanging informat ion, know -how, and 
customer feedback  (Cummings, 2004) as well as coordination (Drach -Zahavy & Somech, 
2010; Marks , DeChurch, Mathieu, Panzer, & Alonso,  2005). In that regard , Faraj and 
Sproull (2000) also found a strong relationship between expertise coordination and team 
performance.  Tortoriello and Krackhardt (2010) presented somewhat conflicting 
evidence . They  showed that informational advantages of cross -organizational boundary 
spanning have no positive impact on innovative performance unless there are at least 
three  boundary -spanning connections in a group. Zhang (2016) presented further 
evidence of a positive relationship between functional diversity and team creativity 
mediated by the two mechanism s of knowledge sharing and group cohesion but only for 
long-standing teams.   
Mitchell and Boyle (2015) found that professional diversity enhances team 
innovation through professional identity salience with second -stage moderation through 
open -mindedness norms. Cheung, Gong, Wang, Zhou, and Shi (2016) presented 
conflicting evidence and found a negative effect of functional diversity on team 
innovatio n through knowledge sharing in teams with low trust and no significant 
relationship with higher trust.  Miron -Spektor, Erez, and Naveh (2011) found that a mix of 
creative and conformist team members enhance s a team’s capability to drive radical   24 
 innovation, mediated by team potency.  Somech and Drach -Zahavy (2007) found that 
functional diversity was positively related to team innovation mediated through team 
processes , such as information exchange and learning,  that w as also influence d by 
interaction frequency . Qu and Liu (2017) empirically verified a moderated mediation 
effect of informational diversity faultlines on team creativity through external knowledge 
acquisition and internal knowledge integration moderated by the team ’s focus on 
cooperation.  Schotter and colleagues  (2017) postulate that managing and coordinating 
across organizational boundaries emerged as a critical  capability required for sustainable 
success. A coordinated and structured approach to innovation is needed  to help teams 
with crucial  activities, such as planning, prioritization, resource mobilization, change 
management , business / IT alignment, and  feedback . Specifically,  on the topic of 
feedback , the research of Hoever, Zhou, and van Knippenberg (2018) provides 
fascina ting insights into how positive and negative feedback  enhance s creativity at the 
team level. They found a moderated mediation relationship between feedback valence 
and team creativity through information elaboration a nd generative processing with first 
stage moderation by informational diversity . Based on these arguments, I suggest the 
following hypothesis of a moderated mediation model  where information elaborat ion 
mediate s the relationship between functional diversity and team innovation , and 
boundary -spanning activities moderate the knowledge integration process . 
Hypothesis 3. The positive relationship between functional diversity and team 
innovation through information elaboration is moderated by boundary spanning such 
that it is stronger for teams that practice boundary spanning .   25 
 Team Climate Perspective  
The second major theme in team innovation research is the team climate 
perspective (Hülsheger et al., 2009; West & Farr, 1990 ; van Knippenberg, 2017 ). Van 
Knippenberg and colleagues  (2004)  postulated that team diversity benefits  are contingent 
on factors motivating information elaboration  and factors preventing diversity’s negative 
effects of social categorization . Schneider and Reichers  (1983 : 22) define d organizational 
climate as “the shared perception s of organizational policies, practices, and 
procedures.”  For innovation research , West (1990) shaped the team climate for 
innovation by defining it as the perception of whether innovation is valued and supported 
within the team. Anderson and West (1998) address the climate for work group 
innovation with a four-factor  theory  and developed a team climate inventory  (TCI) . They 
identified vision, participative safety, task orientation, and support for innovation as 
climate factors associated with team innovativeness.  This logic suggests that innovati ve 
outcomes are driven by  team members who share a commitment to common goals and 
excellen ce and believe  that they can participate safely in team discussions contributing to 
a worthwhile innovation . Based on TCI, Kivim äki and Elovainio (1999)  developed and 
validated a shorter 14 -item version  of the TCI and demonstrated an acceptable item 
coverage and predictive validity compared  to the original version.  Bain, Mann, and 
Pirola -Merlo (2001) found supporting evidence of a positive relationship between the 
climate for innovation and team innovation . The relationship between these factors and 
team innovation has also bee n confirmed by the meta -analysis  of Hülsheger and 
colleagues (2009) . Specifically showing that the climate for innovation is positively 
related to team innovation  in the sense that  teamwork with a focus on shared goals is   26 
 important for team  innovation . Lisak , Erez, Sui, and Lee (2016) found a positive 
influence of team -shared innovation goals . Peltokorpi and Hasu (2014) , adding additional 
nuance , found that participative safety was more relevant for team innovation with 
increasing team size . In my  research , I focus on the moderating effects of shared vision 
following the logic that a shared commitment to team objectives is positively related to 
team innovation.  “Vision is an idea of a valued outcome, which represents a higher -
order goal and motivating force at work” that provides the basis for action within the 
team (West, 1990: 310).  Anderson and West (1 998) summarize that for a team to commit 
to a shared vision , it requires  clarity of the valued outcome , acceptance within the team , 
and attainability , meaning  a relative ly clear line of sight on how to achieve  the desired 
innovation.  Fay and colleagues  (2006) support the argument and postulate that a shared 
vision implies the pursuit of appealing and worthwhile goals . The research from Pearce 
and Ensley (2004) is a good example in that regard showing not only the  positive impact 
of a shared vision on innovation  effectiveness  but also demonstrating effectively that 
shared vision plays a key role in the team innovation process . By applying the same 
logic , Chen , Farh, Campbell -Bush, Wu, and Wu (2013)  focused on support for 
innovation and found a positive relationship with team innovation. Eisenbeiss, van 
Knippenberg, and Boerner (2008) further studied interactions between components of 
climate for innovation and found, besides important insights on  transformational 
leadership , that team innovation only benefited from support for  innovation in team s that 
had a climate for excellence.  In yet another spin, Lyubovnikova, West, Dawson, and 
West (2018) found a positive relationship bet ween shared objectives and team 
productivity and innovation  by exploring the mediating process of generating shared   27 
 objectives . Gong, Kim, Lee, and Zhu (2013) furthermore found that learning goal 
orientation is positively associated with team innovation. In summary, t eams are more 
likely to successfully implement idea s when they have a shared vision giving them  focus 
and direction and allowing them to develop appropriate methods and practical steps.  
Supporting this notion, Fay and colleagues  (2006) found a positive  interaction effect of 
team climate and diversity predicting team innovation . More s pecifically, t he quality of 
team processes, operationalized via a shared vision, a safe climate, working closely,  and 
taking a reflexive approach to work, moderate d the relationship between team diversity  
and innovation such that innovation qualit y benefited from increasing levels of diversity 
only in the case of good team processes. As a result, the following hypothesis is 
suggested : 
Hypothesis 4. Functional diversity is positively related to team innovation, and this 
relationship is stronger for t eams that share a  common vision . 
Further building on van Knippenberg (2017) and team learning theories that  
emphasize the importance of social structures required for effective communication and 
knowledge sharing ( Argote, McEvily, & Reagans, 2003; Tzabbar & Vestal, 2015), it is 
argued that the team climate for innovation moderates the relationship between 
informational resources and information elaboration.  In that sense , the two main themes 
of knowledge integration  and team climate complement each other. T he team climate 
comprises factors that motivate the team to use informational resources in the information 
elaboration process to drive innovation.  By differentiating idea generation and 
innovation, Somech and Drach -Zahavy (2013) found evidence for a moderated mediation 
model showing that both functional background diversity and creativity of the team   28 
 member s predicted innovation through team creativity, but only for teams that operated 
in a favorabl e climate for innovation.  This provides further evidence that the positive 
effects of informational resources are moderated by factors encouraging and stimulating 
their use . 
Similarly, the research from van Ginkel and van Knippenberg (2008) suggests 
that the team climate for innovation has a positive moderating influe nce on the 
relationship between team informational resources and information elaboration. If team 
members can speak up and take risks , they can generate ideas and  identify alternative 
problem -solving approaches when facing barriers during the implementatio n. In contrast, 
if they do not feel they can contribute freely and speak their mind , then diversity has no 
positive effect on elaboration (Edmondson & Lei, 2014).  Rousseau, Aubé, and Tremblay  
(2013) , also looking  at antecedents  of the team climate , found that goal commitment and 
support for innovation  were positively related to team innovation , and both were 
predicted by team coaching.  This leads to my fifth hypothesis , framing  these insights for 
a shared vision  and arguing that shared vision motivates integrating  different functional 
perspectives in the team information elaboration process . 
Hypothesis 5. Functional diversity interacts with shared vision such that it is 
positively related to information elaboration for te ams that share a common vision.  
In further building out the knowledge integration  / team climate model of team 
innovation, like in  hypothesis 3 , I argue that t eam innovation benefits from functional 
background diversi ty when  team s engage in information elaboration and operate in a 
positive climate for innovation (van Knippenberg, 2017 ). In that regard, Hoever and 
colleagues’ (2012) research  is a good example that provides experimental  evidence  for a   29 
 moderated mediation effect of diversity of perspectives on team creativity through 
information elaboration  that is  conditional on member perspective -taking.  
Further extending team creativity research, Bodla, Tang, Jiang, and Tian (2018) 
presented in their study  evidence that an inclusive climate moderates the diversity -
creativity relationship , mediated by knowledge sharing . They also provid ed specific 
recommend ations for managers to cultivate an inclusive climate  by appreciating 
difference, providing equal opportunities, and involving team members in decision 
making as a highly inclusive climate will mitigate the advers e effects of demographic 
diversity . 
In related IT research, Sharma, Mihas, and Kankanhalli (2014) explored how 
organizations can create value from big data and business analytics . They  argue that 
managers need to transform their decision -making and resource allocation processes to 
capture the value of business analytics. Kukenberger  and D'Innocenzo (2020) provide d 
further evidence for moderation effects showing that, on the one side , shared leadership 
benefits from functional diversity in team s operating in a cooperative climate. On the 
other side , demographic diversity harmed  shared leadership for teams working in  a less 
cooperative climate. Resick and colleagues  (2014), in a similar direction but further 
exploring second -stage moderation effects,  showed that team s benefit from information 
elaboration , specifically in turbulent environments.  More formally, I hypoth esize the 
following : 
Hypothesis 6. The positive relationship between functional diversity and team 
innovation through information elaboration is moderated by shared vision such that 
it is stronger for teams that share a common vision .   30 
 The above -presented research  studies are c onsistent and well -aligned with the 
knowledge integration and team climate perspective (van Knippenberg, 2017) and team 
diversity theory (van Knippenberg et al., 2004). The discussed evidence and findings 
show  significant relationships between functional diversity  and team innovation  with 
mediation through information elaboration or similar constructs and moderation  by team 
climate and other factors . Depending on the moderat ing conditions , diversity can have 
positive or negative effects  on information elaboration . Specifically, if teams do not 
operate in a favorabl e climate for innovation  and lack motivation for teamwork , then 
social categorization processes  can prevail , leading to communication breakdowns , 
disruption,  and conflict . Whereas , if team members are motivated to integrate diverse 
perspective s, diversity is a positive force driving  team innovation.

Research Design  
Since research on team innovation is maturing in terms of operationalization of 
constructs, integration of perspectives and theories , as well as the availability of well -
grounded building blocks (van Knippenberg, 2017), c hoosing a quantitative research 
approach appear ed to be appropriate and allows for better gen eralization of results  
(Shadish, Cook, & Campbell, 2002 : 348). I utilized a  cross -sectional company survey 
(positivist, quantitative approach) to gather information about the shared vision of team 
members, information elaboration team processes , and validate team member functional 
backgroun ds along with certain control  variables , such as  team  tenure and executive 
support,  that are explained later on in this section  together with all other measures . I 
developed a new survey instrument for the data collection  by reusing empirically 
validated  measurement scales for the specified constructs  (see Appendix A) . 
To further establish causal relationships  for the impact of boundary spanning , I 
obtained information from the company's field experiment  and combined it with the 
survey results. T he experiment was conducted from Janu ary through  July 2020 and 
involve d 162 customer engagement teams working actively with North American 
customers  across industries.  I used the  experiment team innovation data from January as 
baseline and July data as  a result . The survey was deployed from May 26 through  July 
and was closed before the July team innovation outcome  measurement on August 7, 
2020.  With the combined survey and experiment data , I was able to test the boundary -
spanning assertions and their impact on team innovation through  a quasi -experimental 
intervention that introduc ed an additional team role charged with boundary -spanning    33 
 practices . The m ain tasks of the boundary -spanning role were  coordinating resources 
across departments, focusing  on the customer’s business outcomes, relationship 
management, ongoing process governanc e, and communication. For further details about 
the intervention , please refer to the measure s section. As a result, the company  expected 
that teams that receive d the treatment demonstrate more successful team innovation —
facilitated by a better understanding of the product , targeted customer onboarding, end-
user training, and result s-oriented  solution adoption . Further, in the sense of  overall better 
relationship s with customer s, it was expected that as a result , customers are more likely to 
accept and implement the ir solution s in full . The research and data collection timeline are

Measure s 
Functional Background Diversity  
Based on my theoretical approach and in alignment with previous research ( e.g., 
Kukenberger & D'Innocenzo , 2020; Zhang, 2016 ), I operationalized the diversity of team 
member functional background s with Blau’s index of heterogeneity normalized for 
varying team sizes as recommended by Biemann and Kearney (2010 : 585) across the 
following eleven categories of business function s: Distribution & Logistics, Finance & 
Accounting, Human Resources, Information Technology, Marketing & Communications, 
Production, Research & Development, Supply Chain & Operations, Sales & Customer   38 
 Service, Sourcing & Purchasing, Strategy & Business Devel opment .  I calculated the 
diversity index for each team based on available survey responses and company records 
for team members that did not submit their survey . The index ranges from zero indicating 
no functional diversity to one indicating maximum diversity of functional backgrounds 
among team members.   
Boundary Spanning Treatment  
Teams with treatment status received an additional team member that was 
charged with boundary -spanning activities . These activit ies include ambassadorial 
activitie s to persuade stakeholders to support the team’s goals , access  and mobilize 
critical resources . Scouting activities to gather relevant customer and market information 
and task -coordinator activities to orchestrate key team processes focusing  on the 
customer (Ancona & Caldwell, 1992). The boundary spanner's main tasks  were 
coordinating resources across departments, focusing  on the customer’s business 
outcomes , gathering information , ongoing process governance , relationship management,  
and communication . In other words, the  overall orchestration of the implementation and 
adoption process by understanding the fundamental  problems to be solved, planning  the 
best solution with empathy for the customer, and creating an inclusive team climate that 
involves everyone’s perspective . The boundary -spanning team members were trained for 
this specific  customer engagement methodology before introducing them  to the team and 
their customer s. Upon the beginning of their engagement, the boundary spanners were 
tasked to organize a customer relationship assessment and with it reset the collaboration 
no matter in which engagement phase the customer was at the time (for example, 
exploration of new solutions or adoption of already acquired solutions). With the training ,   39 
 the company ensured  that all boundary spanners operated under the same set of 
guidelines  and operating p arameters.  It is also noteworthy that the company did a pilot 
test of the boundary -spanning intervention  with 30 customers  in 2019 , which resulted in 
several refinements of the engagement methodology  and the training material s that were 
later utilized in the 2020  field experiment . 
The boundary -spanning treatment was measured with a binary variable separating 
treatment and control grou p wherein zero identifie d the control group and one the 
treatment group .  
Shared Vision  
I adapted  six items from Anderson and West (1998 : 241) as defined in their team 
climate inventory  to measure shared vision , for example , “How clear are you about what 
your team’s objectives are? ” and “To what extent do you think they are useful and 
appropriate objectives? ” Team members were asked to rate each item for their team on a 
7-point scale ranging from “not at all” to “completely.” Cronbach’s alpha of .9 5 indicated 
excellent internal homogeneity and reliability. The calculated agreement index median 
Rwg of .87 and intraclass correlations ICC (1) of .74 and ICC (k) of .95 justified aggregation 
to the team level (LeBreton & Senter, 2008).  
Information Elaboration  
To assess information elaboration,  I reused the scale of van Knippenberg, van 
Ginkel, Stam, Giessner,  van Balen, and Ghorbani (2019) . Sample items include  “In our 
team members complement each other by sharing their knowledge ” and “ We discuss 
alternatives before making decisions .” As part of the survey, respondents were asked to 
indicate their agreement to these items within their team on a 5 -point scale ranging from   40 
 “strongly disagree” to “strongly agree.” Cronbach’s alpha of .9 6 indicated excellent 
internal homogeneity and reliability. The calculated agreement index median Rwg of .87 
and intraclass correlations ICC (1) of .80 and ICC (k) of .96 justified aggregation to the team 
level (LeBreton & Senter, 2008).  
Team Innovation  
Team innovation was measured as the usage of an enterprise software technology 
solution within the adopting customer organization. The utilization of the implemented 
technology solution was represented as a percentage of the actual metered usage over the 
purchased software entitlement. For e xample, if a customer purchased 500 users of a 
particular  solution and 100 users a ctually used it, the team innovation index  would be 20 
percent. The utilization percentages were calculated for all participating customers with 
an analytics report of the company . They  were made available for the beginning 
(baseline) and at the end of the measurement period.  The index ranges from zero 
indicating no use to one indicating full use. In case a customer use d multiple software 
solutions integrated into one business process , then the team innovation index was 
calculated as the mean across all solutions.  
Controls  
I included the following control variables in my analyses  to partial out their 
effects on team innovation : Team size, executive support , team tenure , and experience , as 
well as several customer attributes , such as  industry , business familiarity , annual revenue , 
and COVID -19 pandemic impact . 
Team size was measured as the sum of core team members.  The company 
separate s between the core and extended team . By contrast,  members of the extended   41 
 team only join the engagement on a temporary  or as-needed basis  and do not , for 
example , participate in regular team meetings . Accordingly, I focused on the core team  in 
my analysis . I included team size as a control variable because it is often found in team 
research that it influences  team performance  (Fay et al. , 2006; Hülsheger et al., 2009 ; 
Moser et al., 2019 ). Research  evidence suggest s that smaller and larger teams differ in 
many important ways . While larger teams can enjoy  access to more resources , higher 
levels of diversity,  and may appear more legitim ate, they can also experience more 
coordination and communication challenges, member  task conflict , and less cohesion 
(Mathieu, Maynard, Rapp, & Gilson, 2008 ; Moreland, Levine, & Wingert, 1996  in Witte 
& Davis, 2014 ). 
Executive sponsorship  and top management support have been identified as 
critical success factors for innovation  with information systems (e.g., Chandler & 
Thomas, 2015; Lucas  et al. , 2007 ; Shao , Feng, & Hu, 2017 ; Sharma & Yetton, 2003 ), 
specifically because of resources intensi ve implementation projects and the ongoing need 
for end-user support  and training. Executive sponsors are typically important not only 
during the initial stages of a customer engagement to ensure alignment and help mobilize 
required resources but are also critical for ongoing govern ance, business reviews, conflict 
resolution, and prioritization  (Drucker, 2002) . Furthermore, van Meerkerk and Edelenbos 
(2017) found an indirect positive relationship between executive support and boundary 
spanning. Therefore,  I also controlled for differ ences from executive support . I have 
asked team members to rate how pleased they are with the executive sponsorship of their 
customer engagement  using a 5 -point scale ranging from “extremely displeased ” to 
“extremely pleased. ”   42 
 Team tenure  was also included as a control variable since it has been identified as 
a critical  component in team effectiveness model s. Gonzalez‐Mulé , Cockburn , 
McCormick, and Zhao  (2019) , for example,  found that team tenure was positively related 
to team performance. Zhang (2016) furthermore found that team longevity positively 
moderated the relationship between functional diversity and team creativity  through 
knowledge sharing and group cohesion.  
Following the group development theory of Kozlowski, Gully, Nason, and Smith  
(1999) , I calculated  the collective  team tenure as the mean amount of time  individual 
members have been together on the same team , allowing them to gather shared 
experiences . The data has been collected with the survey instrument  wherein  I asked team 
members how long they work ed with their team . Beyond the collective tenure, building 
on Becker’s (1 964 in Gonzalez‐Mulé et al., 2019 : 154) human capital theory , I measured 
the work experience  of the team , which is also referred to as additive team tenure  
capturing the differences  in skills , experience,  and knowledge available within the team . 
This is consistent with team composition research (e.g., Bell, 2007) , where individual 
attributes of team members are aggregated to team -level factor s. Work experience has 
been captured with a simple survey question asking respondents for how many years they 
have been in the workforce . Like Haas (2010) , I calculated it as the mean amount of years 
that team members have spent in the workforce . 
Finally, I controlled for the industry  segment of the customers.  The industry data 
for each team and customer has been obtained from company records . Customers were 
assigned to one of the following six industry clusters: Consumer, manufacturing, energy 
& natural resources, financial services, public sector, and service s industries.    43 
 The industry cluster assignment for each team was coded with dummy variable s. 
Besides the industry, I also controlled for business familiarity  measured as the number of 
days since becoming a customer , its annual revenue , and COVID -19 impact . Specifically,  
for customers impacted by the COVID -19 pandemic , the company data set included an 
indicator flagging customers who requested concessions, such as extended payment terms 
or deferred maintenance payments, based on hardship situations  in their industry , most 
prominently in the airline or hospitality industry . 
Manipulation Checks  
In order to assess the effectiveness of the experimental intervention , I used the 
following direct and indirect manipu lation check s (Ejelöv & Luke, 2020 ). First, I check ed 
directly whether a boundary -spanning team member  was assigned to the t reatment group 
teams . This information was available in the data set of the company experiment. Beyond 
this essential  check , I analyzed whether the boundary spanner s performed  the trained  
intervention . As elaborated abo ve, boundary spanners were trained to start or res et the 
customer engagement with a relationship assessment workshop  and document goals and 
progress in an outcome success plan. In my survey , I ask ed team members to what extent 
they agree or disagree  (5-point scale ranging from “strongly disagree” to “strongly 
agree”) that an outcome success plan was developed by the team and shared with the 
customer  since this represents the most salient and tan gible measure  of the intervention . 
Furthermore, I reused Faraj and Yan’s (2009 : 611) scale to measure boundary 
spanning team activities with my survey instrument. Respondents were asked to indicate 
their agreement to the four items on a 5 -point scale ranging from “strongly disagree” to 
“strongly agree.” For example: “Does your account team value team members for making   44 
 use of their relationships with others on behalf  of the team?” Cronbach’s alpha of .91 
indicated excellent internal homogeneity and reliability. The calculated agreement index  
median  Rwg of .79 and intraclass correlations ICC (1) of .70 and ICC (k) of .90 justified 
aggregation to the team level (LeBreton & Senter, 2008).  
Analytical Approach  
To test the hypothesized model of relationships, I utilized the IBM SPSS software 
version 26 with the PROCESS macro version 3.4 developed by Hayes (2018 a). 
PROCESS uses an ordinary least squares or logistic regression -based  path analysis to 
estimate direct and indirect effects in mediator models, moderation models, and 
conditional effects in moderated mediation models (Hayes, 2018 a). 
Before testing the hypothesized relationships,  I first tested scale reliability and 
validity , calculated  Rwg as an assessment of the within -group agreement and intraclass 
correlation s (ICC s) to ensure the appropriateness of item aggregation  (LeBreton & 
Senter, 2008) . All Rwg values did reach the defined cut -off values of .7 (Klein et al., 
2000 ). I also assessed how well the previously empirically validated  measure ment  items 
represent ed the constructs with a confirmatory factor analysis. According to Kline 
(2005) , I used the following suggested cut -off values : Comparative fit index ( CFI) ≥.9, 
non-normed fit index  (NNFI) ≥.95, root mean square error of approximation (RMSEA) 
<.08, and a non -significant model chi -square p -value >.05. The expected three -factor 
model demonstrate d good model fit (X2(101) = 550.03, p<.01, CFI = .97, NNFI  = .95, 
RMSEA  = .07). I then aggregated individual survey responses to the team level  and 
computed the constructs from the single items . Afterward, I merged the team survey data 
with the team diversity and team innovation experiment data.    45 
 I mean centered non-binary study and control  variables  (Cohen, Cohen , West, & 
Aiken, 2003 ). For creating and testing interaction terms, I relied on PROCESS since it 
automatically does that for the modeled interaction s (Hayes, 2018 a). 
I evaluated t he fit of the model and the statistical significance of the interaction 
terms as well as direct and indirect effects to determine if the hypothes es were supported  
(or, more preci sely, if the null hypotheses could be rejected ). In doing so,  I utilized the 
PROCESS Model 10, which is design ed to estimate, test, and probe interactions as I have 
specified in my research model . It is important to note that in PROCESS Model 10 , 
functional diversity is an additive linear function of boundary spanning and shared vision  
(Hayes, 2018b) , meaning that the change in functional diversity’s effect as boundary 
spanning changes is independent of the value of shared vision . Likewise, t he ch ange in 
functional diversity’s effect as shared vision changes is independent of boundary 
spanning.  The moderated mediation test  is based on an  interval estimate of the parameter 
that links the indirect effect to moderator values, providing  moderated media tion indices  
(Hayes , 2018a). 
I used the setting of 20,000 iterations for the bootstrapping analyses . 
Bootstrapping is considered the right  approach for studies of indirect effects with small 
sample sizes  (MacKinnon, Lockwood, & Williams,  2004 ).    46

One essential  part of the answer is functional diversity. Functional diversity 
provides teams with the knowledge, information, expertise,  and perspectives that help the 
team develop  innovative ideas and overcome implementation challenges  (van

spanning . With it, I confirmed the boundary -spanning treatment as facilitating condition 
and functional diversity as a critical informational resource required for team innovation.  
The overall positive treatment effect set a side;  the treatment has in a way a 
negative downside that reduces the positive influence of functional diversity  on 
information elaboration . I found a significant negative moderation effect of the treatment 
on the relationship between functional diversity on in formation elaboration reported in

information about the client and  focus  on the customer perspective . However, i nstead  of 
the anticipated boost on information elaboration, I found that the treatment rather works 
as an al ternative source of customer information (likely via scouting) . Teams who  
received the treatment relied less on functional diversity as an informational resource.  
Nevertheless, boundary spanning affects not only the quantity  and how much the 
team elaborate s (what I captured with my survey  measure ) but also the elaboration topics   67 
 and content quality . As confirmed in Hypothesis 1, creating this client focus leads to 
better use of functional diversity , and in that way , boundary spanning substitutes for 
informatio n elaboration . As evidenced by the data, the boundary -spanning effect seems to 
be expressed more in the quality of information elaboration than in quantity . 
For the same reasons  and findings concerning  Hypothesis 2 , also Hypothesis 3 is 
rejected.  Although I found a significant moderated mediation effect , the index of 
moderated mediation reported in Table 4 captures a negative effect indicating an overall 
less positive effect of functional diversity on team innovation through information

 CHAPTER 5: DISCUSSION  
In this chapter , I first recap the aim of my study briefl y. Then, I dig into my 
findings' theoretical contribution s and address practical and managerial implications 
focusing on recommendations and how to implement the results. Afterward , I outline the 
limitations and shortcomings of my research design and how future resea rch could 
address these. Finally, I conclude with a punchline summary of my study, the key 
findings, and their importance.  
The goal of my study was to better understand the role of boundary spanning for 
team innovation success and develop a deeper level of understanding under what 
conditions customer engagement teams in technology companies are likely to benefit 
from functional background diversity in pursuing  digital  innovation challenges  together 
with their clients . Specifically , exploring the effect s of shared vision and a boundary -
spanning treatment with a field experimental research design . The central thesis is that 
team innovation benefits from informational resources through its relationship with 
information elaboration and e ngenders team innovation success only if teams operate in a 
customer -centric climate for innovation  with a common goal shared by the team . In this 
context, the boundary -spanning treatment was designed to bring in information about the 
client and create a focus on the client's perspective,  resulting in increased software 
solution use.  
Theoretical Contributions  
I applied van Knippenberg’s (2017) knowledge integration  / team climate model 
of team innovation in my research . My study results provide supporting evidence for the 
proposed moderated mediation effect of  functional background diversity – as an   72 
 informational resource  – on team innovation through information elaboration that is 
conditional on shared vision as one key factor of the broader team climate  construct . 
Most notably, b y integrat ing the team climate and knowledge integration perspectives 
and combining it with the notion of boundary spanning, I offer a rich and novel 
investigati on of  the role of boundary spanning  for idea implementation  in customer 
engagement teams . My study contributes to the diversity and information elaboration 
theor ies and research by revealing that in the information elaboration process , we need to 
account for the moderating role of boundary spanning and shared vision for functional 
diversity effects on team innovation. My research findings are consistent with significant  
reviews (e.g., van Knippenberg, 2017) and meta -analyses (van Dijk et al., 2012) in 
suggesting that functional diversity can have positive and negative effects on team 
innovation . Specific moderating influences are required to unlock the potential of 
funct ional diversity. Both boundary spanning and shared vision are such moderators . My 
study provides evidence that functional diversity positively affects  team innovation 
through information elaboration in teams with a shared vision  of their work . 
When team  members lack a shared vision for their work , then the categorization 
process prevails over information elaboration. In such a situation, f unctional diversity 
negatively affect s teamwork , defined as sharing , discussi ng, and integrating  task-relevant 
inform ation (van Knippenberg et al., 2004) . By contrast,  when team members share a 
common, higher -order goal within the team , information elaboration becomes dominant 
so that cross -functional teams create synergies from the different perspectives and 
integrate their knowledge , which then engenders innovative outcomes.  This finding is 
consistent with  Somech and Drach -Zahavy (2013) and  Pearce and Ensley (2004) , who   73 
 arrived at similar conclusions for primary care teams and product and process innovation 
teams , respectively . These find ings provide answers to my first research question  
explaining how and under what conditions teams are more likely to benefit from diverse 
informational resources in pursuing innovation challenges .  
Focusing  on the company experiment , I explored the impact of functional 
background diversity on team innovation as impacted by the boundary -spanning 
treatment , providing evidence that functional diversity is positively related to team 
innovation . This relationship is stronger for teams that practice boundary spanning. I also 
provide evidence of an interaction in which boundary spanning moderates the functional 
diversity -information elaboration relationship, impacting  team innovation . However,  in 
contrast to my prediction , I found a negative moderation effect.  While several studies 
(e.g., Somech & K halaili, 2014) found negative effects of specific forms of boundary 
spanning, in the vein of  buffering or boundary -tightening activities, this must not be 
confused w ith the evidence I found . In teams that practice boundary spanning , I found 
evidence that information elaboration is less driven by the team's functional background 
diversity . The treatment reduce d the informational effect of functional diversity  and 
boundary spanning substitute d for information elaboration.  Althou gh Hypot hesis 2 and 3 
were not supported because of the negative moderation effect, it seems that the boundary 
spanning effect is presumably more expressed in the quality of information elaboration 
than in quantity . This explanation  is consistent with the theory  and other empirical 
evidence . For example, Somech and Khalaili (2014) found a positive relationship 
between team functional heterogeneity and team innovation mediated through boundary -
spanning activit ies, indicating  that boundary spanning , speci fically the task -coordination   74 
 activities , can substitute for information elaboration  as a key process for knowledge 
integration . My supplementary analysis  further  provides evidence that boundary spanning 
brings in information about the customer , focuse s on the customer , and influences  what 
the team elaborates . However, as shown with the support of Hypothesis 1 , teams that 
practice boundary spanning can also develop and implement innovative solutions with 
their clients without extensive information elaboration in the form of detailed discussions 
of perspectives . This is consistent with Sung and Choi 's (2012) research an d Nadkarni 
and Narayanan (2007) , who specifically point  out that  team s build on past experiences 
and procedures that are known to be working . Since most of the teams in my sample 
worked on incremental innovation s with available best practices instead of  radical or 
disruptive innovation s, this finding is consistent with the broader body of knowledge  
(Alexander & van Knippenberg, 2014) . Interestingly  and in contrast to the boundary 
spanning interaction effect , the shared vision did not significantly interact with functional 
diversity to create a  stronger relationship with team innovation (Hypo thesis 4) . While  a 
shared vision and focus should help to mobilize functional diversity resulting in increased 
team innovation (Fay et al., 2006 ), it emphasizes the role of information elaboration as a 
key team process that enables teams to leverage their informational resources to drive 
innovation . Specifically  in more complex  service engagements with technology providers 
that require business and IT alignment (Hoever  et al., 2012 ; Sabherwal et  al., 2001 ). 
However, it is also possible that I have  a restriction of range in functional diversity and 
shared vision  in my sample  teams. M eaning that all teams have enough of an 
understand ing of what they are supposed to do together  and that the variation of a shared 
vision between teams is within a restricted range of relatively high levels . Given that my   75 
 sample teams  are customer -facing and given the quality of people on the teams , I do not 
have team s with no shared vision . Therefore , I compare relatively speaking rather 
moderate  to high levels of shared vision , and because of this, it may suppress effects that 
exist.  Similarly, my study departs from many empirical studies of diversity that include 
completely homo geneous teams  and are often restricted because  the included  teams are 
not very diverse . On the contrary, t he teams in my data set are at the higher end of 
functional diversity , with almost none being homogeneous in functional background s 
within the team.  Therefore, it is possible that some of these limitations in range suppress 
the theoretically expected  effects.  This phenomenon also needs to be considered when 
interpreting the three -way interaction effect.  Although I found a positive significant 
three -way interaction effect, this analy sis relies on the less frequent occurrence of low 
shared vision in the treatment group even when treatment tends to increase shared vision , 
pointing  to potential unmeasured covarying variables . On the contrary, the treatment -
centric analysis of the serial mediation through a shared vision and information 
elaboration with functional diversity as second -stage moderator is more robust . It 
provides novel insight that  boundary spanning (via ambassadorial activities) increase s 
shared vision.  Whereas my focal analysis  suggested that boundary spanning team s make 
less use of informational resources from the functional diversity in information 
elaboration, my supplementary analysis and qualitative insight suggest that boundary 
spanning creates a focus on the client through a shared vision and  increases information 
elaboration.  This insight also links to the related research on cooperative goals and shared 
identity  that highlights the importance of teamwork , focusing  on shared goals  as a 
requirement  for team s to be innovative (van Knippenberg, 2017).  Hülsheger and   76 
 colleagues  (2009) , to that point, provide meta -analytic evidence for the positive influence 
of team cohesion and goal interdependence  on team innovation.  Tjosvold , Tang, and 
West  (2004) show that team innovation benefits from cooperative goals mediated through 
team reflexivity.  
Beyond previous research focused  on a shared vision and goals (e.g., Lisak et al. , 
2016 ), the notion of team reflexivity also , in a way similar to boun dary spanning, 
connects to the notion of design thinking , which  helps to focus on the user ’s needs and 
bring s in the user perspective  to iteratively  ideate and implement solutions that address 
critical  business issues  (Cousins, 2018; Venkatesh et al., 2012 ). I have theorized and 
empirically validated a model of how the relationship between functional diversity and 
team innovation is moderated by boundary spanning , addressi ng my second research 
question. Here m y contribution is the finding that illustrate s how boundary spanning 
enhances team innovation for cross -functional customer engagement teams . 
Managerial Implications  
Most notably, s upport for my research model suggest s that technology providers 
in particular , or organizations in general , benefit from boundary -spanning team activities 
and cross -functional team setups  to drive customer innovation . As evidenced by the field 
experiment results , the boundary -spanning treatment had a significant pos itive effect on 
team innovation as measure d by customer solution usage and on information elaboration 
within the team and the team ’s shared vision . These  are critical for teams to leverage 
diverse insight s and perspectives in achieving business outcomes for their customer s. 
Driving customer success when many organizations struggle with their digital 
transformation initiatives is critical for both the client’s sustained competitive advantage   77 
 in the indus try and the technology provider's ability  to retain customers  and maintain 
profitable recurring revenue streams.  In the company experiment , it was found that 
customers in the treatment group ach ieved on average 12% higher usage of their software 
solutions  during the measurement period , and customers with increasing usage rates  show 
14% in revenue growth . In contrast,  customers with stable or declining utilization show 
no revenue growth. Based on this experime ntal evidence, the company i ncreases  its 
investments in customer success  and boundary -spanning team roles  to drive customer 
lifetime value.  
Furthermore, the vast academic debate of research design s with mediation models 
show s that such models are efficient  because of the intended causal relationships  
providing step-by-step guidance for crucial  factors and focus areas in the process to 
achieve the desired outcomes  (e.g., Harve y et al., 2019) . In that sense , functional 
background diversity should be considered a  critical factor for team composition when 
creating account teams . It is one of the primary sources of knowledge, information, 
expertise, and perspectives that help teams develop  innovative ideas and overcome 
implementation challenges. Leaders  and executive s should consider investments in 
boundary -spanning to facilitate knowledge integration and leverage the team's diverse 
perspectives and expertise  to benefit  the customer. Knowledge integration in the form of 
information elaboration in cross -functional teams is an essential step in the team 
innovation process  recommended as a focus area for leaders . Depending on the context, 
boundary spanners can take on an orchestration role for the transformation  effort and 
increase the overall chances for  success.  In that vein, companies should also train their 
account teams for joint success planning and create a shared vision since the idea of a   78 
 valued outcome represents a substantial  motivating force at work. It is critical that the 
work is personally important to team members and that all members  believe in their 
work's purpose . Although it  is undoubted ly intuitive and straightforward t hat teams 
should have motivating goals, my supplementary analysis shows that boundary spanning , 
specifically through ambassadorial activities, positively affects  the team's shared vision. 
It also creates a client focus in information elaboration . By considering all these benefits 
together , executives  and leaders have a strong case to invest in boundary -spanning  for 
teams  pursuing  innovation challenges . 
Finally, I found a significant three -way interaction effect of boundary spanning, 
functional diversity , and shared vision . Although  there is a risk of unmeasured variables 
that covary  in the three -way analysis  (specifically because low shared vision is rare in the 
treatment group ), it is valuable to note for executives  that not only is it worth investing in 
these factors  in isolation , but the y should be developed in combination  to create high ly 
innovative work teams . In summary, on their relentless mission to develop the perfect 
team, executives and team leaders should put boundary spanning on the agenda  in order 
to achieve new levels of customer -centricity  and facilitate innovation management in 
cross -functional groups . 
Limitations and Future Research  
In contrast to  prior team innovation studies, which mostly analyzed teams with 
perceptual outcome measures, I analyze d cross -functional customer engagement teams 
with an objective measure of  team innovation  by evaluating customer usage  as the 
ultimate measure of innovation success . While this addressed some limitations of 
previous research, my research suffers from its own shortcomings , which I elaborate on   79 
 in the following sections . First, the company field experiment was not without 
challenges . For example , a few customers reject ed the treatment , and the COVID -19 
crisis emerg ed in the middle of the experiment . However, despite the boundary -spanning 
treatment work ing out very well, functional background diversity  relied on natural 
variation given the quasi -experimental desig n. Therefore, I do not know to what extent 
other team characteristics  that covary with functional diversity  cause its effect . Ideally, 
future research would manipulate functional background diversity  to reduce the potential 
for alternative interpretations . 
Second, my survey measure of information elaboration captur ed how much teams 
elaborate but does not provid e insights into their elaborat ion content . While in my context 
boundary spanning is presumabl y more about what is important for the client,  for some 
interpretations , I am relying on the unmeasured variable of the content of elaboration . 
Therefore, future research would ideally provide mo re info rmation  about team 
information elaboration quality and content to substantiate my interpretations further . 
Such an analysis would also benefit from a broader perspective on different types of 
informational resources. For example, studying  social network diversity ( e.g., Hirst, van 
Knippenberg, Zhou, Quintane, & Zhu, 2015 ; Reagans & Zuckerman, 2001 ) and potential 
interdependencies of functional diversity and network diversity  where, at least in the 
context of customer engagement teams, one may argue that increased functional diversity 
leads to increased network diversity . Like functional background diversity, team 
networks are considered  critical informational resources that  help teams drive innovative 
outcomes if leveraged in the right way  (Balkundi & Harrison, 2006 ; van Knippenberg, 
2017). The notion of increased diversity lead ing to an increased performance also raises   80 
 the question for a more nuanced analysis of the effects of diversity by , for example , 
looking at the effects of adding specific functional backgrounds to the team ( van 
Knippenberg, Dawson, West, & Homan, 2010 ) and better u nderstand ing potential 
curvilinear relationships where diversity is potentially only better up to a specific point  
(e.g., Tzabbar & Vestal, 20 15). 
Third , I only partially cover the two main themes of knowledge integration and 
team climate in the broad field of team innovation research (van Knippenberg, 2017) . 
Analyzing the effect s of further factors of the team climate inventory ( Anderson & West, 
1998 ), such as psychological safety (Edmondson, 2003; Harvey et al., 2019) , would 
provide a more comprehensive  view on how the use of informational resources in the 
team process of information elaboration can be stimulated. In that regard , the team 
creativity and innovation body of knowledge would also benefi t from further 
consolidation of potentially overlapping constructs . This includes elements of the  team 
climate inventory, such as participative safety , which is related to psychological safety4 
and trust, or shared vision that is related to cooperative goals and shared iden tity (e.g., 
Tjosvold et al., 2004) . For example, van Knippenberg and Hoever (in press)  also address 
the latest developments and importance of  team leadership in their literature review . 
Especially for customer engagement teams working with clients on strategic digital 
transformation initiatives , it would be fascina ting to understand better  how leadership can 
impact team innovation by motivat ing knowledge integration. D’Innocenzo, Mathieu, and

Conclusion  
The results of my research, withstanding some limitations, provide a detailed 
understanding of the role of boundary spanning for team innovation and emphasize the 
importance of shared vision  and the information elaboration process in  cross -functional 
teams . My study advances information elaboration theory and shows that the relationship 
between functional diversity and team innovation is mediated by information elaboration 
and is contingent on a shared vision and boundary spanning . I found that team innovation 
benefit s from functional diversity and boundary spanning only in teams with a relatively 
high shared vision . While functional diversity and boundary spanning can increase 
information elaboration , boundary spanning can also substitute for information   82 
 elaboration and not only affects how much teams elaborate but also about what they 
elaborate , meaning  what is vital for the client . By bringing in information about the client 
and focusing on the customer perspective, boundary spanning leads to  better use of 
functional diversity  that directly feeds into client usage . My research provides 
experimental evidence  that boundary -spanning team activities foster knowledge 
integration and enhance team innovation . Technology providers seek ing to enhance 
customer team innovation  should  create teams with divers e functional backgrounds and 
focus  on boundary -spanning activities  since that not only puts the customer in the center 
of all team activities but also enhances the shared higher -order goals of the team  to 
deliver for their clients . For customer engagement teams  to benefit  from informational 
resources in general and functional background diversity of team members in particular, 
they need to integrate diverse information and invest in boundary spanning and a shared 
commitment to common goals  since these factors enhance each other’s effect on team 
innovation . Therefore, my findings contribute actionable insight into how to enable cross -
functional engagement teams to drive customer success appropriately .    83

 APPENDIX A: Measure s 
Boundary Spanning  
I reused the following items from Faraj & Yan (2009, p. 611) to measure boundary 
spanning:  
• Encourage its members to try to influence important stakeholders elsewhere in 
and/or beyond the division on behalf of the team and its work?  
• Encourage its members to solicit info rmation and resources from elsewhere in 
and/or beyond the division?  
• Value team members for making use of their relationships with others on behalf 
of the team?  
• Depend upon information and resources actively solicited by team members?  
Survey respondents wer e asked to indicate their agreement to these items on a 5 -point 
scale ranging from “strongly disagree” to “strongly agree.”  
Shared Vision  
For shared vision , I reused the following measurement items from Anderson & West 
(1998 , p. 241 ) as defined in their team climate inventory : 
• How clear are you about what your team ’s objectives are?  
• To what extent do you think they are useful and appropriate objectives?  
• How far are you in agreement with these objectives?  
• To what extent do you think your team's objectives are clearly understood by 
other members of the team?  
• To what extent do you think your team's objectives can actually be achieved?  
• How worthwhile do you think these objectives are to the organization?  
Team  members were asked to rate each item for their team on a 7-point scale ranging 
from “not at all” to “completely .”   95 
 Psychological Safety  
I measured psychological safety with four items defined by Harvey and colleagues 
(2019):  
• In this team, it is easy to speak up about what is on your mind  
• People in this team are usually comfortable talking about problems and 
disagreements  
• People in this team are eager to share information about what does and doesn’t 
work  
• If you make a mistake in this team, it is often hel d against you (reverse scored)  
Each item was rated for the team on a 7 -point scale ranging from “strongly disagree” to 
“strongly agree.”  
Information Elaboration  
I measured information elaboration with six items defined by  van Knippenberg and 
colleagues  (2019) : “In our team…”  
• Members complement each other by sharing their knowledge  
• We discuss alternatives before making decisions  
• We combine information from different members to gain a better understanding 
of the task  
• We combine the input of different members  
• We consider the implications of the combination of different members’ 
viewpoints  
• We exchange different ideas and perspectives on the task  
As part of the survey , respondents were asked to indicate the ir agreement to these items 
within their team on a 5-point scale ranging from “strongly disagree” to “strongly agree .”
