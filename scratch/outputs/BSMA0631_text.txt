ABSTRACT
This study is the first to examine the mediating role of boundary-­spanning search and the moderating role of partner similarity 
in horizontal coopetition for sustainable product innovation. Analyzing empirical data from 236 Chinese firms, we found that 
horizontal coopetition positively impacts both proactive search (aimed at surpassing competitors) and responsive search (aimed 
at following competitors), with a stronger influence on proactive search. Both search types mediate the relationship between 
horizontal coopetition and sustainable product innovation. Furthermore, greater competing similarity (e.g., market coverage) 
between firms and their partners increases firms' inclination toward proactive search under the influence of horizontal coo-
petition but weakens their tendency for responsive search. Conversely, greater cooperating similarity (e.g., corporate culture) 
increases firms' inclination toward responsive search under the influence of horizontal coopetition but weakens their tendency 
for proactive search.
1   |   Introduction
The global surge in industrialization, population growth, and 
increased production has spurred economic development but 
has also caused significant environmental degradation (Severo 
et al. 2017; Hussain et al. 2020; C. H. Wang and Juo 2021). As 
the main creators of carbon emissions, commercial enterprises 
face the challenge of mediating environmental concerns while 
pursuing economic development, most commonly through sus-
tainable product innovation (SPI) (Severo et  al.  2017; Meiros 
et al. 2022; Calik 2024). SPI refers to balancing economic, en-
vironmental, and social goals in the process of product creation 
and improvement, minimizing negative environmental impact 
while gaining economic benefits (Severo et  al.  2017; Meiros 
et al. 2022; Calik 2024). However, its complexity and uncertainty 
often need collaborative relationships with various stakeholders 
(Mousavi et al. 2019; Meiros et al. 2022), including cooperation 
with direct competitors, which is also known as horizontal coo-
petition (Le Roy et al. 2022; Xie et al. 2023).
According to coopetition theory, coopetition refers to the co-
existence of competition and cooperation between enterprises. 
Horizontal coopetition refers specifically to the cooperation 
between two enterprises at the same stage of the value chain 
to jointly create, produce, and sell new products while compet-
ing with other products in the same market (Le Roy et al. 2022;

Huo et al. 2024). Unlike other types of collaboration, horizon-
tal coopetition means that companies share similarities with 
their partners in technology or product positioning, which 
makes it easier to increase opportunities for mutual compe-
tence growth in the process of knowledge exchange (Massari 
and Giannoccaro 2021; Le Roy et al. 2022; Xie et al. 2023). This 
collaboration can also share the risks and costs of research and 
development (Massari and Giannoccaro  2021). For example, 
although Toyota and BMW are direct competitors in the auto-
motive market, they have established partnerships to jointly de-
velop hydrogen fuel cell technology for drive systems. However, 
the inherent coopetition paradox implies that coopetition may 
lead to opportunism, knowledge leakage, and power imbalances 
(Bouncken and Kraus 2013; Le Roy et al. 2022; Corbo et al. 2023; 
Huo et al. 2024). Therefore, the answer to the question of how 
the value of horizontal coopetition for SPI can be controlled, in-
creased, and improved becomes a critical issue.
Although scholars argue that coopetition is linked to firms' 
sustainability (Planko et  al.  2019; Munten et  al.  2021; Corbo 
et  al.  2023; Mwesiumo et  al.  2023), additional mechanisms 
are still required in horizontal coopetition to improve SPI 
(Mwesiumo et al. 2023). Specifically, the tension in horizontal 
coopetition creates explicit competitive pressure even during 
collaboration (Raza-­Ullah and Kostis  2020; Czakon and 
Czernek-­Marszałek  2021; Huo et  al.  2024). Faced with com-
petitive pressure, companies must continuously span organiza-
tional boundaries to search for diverse and valuable resources, 
providing new insights and opportunities for innovation (Li 
et al. 2024). Boundary-­spanning search involves sourcing het-
erogeneous resources from diverse external sources (J. Wang 
et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2021; Li et al. 2024). Specifically, proactive 
search is directed toward gaining a competitive edge by staying 
ahead of rivals, whereas responsive search is aimed at follow-
ing them (J. Wang et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2021; Li et al. 2024). 
Both proactive search and responsive search serve as sources of 
heterogeneous innovation resources for firms (Yang et al. 2021; 
Lee et al. 2024; Li et al. 2024) and may lay a solid foundation for 
SPI. Relevant questions that arise are: Does horizontal coopeti-
tion impact proactive search and responsive search? If so, are 
there differences in impact? And does this impact on these two 
search modes also affect firms' SPI? In summary, this study will 
examine whether proactive and responsive search each serve as 
significant intermediary pathways in the relationship between 
horizontal coopetition and SPI.
Furthermore, partner similarity, including competing and co-
operating similarity, influences the behavior of collaborating 
enterprises and the outcomes of the collaboration (Kim and 
Parkhe 2009; Luo and Deng 2009; Gao et al. 2017). Therefore, we 
substantiate that partner similarity may be a contingent factor 
affecting the functioning of horizontal coopetition. Specifically, 
competing similarity refers to similarity in geographic market 
coverage, product market coverage, and areas of technologi-
cal strength among partners (Kim and Parkhe 2009), possibly 
triggering a ‘learning race’, where firms gain competitive ad-
vantages only through being better at accumulating compe-
tencies than their partners (Bouncken and Kraus  2013; Gao 
et  al.  2017). Conversely, cooperating similarity refers to simi-
larity in corporate culture and management practices (Kim and 
Parkhe 2009), which is beneficial for promoting coordination of 
the collaboration (Kim and Parkhe 2009; Vaez-­Alaei et al. 2022). 
Resource-­advantage theory suggests that competition is a dy-
namic process. This means firms typically make different stra-
tegic choices based on the competitive environment to gain 
relative competitive advantages (Hunt and Morgan 1996). Thus, 
in the context of horizontal coopetition, a central question is: Do 
different degrees of cooperating similarity and competing sim-
ilarity affect firms' preference for different types of boundary-­
spanning search? Overall, this paper will further explore 
whether and how partner similarity affects the relationship be-
tween horizontal coopetition and boundary-­spanning search.
Given the above, the contribution of this study is threefold. First, 
this study refines the research on coopetition in the field of 
firms' sustainability by exploring the impact of horizontal coop-
etition on SPI. Second, this study shows a new pathway for com-
panies to achieve SPI through horizontal coopetition from the 
perspective of boundary-­spanning search. Third, this study re-
veals the differentiated moderating mechanisms of partner sim-
ilarity in the relationship between horizontal coopetition and 
boundary-­spanning search and further deepens the research on 
the process mechanisms through which companies achieve SPI 
through horizontal coopetition.
The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 
discusses the theoretical background of the research model 
constructed in this study and the formulation of the research 
hypotheses related to it. Section 3 introduces the empirical re-
search methods used to test the hypotheses. Section 4 reports, 
and Section 5 analyzes the survey research outcomes and dis-
cusses the contributions, limitations, and directions for future 
research. Finally, Section 6 concludes with the main answers to 
the research questions and their implications.

2.1   |   Theoretical Framework
2.1.1   |   Coopetition in Firms' Sustainability
Scholars have recently emphasized the important role of coo-
petition in achieving sustainability in enterprises (Vu and 
Fath  2024; Mwesiumo et  al.  2023; Xin et  al.  2023). Vu and 
Fath's (2024) empirical study on the New Zealand alternative 
protein industry shows that enterprises can jointly produce 
sustainable solutions through coopetition, which can not only 
exceed the market growth created by individual enterprises 
but also generate extensive social benefits. The research results 
of Mwesiumo et al. (2023) show that coopetition is conducive 
to cultivating the dynamic capabilities of enterprises (such as 
knowledge development, organizational responsiveness, reflex-
ive control, and fostering sustainability awareness), thereby im-
proving the social, environmental, and economic dimensions of 
enterprise sustainability performance. Mirzabeiki et al. (2023) 
argue that coopetition is conducive to reducing logistics costs 
and carbon footprint, enhancing regional economic competi-
tiveness, and reducing emissions to affect sustainability per-
formance. In addition, some empirical studies have confirmed 
the positive impact of coopetition on carbon footprint reduction

By reviewing the literature, we found that existing studies

industry or market (Le Roy et al. 2022), this study specifically 
examines the impact of horizontal coopetition on SPI, reveals 
the mediating path of horizontal coopetition on SPI, and iden-
tifies the contingent factors that affect the role of horizontal 
coopetition.
2.1.2   |   Boundary-­Spanning Search
Boundary-­spanning search involves sourcing heterogeneous 
resources and information from diverse external sources such 
as customers, suppliers, research institutions, and univer-
sities (J. Wang et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2021; Li et al. 2024). It 
generally shows positive effects on the likelihood of generating 
highly valuable innovations (Park et  al.  2024). Regarding the 
division of boundary-­spanning search, existing literature has 
delineated dimensions of it from different perspectives, such 
as the depth and scope of search (S. S. Xiao et al. 2023), search 
broadness and search narrowness (Li et al. 2019), and interac-
tive and non-­interactive search (Roper et  al.  2017). However, 
as noted by Katila and Chen  (2008), these classifications pri-
marily focus on the company itself and overlook the situation 
in which firms' search activity usually consider competitors' 
strategies and strategic maneuvers. On the one hand, companies 
pursue boundary-­spanning search to surpass competitors and 
gain a first-­mover advantage, which is proactive search (Katila 
and Chen 2008; J. Wang et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2021). On the 
other hand, companies engage in boundary-­spanning search to 
follow and imitate competitors in order to reduce risks, which 
is known as responsive search (Katila and Chen 2008; J. Wang 
et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2021). To gain a deeper understanding of 
the behavioral logic of boundary-­spanning search in competi-
tive environments, this study categorizes it into proactive search 
and responsive search based on their objectives, drawing on the 
works of Katila and Chen (2008) as well as Yang et al. (2021).
At present, scholars have conducted much research on the im-
pact of boundary-­spanning search on organizational innovation, 
such as business model innovation (Zhu et al. 2023), innovation 
performance (Li et al. 2024), and new product innovation (Lee 
et al. 2024). However, except for studies by Shi et al. (2024) and 
Zhu et al. (2023), the antecedents of boundary-­spanning search 
remain underexamined. As Shi et al. (2024) pointed out through 
meta-­analysis, there is a gap in relevant research on the anteced-
ents of boundary-­spanning search. In particular, few scholars 
have explored the role of horizontal coopetition in promoting 
boundary-­spanning search. Therefore, the link between hor-
izontal coopetition and boundary-­spanning search merits fur-
ther research.
2.1.3   |   Coopetition Theory
The concept of coopetition was initially coined in the 1980s by 
Ray Noorda, the founder of Novell, and has since been enriched 
and developed by scholars in the field of strategic management 
(Xie et al. 2023). The essence of horizontal coopetition lies in 
the coexistence of these contradictory logics of cooperation and 
competition, with the overriding motivation for firms to engage 
in coopetition to be able to create value (Gnyawali and Ryan 
Charleton 2018). Coopetition theory suggests that value creation 
through coopetition involves different mechanisms. On the 
one hand, focal firms can unilaterally learn by acquiring skills 
and knowledge from their alliance partner and applying them 
to activities unrelated to the alliance activities and outside its 
boundary (Rai 2016; Rai and Surana 2022). This implies compa-
nies may obtain complementary resources and critical technol-
ogies needed for SPI from horizontal coopetition. On the other 
hand, focal firms create value by competing against their alli-
ance partner to outperform it in activities directly linked to the 
alliance-­related activities but taking place beyond the alliance 
boundary (Rai 2016; Rai and Surana 2022). This implies that 
firms may actively seek opportunities driven by the inherent 
competitive attributes of horizontal coopetition, crossing orga-
nizational boundaries to search for heterogeneous resources to 
support innovation. In summary, coopetition theory is not only 
the theoretical foundation for our study on the mechanism by 
which horizontal coopetition affects SPI but also supports the 
proposal of boundary-­spanning search as the mediating path 
between them.
2.1.4   |   Resource-­Advantage Theory
Resource-­advantage theory, proposed by Shelby Hunt and 
Robert Morgan in the 1990s, is a general competitive the-
ory that describes the evolutionary process of competition 
(Hunt and Morgan  1996; Varadarajan  2023; Agnihotri and 
Gabler  2024). Different from the resource-­based view, which 
emphasizes the importance of VRIN (valuable, rare, inimita-
ble and non-­substitutable) resources for gaining competitive 
advantage, resource-­advantage theory highlights the compar-
ative advantage of resources, suggesting that in market envi-
ronments, there is not just one competitive strategy to achieve 
superior performance (Varadarajan 2023; Hansen et al. 2023). 
For instance, firms can pursue different competitive strategies 
(cost leadership, differentiation, focused cost leadership, or fo-
cused differentiation) and market entry strategies (pioneering, 
early followers, or late entrants) (Varadarajan 2023). Moreover, 
resource-­advantage theory emphasizes that competition is a dy-
namic process, and firms will evaluate their resources accord-
ing to changes in the competitive environment to make different 
strategic choices to obtain relative competitive advantages (Hunt 
and Morgan 1996).
Based on this theory, we argue that, in the context of hori-
zontal coopetition, the different types and degrees of partner 
similarity will lead firms to prefer different search strategies 
for varying purposes. Increased competing similarity can 
stimulate firms' competitiveness (Runge et al. 2022), driving 
them to proactive search to seek new technologies different 
from their partners for a competitive edge. Conversely, greater

2.2   |   Research Hypotheses
2.2.1   |   Horizontal Coopetition and SPI
We expect that horizontal coopetition affects SPI. Firstly, firms 
can acquire the resources, critical technologies, and knowl-
edge required for SPI from horizontal coopetition, while its 
competitive attribute prompts more efficient integration and 
allocation of resources. SPI is a resource-­intensive practice, 
so it largely depends on extensive resource support (Meiros 
et  al.  2022). Research indicates that initial capital (Hussain 
et al. 2020), advanced technologies (Rusch et al. 2023), intel-
lectual capital (C. H. Wang and Juo 2021), among others, play 
crucial roles in SPI. Within horizontal coopetition, firms not 
only gain diverse innovation resources but also market align-
ment and similar objectives facilitate easier technology sharing 
and integration among partners. Secondly, horizontal coope-
tition provides a mechanism for the collaborating parties to 
share new resource costs, spread risks, and reduce uncertain-
ties (Corbo et al. 2023). Specifically, SPI is frequently regarded 
as a high-­risk R&D investment, and companies face multiple 
risks such as poor market response, immature technology, 
and unclear investment returns (Moshood et al. 2022). In the 
process of horizontal coopetition, partners integrate their core 
resources and capabilities to alleviate the financial risk of SPI 
and reduce uncertainties in the innovation process. Third, 
horizontal coopetition can make companies more sensitive to 
market demand and customer feedback (Chen et al. 2021). In 
this way, companies can more accurately judge market trends 
and consumer preferences, enabling them to proactively ad-
just the design and functionality of sustainable products to 
meet changing market demands. By combining the preceding 
considerations, the first hypothesis is as follows:
Hypothesis 1.  Horizontal coopetition is positively correlated 
with SPI.
2.2.2   |   Horizontal Coopetition 
and Boundary-­Spanning Search
We expect that horizontal coopetition affects boundary-­
spanning search. On the one hand, according to the coopetition 
theory, an important mechanism for enterprises to gain bene-
fits from horizontal coopetition is the opportunity to learn from 
competitors (Xie et al. 2023). By observing competitors' inno-
vative practices and market reactions, and linking their actions 
and consequences, firms can better understand market dynam-
ics and competitive patterns (Katila and Chen 2008). Therefore, 
we argue that horizontal coopetition will drive companies to 
search for new knowledge and resources in response to com-
petitors, which not only reduces the risk and cost of knowledge 
search but also enhances its effectiveness. On the other hand, 
horizontal coopetition implies that firms and partners over-
lap in market positioning, which enhances firms' competitive 
awareness and motivation (Runge et al. 2022), prompting firms 
to actively acquire resources that competitors do not possess in 
order to lead competitors and achieve sustainable competitive 
advantage. Furthermore, we argue that compared to responsive 
search, horizontal coopetition has a stronger impact on proac-
tive search. This is because the uniqueness of horizontal coope-
tition lies in companies experiencing competitive pressure even 
in cooperation (Raza-­Ullah and Kostis 2020). Competition is a 
race, and gaining innovative resources that competitors lack 
is crucial; otherwise, they risk losing their competitive edge 
(Katila and Chen 2008). Accordingly, we suggest the following 
hypotheses:

2.2.3   |   The Mediating Effect 
of Boundary-­Spanning Search
Horizontal coopetition triggers competitive responses from 
firms, enabling them to access environmental resources across 
organizational boundaries (Xin et  al.  2023; Li et  al.  2024). 
Meanwhile, complementary resources from outside organi-
zational boundaries open up new possibilities to achieve sus-
tainability (J. Wang et  al.  2020). We therefore assume that 
boundary-­spanning search plays a mediating role in the influ-
ence of horizontal coopetition on SPI. On the one hand, coop-
etition theory holds that competition coexists with coopetitive 
activities—even if the degree of competition is very small (Crick 
et al. 2024). This will induce competitive effects (Park et al. 2014; 
Rai 2016; Rai and Surana 2022) and drive firms to seek opportu-
nities to surpass competitors and pursue first-­mover advantages 
(Mariani and Belitski 2023). Then they need to engage in proac-
tive search, allowing them to access abundant environmental 
resources such as eco-­friendly materials and green packaging, 
which provide robust support for firms in developing and de-
signing sustainable products (J. Wang et al. 2020, 2023; Yang 
et al. 2021). On the other hand, due to similar knowledge bases 
and expertise, coopetition also stimulates a mutual learning mo-
tivation among firms (Park et al. 2014). Therefore, firms may 
choose to engage in responsive search to acquire market infor-
mation and innovation resources (Mariani and Belitski 2023). 
This is because, by observing competitors' practices, innova-
tive products and technologies, responsive search seems more 
effective in acquiring knowledge (Yang et  al.  2021). Efficient 
knowledge acquisition facilitates the flow, communication, and 
exchange of knowledge required for SPI (Bossink 2018), thereby 
enhancing the efficiency of firms' SPI (Liao 2018). As such, we 
hypothesize the following:
Hypothesis 3a.  Proactive search mediates the relationship 
between horizontal coopetition and SPI.
Hypothesis 3b.  Responsive search mediates the relationship 
between horizontal coopetition and SPI.
2.2.4   |   The Moderating Effects of Competing Similarity
Competing similarity refers to the degree of similarity between 
firms and partners in terms of geographical market coverage, 
product market coverage, and technological advantages (Kim 
and Parkhe  2009). We suggest that the higher the competing 
similarity, the greater the influence of horizontal coopetition 
on proactive search. When firms and partners exhibit signifi-
cant resemblance in market positioning and business models, 
the pressure from horizontal coopetition accentuates the neces-
sity for differentiated strategies (Runge et al. 2022). This may 
prompt increased involvement in boundary-­spanning search 
aimed at outpacing competitors and venturing into new mar-
ket domains. Conversely, we suggest that competing similarity 
diminishes the beneficial effects of horizontal coopetition on 
responsive search. As mentioned earlier, under the drive of hor-
izontal coopetition, firms can acquire market information and 
innovation resources through responsive search. However, the 
higher the competing similarity between firms and their part-
ners, the greater the similarity of market pressures and R&D 
challenges faced by both parties (Kim and Parkhe 2009). So they 
may be inclined to protect their core technologies and resources 
from disclosure in order to prevent competitors from gaining a 
competitive advantage (J. Xiao and Bao 2022). Therefore, knowl-
edge sharing and coordination in the cooperation process will 
become more difficult; the feasibility of proactive search for en-
terprises will likely diminish. Accordingly, we propose the fol-
lowing hypotheses:
Hypothesis 4a.  Competing similarity strengthens the positive 
relationship between horizontal coopetition and proactive search.
Hypothesis 4b.  Competing similarity weakens the posi-
tive relationship between horizontal coopetition and responsive 
search.
2.2.5   |   The Moderating Effects 
of Cooperating Similarity
Cooperating similarity refers to the degree of similarity between 
firms and partners in terms of values, behavioral norms, so-
cial or corporate culture, and management practices (Kim and 
Parkhe 2009). We argue that cooperating similarity strengthens 
the impact of horizontal coopetition on responsive search. A 
high level of cooperating similarity implies mutual recognition 
and similar practices between firms and partners. This facili-
tates effective communication and coordination, thereby reduc-
ing collaboration costs and promoting the transfer and sharing of 
tacit knowledge such as critical technologies (Oh and Yoo 2022). 
At the same time, based on similar behavioral norms and values, 
firms can more effectively coordinate internal and external re-
sources, rapidly understand, absorb, and assimilate new knowl-
edge and technologies. Conversely, the higher the cooperating 
similarity, the more firms and partners tend to align in terms 
of values, norms, work processes, employee expectations, and 
management practices (Kim and Parkhe 2009). This reduces the 
cognitive gap between the parties, potentially leading to cogni-
tive rigidity and group inertia (Oh and Yoo 2022). In such cir-
cumstances, firms may be constrained by established cognitive 
frameworks when facing the competitive pressure generated by 
horizontal coopetition. This may hinder the emergence of novel 
ideas and perspectives in collaboration and restrict firms' ex-
ploration of heterogeneous and diversified resources, thereby 
suppressing boundary-­spanning search aimed at surpassing 
competitors. Accordingly, we suggest the following hypotheses:
Hypothesis 5a.  Cooperating similarity strengthens the pos-
itive relationship between horizontal coopetition and responsive 
search.
Hypothesis 5b.  Cooperating similarity weakens the positive 
relationship between horizontal coopetition and proactive search.

3   |   Methodology
3.1   |   Samples and Data Collection
This study employed a questionnaire survey to collect data, and 
the content validity of the questionnaire was based on existing 
literature. We chose the manufacturing industry as the research 
sample because it plays a predominant role in driving China's 
economic advancement and has the highest carbon dioxide 
(CO2) emissions among industrial sectors (Zhao et  al.  2017). 
Hence, under China's climate crisis response policies, there 
exists an urgent necessity for SPI within the manufacturing in-
dustry to facilitate the transition from extensive growth to sus-
tainable development.
We developed an English language questionnaire based on 
existing literature and enlisted two independent translators 
to translate the questionnaire into Chinese language and per-
form back translation. Prior to the official survey, a pilot sur-
vey was conducted with 30 companies in the Chinese Shaanxi 
Province, and the questionnaire content was adjusted accord-
ingly. To ensure representative sampling, we systematically 
selected firms across various regions of China, including the 
northwestern, southern, eastern, and central areas. With the 
help of established partnerships with local governments and 
industry associations, we obtained local business directories 
for each region and randomly chose 250 firms from the local 
business directory within each of the four regions, totaling 
1000 firms. Subsequently, professional interviewers were 
hired to contact the senior managers of these companies, ei-
ther by phone or email, explaining the academic objectives of 
the survey and the confidentiality policy. A total of 530 firms 
consented to participate in this study. Subsequently, after 
scheduling appointments, our interviewers visited the com-
panies to conduct the surveys. During the completion of the 
questionnaires, we thoroughly described the process and an-
swered any questions posed by the respondents. Each compa-
ny's survey took an average of about 1h to complete.
To avoid potential common method bias, we employed a 
multiple-­informant approach to collect our data (Podsakoff 
et al. 2012). First, we selected two senior managers from each 
company to serve as our key informants. These senior manag-
ers possess extensive knowledge about their firms' interfirm 
cooperative activities and innovation performance. Two se-
nior managers from each company were instructed to focus on 
their most important partner within the past 3 years and pro-
vide the name of the cooperating company and the duration 
of the cooperation. Then, one manager provided responses to 
basic company information, independent variables (e.g., hor-
izontal coopetition, proactive search, and responsive search), 
and control variables (e.g., firm age, size), while the scales of 
the moderating variables (competing similarity, cooperating 
similarity) and the dependent variable (SPI) were assessed by 
another manager.
In total, 321 questionnaires were returned, and after rigorous 
screening, 85 invalid questionnaires were excluded, resulting 
in a total of 236 responses received. The exclusion of invalid 
questionnaires was due to data missing content and obvious 
errors in common sense content. The final sample included 
companies from 18 cities in China, primarily concentrated 
in provinces and cities such as Shaanxi, Jiangsu, Henan, 
and Guangzhou. The industries mainly comprised manufac-
turing sectors including construction materials, automotive 
and parts, environmental protection and landscaping, as 
well as clothing and textiles. It consisted of 67 state-­owned 
enterprises, 107 private enterprises, and 62 other types of 
enterprises.
3.2   |   Measures
A mature 7-­point Likert scale was used for all the scale items 
(Table 1), with 1–7 indicating a range from Strongly disagree to 
Strongly agree.
3.2.1   |   Horizontal Coopetition (HC)
Based on Bouncken and Kraus  (2013), we used three items 
(Cronbach's α = 0.788) to measure horizontal coopetition. An 
example item is “Our company collaborates with direct compet-
itors to achieve common goals.”
3.2.2   |   Boundary-­Spanning Search
Drawing on the research of Katila and Chen  (2008), as well 
as Atuahene-­Gima and Murray  (2007), we used four items 
(Cronbach's α = 0.953) to measure proactive search (PS). An exam-
ple item is “Our company prefers to collect information with no 
identifiable strategic market needs to ensure experimentation in 
the project, aiming to surpass competitors” in boundary-­spanning 
search activities. Additionally, we used four items (Cronbach's 
α = 0.824) to measure responsive search (RS). An example item is 
“Our aim is to search for information to refine common meth-
ods and ideas in solving problems in the project in order to follow 
competitors” in boundary-­spanning search activities.
3.2.3   |   Partner Similarity
Based on Kim and Parkhe (2009) and Zhang et al. (2010), we 
utilized four items (Cronbach's α = 0.872) to measure compet-
ing similarity (CMS). An example item is “Our company has the 
same production line as our partner.” Drawing on the research 
of Geringer (1991) and Kale et al. (2000), we used three items 
(Cronbach's α = 0.824) to measure cooperating similarity (COS). 
An example item is “Our company shares the same organiza-
tional culture with our partner.”
3.2.4   |   Sustainable Product Innovation (SPI)
Drawing on the research of Klewitz and Hansen (2014) and Peng 
and Lin (2008), the five items (Cronbach's α = 0.884) were used 
to measure SPI. An example item is “Our company develops 
products according to the product lifecycle method.”

Regarding Hypothesis 1, our research findings indicate that 
horizontal coopetition has a significant positive impact on 
SPI. This finding is similar to the findings of Mwesiumo 
et al. (2023), Vu and Fath (2024), and Xin et al. (2023); that 
is, coopetition has a positive impact on corporate sustainabil-
ity. As Corbo et al. (2023) argue, although there are potential 
drawbacks in coopetition, an abundance of empirical and 
anecdotal evidence supports the rationale that coopetition 
benefits innovation-­related activities. Our research focuses 
on the context of corporate sustainability and confirms that 
horizontal coopetition has a promoting effect on SPI. That is, 
collaboration with companies of similar market positioning or 
technological expertise not only enhances the environmental 
performance and economic efficiency of new products but also 
generates social benefits. This is a crucial result, since it offers 
empirical support to scholars' calls for investigating the role 
of coopetition in sustainable innovation (Munten et al. 2021; 
Corbo et al. 2023; Xin et al. 2023).
Concerning Hypothesis 2, we found that horizontal coopeti-
tion positively impacts both proactive and responsive search. 
As Shi et al. (2024) pointed out, there is a significant lack of 
research on the antecedents of boundary-­spanning search. 
Our study confirms horizontal coopetition not only stimulates 
enterprises to engage in boundary-­spanning search with the 
purpose of surpassing competition, but also stimulates enter-
prises to conduct such searches in response to competitors. 
This finding also supports the research by Shi et al. (2024) and 
Ortiz et  al.  (2018) that firm partnerships are a prerequisite 
for external knowledge search. More interestingly, we found 
that horizontal coopetition has a stronger impact on proactive 
search than on responsive search. Our explanation for this is 
that in the current competitive environment where customer 
preferences change rapidly and technological advancements 
are significant, if companies want to benefit from horizontal 
coopetition, they must quickly launch products (Nemeh and 
Yami 2019). This further promotes the need for companies to 
proactively search for resources that are significantly different 
from their competitors, thus gaining an advantageous position 
in the market.

diating role in the relationship between horizontal coopetition 
and SPI. Although existing research on the mediating effects of 
horizontal coopetition on SPI is scarce, studies related to how 
coopetition influences firms' innovation, such as that of Zhang

Regarding Hypothesis 4, cooperating similarity enhances the 
positive impact of horizontal coopetition on responsive search, 
while it weakens the positive impact of horizontal coopetition

Bacon et al. (2020) and Gao et al. (2017), who argue that con-

beliefs, values, and management practices facilitates tacit 
knowledge transfer. This will further promote firms' learning 
from competitors, thereby strengthening the impact of hori-

may lock participants into a set of decision-­coordination norms, 
increasing transaction costs and preventing any party from 
independently launching new market products (Mariani and 
Belitski 2023), thereby weakening the impact of horizontal coo-
petition on proactive search.
In Hypothesis 5, competing similarity strengthens the posi-
tive impact of horizontal coopetition on proactive search while 
weakening its positive impact on responsive search. This also 
confirms the view of Luo (2007) and Farazi et al. (2024) that 
within coopetitive relationships, the higher the competing simi-
larity, the more challenging it is for a company to obtain a larger 
market share and gain a competitive advantage over its compet-
itors. This further strengthens the company's tendency to gain 
a competitive advantage through boundary-­spanning search 
aimed at surpassing its competitors. Simultaneously, as Ritala 
and Hurmelinna-­Laukkanen  (2009) and Rouyre et  al.  (2024) 
pointed out, competing similarity also intensifies opportunistic 
behavior, so that prompt participants to establish mechanisms 
to prevent imitation and knowledge theft. This, in turn, inhibits 
firms' pursuit of boundary-­spanning search aimed at following 
competitors.
5.2   |   Theoretical Implications
The current study has several theoretical implications. First, 
by revealing the relationship between horizontal coopetition 
and SPI, this research expands the current body of literature on 
the relationship between coopetition and firms' sustainability. 
Recently, the significance of coopetition in firms' sustainability 
has gradually attracted scholars' attention (Planko et al. 2019; 
Munten et al. 2021; Corbo et al. 2023; Mwesiumo et al. 2023). 
However, SPI, as a crucial pathway for firms to achieve sustain-
ability, has been seldom empirically supported for its association 
with horizontal coopetition. Therefore, this study fills the gap 
in empirical research on the value of horizontal coopetition in 
enhancing SPI and also serves as a beneficial supplement to the 
study of preconditions for SPI.
Second, by innovatively introducing boundary-­spanning search 
into the research framework of the relationship between hor-
izontal coopetition and SPI, this study expands and enriches 
the research on the underlying mechanisms between them. 
Mwesiumo et al. (2023) pointed out the lack of exploration into 
the underlying mechanisms through which coopetition affects 
firms' sustainability. The scholars argued that the lack of explo-
ration into the underlying mechanisms hinders the advancement 
of coopetition theory and also impedes our understanding of the 
value creation process of coopetition. Therefore, by introducing 
boundary-­spanning search, this study not only bridges the gap 
between horizontal coopetition and SPI but also advances the 
development of coopetition theory.

ory and resource advantage theory. Coopetition theory posits

5.3   |   Practical Implications
The present study has several practical implications. First, given 
the significant role of horizontal coopetition in SPI, managers 
should maintain an open mindset and actively identify and es-
tablish cooperative relationships with trustworthy competitors. 
Managers should realize that, despite firms being in competition 
with direct rivals in the market, the value created through col-
laboration with a specific competitor in today's vast and complex 
market environment can be utilized to address challenges posed 
by other competitors. Particularly in the context of SPI, compa-
nies face issues such as scarcity of resources and technological 
deficits, while also considering risk factors. Competitors often 
encounter similar dilemmas; therefore, collaboration not only 
provides access to resources but also enables joint risk mitigation. 
Additionally, business managers should realize that horizontal 
coopetition directly drives boundary-­spanning search activi-
ties within companies. During the process of cooperating with 
competitors, managers should carefully observe and learn from 
competitors' management practices and operational strategies, 
drawing experiences and lessons to select appropriate boundary-­
spanning search strategies based on their own circumstances. 
Whether conducting boundary-­spanning search strategies aimed 
at surpassing competitors or following competitors' lead, man-
agers should closely monitor competitors' dynamic changes, es-
tablish effective information retrieval mechanisms, and better 
formulate boundary-­spanning search strategies.
Second, our study offers new potential pathways for companies 
to achieve SPI through horizontal coopetition. The research finds 
that horizontal coopetition can positively influence SPI through 
both proactive and responsive search. On the one hand, driven 
by horizontal coopetition, managers should value the role of re-
sponsive search aimed at following competitors in SPI. By ob-
serving and learning from competitors' innovative practices and 
experiences, managers can better understand and grasp market 
dynamics and industry trends. Thus, managers can reduce the 
trial-­and-­error costs and risks in SPI by engaging in responsive 
search aimed at following competitors, thereby increasing the like-
lihood of successful SPI. On the other hand, driven by horizontal 
coopetition, proactive search aimed at surpassing competitors can 
similarly foster SPI. Managers should encourage teams to think 
beyond traditional frameworks and actively explore innovative 
opportunities surpassing competitors. By conducting search 
across organizational boundaries to explore new technologies and 
concepts from different industries or domains, new perspectives 
can be provided for SPI, enhancing product differentiation and 
further strengthening the company's competitiveness.
Finally, our study suggests that managers should clearly define 
selection criteria when establishing cooperative relationships 
with competitors and choose different types of partners based 
on different strategic objectives. On the one hand, if a company 
intends to engage in boundary-­spanning search aimed at fol-
lowing competitors in the future, managers should investigate 
and evaluate the similarities in culture, values, and manage-
ment practices of potential partners. They should actively es-
tablish good cooperation with companies with high similarity. 
Cooperating similarity not only enhances the stability of cooper-
ation but also makes it easier for both parties to understand each 
other's expectations and behavioral norms, facilitating knowl-
edge sharing and learning. This accelerates the understanding 
and absorption of competitor practices, thereby achieving a 
more effective boundary-­spanning search aimed at following 
competitors. On the other hand, if a company intends to engage 
in boundary-­spanning search aimed at surpassing competitors 
in the future, managers should choose companies with high 
similarity in competitive aspects such as geographical market 
coverage, product market coverage, and technological advan-
tage areas. The higher the competing similarity, the more sim-
ilar the company's market positioning and resource allocation 
are with the partner. In this coopetition relationship, managers 
should have a deep understanding of competitors' product ad-
vantages and market competition situations and formulate strat-
egies more targeted to surpass competitors.
5.4   |   Limitations and Future Research
Although this study explores the mechanism of horizontal coo-
petition from both theoretical and empirical perspectives, some 
research limitations still need to be further investigated and im-
proved in future research. First, our sample is limited to manufac-
turing companies and does not include other industries engaged 
in SPI. Moreover, since our research results are based on samples 
from Chinese companies, the generalizability of the research con-
clusions may be limited. Therefore, in future research, these find-
ings should be validated in other countries and industries.
Second, although we implemented a multiple-­informant approach 
when collecting questionnaires to reduce common method bias, 
the subjective judgments of the respondents inevitably lead to 
some degree of common method bias in the data analysis process. 
Therefore, in future research, scholars should adopt a combination 
of subjective and objective data to avoid this issue. Additionally, 
the cross-­sectional data used in this study may not fully represent 
dynamic causal conclusions. Therefore, future research can avoid 
this issue by tracking data from multiple time points.
Third, the outcomes of sustainable innovation are not only re-
flected in product development but also in processes. Therefore,

6   |   Conclusion
This study is based on coopetition theory and constructs a the-
oretical model of “horizontal coopetition—boundary-­spanning 
search (proactive/responsive)—SPI.” Building upon this frame-
work, the study integrates resource-­advantage theory to analyze 
the moderating effect of partner similarity (cooperating/com-
peting) on the relationship between horizontal coopetition and 
boundary-­spanning search. Using survey data from 236 Chinese 
manufacturing firms, the results indicate that horizontal coopeti-
tion has a positive impact on SPI. Furthermore, horizontal coope-
tition positively influences both proactive and responsive search, 
with a stronger effect on proactive search compared to responsive 
search. Additionally, our findings suggest that both proactive and 
responsive search mediate the relationship between horizontal 
coopetition and SPI. Finally, we reveal the moderating effect of 
partner similarity on the relationship between horizontal coop-
etition and boundary-­spanning search. Specifically, competing 
similarity strengthens the positive effect of horizontal coopetition 
on proactive search but weakens the positive effect on responsive 
search; while cooperating similarity strengthens the positive ef-
fect of horizontal coopetition on responsive search but weakens 
the positive effect on proactive search.
