Abstract
Boundary spanners are pivotal in developing effective
public–private collaboration in public infrastructure pro-
jects. As boundary spanners have to account for different
interests, perspectives, and ways of working in public–
private collaborations, engaging in boundary-spanning
activities often comes with role stress, which can nega-
tively impact their job performance. However, despite
the significant levels of role stress associated with
boundary spanning, there is a dearth of empirical studies
on the role stress of boundary spanning public managers
and how it can be reduced. In this study, we empirically
investigate
the
reciprocal
relationship
between
role
stressors and boundary spanning and test if supporting
organizational conditions can alleviate role stressors. The
findings show that especially role conflict is detrimental
for boundary-spanning activities. We further find that
top-management support can diminish both role conflict
and role ambiguity and that co-worker support can help
in reducing the role ambiguity of boundary spanning pub-
lic managers.

INTRODUCTION
Public–private collaboration has the potential to improve the quality and operational efficiency of public projects
that are implemented in complex environments, such as in the case of the delivery of public infrastructure projects
(Kwak et al., 2009). This can be achieved by joining efforts to solve issues that a single organization could find diffi-
culty in resolving by itself (Ansell & Gash, 2007). Public management literature suggests, however, that barriers to
developing good collaboration between public and private partners can arise from conflicting perceptions between
the actors involved (Klijn & Teisman, 2003). Boundary spanners play a key role in implementing good collaboration
by developing, maintaining, and coordinating the collaboration between public and private sector partners in public
infrastructure projects (Noble & Jones, 2006; Van Meerkerk & Edelenbos, 2018a; Williams, 2012). They help to cap-
ture the benefits of collaboration, for instance by developing trust (Delozier & Burbach, 2021). The level of trust can
be seen as a determinant of the quality of the collaboration (Warsen et al., 2019); and boundary spanners could aid
in developing trust by searching for common ground, building sustainable relationships, and by communicating across
boundaries in order to understand the partner's needs through active listening (Van Meerkerk & Edelenbos, 2018a;
Williams, 2012). Some of the other activities they undertake include information processing (Marrone, 2010), exter-
nal representation of the organization (Ancona & Caldwell, 1992), recognizing opportunities, handling setbacks by
managing conflicts (Delozier & Burbach, 2021; Williams, 2012), and transferring and managing information flows
(Tushman & Scanlan, 1981; Williams, 2012).
Although some boundary-spanning activities may be explicitly part of a public manager's job description or orga-
nizational role, the demand, and extent of engaging in these activities is often not specified in a job profile but rather
is based on one's own perception of the project requirements (Van Meerkerk & Edelenbos, 2018a). This is challeng-
ing because boundary spanning public managers have to adapt to continuously changing project environments,
engaging in collective sensemaking with different stakeholder groups in public–private collaborations (Noble &
Jones, 2006). Their engagement in boundary-spanning activities and dealing with various intra-organizational and
extra-organizational demands and interests—which is particularly pertinent in the case of public–private collabora-
tion, where public and private values easily conflict with each other (e.g., Klijn & Teisman, 2003; Weihe, 2008)—often
causes boundary spanners to experience high levels of role stress (as compared to other organizational employees),
as highlighted in business administration research (e.g., Nygaard & Dahlstrom, 2002; Singh, 1998; Woisetschläger
et al., 2016). Role stress results from a mismatch between an individual's expectation and operation of a role. Two
key and well-known role stressors are role conflict, which may occur due to conflicting role demands (Stamper &
Johlke, 2003), and role ambiguity, which is the lack of clarity regarding the expectations from one's role (Rizzo
et al., 1970). These role stressors are experienced by persons internally, which can negatively impact the public man-
ager's job performance and the job satisfaction of boundary spanners, their boundary-spanning activities, as well as
their organizational commitment (Bettencourt & Brown, 2003; Chebat & Kollias, 2000; Crosno et al., 2009;
Stamper & Johlke, 2003). There is substantial evidence that, in general, public managers are confronted with con-
flicting or confusing demands, triggering role stressors in public management (Pandey & Wright, 2006). This is espe-
cially the case for public managers who operate in public–private partnerships or networks and in their home
organization simultaneously (Davis & Stazyk, 2016; Weihe, 2008). The relationships between boundary spanning
and role stress have generally been studied in two ways. Some studies focus on the negative effects of role stressors
on a boundary spanner's job performance (including their spanning activities), job satisfaction, and/or organizational
commitment (Chebat & Kollias, 2000; Crosno et al., 2009; Stamper & Johlke, 2003). Other studies focus on the
effects of engaging in boundary-spanning activities on the experience of role stress (e.g., Nygaard &
Dahlstrom, 2002). Most studies have either examined the effect of role stressors on boundary spanning or investi-
gated the opposite effect of boundary spanning on role stressors (Davis & Stazyk, 2016; Robertson, 1995). However,
very few studies have simultaneously investigated these relationships, to see if there is in fact a reciprocal relation-
ship between role stressors and boundary spanning.

Although there is increasing research on the importance of relational management and relational governance
mechanisms for enhancing the performance of public–private collaborations in public infrastructure projects
(e.g., Warsen et al., 2019), attention to the challenging role of boundary spanners in managing these collaborations,
and specifically how role stressors could be coped with, is scarce (cf. Noble & Jones, 2006; Van Meerkerk &
Edelenbos, 2018a). This is an under-researched topic in the public administration literature (Van Meerkerk &
Edelenbos, 2018a; cf. Davis & Stazyk, 2016).
The aim of this study is twofold. First, to test the mutual relationship between two key role stressors—that is,
role conflict and role ambiguity—on one hand, and engagement in boundary-spanning activities on the other. Second,
to test the impact of the three supporting organizational conditions on role conflict and role ambiguity. Drawing on
social psychology, business administration, and public administration, scholars suggested that top-management sup-
port, co-worker support, and role autonomy are three important organizational conditions that can facilitate
boundary-spanning activities by reducing boundary spanners' role stress (Chebat & Kollias, 2000; Eldor, 2018;
Perrone et al., 2003; Sloan, 2017). Translating them to the context of public–private collaboration, we aim to put
these factors to the test. A suitable empirical setting to study these relationships concerns the infrastructure devel-
opment sector, as public–private collaboration is a popular policy instrument for developing public infrastructure pro-
jects (Busscher et al., 2022; Warsen et al., 2019). The research data are composed of 158 survey responses from
public managers involved in developing public infrastructure projects in the Netherlands.

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK AND HYPOTHESES
We first discuss boundary-spanning activities and their contribution to public–private collaborations. We then dis-
cuss role theory and two key role stressors often experienced by boundary spanners—that is, role ambiguity and role
conflict—and develop hypotheses about how they relate to boundary-spanning activities. We subsequently discuss
different supporting organizational conditions—top-management support, co-worker support, and role autonomy—
and how they could reduce role stressors.

Boundary-spanning activities in public–private collaboration in infrastructure
projects
Boundary
spanners
are
essential
in
establishing
and
maintaining
collaborative
relationships
(Ancona
&
Caldwell, 1992). They can be defined as “people who proactively scan the organizational environment, employ activi-
ties to cross organizational or institutional boundaries, generate and mediate the information flow and coordinate
between their ‘home’ organization or organizational unit and its environment, and connect processes and actors
across these boundaries” (Van Meerkerk & Edelenbos, 2018a, p. 58). Based on role typologies developed in the liter-
ature, different types of boundary-spanning activities can be distinguished (Van Meerkerk & Edelenbos, 2018a;
Williams, 2012). The first one is information exchange and knowledge sharing. Boundary spanners manage informa-
tion exchange by collecting, processing, and translating relevant information across boundaries (Tushman &
Scanlan, 1981). They mediate the flow of information and prioritize information, which can foster informed decision-
making processes (Delozier & Burbach, 2021). The second is relational activities. Relational activities involve devel-
oping the personal network, which also facilitates knowledge and information exchange across boundaries
(Ancona & Caldwell, 1992; Brion et al., 2012). Third is the mediation and facilitation of collaboration. Boundary span-
ning requires an ability to interact with external parties, that may have diverging interests to that of the home organi-
zation, and to redirect the opinions of the partners to the benefit of the project (Lundberg, 2013). A fourth type of
activity concerns coordination and negotiation between the home organization and partner organizations. This
allows for tighter coupling and smooth running of cross-boundary collaboration (Ancona & Caldwell, 1992;

Role theory and role stressors
Role theory conceptualizes organizations as interactive social systems, where the actions of employees are con-
strained and directed by the expectations of the others in the collaborative environment (Kahn et al., 1964; Katz &
Kahn, 1978). Therefore, the behavior of boundary spanners is determined by influences, expectations, and pressures
from groups and members of their own organization, but also from groups interacting with the organization, such as
members from collaborating organizations (Van Meerkerk & Edelenbos, 2018a). Because these different groups have
different interests and values, boundary spanners are likely to experience uncertainty and/or conflicting expectations
about how they should perform their role (Klijn & Teisman, 2003). For instance, public managers in public–private
collaborations often have a “best for project” goal that prioritizes collaboration and project outcomes, but at the
same time, they have a position as clients and auditors of the private partner organizations (see e.g., Verweij, 2012).
These uncertainties or conflicts can lead to the development of role stressors, which commonly occur when
there is an incongruence between the expectation and the execution of a role. Role conflict and role ambiguity are
seen as the most critical stressors for boundary spanners (Bettencourt & Brown, 2003; Crosno et al., 2009; Wang
et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2019). According to role theory, an increase in role conflict or role ambiguity could lead to a
decrease in (job) performance (Kahn et al., 1964; Rizzo et al., 1970). In addition, role stressors are found to create
tension and dissatisfaction for boundary spanning employees and are found to be the cause of poor judgment, bad
decisions, mismanagement of resources, and overall low morale (Lysonski et al., 1988), in turn causing them to expe-
rience role stressors (Robertson, 1995).
Role conflict refers to incompatible expectations and demands associated with one's work role (Rizzo
et al., 1970), for example, incompatible expectations coming from supervisors, co-workers, and partners in the collab-
oration (Wu et al., 2019). Role ambiguity occurs when an employee lacks the information needed to effectively enact

Boundary spanners often spend much of their time interacting with stakeholders, addressing their variable, complex,
and distinctive needs and interests (Stamper & Johlke, 2003). Although these stakeholders can be manifold—for
example, citizens, customers, and/or clients—in this study, we focus only on the public and private organizations as
stakeholders, as they are directly involved in the collaboration, between whom also often multiple and contrasting role
expectations exist (Van Meerkerk & Edelenbos, 2018a). As a consequence, boundary spanners are likely to experience
relatively high levels of role conflict and role ambiguity. This leads to the following hypotheses:
Hypothesis 1a. (H1a): Public managers engaging in more boundary-spanning activities in public–
private collaboration in public infrastructure projects experience more role conflict.
Hypothesis 2a. (H2a): Public managers engaging in more boundary-spanning activities in public–
private collaboration in public infrastructure projects experience more role ambiguity.
Previous research has established that boundary spanners are vulnerable to role conflict (Kahn et al., 1964), and that
role conflict (through lower job satisfaction) negatively affects boundary spanning behavior (Bettencourt &
Brown, 2003). Role conflict arising from conflicting demands could even result in burnout (Crosno et al., 2009). This
means that role conflict becomes a hindrance in accomplishing boundary-spanning activities and affects the bound-
ary spanner's performance. In the long run, prolonged exposure to role conflict might lead to withdrawal from one's
job. This is undesirable, as changes in managerial positions in public–private collaborations may derogate relation-
ships built and may result in lower project performance (Busscher et al., 2022). In line with this, we hypothesize that
role conflict negatively impacts boundary spanning because the ability to perform boundary-spanning activities
decreases when dealing with stress due to role conflict (Cohen & Wills, 1985):
Hypothesis 1b. (H1b): Role conflict decreases the extent of boundary-spanning activities undertaken
by public managers in public–private collaboration in public infrastructure projects.
Existing studies on role ambiguity indicate that its influence on boundary spanning is mixed (i.e., positive, negative,
or neutral influence can all be found) but it appears it is predominantly a hindering or a negative stressor (Babin &
Boles, 1996; Singh, 1998; Woisetschläger et al., 2016). Often, role ambiguity is caused either by incongruent role
expectations (Rizzo et al., 1970), or by discrepancy in available information (Kahn et al., 1964). The consequence of
role ambiguity is associated with negative emotions, as it could lead to experiencing lower levels of self-confidence,
increase in anxiety, and depression (Lee & Ashforth, 1996). In a public–private collaboration, it is likely that public
project managers will experience role ambiguity because of a limited understanding about their role, due to insuffi-
cient interaction or information when working across boundaries (Wu et al., 2019). Role ambiguity could be seen as
a threat that lowers job satisfaction (Crosno et al., 2009; Kahn et al., 1964), which can reduce the extent of
boundary-spanning activities being performed in a public–private collaboration (Nygaard & Dahlstrom, 2002). This
leads to the following hypothesis:
Hypothesis 2b. (H2b): Role ambiguity decreases the extent of boundary-spanning activities under-
taken by public managers in public–private collaboration in public infrastructure projects.

employee's needs, such as organizational membership, esteem, and role status—and the reward for their boundary-

clearer about the job expectations (Hobfoll, 1989). Top-management support can be seen as a counteracting factor
against role stressors and a steady support from top-management allows the boundary spanners to cope with role
conflict and ambiguity when engaging in boundary-spanning activities. This leads to the following hypotheses:
Hypothesis 3. (H3): Top-management support reduces role conflict of public managers engaged in
public–private collaboration in public infrastructure projects.
Hypothesis 4. (H4): Top-management support reduces role ambiguity of public managers engaged in
public–private collaboration in public infrastructure projects.

Co-worker support
Research shows that support from co-workers can help in dealing with work-related issues (Nguyen & Tuan, 2022),
and diminish the negative effects of role stressors through emotional support (Cohen & Wills, 1985). Downplaying
the conflicting demands that boundary spanners face may be experienced as a lack of acknowledgment for their
efforts, which would lessen the motivation to deal with role conflict and role ambiguity (Babin & Boles, 1996;
Sloan, 2017). Instead, supporting them emotionally and assisting them in completing tasks can empower the bound-
ary spanner (Sloan, 2017). The interactions in their working environment can positively influence their behavior
(Hammer et al., 2004). Having co-worker support can reduce stress (Babin & Boles, 1996), by providing information
on how they handled similar situations in the past, and this develops trust and positive attitudes toward work
engagements (Nguyen & Tuan, 2022). On this basis, the following hypotheses can be developed:
Hypothesis 5. (H5): Co-worker support reduces role conflict of public managers engaged in public–
private collaboration in public infrastructure projects.
Hypothesis 6. (H6): Co-worker support reduces role ambiguity of public managers engaged in public–
private collaboration in public infrastructure projects.

|
Role autonomy
Earlier studies showed that having autonomy can help in coping with role stress and can increase job performance
(Singh, 1993; Singh, 1998). Role autonomy is experiencing the freedom and flexibility to interpret the role and have
the discretionary power to proceed as an individual sees fit under specific circumstances (Nygaard &
Dahlstrom, 2002; Perrone et al., 2003). For a boundary spanner, the uncertainty associated with operating across
boundaries can induce role stressors (Lysonski et al., 1988). When the desired policy outcome is complex, micro-
controlling an employee's actions could potentially hinder the task performance (Pandey & Wright, 2006). With

Hypothesis 7. (H7): Role autonomy reduces role conflict of public managers engaged in public–private

Organizations can benefit from recognizing the role of boundary spanners, providing them the room to maneu-

(Nygaard & Dahlstrom, 2002). In a study involving stakeholders from integrated water resource management, the
centrality of a boundary spanner in a multi-stakeholder network was acknowledged by the participants (Delozier &

of autonomy from their own organization and this helps boundary spanners to interact in an unbiased manner
between stakeholders (Delozier & Burbach, 2021). This helps the boundary spanner with a stronger position to bet-
ter manage the relationships with collaborators (Perrone et al., 2003). In other words, having a degree of autonomy
might not only reduce role stressors experienced by the boundary spanner, but also have a direct effect on boundary
spanning itself (Brion et al., 2012). Therefore, we hypothesize that:
Hypothesis 9. (H9): Role autonomy increases boundary-spanning activities of public managers
engaged in public–private collaboration in public infrastructure projects.

DATA AND METHODS
3.1
|
Sample and data collection
Data were collected from lead public managers through a survey questionnaire (Satheesh et al., 2022). The public
managers were selected based on their involvement in the implementation phase of infrastructure projects. These
managers are affiliated with Rijkswaterstaat, the executive agency of the Ministry of Infrastructure and Water Man-
agement in the Netherlands. The questionnaire included items derived from previous scales and new items devel-
oped based on the literature. The survey was developed in English and then translated to Dutch, as the respondent
pool comprised of Dutch speakers. Subsequently, the questionnaire was translated back to English for the analysis. A
pilot survey was distributed among Rijkswaterstaat managers who were not later invited as participants in the sur-
vey, and the received suggestions were used to refine the measurement items. The survey was online and data were
anonymized.
The surveyed projects were selected based on two criteria: they were major infrastructure development projects
(major highways, water works, bridges, and/or tunnels) and the contract type for partnering with the private sector
was either Design-and-Construct (D&C) or a Design-Build-Finance-Maintain (DBFM) (see more about the contract
types in Verweij & Van Meerkerk, 2020). Important distinguishing elements of DBFM—compared to D&C—are that
DBFM includes private financing, performance-dependent payments based on services, the inclusion of infrastruc-
ture maintenance, and increased risk transfer to the private consortium (Koppenjan et al., 2022). Project records
were available for projects that started between 2006 and 2018. Minor maintenance work projects, knowledge pro-
jects or programs, and so on were excluded because these involve different stakeholder networks, procurement

To test for bias in nonresponse, we examined the differences between the first and the last 79 responses. The
test showed that there were no significant differences between the two groups in demographic characteristics such
as age, years of experience, and gender, nor in project characteristics such as contract type, infrastructure system,
stakeholder complexity, and spatial complexity (p > 0.05).
There is the possibility of having common method bias in the tested relationships, as the test is self-reported. To
limit this bias, we had taken research design remedies (George & Pandey, 2017), such as contextualizing the study
items to fit the framework of public managers in public–private collaboration in public infrastructure projects and
ensuring anonymity of the survey respondents to prevent social desirability bias (Podsakoff et al., 2003). Harman's
single factor test was further used to test for variations in responses that can be attributed to the measurement
method leading to systemic measurement errors. The results showed that a single factor could account for only 29%
of the variance, which is much less than the threshold of 50% (Harman, 1976). This is an indication that it is unlikely
that common method bias might be a threat to this study. In addition, it can be noted from the correlation matrix that
both positive and negative correlations exist between the factors, as well as nonsignificant correlations. This demon-
strates that common method bias is probably not a major concern in this dataset (George & Pandey, 2017).

Measurement of variables
All the items for this study, presented in Table 2, were measured using a 7-point Likert scale. The choices for the
boundary spanning measurement scale ranged from 1 (“very little involvement”) to 7 (“involvement to a great
extent”). The items for the organizational conditions investigated how they experienced their role within the collabo-
ration of the specified project and it was measured by checking the extent to which the respondent was in agree-
ment with the item scaling from 1 (“strongly disagree”) to 7 (“strongly agree”).
Role conflict and role ambiguity were also measured with 1 indicating “strongly disagree” to 7 indicating
“strongly agree.” Role ambiguity was conceptualized as a lack of clarity regarding the expectation of the role
(Babin & Boles, 1996). The items for role ambiguity in Table 2 are reverse-coded, as the survey items measured the
degree of clarity present in role definition.

ANALYSIS AND RESULTS
The hypotheses were tested using a nonrecursive structural equation model in AMOS, which explores the causal
relationships among the latent variables with reciprocal relationships (Wong & Law, 1999). The best model fit was
significant (χ2 = 870, df = 516, p < 0.001) (Byrne, 2001). The model has a reasonably good overall fit with Tucker–
Lewis index (TLI = 0.903), comparative fit index (CFI = 0.916), and root mean square error of approximation
(RMSEA = 0.066).
As a first step in nonrecursive modeling, we need to establish that the model is stable by examining if the stabil-
ity index is between 1 and + 1 (Arbuckle, 2007). In our case, the stability index is 0.319, indicating that it is within
the range. Second, an exogenous variable, referred to as an instrumental variable, is added to deal with endogenous
regressor problems in the feedback loop which could create issues for model identification (Wong & Law, 1999). The
instrumental variables in this study are top-management support and co-worker support. Third, to satisfy the order
and rank conditions, we assign exogenous variables to each variable in the feedback loop.

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
Previous studies have shown that boundary spanners are important in facilitating public–private collaboration pro-
cesses, and in that way contribute to project performance (Satheesh et al., 2022). They do this by managing informa-
tion flows, mediating between different interests, building sustainable relationships, and coordinating across public
and
private
organizational
boundaries
(Delozier
&
Burbach,
2021;
Van
Meerkerk
&
Edelenbos,
2018a;
Williams, 2012). Scholars have even suggested that boundary spanners may be important to achieve potential collab-
orative advantages (Verweij & Satheesh, 2022). However, for boundary spanners to live up to this potential, reducing
role stressors—role conflict and role ambiguity—will be pivotal. The present study examined the role stressors experi-
enced by boundary spanning public managers in public–private collaboration on public infrastructure projects, as well
as key supporting organizational conditions which could reduce this role stress. The findings show that in particular
role conflict negatively affects boundary-spanning activities, and that top-management support is a key supporting
organizational condition that can reduce role conflict. In addition, both co-worker support and top-management sup-
port can reduce the role ambiguity of boundary spanning public managers.
This study is the first, to our knowledge, to examine the reciprocity between role stressors and boundary-span-
ning activities. Using a nonrecursive structural equation model, we found that boundary-spanning activities strongly

and positively influence experienced role conflict. This is in line with previous studies conducted in business adminis-
tration (Lysonski et al., 1988; Stamper & Johlke, 2003). In turn and in addition, a higher level of experienced role con-
flict reduces boundary-spanning activities. In relation to experiencing a high level of role conflict, we observe a
decrease in the number of activities undertaken by the public managers. This is in line with the findings of Wang
et al. (2020), who also found a negative influence of role conflict on boundary spanning behavior in the hospitality
sector. A possible explanation for this finding is that the difficulty to meet conflicting demands and diverging expec-
tations causes boundary spanners to refrain from performing further boundary-spanning activities, and instead
incentivizes them to focus on communication and coordination with their home organization. When experiencing
role conflict, they might adopt a behavior of being more reserved and intra-organizational oriented, instead of com-
municating and relating to the external environment which would be experienced as increasing the complexity and
stress of their management task. Given its relatively strong negative influence on boundary-spanning activities, role
conflict implies an important risk for managing the collaboration and public infrastructure projects, as it could lead to
reducing information exchange, relationship building activities, and alignment between collaborating public and pri-
vate organizations, while such activities are essential for improving project performance (Brion et al., 2012;
cf. Warsen et al., 2019; Satheesh et al., 2022).
Contrary to our expectations, we did not find significant relationships between boundary-spanning activities and
experienced role ambiguity. The empirical context of our study might be an important explanation here. The manage-
ment roles of public project managers are clearly defined and institutionalized within Rijkswaterstaat. Therefore, the
managers may know quite well what their goals, duties, and responsibilities are, and what is expected from them—
which implies they would experience a lower degree of role ambiguity. This can also be observed from Table 3,
where the mean for role ambiguity is clearly much lower than the means for all the other core variables in the study.
This situation might be different for public managers in a context where which collaboration and cross-boundary
interaction are more emergent and where boundary spanning roles are less clear or emergent (cf. Van Meerkerk &
Edelenbos, 2018a; Williams, 2012).
A second key finding of our study concerns the role of several organizational conditions in reducing role
stressors. We found especially top-management support to be important for reducing both role conflict and role
ambiguity. Top-management support can relieve some pressure from role stressors on boundary spanning public
managers. Given the previous finding on the negative impact of role conflict on boundary-spanning activities, this
underlines the importance of top-management support in facilitating boundary spanning public managers.
Augmenting top-management support by showing commitment, concern, and providing support when needed, helps
boundary spanning public managers in dealing with role conflict and contributes to reducing emotional exhaustion
(Stamper & Johlke, 2003). This finding underlines the role of top-management, not only as project champions in the
start-up of public–private collaboration (Noble & Jones, 2006), but also during the implementation phase, and as
facilitators of boundary spanning behavior (Van Meerkerk & Edelenbos, 2018b).
In addition, we find that co-worker support can be particularly relevant for reducing role ambiguity experienced
by boundary spanning public managers. Having a supportive team helps in developing a positive orientation towards
the organization by increasing trust in co-workers and supervisors (Eskerod et al., 2017). The realization that help is
available when needed increases the self-efficacy of a boundary spanner, thereby lessening role ambiguity (Eskerod
et al., 2017). This can help boundary spanners in making expectations clearer (towards them) and in this way can also
lead to more perceived self-efficacy about their spanning activities. However, we did not find a significant effect of
co-worker support on role conflict. This implies that co-worker support, according to our study, does not change or
help in dealing with experienced stress due to conflicting demands from different intra- and extra-organizational
stakeholders. To alleviate role conflict, especially top-management support is needed.
Contrary to our expectations, we did not find significant relationships between role autonomy on one hand and
experienced role stress and boundary-spanning activities on the other hand. Again, this might be explained by the
specific context of the study. When working in a project-oriented organization, the project team is seen as a refer-
ence point. In developing public infrastructure projects, the expertise of many individuals is combined and this

in-depth research should investigate how plausible this explanation really is.
This study contributes to systematically testing the role of supporting factors—organizational factors—and hin-
dering factors—role stressors—in relation to boundary-spanning activities in public–private collaborations. A future
study could assess boundary spanning roles and how these are affected by supporting organizational conditions and
role stressors, the idea being that activities come together in roles and that activities may be performed differently
depending on roles (Van den Brink et al., 2019). Future research could also explore the various coping mechanisms
and the role of personality traits and intrinsic motivation of boundary spanning public managers in dealing with role
stressors.
