Front-line managers as boundary spanners: effects of span and time on nurse
supervision satisfaction
Aim To examine the inﬂuence of nurse manager span (number of direct report
staff), time in staff contact, transformational leadership practices and operational
hours on nurse supervision satisfaction.
Background Increasing role complexity has intensiﬁed the boundary spanning
functions of managers. Because work demands and scope vary by management
position, time in staff contact rather than span may better explain managers
capacity to support staff.
Methods A descriptive, correlational design was used to collect cross-sectional
survey and prospective work log and administrative data from a convenience sample
of 558 nurses in 51 clinical areas and 31 front-line nurse managers from four acute
care hospitals in 2007–2008. Data were analysed using hierarchical linear
modelling.
Results Span, but not time in staff contact, interacted with leadership and opera-
tional hours to explain supervision satisfaction.
Conclusions With compressed operational hours, supervision satisfaction was lower
with highly transformational leadership in combination with wider spans. With
extended operational hours, supervision satisfaction was higher with highly trans-
formational leadership, and this effect was more pronounced under wider spans.
Implications for Nursing Management Operational hours, which inﬂuence the
managers daily span (average number of direct report staff working per weekday),
should be factored into the design of front-line management positions.
Keywords: health services research, managers, nursing leadership, satisfaction, span of
control, time studies

Introduction
Nursing management roles and structures have been
frequently redesigned or eliminated in response to cost-
reductions, decentralization, delegation of activities to
advanced practice nursing roles and a new public
management paradigm (Dufﬁeld 1991, Dufﬁeld &
Franks 2001, Canadian Nursing Advisory Committee
2002, Mahon & Young 2006). In many developed
countries, the scope of the traditional unit-based head
nurse role has expanded to include operations, ﬁnances
and the supervision of multidisciplinary staff across
multiple areas in programme management structures,
with increased role complexity reported (McGillis Hall
& Donner 1997, Mahon & Young 2006, Shirey et al.
2008). Although integral to the retention of a satisﬁed
and committed nursing staff (Hayes et al. 2006, Las-
chinger et al. 2009), the capacity of front-line managers
to lead, implement and evaluate system change among
staff is inﬂuenced by the scope of their role. Role
expansion as a result of reorganization is compounded
by an ageing managerial workforce and by recruitment
challenges (Laschinger et al. 2008, Shirey et al. 2008).
These trends highlight the need for evidence to effec-
tively redesign nursing management roles.
Past research on managerial effectiveness has empha-
sized managers personal attributes without necessarily
considering the organizational context (Page et al. 2003).
Span (i.e. the number of employees reporting directly to
the manager) is an important structural variable that may
inﬂuence staff and consumer outcomes. Wider spans
have been negatively associated with new nurse retention
(McCutcheon
2004),
nursing
work
environments
(McGillis Hall et al. 2006), worker engagement (Cath-
cart et al. 2004) and patient satisfaction (McCutcheon
et al. 2009) in healthcare, as well as teamwork (Gittell
2001) and performance (Bohte & Meier 2001) in the
airline and education sectors. Positive associations
between wider spans and hospital nurse turnover
(McCutcheon 2004) and staff accidents and unsafe
behaviours
in
manufacturing
settings
(Hechanova-
Alampay & Beehr 2001) have been documented.
However, when managerial leadership style and span
are examined in combination, span has been observed
to moderate the relationship between leadership and
outcomes. McCutcheon et al. (2009) found that span
moderated the relationship between managerial lead-
ership and nurse job satisfaction and patient satisfac-
tion; even highly transformational leaders could not
overcome spans that were too wide to positively inﬂu-
ence these outcomes. Similarly, the beneﬁcial inﬂuence
of emotionally intelligent leadership by nurse managers
on staff nurse empowerment was lessened under wider
spans (Lucas et al. 2008). In contrast, strong leader–
member exchange enhanced staff organizational com-
mitment under wider spans in the banking industry
(Schriesheim et al. 2000). The purpose of the present
study was to examine the relationships among span,
leadership and other covariates and nurse satisfaction
with managers supervision.
Theory
The framework was based on Open System Theory and
the boundary spanning functions of managers. A large-
scale healthcare organization is a social structure that
was conceptualized as an open system comprised of
supportive, maintenance, adaptive, production and
management
subsystems
(Meyer
&
OBrien-Pallas
2010). The management subsystem resolves conﬂicts
between hierarchical levels, coordinates substructures
and balances external demands with internal resources
and needs (Katz & Kahn 1978). Boundary spanning,
which refers to movement and coordination across
internal
and
external
system
boundaries,
involves
negotiating the inﬂow and outﬂow of energy between
subsystems and with the external environment across
semi-permeable boundaries (Gittell 2003). Boundary
spanning is assigned to organizational roles (e.g. man-
agers)
and
may
include
acquiring,
ﬁltering
and
importing information from outside entities for distri-
bution to internal users (Tushman & Scanlan 1981);
regulating the ﬂow of inputs and negotiating for needed
resources (Ancona & Caldwell 1992); and engaging in
relationship
management
to
buffer
and
inﬂuence
external and internal unit relations (Ancona & Caldwell
1992, Gittell 2003). Front-line managers are boundary
spanners who typically coordinate and integrate inputs
(e.g. information, materials and human resources),
throughput
processes
and
outputs
across
internal
boundaries.
Supervision generates boundary spanning work for
managers across subsystem, functional, spatial and
hierarchical boundaries. Nurses do not provide nursing
care in isolation; nursing work is reliant on the ﬂow of
inputs (e.g. patients and equipment), on other subsys-
tems (e.g. admissions department and laboratories) and
on other healthcare providers to accomplish interde-
pendent goals. Managers coordinate across functional
boundaries (e.g. professions and roles) and travel
physically across spatial subsystem boundaries (e.g.
departments and clinical areas) to engage in face-to-face
communication, to integrate work processes and to
directly supervise staff (Gittell 2003). Managers also

Boundary

employees reporting to the manager (i.e. span). Time in
staff contact, which enables the manager to engage,
supervise and coach staff (Ouchi & Dowling 1974,
Gittell 2003, Kramer et al. 2007), also quantiﬁes
boundary spanning activity.
By understanding the vocabulary, semantics, shared
beliefs and context, boundary spanners can effectively
meet the contrasting information needs and demands of
external areas; prevent the distortion and bias of com-
munication across boundaries; and link internal and
external areas (Tushman & Scanlan 1981). Managerial
leadership, communication and support have been
associated with nurse well-being, work relations and
satisfaction (e.g. Greco et al. 2006, Hall 2007, Cum-
mings et al. 2010, Hayes et al. 2010). Boundary span-
ners with work expertise better understand internal
processes, demands and roles and are more often con-
sulted by internal system users (Tushman & Scanlan
1981). Managerial experience has been associated with
more ﬂuid work styles, less overtime (Dunn & Schilder
1993) and more effective coping (Shirey et al. 2010) by
managers.
Rationale, research objectives and study
framework
Comparisons of span within and across organizations
can misrepresent managerial capacity to support staff
because managers may not spend the same time in staff
contact because work demands and scope vary by
management position (Ouchi & Dowling 1974). The
purpose of the present study was to examine the inﬂu-
ence of (1) span, as a measure of formal reporting
relationships and (2) time in staff contact, as a measure
of the closeness of contact between the manager and
staff, on nurse satisfaction with managers supervision.
Building on previous research (Ouchi & Dowling 1974,
Schriesheim et al. 2000, Gittell 2001, Doran et al.
2004), the interactions among span, time in staff con-
tact and leadership were examined. Operational hours
were investigated because of variation in work demands
(Morash et al. 2005) and time allocation. Thus the
main effects of span, time in staff contact, leadership,
operational hours and other manager covariates on
nurse supervision satisfaction were examined. The
interaction effects of span and time in staff contact with
leadership and operational hours were tested. The ex-
tent to which models with either span or time in staff
contact explained variation in nurse supervision satis-
faction was determined.
The main and interaction effects examined are de-
picted in Figure 1. The ﬁrst-order predictors reﬂected
boundary spanner functions (i.e. span and time in staff
contact) and characteristics (i.e. leadership) as well as
operational
hours.
Manager
covariates
included
boundary spanner characteristics (i.e. education and
experience), span characteristics (i.e. administrative and
clinical support roles) and control variables (i.e. man-
agers position tenure and worked hours). Nurse age,
day shift employment, education and registration were
nurse control variables because of associations with job
or supervision satisfaction (Blegen & Mueller 1987,
Shields & Ward 2001, Ward 2002).

Sample
Acute care hospitals in a large urban city in Ontario,
Canada were selected through purposive sampling. Out
of six hospitals invited to participate, four agreed.
Three were academic teaching hospitals and one was a
large academic-afﬁliated community hospital. A con-
venience sample of managers assigned patient care
units, clinics and/or services was recruited. The inclu-
sion criteria was employment in a current front-line
management position for at least 3 months with at least
one direct patient care area. From across all sites, 35 out
of 105 (33.3%) managers agreed to participate and of
these, 31 (88.6%) completed the study. Most managers
(96.8%) did not engage in direct patient care. Of a
potential 1786 nurses, a convenience sample of 558
nurses (31.2%) was recruited through meetings in 51
clinical areas. The mean nurse response rate per man-
ager was 33.6% (range: 10.8–57.7%). The inclusion
criterion for nurses was employment in the current
position for at least 3 months. The dataset was hierar-
chical with nurses grouped under managers. These two
levels of analysis are hereafter identiﬁed as level-1 for
nurses (at the individual level) and level-2 for managers
(at the group level). The ﬁnal sample numbered 31
managers (level-2) and an average of 18 nurses (level-1)
per manager. According to Scherbaum and Ferreters
(2009) estimates for hierarchical level models examin-
ing ﬁxed effects, the sample size was adequate to
achieve a power of 0.80. To reduce common method
effects produced by the same participant rating the
predictor (i.e. leadership) and outcome (i.e. supervision
satisfaction) variables, leadership inventories for each
manager (mean = 3.8, range: 2–7) were completed by a
separate sub-sample (n = 117) of nurses and directors
recruited through meetings or email.
Instrumentation
A measure of transformational leadership was selected
because of its positive associations with staff autonomy
(Kouzes & Posner 2002) and nurse outcomes (e.g.
Cummings et al. 2010). Transformational leadership
emphasizes how visionary, charismatic and relation-
ship-oriented behaviours generate positive affective
responses by followers and foster outstanding accom-
plishments (House & Aditya 1997). Transformational
leadership was rated by nurses and directors familiar
with the managers behaviour using the Leadership
Practices Inventory (Other Version; Posner & Kouzes
1988). Thirty items are rated on a 10-point scale
(1 = almost never, 10 = almost always). Previous alpha
reliabilities range from 0.81 to 0.92 (Posner & Kouzes
1993) for the ﬁve subscales (six items per subscale)
which consist of: challenging the process, inspiring
shared vision, enabling others to act, modelling the way
and encouraging the heart. In the present study,
exploratory factor analysis using principal components
methods indicated a single-factor solution explaining
59.9% of the variance. A mean leadership score was

of the number of direct report employees (by head-
count) assigned to the manager.
Operational hours for each assigned area were ob-
tained from managers. A measure of weekly operational
hours weighted by the number of direct reports per
assigned area was created at the manager level. Oper-
ational hours were calculated per manager as the sum of
the number of direct reports per area multiplied by the
number of weekly operational hours per area divided by
the total number of direct reports. The weighted value
was then categorized as extended or compressed. Ex-
tended operational hours consisted of mainly inpatient
units running 24 hours per day, 7 days a week. Com-
pressed operational hours collapsed all managers as-
signed areas with purely compressed hours only (e.g.
clinics) or a mix of compressed and extended areas (e.g.
clinic and an inpatient unit).
The work log method and time in staff contact met-
rics were piloted by shadowing managers (n = 3). For
inter-observer agreement, the ﬁrst author and managers
rated the presence or absence of time in staff contact at
half hourly intervals (n = 16). Cohens kappa coefﬁ-
cient, a statistical measure of inter-rater agreement, was
0.86. A time study quantiﬁed the time managers allo-
cated to staff contact for a 1-month cycle. Managers
completed work logs on 20 randomly assigned days
stratiﬁed by weekday (i.e. four Mondays and four
Tuesdays, etc.) over 10 weeks. On average, managers
completed 18.7
work
log
days.
Prospective,
self-
reported work logs were completed by managers at half
hourly intervals using automatic reminders via a pager.
Inter-observer agreements by the ﬁrst author and each
manager evaluated the presence or absence of time in
staff contact at half hourly intervals (mean = 12.7).
Cohens Kappa averaged 0.82. Time in staff contact was
the average daily amount of time (in hours) spent by the
manager interacting with direct and non-direct report
staff, physicians and students working in the area(s)
assigned to the manager. Contact included verbal,
written and email communication and person-to-person
interaction. Time in staff contact was calculated as the
average hours in staff contact per weekday.
Worked hours were the average number of hours
worked by the manager per weekday. On average,
managers recorded worked hours for 34 weekdays. The
count included overtime hours, but excluded on-call
hours and lunch breaks. Worked hours were calculated
as the sum of the worked hours per weekday divided by
the number of valid weekday entries.
The Satisfaction with My Supervisor Scale (Scarpello
& Vandenberg 1987) assessed nurses satisfaction with
the managers technical, administrative and relational
abilities with respect to supervision. Eighteen items
were rated on a ﬁve-point Likert scale (1 = very dis-
satisﬁed, 5 = very satisﬁed). Cronbachs alphas in other
studies range from 0.88 to 0.96 (Scarpello & Vanden-
berg 1987, Vandenberg & Scarpello 1992, Jones et al.
1999). Cronbachs alpha was 0.97 in this sample.
Staff surveys included demographics for age, day shift
employment (i.e. regular employment on a day shift
schedule as opposed to a rotating schedule), education
(i.e. highest level of nursing education achieved by the
nurse) and registration [i.e. registered nurse (RN) or
registered practical nurse (RPN)].
Data analyses
Statistical analyses were conducted using Statistical
Package for Social Scientists (SPSS Version 16 for Win-
dows, Chicago, IL, USA). Descriptive statistics were
performed for all variables and outliers were corrected.
Variables in the two-level hierarchical linear models
were grand-mean centred (Raudenbush & Bryk 2002).
First, a one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) assessed
within and between group variance in the outcome to
determine the intra-class correlation coefﬁcient; second,
a random coefﬁcient regression model identiﬁed signif-
icant level-1 nurse predictors; and third, intercepts-as-
outcome regression models determined whether the
outcome varied in relation to level-2 manager predictors
for main and interaction effects among manager-level
predictors. Covariates with t ratios <1 were excluded
(Raudenbush & Bryk 2002). Testing of the three-way
interaction involved a test of the main effects of the

As managerial work ﬂow observations revealed,
managers coordinated and integrated work processes,
resources, information and relationships within and
among subsystem boundaries across time. For example,
given an average 42-hours workweek, with compressed
operational hours, a manager of a 50-hours weekday
clinic covered 84% of the operational hours and the
number of staff was high during the managers workday.
That is, on any given weekday, the manager potentially
interfaced with 100% of clinic staff (Figure 3a). Thus
the managers daily span was nearly equivalent to the
total span. In contrast, with extended operational hours,
managers worked hours did not cover most of the staff
hours. For example, given an average 42-hours work-
week, a manager assigned extended operational hours
typically covered only 25% of the operational hours, yet
was responsible for work processes and staff around-
the-clock
(Figure 3b).
Extended
operational
hours
lower the number of direct report staff working during
the managers workday. That is, on any given weekday,
the manager only interfaced with approximately 7% (1/
14) of unit staff (assuming 12-hours shifts). Thus the
managers daily span was much lower than the total
span. The study ﬁndings highlight that time, in the form
of operational hours, is another boundary that manag-
ers must manage to ensure organizational functioning.
Because operational hours inﬂuence not only the hours
covered by the manager, but also the daily span of the
manager, operational hours are an important factor to
consider for front-line management positions. This
ﬁnding extends the boundary spanning literature with
regard to temporal boundaries.
The key ﬁnding for nurse supervision satisfaction was
that the relationship between span and leadership var-
ied by operational hours. When managers had highly
transformational leadership styles and were assigned
compressed operational hours in combination with
wide spans, nurse supervision satisfaction was lower.
This interaction makes sense because with compressed
operational hours, the managers daily span is high. One
interpretation is that with compressed operational
hours, large numbers of staff may overwhelm the
capacity of even highly transformational managers to
positively inﬂuence supervision satisfaction. No com-
parative studies examining these relationships were
located.
Alternatively, this interaction may indicate that some
nurses were more satisﬁed with supervision when
managers with compressed operational hours had less
transformational leadership styles in combination with
wide spans. Under wide spans, managers could have
engaged in other types of behaviours (e.g. more task-
focused leadership) to resolve work process and rela-
tional issues that impeded nurses workﬂow, hence
enhancing nurse supervision satisfaction. In the present
study, managers assigned compressed operational hours
often oversaw areas characterized by high volume
throughput (e.g. clinic visits and procedures). Shorter
and frequent work cycles intensify coordination de-
mands between work roles and groups. Nurses may
have been more satisﬁed with supervision when man-
agers focused more on tasks or managed by exception,
which could have, in turn, enhanced nurses workﬂow
and productivity. The leadership instrument used in the
present study was developed based on project leaders. A
possible conjecture is that other leadership styles may
be effective when managing high volume operations.
Although person-focused leadership, including trans-
formational leadership, has been frequently associated
with higher staff satisfaction, equivocal or positive re-
sults have also been observed in relation to task-focused
leadership (Cummings et al. 2010). In the present study,
task-focused behaviours may have been conducive to
meeting nurses needs for supervision when managers
with compressed operational hours managed wide
spans in areas with high volume throughput.
In contrast, with extended operational hours, nurses
were more satisﬁed with supervision when managers
were highly transformational, and this effect was even

spans.

counterintuitive, this interaction illustrates the impor-
tance of operational hours. With extended operational
hours, narrow spans resulted in very low daily spans,
whereas wide spans produced more moderate daily
spans. In the present study, extended operational hours
combined with wide spans may have enabled highly
transformational managers to interface with more staff
during weekdays (i.e. more moderate daily spans) to
resolve issues. This is consistent with Blau (1968) who
theorized that access to managerial support for resolu-
tion of complex work problems is a central underlying
mechanism of an effective reporting structure between
managers and skilled professionals. The present study
result is similar to Schriesheim et al.s (2000) observa-
tion that stronger supervisory leadership was associated
with higher staff organizational commitment under
wider spans.
However, the results are dissimilar to two studies
which reported the negative moderating effect of wide
spans on nurse job satisfaction (McCutcheon et al.
2009) and empowerment (Lucas et al. 2008) in spite of
strong person-focused leadership. A possible explana-
tion for the contrary ﬁnding is that the outcomes of
nurse job satisfaction and empowerment are broader
concepts that reﬂect multi-faceted aspects of nurses
work experiences. For example, workload, work-life
balance and opportunities for professional development
may not necessarily be amenable to intervention by the
manager, but may be more sensitive to other factors
(e.g. availability of organizational resources). In com-
parison to job satisfaction and empowerment, nurse
supervision satisfaction is a narrower concept focused
on the direct-report relationship.
The present study results suggest that trade-offs may
exist in the staff outcomes associated with wide vs.
narrow spans. That is, outcomes such as supervisor or
organizational commitment and organizational citizen-
ship behaviours could be theorized to improve under
wide spans in combination with extended operational
hours. Replication of the study design is warranted.
Future studies should consider the temporal boundaries
(i.e. operational hours) assigned to managers as well as
outcomes sensitive to managerial coverage and to daily
spans.
Although managers require time to interact, to build
relationships and to coach staff (Ouchi & Dowling
1974, Gittell 2003, Kramer et al. 2007), time in staff
contact did not predict nurse satisfaction with man-
agers supervision. However, time in staff contact may
interact with span and this was not tested; a four-way
interaction that includes span, or a sample with equiv-
alent operational hours, may clarify these relationships.
Alternatively, Arman et al. (2009) noted that managers
may spend time with the same, instead of varied,
employees. Future studies of time in staff contact may
beneﬁt from more detailed measurement of social rela-
tionships (e.g. social network analysis).
Limitations
The cross-sectional study design limits claims between
cause and effect. Although managers were less tenured,
less experienced and more likely to hold graduate de-
grees than their counterparts in previous Canadian
studies (Laschinger et al. 2008, Lucas et al. 2008,
McCutcheon et al. 2009), these covariates did not
explain variation in nurse supervision satisfaction. The
potential response bias of self-report measures from a
convenience sample must be considered. Common
method bias was reduced by collecting data using dif-
ferent raters, sources and media.
Although
statistically
signiﬁcant,
the
three-way
interaction ought to be interpreted cautiously and
warrants further investigation. The present ﬁnding
may have occurred by chance; however, Bonferronis
correction was applied and the power to detect dif-
ferences is lessened with higher order terms in inter-
actions (Aiken & West 1991). The analysis accounted
for clustering effects of staff under managers. Unit-
level variation was not considered because one-third of
managers had only one unit, and too few hospitals
participated to consider the inﬂuence of hospital-level
variables.
Implications for nursing management
In the present study, transformational leadership posi-
tively inﬂuenced nurse supervision satisfaction. Given
that transformational leadership practices can be learned
(Kouzes & Posner 2002), managers and organizations
are encouraged to continue to invest in professional
development opportunities to enhance leadership skills.
This strategy maximizes existing managerial resources
without increasing the number of managers. However,
neither leadership, nor span, can be considered in isola-
tion. Operational hours also need to be factored into
front-line management job descriptions. In the present
study, when managers were highly transformational
leaders, moderate daily spans for nurse supervision sat-
isfaction were enabled by narrow spans with compressed
operational hours and by wide spans with extended
operational hours.
Co-manager models, which have been recommended
to improve manager outcomes and staff retention

Conclusion
As
healthcare
organizations
contend
with
stafﬁng
shortages, front-line managers are critical to the reten-
tion and supervision of human resources. The span and
operational hours assigned to managers affect their
capacity to inﬂuence nurse supervision satisfaction.
Although outcomes may improve under narrower (or
wider) spans, the interactive effects of span, leadership,
operational hours and other covariates merit consider-
ation. Additional research is needed to explicate the
conditions under which managers inﬂuence organiza-
tional, staff and healthcare consumer outcomes.
