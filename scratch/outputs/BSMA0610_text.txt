Previous studies on IT alignment have attempted to address if, and how, the presence of IT outsourcing can help 
attain IT alignment. In this study, we advance the literature by investigating how organizations appropriate IT 
outsourcing for achieving IT alignment. Specifically, drawing on the adaptive structuration theory (AST), we 
construct a model examining factors associated with outsourcing appropriation, including consensus of appro -
priation (COA) and faithfulness of appropriation (FOA), and how the latter are associated with IT alignment. To 
test our proposed model, we use a questionnaire survey of 170 IT executives working in Chinese firms. Our 
results indicate that both shared IT affordance for knowledge acquisition and boundary spanning positively affect 
COA and FOA. Boundary buffering positively affects the FOA, but not the COA. Further, our results also show the 
positive effects on IT alignment from COA and FOA, and the significant moderating effects of adoption of cloud 
computing and the non-significant moderating effects of degree of outsourcing in the relationships. Interestingly, 
the moderating effects of three cloud computing adoption modes (SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS) on the impact of 
outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment vary significantly. These findings enrich our understanding towards 
how to appropriate IT outsourcing for IT alignment and contribute to the literature on IT alignment in IT 
outsourcing.   
1.Introduction 
Nowadays, firms capitalize on information technologies (IT) to 
enhance business value and survive in the volatile global marketplace 
(Bernat and Karabag, 2019 ; Queiroz, 2017 ). The enabling roles of in-
formation technology, however, are heavily dependent on IT alignment, 
i.e., the degree of alignment between organizational IT strategies and 
business structures (e.g., business mission, objectives, and processes) 
(Henderson et al., 1996 ). Prior research has confirmed the strategic 
value of IT alignment in optimizing IT spending, gaining IT agility, and 
achieving competitive advantage (Bernat and Karabag, 2019 ; Reich and 
Benbasat, 2000 ; Trang et al., 2021 ). However, since alignment tends to 
consume substantial time, human, financial, material, and other assets, 
the achievement of IT alignment is difficult in reality (Saldanha et al., 
2020 ). To mitigate these restrictions, some firms adopt IT outsourcing 
solutions that enable firms to gain IT capabilities and resources without 
increasing new IT spending in deploying hardware and software. 
Despite this appealing solution, prior research has revealed some mixed findings regarding both a positive and negative relationship be-
tween IT outsourcing and IT alignment (see Appendix A). Some research 
has found that outsourcing, by providing leading edge technology for 
supporting business strategies or by offering talents and new IT solutions 
to improve IT capabilities, contributes to IT alignment (Park et al., 2017 ; 
Wallenburg et al., 2019 ). However, others have found that outsourcing 
risks related to improper appropriation of suppliers and service contents 
may impede IT alignment. For example, ineffective outsourcing activ-
ities deriving from improper outsourcing appropriation strategies may 
introduce unexpected risks, such as opportunistic behavior, added costs, 
and even business failure (Das and Grover, 2018 ; Lioliou et al., 2019 ). As 
the process of alignment between IT strategies and business structures is 
dynamic (Sha et al., 2020 ), unsuitable appropriation may also result in 
outsourcing inertia or lock-in with unsuitable vendor, which will 
adversely affect IT alignment (Hirschheim and Sabherwal, 2001 ; Mol 
and Kotabe, 2011 ). 
To reconcile these mixed findings, some scholars seek to determine 
the criteria for suitable outsourcing appropriation (Kunttu, 2017 ; Liou

outsourcing appropriation and have not adequately connected 
outsourcing appropriation to IT alignment. Their findings thus shed few

may occur. Recently, several attempts have been made to address this

in these studies towards the antecedents of outsourcing appropriation 
and their impacts on IT alignment. 
We construct our research based on the adaptive structuration theory 
(AST) perspective. In particular, AST provides two important concepts: 
consensus of appropriation (COA) and faithfulness of appropriation 
(FOA), as a unique perspective for understanding the appropriation of IT 
outsourcing (DeSanctis and Poole, 1994; Ko et al., 2021). COA refers to 
the extent of agreement between IT department and business depart -
ment regarding the appropriation of IT outsourcing (Deng et al., 2021; 
Kang et al., 2012). FOA is defined as the extent of which the outsourcing 
services are appropriated in a way faithful to the structural features (e. 
g., IT capabilities, technical functions, or service agreement) and spirit 
(e.g., value and objectives) contained in these services (Deng et al., 
2021; Guan et al., 2020). 
The two key concepts have important implications for our under -
standing of how organizations appropriate IT outsourcing for IT align -
ment. First, given that an important criterion for measuring IT alignment 
maturity is the partnership between the IT and business departments 
(Kearns and Sabherwal, 2006; Tallon et al., 2016; Valorinta, 2011), the 
attitudinal consensus between the two parties towards outsourcing 
appropriation may be a key factor that affects the partnership quality, 
which contributes to a better understanding of how organizations 
appropriate IT outsourcing for IT alignment from the internal relation -
ship perspective. Second, faithful or unfaithful appropriation indicates 
whether an organization appropriately follows the IT outsourcing sup-
plier's original design intentions (Nguyen et al., 2021), which contrib -
utes from the external relationship perspective. Therefore, the COA and 
FOA perspectives in AST are adaptive for deepening our understanding 
of how organizations appropriate IT outsourcing for IT alignment. 
Taken together, it is essential to explore the antecedents of COA and 
FOA, and their impacts on IT alignment. In addition, according to AST, 
the outcomes of structure appropriation may vary depending on the 
organizational environment (DeSanctis and Poole, 1994; Nguyen et al., 
2021). Hence, we also attempt to reveal the moderating factors on the 
impact of outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment. Specifically, we 
attempt to answer the following questions: 
RQ-1. What factors could promote COA and FOA towards IT 
outsourcing? 
RQ-2. Does COA and FOA improve IT alignment? What are the 
boundary conditions of these relationships? 
Our study makes the following three contributions to the IT 
outsourcing and AST literature. First, although prior research attempts 
to connect IT outsourcing to IT alignment, they draw a series of con-
tradictory arguments regarding whether IT outsourcing promotes or 
hinders IT alignment (Lioliou et al., 2019; Silvius et al., 2013). We seek 
to address the “IT outsourcing-alignment” paradox from the outsourcing 
appropriation perspective, and based on AST, divide it into two di-
mensions, i.e., COA and FOA. Second, although the COA and FOA are 
likely to be important for IT alignment, it is unclear how organizations 
form the attitudinal consensus and structural faithfulness towards 
outsourcing appropriation. Based on AST, we investigate the anteced -
ents of COA and FOA towards outsourcing appropriation from the 
technology and team characteristics perspectives. In doing so, this study not only adds to our knowledge on the antecedents of suitable 
outsourcing appropriation for IT alignment, but also provides valuable 
suggestions about how organizations achieve the suitable outsourcing 
appropriation. Third, there has been little focus in previous studies 
regarding the moderating factors on the impact of outsourcing appro -
priation on IT alignment. To further deepen our understanding of the 
impact of outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment, this study also 
reveals the boundary conditions of these relationships by considering 
two organizational environment factors: degree of outsourcing and 
cloud computing adoption, as moderators. 
2.Theory and hypothesis development 
2.1. Adaptive structuration theory (AST) 
AST focuses on studying differences in organizational outcomes that 
may occur when an advanced technology is used (Kang et al., 2012; 
Romanow et al., 2018). Based on AST, these differences are more likely 
to result from the appropriation of social structures embedded in an 
advanced technology (Kang et al., 2012). AST posits the core concept of 
appropriation, which is defined as the selection and adaption of social 
structures contained in an advanced technology (Deng et al., 2021; Ko 
et al., 2021). It emphasizes that appropriation includes two unique 
features, i.e., consensus of appropriation (COA) and faithfulness of 
appropriation (FOA). To be specific, on the one hand, IT users are likely 
to have different opinions regarding which and how features and func-
tions should be used (Kang et al., 2012). For example, IT departments 
may mostly focus on some function modules that can integrate with 
existing modules, while other business departments may pay more 
attention to some function modules that can align with organizational 
business structures. On the other hand, IT users can use these functions 
in a manner faithful or unfaithful to the original intents of designers 
(Romanow et al., 2018). Together, AST provides a highly relevant lens 
for studying how organizations appropriate an advanced technology. 
Prior research on AST mostly focuses on the IT or IS appropriation, e. 
g., information and communication technologies (ICT), accounting in-
formation system (AIS), and group decision support system (GDSS) (Ko 
et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2021). However, little is devoted to analyzing 
appropriation of outsourcing from the AST perspective. As posited by 
Deng et al. (2021), IT outsourcing, as a service mode triggered by 
advanced technologies, also has the issue of structure appropriation. The 
structure appropriation towards IT outsourcing involves supplier types 
(e.g., focused IT service suppliers or IT solution suppliers), contract 
types (e.g., contract terms, payment schedule agreements, or service- 
level agreements), delivery types (e.g., on-time delivery or quality de-
livery), service contents (e.g., system development, operation, or 
maintenance), and other aspects (Deng et al., 2021; Perçin, 2019). 
Despite adopting AST to explain how users select and use outsourcing in 
these studies (Deng et al., 2021), it remains unclear on how users ach-
ieve COA and FOA towards IT outsourcing and their impacts on IT 
alignment. 
Different from individual level appropriation towards individual- 
based technology (Nguyen et al., 2021; Schmitz et al., 2016), we focus 
on team level appropriation towards outsourcing services triggered by 
advanced technologies (Kang et al., 2012). As proposed by Kang et al. 
(2012), IT services adopted by organizations today are more “team- 
based”, rather than “individual-based”, because the maximum effect of 
IT services can be attained if team members use IT services together. 
Accordingly, we adopt the AST for team-based to examine the ante-
cedents of outsourcing appropriation and its impacts on IT alignment. 
Based on AST for team-based, appropriation is mainly affected by 
two factors, i.e., technology and team characteristics (DeSanctis and 
Poole, 1994). With respect to the technology factor, we adopt a tech-
nology affordance view to understand the role of IT in outsourcing 
appropriation. Prior research has suggested that technology affordance 
is a promising lens for predicting technology use or appropriation T. 3(Abhari et al., 2017; Chatterjee et al., 2020; Leonardi, 2013). Technol -

features of a technology offer to its users to attain objectives (Abhari 
et al., 2017; Lin and Kishore, 2021). Markus and Silver (2008) also

appropriation moves. Given that this study focuses on the team-based 
appropriation, we adopt the shared affordance perspective (Leonardi, 
2013), i.e., the affordance that is shared by team members. Furthermore, 
drawing on the emerging literature on technology affordance, we focus 
on shared IT affordance for knowledge acquisition i.e., the knowledge 
acquisition potential afforded by IT feature use for its users. This is 
highly relevant for outsourcing appropriation because the knowledge or 
information owned by teams is the most important determinant for how 
teams appropriate IT outsourcing (Deng et al., 2021; Kearns and Sab-
herwal, 2006). As such, we seek to investigate the role of shared IT 
affordance for knowledge acquisition in outsourcing appropriation. 
With respect to the team characteristic factors, we focus on boundary 
behavior characteristics of IT department. A line of research adopts a 
boundary view of outsourcing appropriation, and suggests that 
outsourcing appropriation is a boundary decision where the boundaries 
of the function and relevant IT activities of IT departments have changed 
significantly because of the participation of the outsourcing supplier (Du 
and Pan, 2013). Therefore, we also seek to examine the linkage between 
boundary behaviors and outsourcing appropriation. Existing research 
suggests two types of boundary behaviors: boundary spanning and 
boundary buffering (Zhang et al., 2022). Boundary spanning is 
conceptualized as activities a team implements to gain resources and 
support from its external environment (Klueter and Monteiro, 2017). 
Prior research has widely acknowledged the key roles of team boundary 
spanning activities, such as forging relationship, knowledge sharing, and 
lobbying others for support, in obtaining a team access to needed in-
formation for decision making (Du and Pan, 2013; Faraj and Yan, 2009). 
In comparison with boundary spanning, boundary buffering is a disen -
gagement strategy, and refers to behaviors a team undertakes to shield 
itself against uncertainties and disturbances from the external environ -
ment (Wu et al., 2020). 
Furthermore, the appropriation process, in essence, reflects a social 
interaction among social structures, technologies, and users triggered by 
advanced technologies (Kang et al., 2012). Specifically, based on AST, 
the adoption of advanced technologies can be considered as the input of 
a social structure, which coupled with other organizational structures (e. 
g., business structures), stimulates the social interaction among social 
structures, technologies, and users, and consequently forms a new social 
structure that influences organizational outcomes. The process of 
forming a new social structure via social interaction is termed as 
structuration (Kang et al., 2012). As proposed by Deng et al. (2021), IT 
alignment can be regarded as the result of mutual coordination of 
organizational IT with business structures. However, prior research has 
largely overlooked IT alignment as an outcome of structuration. 
Accordingly, we suggest that IT alignment may be the outcome of 
structuration, and be affected by COA and FOA. 
Finally, AST also posits that organizational environment factors play 
key roles in the structuration process (DeSanctis and Poole, 1994; 
Nguyen et al., 2021). We expect that the effect of outsourcing appro -
priation on IT alignment may vary in the two contexts of traditional IT 
outsourcing and cloud computing. With respect to traditional IT 
outsourcing, we focus on investigating whether the linkage between 
outsourcing appropriation and IT alignment is moderated by the degree 
of outsourcing. With respect to cloud computing, in essence, cloud 
computing is also a service mode triggered by advanced IT where con-
figurable computing resources or services, e.g., software, hardware, 
infrastructure, servers, and platform, are stored in a shared pool and are 
transmitted on demand to clients through network (Gao and Sunyaev, 
2019; Mell and Grance, 2010). In contrast with IT outsourcing, cloud 
computing possesses certain unique features, e.g., pay per use, scal-
ability, elasticity, resource sharing, configurability, and ubiquity, which contribute to forming low-cost and agile IT solutions (Arvanitis et al., 
2017; Mitra et al., 2018). The services provided by cloud computing can 
be conceptualized as a three-dimensional model, i.e., software as a 
service (SaaS), platform as a service (PaaS), and infrastructure as a 
service (IaaS) (Lal and Bharadwaj, 2016). However, earlier research on 
cloud computing is mostly conducted in a holistic view (Liu et al., 2018). 
Scant research on this topic, especially for empirical research, have 
examined the impacts of the three-dimensional model respectively, nor 
related the three dimensions to IT alignment. As the effectiveness of the 
mechanism by which outsourcing appropriation impact IT alignment 
may vary with the contextual variables, research on the impacts of the 
adoption of cloud computing (including SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS) on the 
linkage between outsourcing appropriation and IT alignment is neces -
sary to clarify this issue. 
2.2. Theoretical model and hypotheses 
Drawing on the adaptive structuration theory and relevant literature, 
we construct a research model to understand the antecedents of COA 
and FOA, their impacts on IT alignment, and boundary conditions of the 
impact of outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment (see Fig. 1). Spe-
cifically, we examine the antecedents of outsourcing appropriation from 
the shared IT affordance for knowledge acquisition, boundary spanning, 
and boundary buffering perspectives. Furthermore, we also investigate 
the effects of COA and FOA on promoting IT alignment. Finally, we 
explore the moderating effects of the degree of outsourcing and cloud 
computing adoption (including SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS) on the impact of 
outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment. 
2.2.1. Shared IT affordance for knowledge acquisition and outsourcing 
appropriation 
Shared IT affordance for knowledge acquisition refers to the 
knowledge acquisition potential afforded by IT feature use for its users 
(Abhari et al., 2017; Leonardi, 2013; Nambisan and Baron, 2010). Prior 
research has confirmed that the knowledge acquisition capability 
afforded by some IT features, e.g., visibility, reviewability, association, 
and other aspects, makes it convenient for teams to interact with others 
(Sun et al., 2019). According to AST, outsourcing appropriation means a 
social interaction between IT departments, business departments, and 
suppliers regarding how organizations appropriate outsourcing struc-
tures (Deng et al., 2021; Kang et al., 2012; Kearns and Sabherwal, 2006). 
Some IT tools (e.g., wikis, blogs, or other social networking applica -
tions), served as social interaction platforms, can support different forms 
of knowledge acquisition activities among IT users, such as discussion, 
knowledge sharing, and social networking, for decision-making pro-
cesses towards how organizations appropriate IT outsourcing (Lin and 
Kishore, 2021; Wagner et al., 2014). 
From the internal interaction perspective, these knowledge acquisi -
tion activities enabled by IT tools help to achieve the joint participation 
of IT departments and business units in IT and business planning (Kearns 
and Sabherwal, 2006; Wagner et al., 2014). That is, IT departments can 
strengthen the understanding of how to better support business strate -
gies through IT, and business departments can deepen the recognition of 
the intentions of outsourcing strategies made by IT departments. 
Consequently, both parties are more likely to attain an agreement on 
how a firm appropriates outsourcing services to achieve business ob-
jectives. From the external interaction perspective, these IT tools also 
provide more communication opportunities for the two sides of IT de-
partments and suppliers (Abhari et al., 2017; Autio et al., 2018), thereby 
allowing suppliers to clearly understand firms' IT construction demands 
and reasonably design outsourcing structures for firms' demands. It also 
enables IT departments to obtain more information on structural fea-
tures and spirit from outsourcing services, which can help a firm more 
faithfully select suppliers and service contents. Accordingly, we posit

2.2.2. Boundary spanning and outsourcing appropriation 
Extant research has adopted the boundary spanning and boundary 
buffering perspectives to explore how the team boundary management 
impacts decision-making processes related to IT outsourcing (Valorinta, 
2011 ). A stream of research has widely acknowledged the benefits of 
cross-boundary interactions (Klueter and Monteiro, 2017 ), and suggests 
that some boundary spanning activities, such as forging relationship, 
knowledge sharing, and lobbying others to obtain support, help teams to 
gain needed information for outsourcing decision (Hawkins and Reza-
zade, 2012 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ). 
Employees of IT departments engaged in boundary spanning activ-
ities are able to acquire the information related to organizational busi-
ness objectives (Zhang et al., 2022 ), and based on this information, to 
design an adaptive IT appropriation planning that can be recognized by 
business departments. In addition, some cross-interaction behavior of IT 
departments, e.g., building relationships with business unit or 
persuading business unit to support the team's work (Klueter and 
Monteiro, 2017 ), can strengthen the mutual understanding of each 
other's needs, and effectively mitigate conflicts arising from the differ -
ences of understanding each other's needs, thus contributing to the COA. 
Further, boundary spanning activities cover not only the interaction 
between departments within organization, but also the linkage between 
firms and suppliers (Deng et al., 2022 ). Faraj and Yan (2009) proposed 
that organization department rarely work independently of external 
support and rarely possessed all of information they require within the 
organization. As such, firms which employ IT outsourcing businesses 
tend to adopt some boundary spanning strategies, e.g., setting “task 
coordinators ” (Faraj and Yan, 2009 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ). Their work 
mainly contacts with outsourcing suppliers where they can gain useful 
information regarding how to use IT outsourcing effectively, which 
contributes to firms' appropriating IT outsourcing in a faithful way to the 
original intent of suppliers. Additionally, in the interaction between 
firms and suppliers, suppliers can understand the outsourcing objectives 
of firms more comprehensively and develop reasonable outsourcing 
modes for clients (Teo and Bhattacherjee, 2014 ), which makes it 
possible for the client to choose outsourcing services in ways that are 
intended by suppliers, thereby contributing to the FOA. 
H2a.Boundary spanning is positively associated with COA. 
H2b.Boundary spanning is positively associated with FOA. 2.2.3. Boundary buffering and outsourcing appropriation 
Compared with boundary spanning, which is characterized as an 
engagement strategy dependent of external support, boundary buffering 
is a disengagement strategy free from external disturbance and overload 
needs (Faraj and Yan, 2009 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ), which may hinder the 
COA. As for business units, their strategic focus lies in whether and how 
business objectives can be achieved, rather than how to achieve objec -
tives through IT outsourcing, mainly because of the complexity and 
specialization of technological details (Deng et al., 2021 ). In other 
words, business units may not always be able to participate in 
outsourcing appropriation in a positive and proactive manner. If 
boundary buffering is imposed, business departments are even disen -
gaged from outsourcing appropriation. Their options of outsourcing 
from business departments are not fully considered and accepted, 
rendering it difficult to achieve a high level of COA. 
In addition, boundary buffering may promote the FOA. In the 
outsourcing appropriation process, IT departments are frequently 
affected by disturbances from the external environment, such as 
outsourcing relationship conflicts or information overload (Schmidt and 
Uydess, 2012 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ), which may bring challenges to the 
FOA. In particular, an IT department may face lobbying by suppliers and 
other departments within the client's organization (Lioliou et al., 2019 ; 
Teo, 2012 ). The employees of IT departments often face different or even 
contradictory outsourcing objectives from senior leaders, business de-
partments, or other departments, which may easily lead to role conflict 
(Wu et al., 2020 ). Through buffering, IT departments can protect 
themselves from uncertainty and turbulence to increase the possibility 
of rational action. Boundary buffering helps to effectively transfer and 
manage different or even contradictory demands on IT departments via 
formal rules or informal norms (Faraj and Yan, 2009 ; Wu et al., 2020 ); 
this is also helpful for IT departments to buffer pressures from the 
external environment, thereby allowing them to devote more energy to 
the rational consideration of the service features and service spirit 
contained in these services from suppliers. Protected by boundary 
buffering, they can formulate rules to set priorities of the needed service 
features and service spirit from suppliers via communication and dis-
cussion within the department. In sum, boundary buffering is help for 
the FOA. Thus, we hypothesize that: 
H3a.Boundary buffering is negatively associated with COA. 
H3b.Boundary buffering is positively associated with FOA.

the outcome of structuration, and be influenced by outsourcing appro -
priation (including COA and FOA) (DeSanctis and Poole, 1994). 
In terms of COA, it means that both the IT and business departments 
participate in outsourcing appropriation, and reach a consensus 
regarding how organization appropriate IT outsourcing (Deng et al., 
2021; Kang et al., 2012). These processes promote the knowledge inte-
gration of IT and business managers, and help them create a “cross 
function interface”, e.g., brainstorm, conversation, and session discus -
sion (Valorinta, 2011). This offers opportunities for both parties to 
conduct “collective learning”, which further contributes to providing 
adaptive IT outsourcing solutions for business strategies. Further, the 
attitudinal consensus towards IT outsourcing appropriation reflects the 
partnership between the IT and business departments (Kearns and 
Sabherwal, 2006; Valorinta, 2011), which enables them to deepen the 
understanding of each other's values, objectives, and missions. As such, 
both parties are likely to coordinate their work for work completeness 
and consistency when there are conflicts in their work, thereby 
contributing to IT alignment. 
In terms of FOA, it reflects the degree to which a client's appropriate 
IT outsourcing in a way alignment with the general objectives and 
procedures intended to be provided and affordable by the supplier (Deng 
et al., 2021; Kang et al., 2012). A high degree of FOA helps clients to 
build the partnership with suppliers because suppliers believe that they 
are trustworthy by clients (Teo, 2012). Therefore, it is more likely for 
suppliers to design suitable IT outsourcing solutions to support clients' 
business objectives. In addition, prior research has proposed that using 
outsourcing structures in a similar way enables suppliers to better 
display their core competencies and advantage resources (Deng et al., 
2021), which contributes to achieving IT alignment with the help of IT 
outsourcing suppliers. Accordingly, we hypothesize: 
H4a. COA is positively associated with IT alignment. 
H4b. FOA is positively associated with IT alignment. 
2.2.5. Moderating effect of the degree of outsourcing 
The degree of outsourcing is defined as the degree to which the work 
associated with IT activities is outsourced (Hanafizadeh and Ahad, 
2018). When the degree of outsourcing is high, it is clear that the firm 
relies more on external IT resources and capabilities to achieve IT 
alignment. Unreasonable outsourcing appropriation is more likely to 
lead to the occurrence of negative outsourcing events, such as suppliers' 
speculative behaviors (Lioliou et al., 2019). Conversely, when the de-
gree of outsourcing is low, internal resources and capabilities make a 
chief contribution to the construction and maintenance of IT infra-
structure, and the impact of outsourcing appropriation on achieving IT 
alignment is minor. As such, we suggest that the degree of outsourcing 
may moderate the impact of outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment. 
Specifically, in the high degree of outsourcing adoption context, 
although firms can gain more external IT resources and capabilities, they 
may need to take more outsourcing risks simultaneously (Lioliou et al., 
2019), e.g., the failure of large outsourcing investment and the in-
compatibility between external services and internal business system. In 
such case, COA is more crucial for IT alignment. This is because, COA, as 
the results of a joint agreement between the IT and business departments 
towards outsourcing appropriation, reflects a prudent outsourcing in-
vestment adapted for the technology and business needs of firms (Kang 
et al., 2012), which contributes to successfully developing and 
strengthening outsourcing investment capabilities to support IT align -
ment under the high degree of outsourcing context. Additionally, the 
high degree of outsourcing may lead to unintentional clusters of frag-
mented technology silos that constrain the organization from effectively 
executing routine business activities let alone launching IT alignment 
(Lu and Ramamurthy, 2011). Prior research has demonstrated that as 
COA comprehensively considers the technology value and business value, it could promote perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use 
for a technology or a service (Kang et al., 2012). Therefore, COA pro-
motes the identification of the value of a technology or a service and 
avoids making misjudgments on the appropriation of outsourcing ser-
vices that leads to fragmented silos, which contributes to breaking the 
fragmented technology silos in the high degree of outsourcing and at-
tains a higher degree of IT alignment. 
Furthermore, outsourcing services are designed with the general 
intention of supporting their clients, such that they can have energies 
and resources to focus on core competence, rather than on some tech-
nical details, e.g., system specifications, implementation, and deploy -
ment (Guan et al., 2020; Kroes and Ghosh, 2010). When the degree of 
outsourcing is high, firms that faithfully adopt these services with such 
design intention would find that these services dramatically reduce 
technology development time and translate time into the focus on a 
higher degree of IT alignment. In addition, the high degree of 
outsourcing means that outsourcing suppliers may design more adaptive 
service structures (e.g., technical resources, technical capabilities, or 
service rules) for their clients and embed their own technical and 
resource advantages into these structures, because outsourcing suppliers 
also involve more interests (Deng et al., 2022; Koo et al., 2019; Teo, 
2012; Teo and Bhattacherjee, 2014). Under such circumstances, if firms 
faithfully appropriate these structures, they would promote IT align -
ment efficiently and effectively with the support of suppliers' advantage 
resources and capabilities. Accordingly, we hypothesize: 
H5a. COA makes a greater contribution to IT alignment when degree 
of outsourcing is high compared with when it is low. 
H5b. FOA makes a greater contribution to IT alignment when degree 
of outsourcing is high compared with when it is low. 
2.2.6. Moderating effect of the adoption of cloud computing 
Cloud computing, as an IT service mode, provides technology ser-
vices such as software, platform, and infrastructure for users through the 
network (Arvanitis et al., 2017; Gao and Sunyaev, 2019). Given that 
different types of cloud computing provide some unique characteristics 
for enterprises, this study attempts to explore the moderating effect of 
the adoption of cloud computing on the relationship between 
outsourcing appropriation and IT alignment from the SaaS, PaaS, and 
IaaS perspectives. 
SaaS is a service mode that provides software services for firms 
through the network in on-demand payments, configurability, and ser-
vice rental manner (Safari et al., 2015). Although COA enables firms to 
form an adaptive IT outsourcing solutions for business strategies, the IT 
solutions jointly determined by the IT and business departments may not 
necessarily conform to the core competence of suppliers (Benlian, 
2009), and may lead to suppliers' speculative behaviors in the process of 
solution implementation that are not conducive to IT alignment. When 
firms adopt the SaaS mode, the IT solutions produced by COA can be 
designed and met with the support of ubiquitous access and faster 
implementation times, transparent and predictable subscription pricing, 
and configurable application packages (Benlian, 2009; Liu et al., 2018), 
thereby contributing to avoiding some speculative behaviors (e.g., 
transferring businesses to a third party or excessive pricing) and 
attaining a higher degree of IT alignment. 
PaaS is a service mode that provides a software development and 
deployment platform, including application design, application devel -
opment, application testing, and application hosting, for users (Cohen 
et al., 2013). COA can strengthen the interaction between the IT and 
business departments and form an IT outsourcing solution that reflects 
business demands needed to be supported by IT. However, the imple -
mentation of the IT solution depends on external suppliers and may 
experience some acknowledged risks, e.g., reliability, data privacy se-
curity, and process dependence (Benlian, 2009). PaaS provides firms 
with the support of the application code base and the tools in a shared 
manner, and allows them to develop, test, and deploy applications and T. Wang et al.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   
Technological Forecasting & Social Change 192 (2023) 122581
6services in their own ways (Walraven et al., 2014). Under such cir-
cumstances, the IT solutions jointly determined by the IT and business 
departments enable firms to effectively avoid these unpredicted risks 
and be met in an expected way, contributing to a higher degree of IT 
alignment. In addition, PaaS is also characterized by the dynamic 
extension of the development environment (Walraven et al., 2014). The 
IT outsourcing solutions formed by COA often cover diversified tech-
nical demands and may exceed the upper limits of enterprise IT resource 
occupancy. Under such circumstances, the role of COA in achieving IT 
alignment is relatively limited. After the adoption of PaaS, the positive 
impact of COA on IT alignment may be further strengthened, because 
PaaS suppliers can monitor the occupancy of service resources, auto-
matically conduct extension based on certain rules, and quickly create 
the development environment required for tests, demonstration, and up- 
limit extension in the case of exceeding limit. 
IaaS is a service mode in which cloud suppliers provide IT in-
frastructures, e.g., servers, storage, and computing capabilities, to users 
via the Internet (Liu et al., 2018; Walraven et al., 2014). The IT solutions 
formed by the COA depend heavily on the support of the IT infrastruc -
ture. With the rapid change of business demands, the IT solutions jointly 
agreed by the IT and business units need the support of a flexible IT 
infrastructure. In practice, IaaS suppliers provide some IT in-
frastructures for firms in a service rental manner (Liu et al., 2018). The 
service rental model enables firms to acquire hardware resources on 
demand and achieve a higher degree of resource flexibility (Armbrust 
et al., 2010), such that firms can reduce the possibility of over-purchase 
(i.e., waste of resources) and under-purchase (i.e., loss of potential 
revenues) (Alali and Yeh, 2012). In addition, before the adoption of IaaS, 
due to the relatively stereotyped or standardized service resources, ca-
pabilities, and rules, an IT department for IT alignment may need to 
frequently interact and coordinate with business units regarding IT 
outsourcing appropriation, and adjust outsourcing tactics to respond to 
the changeable business demands (Liu et al., 2018). Therefore, the IT 
alignment driven by COA is based on the higher adjustment or coordi -
nation costs between the IT and business units. After the adoption of 
IaaS, cloud suppliers deploy these services on their own servers and 
implement central management through the Internet (Mitra et al., 2018; 
Nieuwenhuis et al., 2018). When IaaS is adopted, firms are able to 
quickly obtain hardware resources on-demand through the network and 
automatically configure resources on suppliers' servers to meet the dy-
namic needs of outsourcing (Mitra et al., 2018). These technical char-
acteristics reduce the adjustment costs in achieving IT alignment 
through COA. Therefore, IaaS may impose a significant impact on the 
relationship between COA and IT alignment by increasing the resource 
flexibility and reducing the costs. Therefore, we posit that: 
H6a. COA makes a greater contribution to IT alignment when SaaS 
model is adopted compared with when it is not adopted. 
H7a. COA makes a greater contribution to IT alignment when PaaS 
model is adopted compared with when it is not adopted. 
H8a. COA makes a greater contribution to IT alignment when IaaS 
model is adopted compared with when it is not adopted. 
Furthermore, based on the above analysis of the characteristics of 
SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS respectively, we also expect that the faithfulness of 
the appropriated cloud services to these technical features would ach-
ieve IT alignment more conveniently and at lower cost. SaaS is designed 
with the general characteristics of on-demand payments, config -
urability, and service rental (Safari et al., 2015), such that firms can 
conveniently and economically achieve IT alignment. PaaS is designed 
with the general characteristics of reconfigurability and dynamic 
extension (Cohen et al., 2013; Walraven et al., 2014), such that firms can 
attain IT alignment free of path dependence and space up-limit. IaaS is 
designed with the general characteristics of service rental and flexibility 
(Liu et al., 2018; Walraven et al., 2014), such that firms can dynamically 
achieve IT alignment in a flexible and low-cost manner. To realize these characteristic advantages, cloud suppliers often need to impose certain 
structures and procedures that their clients can adopt. Hence, firms that 
faithfully adopt such structures and procedures would greatly enhance 
the alignment between the appropriated services and business proced -
ures in a flexible, low-cost, convenient, and fast implementation 
manner, thereby contributing to a higher degree of IT alignment. 
Accordingly, we hypothesize: 
H6b. FOA makes a greater contribution to IT alignment when SaaS 
model is adopted compared with when it is not adopted. 
H7b. FOA makes a greater contribution to IT alignment when PaaS 
model is adopted compared with when it is not adopted. 
H8b. FOA makes a greater contribution to IT alignment when IaaS 
model is adopted compared with when it is not adopted. 
3.Research method 
3.1. Data collection 
This study adopted a questionnaire-based survey to collect data for 
testing our proposed research model. We chose the questionnaire 
method for the following reasons. First, the scales of constructs adopted 
by our model have been developed by prior research, and their psy-
chometric properties have also been well confirmed. Second, a ques-
tionnaire survey enabled us to obtain enough samples from large 
quantities of respondents, which contributed to a more robust and 
generalizable conclusion. Third, considering the nature of question -
naire, e.g., anonymous and voluntary, social desirability bias can be 
effectively mitigated. 
To ensure its validity and reliability, our questionnaire was devel -
oped using a four-step process. First, the questionnaire items were 
adapted from existing literature in which psychometric properties have 
been established. Second, we conducted backward translation to ensure 
the agreement regarding the meaning of the items between the Chinese 
version and the English version of the questionnaire. We also invited 
four experts on IT outsourcing to conduct the pre-test for content val-
idity. Further, we also organized semi-structured interviews with one 
professor and three doctoral students as interviewers and five IT man-
agers as interviewees, aiming to help us check the words, grammar, and 
clarity in these items. Third, given that our discussion on COA and FOA 
focused on IT outsourcing appropriation situation, rather than tradi-
tional IT or IS appropriation situation in extant studies, there may exist 
the issues of content overload and ambiguity in our scales. For this 
reason, we adopted item-sorting, and consequently found no significant 
misclassification. Fourth, we implemented a pilot test with 32 IT man-
agers or above who have ever been in charge of or participated in IT 
outsourcing business, and removed some low item-to-total correlation 
items. By evaluation and revision, the validity and reliability of our 
questionnaire have been effectively improved. Finally, we further 
examined the validity and reliability of our scales using some analytical 
tools. The specific testing results are shown in Data analysis and results 
section. 
Our targeted respondents are mostly from IT departments in some 
manufacturing and service firms. Based on the principle of theoretical 
sampling, IT department managers or other unit executives that took 
charge of or participated in outsourcing businesses were more suitable 
as our targeted respondents, because they were the key participants and 
boundary spanners for IT alignment through outsourcing appropriation. 
For this reason, we set a screening item at the beginning of our ques-
tionnaire, i.e., “If your position belongs to the IT department team 
leader, IT department manager, IT department management, or other 
unit executives that take charge of or participated in outsourcing busi-
nesses, please continue to answer the following questions, otherwise the 
questionnaire ends”. 
The questionnaire was mainly sent through two channels. First, we T. 3.2. Measures 
All measurement items, including shared IT affordance for knowl -
edge acquisition, boundary spanning, boundary buffering, COA, FOA, 
and IT alignment, have been developed by existing literature in which 
psychometric properties were well confirmed. We further adapted 
certain items aiming to promote the alignment of these items and our 
research focus. In addition, we adopted a 7-point response format to 
measure all items. 
The shared IT affordance for knowledge acquisition scale was 
derived from Abhari et al. (2017) and Leonardi (2013) . It consisted of six 
items covering the knowledge acquisition potential afforded by IT tools 
used by IT innovation collaborators in two aspects, i.e., knowledge 
searching and knowledge integration. An example for knowledge 
searching was “When our innovation collaborators (such as business 
departments, vendors, and customers) and our IT department seek IT 
innovation activities, the IT used enables us to: easily access data be-
tween ours and innovation collaborators' systems. ” An example for knowledge integration was “When our innovation collaborators (such as 
business departments, vendors, and customers) and our IT department 
seek IT innovation activities, the IT used enables us to: easily aggregate 
relevant information. ” The coefficient alpha of this scale was 0.866. 
The boundary spanning and boundary buffering scales were 
measured using four items adopted by Faraj and Yan (2009) , and were 
mainly concerned with the boundary spanning and boundary buffering 
behavior of IT departments with the IT department as the boundary. An 
example for boundary spanning was “To what extent does the IT 
department encourage its staff to solicit information and resources from 
elsewhere in and/or beyond the department ”. An example for boundary 
buffering was “To what extent are outside pressures deflected or 
absorbed so that the IT department can work free of interference ”. The 
coefficient alpha of the two scales was 0.760 and 0.732 respectively. 
The COA scale, which consisted of five items measuring the agree -
ment level between IT department and business unit towards appro -
priation of IT outsourcing, was derived from Deng et al. (2021) . The FOA 
scale, which involved six items measuring the structural faithfulness in 
two aspects: structural features (e.g., IT resources and capabilities, 
technical functions, or service rules) and spirit (e.g., service philosophy, 
value, and objectives), was derived from Deng et al. (2021) . An example 
for COA was “Between our company's IT department and business unit: 
there is mutual understanding in our company on how we should adopt 
IT outsourcing strategy ”. An example for FOA was “As for the supplier 
and the IT outsourcing services we choose from them: our IT outsourcing 
strategy objectives are matched with the vendor's beliefs or strategic 
objectives ”. The coefficient alpha of the two scales was 0.881 and 0.891 
respectively.

4.Results analysis 
4.1. Validity and reliability 
To assess the reliability and validity of our questionnaires, this study 
used Lisrel 8.8 as a statistical analysis tool to conduct confirmatory 
factor analysis. The expected six-factor model including the shared IT 
affordance for knowledge acquisition, boundary spanning, boundary 
buffering, COA, FOA, and IT alignment displayed an acceptable fit 
(RMSEA 0.067, χ2 638.27, df 362, χ2/df 1.76, CFI 0.97, 
NNFI 0.97, IFI 0.97). The χ2/df value was D2. The CFI, NNFI, and 
IFI values were F0.90 respectively, while the RMSEA value was 0.067, 
lower than the threshold value of 0.08. Therefore, construct validity of 
our model was basically acceptable. 
We conducted construct reliability tests in two ways. First, we used 
the indicator of Cronbach's alpha (α) to assess internal consistency 
reliability. Second, we also employed the indicator of composite reli-
ability (CR) to evaluate internal consistency reliability. The results of the two indicators for each construct are listed in Table 2. Results showed 
that all values of α and CR were F0.73, indicating adequate reliability. 
To verify construct convergent validity, we regarded the value of 
item factor loading as a judgement criterion. The results of factor 
loading values are also shown in Table 2. Most loading values were 
above 0.70, and only few items had relatively lower values ranging from 
0.59 to 0.68 (but over the cutoff point of 0.50). Therefore, convergent 
validity of all constructs was acceptable. 
Further, we conducted discriminant validity tests as follows. First, 
we calculated the values of average variance extracted (AVE) for all 
constructs to evaluate discriminant validity. The results of descriptive 
statistics for each construct, including mean, standard deviation, cor-
relations, and AVE, are presented in Table 3. Results showed that the 
square roots of AVE for all constructs were higher than the correlations 
with other constructs, showing acceptable discriminant validity. Sec-
ond, we examined discriminant validity by using correlation indicators. 
As shown in Table 3, all correlations between the construct and others 
were lower than 0.7, thus indicating good discriminant validity. Third, 
we employed chi-square difference tests to evaluate discriminant val-
idity. Specifically, we compared one CFA model freeing correlations 
between the two constructs to another CFA model fixing a perfect cor-
relation. According to six constructs adopted by our model, we con-
ducted 15 round comparisons between the two constructs. Through 
computing, we adopted the Bonferroni correction p-value of 0.033333 
because we conducted 15 rounds of repeated test (Kroes and Ghosh, 
2010 ). Results showed that each round chi-square difference compari -
son was significant, therefore showing acceptable discriminant validity. 
4.2. Common method bias 
Our study experienced a threat of common method bias, because we 
adopted self-reported data collected by subjective instruments. For this 
reason, to check for common method bias, we subsequently employed 
four techniques that have been widely used in prior research. First, as 
suggested by Podsakoff et al. (2003) , we adopted some ex-ante proce -
dural remedy measures, e.g., disrupting the item order, ensuring ano-
nymity, and setting screening question. Second, we conducted Harman's 
single factor test, aiming to assess whether common method bias existed 
or not. The results indicated that the variance explained by the first 
factor accounted for 38.3 % of total variances, which was far D50 %. As 
such, the single factor that may explain the majority of total variances 
does not exist. Third, we use the indicators of variance inflation factor 
(VIF) to check for the collinearity issue. The VIF values were all lower 
than the threshold value of 3.0, thereby showing that collinearity was 
not a serious problem. Fourth, we constructed a single-factor analysis 
model, including shared IT affordance for knowledge acquisition, 
boundary spanning, boundary buffering, COA, FOA, and IT alignment, 
and conducted a confirmatory factor analysis. The constructed single- 
factor model displayed an unsatisfactory fit (RMSEA 0.141, CFI 
0.86, NNFI 0.85, IFI 0.86). Conversely, the expected six factor 
model attained a significant improvement consistent with our data. In 
sum, the threat of common method bias in this study was not a serious 
problem.

5.Discussion 
5.1. Theoretical contributions 
IT outsourcing, as an important external source of firms' IT resources 
and capabilities, has long been regarded as a promising way to attain IT 
alignment. However, firms sometimes hesitate to seek IT outsourcing solutions to attain IT alignment because of outsourcing risks arising from 
improper outsourcing appropriation (Deng et al., 2021 ; Lioliou et al., 
2019 ). Therefore, researchers turn to the topic on the criteria for suitable 
outsourcing appropriation. This study empirically extends extant the-
ories on IT outsourcing and AST by investigating the antecedents of 
outsourcing appropriation (referring to COA and FOA in this study), 
their impacts on IT alignment, and boundary conditions of the impacts 
of outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment. 
Our study contributes to the IT outsourcing and AST literature in the 
following several ways. First, different from prior literature that 
attached great importance to the outcomes of outsourcing appropriation 
(Deng et al., 2021 ), based on AST, our study revealed the antecedents of 
outsourcing appropriation from the technology and team characteristic 
perspectives. From the technology perspective, our results suggest that 
shared IT affordance for knowledge acquisition promotes both COA and 
FOA. Corresponding with previous literature that focused on the posi-
tive roles of IT tools in decision-making processes (Sun et al., 2019 ), we 
find that the knowledge acquisition potential afforded by the IT con-
tributes to achieving the attitudinal consensus and structural faithful -
ness towards outsourcing appropriation decisions. From the team 
characteristic perspective, our results also suggest that boundary span-
ning has a positive effect on both COA and FOA. Furthermore, boundary 
buffering has a positive effect on FOA, but no significant effect on COA. 
One possible explanation for the non-significant result is that boundary 
buffering may impose both positive and negative impacts on COA. On 
the one hand, boundary buffering may indeed cause conflicts and

boundary behaviors, including boundary spanning and buffering, 
differentially change the COA and FOA. 
Second, compared with the contradictory arguments on the linkage 
between IT outsourcing and alignment in prior literature (Silvius et al., 
2013), this study provided in-depth insights for addressing the “IT 
outsourcing-alignment” paradox from the COA and FOA perspectives. 
Our empirical results indicate that COA and FOA contribute to pro-
moting IT alignment respectively. These findings show the importance 
of the attitudinal consensus and structural faithfulness towards IT 
outsourcing appropriation on achieving IT alignment, thus mitigating 
the concern for outsourcing risks arising from improper outsourcing 
appropriation in existing literature. In addition, although extant studies 
have highlighted the key roles of COA and FOA in achieving outsourcing 
objectives (e.g., IT agility) (Deng et al., 2021), few has related the atti-
tudinal consensus and structural faithfulness towards IT outsourcing to 
IT alignment. This study empirically reveals that the IT alignment can be 
equally viewed as a key outcome of COA and FOA. 
Third, this study further reveals the boundary conditions of the ef-
fects of outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment. Scant research has 
attempted to investigate the impacts of COA and FOA on IT alignment, 
nor has focused on the boundary conditions of these relationships. This 
study seeks to fill these gaps by investigating the moderating roles of 
degree of outsourcing and cloud computing adoption. Our results find 
that the moderating effects of the degree of outsourcing on the effect of 
outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment are not significant. This 
finding further supports the previous point that the effects of 
outsourcing appropriation on IT alignment are quite significant and 
close. In other words, regardless of the high or low degree of 
outsourcing, based on the consideration of risk aversion, firm executives 
in both cases attach great importance to the attitudinal consensus and 
structural faithfulness towards IT outsourcing to avoid the outsourcing 
risks arising from unreasonable outsourcing appropriation, and further 
attain IT alignment by leveraging the key roles of COA and FOA. 
Further, differing from prior research on the adoption of cloud 
computing from a holistic view (Liu et al., 2018), our study also 
empirically investigates the moderating effects of the different di-
mensions of cloud computing adoption on the effects of outsourcing 
appropriation on IT alignment. Our results indicate that when SaaS or 
PaaS is adopted, the impact of the COA on IT alignment is lower than 
that when they are not adopted. One possible explanation for this is that 
SaaS and PaaS models are more standardized (Fardinpour et al., 2020; 
Singh et al., 2016), and it is therefore harder for them to achieve on- 
demand personalization. Thus, although COA is reached, the failure of 
SaaS or PaaS in the configuration based on the individual needs of cli-
ents might lead to the relatively limited improvement of IT alignment. 
However, if SaaS or PaaS is not adopted and more traditional 
outsourcing or IS development by ownself are considered instead, it 
might be easier for the outsourcing supplier or internal IT department to 
meet individual needs, and it may be easier for the COA to achieve IT 
alignment. Comparably, we also find a non-significant impact on IT 
alignment from the interaction term between the COA and IaaS. In sum, 
these results suggest that when cloud computing is adopted at a higher 
application level rather than at an infrastructure level, the positive role 
of the COA in alignment may be weakened. Our results also suggest that 
when PaaS or IaaS is adopted, the positive impact of the FOA on IT 
alignment is higher than that when they are not adopted, but the 
moderating role of SaaS is not significant. The non-significance associ -
ated with SaaS suggests that the role of the FOA in IT alignment does not 
vary significantly with the adoption of SaaS. On the one hand, the adoption of SaaS may indeed strengthen the positive effect of the FOA on 
IT alignment owing to its quick and low-cost configurability and 
extendable technical advantages (Khayer et al., 2020). On the other 
hand, due to its relative standardization and difficulties in adapting to 
personalized business needs (Fardinpour et al., 2020), the adoption of 
SaaS may also weaken the positive role of the FOA on IT alignment. It 
may therefore be the combination of positive and negative effects that 
leads to the non-significance of the moderating effects of SaaS. In sum, 
the overall results indicate that when cloud computing is adopted at the 
high infrastructure or platform levels rather than at the application 
level, the FOA is more conducive to achieving IT alignment. Therefore, 
our study provides in-depth insights regarding how different dimensions 
of cloud computing adoption impose a significant impact on this 
relationship. 
5.2. Managerial implications 
Our study offers some valuable practical guidance for department 
managers of outsourcing users at various levels, e.g., IT department 
managers and business unit managers, and outsourcing suppliers. First, 
our results enable organizations to deepen the understanding of what 
factors promote the attitudinal consensus and structural faithfulness 
towards IT outsourcing appropriation. Specifically, our results highlight 
the importance of the knowledge acquisition potential afforded by IT 
tools on the consensus and faithful appropriation towards IT 
outsourcing. Therefore, firm executives should attach great importance 
to the roles of IT support in outsourcing appropriation decisions, and 
especially, regard some IT functional modules aiming to improve orga-
nizations' knowledge acquisition capabilities as a key criterion of IT 
investments. Our results also show that the boundary behaviors for 
teams play crucial roles in outsourcing appropriation. On one hand, the 
IT department should conduct some boundary spanning behaviors 
together with other departments and suppliers, such as forging rela-
tionship, knowledge sharing, and lobbying others to obtain support, to 
strengthen the mutual understanding of how outsourcing services are 
appropriated. On the other hand, the IT department should be sensitive 
to the risks with boundary work to protect itself against disturbances 
from suppliers' lobbying in the outsourcing appropriation process. 
Second, our results emphasize the crucial role of outsourcing 
appropriation, and the key point for achieving IT alignment via IT 
outsourcing is to promote COA and FOA towards IT outsourcing. In 
terms of COA, it means the joint participation of both IT departments 
and business units towards IT outsourcing appropriation, and the two 
parties reach a consensus on this issue. In other words, COA requires the 
mutual cooperation between IT managers and business executives to-
wards outsourcing planning and business planning, which is important 
to improve the business impacts of IT. For example, regular meetings 
with the IT managers and business managers would strengthen an in- 
depth understanding regarding the linkage between outsourcing objec -
tives and business goals needed to be supported by IT, and more easily 
reach an agreement regarding how firms achieve IT alignment through 
IT outsourcing. In terms of FOA, our empirical results indicated that the 
structural faithfulness towards IT outsourcing plays a key role in IT 
alignment. Therefore, to attain IT alignment by using the core technical 
competencies and advantage resources of suppliers, firms are supposed 
to appropriate IT outsourcing in a style alignment with the general ob-
jectives and procedures intended to be provided by the supplier. 
Third, our results also reveal that the moderating effects of SaaS, 
PaaS, and IaaS on the linkage between outsourcing appropriation and 
alignment vary significantly. Therefore, to achieve IT alignment, the 
levels of the COA and FOA should vary depending on the adoption of 
different cloud computing models. Specifically, our results suggest that 
when PaaS or IaaS are adopted, the FOA may have a more important 
impact on attaining IT alignment compared with when they are not 
adopted. Therefore, when PaaS or IaaS are adopted, it is more critical for 
outsourcing users to understand the technical characteristics and service T. Wang et al.

great extent. In contrast, results also indicate that when SaaS or PaaS are 
adopted, the role of the COA in IT alignment seems to be weakened as 
compared to when they are not adopted. Thus, when cloud computing is 
adopted at the application level in terms of SaaS or PaaS, the COA should 
not be heavily relied upon to promote IT alignment. 
5.3. Limitations and future research 
Our study has the following limitations that might constrain its 
generalizability, and provides several topics for future research. First, 
given that IT alignment is a time-consuming and dynamic process, 
therefore using a cross-sectional data as our samples to investigate the 
formation of IT alignment may limit the generalizability of our results. 
Therefore, future research might adopt a case study or longitudinal 
design that can reflect the time effect, aiming to reveal the dynamic 
process of IT alignment through outsourcing appropriation. Second, the 
alternative antecedents of outsourcing appropriation advocated by AST 
have not been fully considered in this study, e.g., task characteristics. 
Meanwhile, we merely focus on the shared IT affordance for knowledge 
acquisition from the technical perspective. Therefore, future researchers 
can extend our findings by focusing on other types of antecedents of 
outsourcing appropriation, e.g., task characteristics or other technology 
factors (such as sophistication and comprehensiveness). Third, our study 
employs a unilateral view from outsourcing users to collect data aiming 
to solve how organizations appropriate IT outsourcing for IT alignment. 
However, outsourcing suppliers, acted as the direct provider of services, 
can be seen as another key participant in outsourcing appropriation. 
Therefore, future research can adopt a bilateral perspective from 
outsourcing users or suppliers to extend our findings. Fourth, future 
research could conduct a larger sample survey to further validate our 
findings. In addition, future research in other contexts, e.g., other 
countries, could also supplement the findings here. 
6.Conclusions 
Outsourcing appropriation, covering the two important aspects of 
COA and FOA, provides a promising lens to answer how to appropriate 
IT outsourcing for IT alignment. Our study investigates the antecedents 
of outsourcing appropriation, their impacts on IT alignment, and 
boundary conditions of the impacts of outsourcing appropriation on IT 
alignment. Our results demonstrate the importance of shared IT 
affordance for knowledge acquisition, boundary spanning, and boundary buffering on outsourcing appropriation apart from the non- 
significance results of the relationship between boundary buffering 
and COA. In addition, our findings indicate that COA and FOA 
contribute to promoting IT alignment respectively, and further advance 
the literature on the antecedents of suitable outsourcing appropriation. 
Further, to reveal the boundary conditions of the impacts of outsourcing 
appropriation on IT alignment, we also consider two environmental 
factors (degree of outsourcing and cloud computing adoption) as mod-
erators. Our results indicate that the adoption of cloud computing sets 
the boundary conditions of the linkage between outsourcing appropri -
ation and IT alignment, whereas the degree of outsourcing does not. Our 
results demonstrate that the moderating effects of different dimensions 
of cloud computing adoption (including SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS) on the 
linkage between outsourcing appropriation and IT alignment vary 
significantly. Notably, the relatively importance of COA and FOA on IT 
alignment may vary based on different cloud computing adoption mode.
