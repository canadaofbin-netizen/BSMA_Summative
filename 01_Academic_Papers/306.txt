The roles of R&D team leaders in
Korea: a contingent approach
Youngbae Kim, Byungwook Min and Jongseok Cha
Graduate School of Management, Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology(KAIST), 207–43,
Chungryangri-dong, Dongdaemun-gu, Seoul. 130–012, Korea.
This study empirically examines the relationship between the role of R&D project leaders and their team
performance using data from 87 project teams in 6 R&D organizations in Korea. The results reveal that:
(1) R&D project leaders played five different roles in performing their jobs – strategic planner, team
builder, gatekeeper, technical expert, and champion;
(2) All but the champion role of a leader is positively related with project team performance;(3) However, this relationship between the role of leader and project team performance varies according
to the characteristics of R&D project teams and their tasks.
Specifically, it becomes important for a leader to focus less on the team building role as the team gets
older. The team building role of a leader, however, is more important for higher performance of relatively
certain R&D projects, while for uncertain R&D projects, the strategic planning role appears to be more
crucial. Based on these results, this study discusses several managerial and theoretical issues related tothe role of a leader in R&D project teams.
1. Introduction
Technological innovation is becoming a buzzword
for managers and policy-makers in both advanced
and developing countries. Indeed, many scholars andmanagers agree that R&D activities that fostertechnological innovation play a dominant role inacquiring and sustaining the competitive advantageof firms in an increasingly competitive global market.Recognizing the importance of effective managementof R&D activities, many studies have explored diverse
aspects of successful R&D projects such as project
planning and evaluation, technical leadership, com-munication behaviour of R&D team members, andorganizational structure and climate conducive toinnovation (Allen, 1977; Farris, 1988; Katz and Tush-man, 1981; Liberatore and Titus, 1983; O’Reilly, 1989;Pelz and Andews, 1966; Tushman and Nadler, 1986).
Unfortunately, however, most of these studies have
been undertaken in advanced countries, and few
researchers focused on innovation activities in devel-oping countries. Many firms in Newly IndustrializingEconomies (NIEs), including Korea, are now facing a
dilemma between technologically competitive forerun-ners from advanced countries and cost competitivefollowers from less developed countries, such as Chinaand other south-east Asian countries. In response totheir aspiration for a higher standard of living, the only
step they can take is to catch up with advanced
countries via accumulation of technological capabilityand development of competitive but high value-addedproducts and services.
Given the limited resources for indigenous technol-
ogy development activities, the effective managementof R&D appears to be one of the most crucial strategicissues in these companies. Nevertheless, due to the
differences in social culture, management practices,
and the level of technological capability, it is notalways relevant nor effective for these firms to imitateor follow R&D management systems developed inadvanced countries. What is needed for them is todevelop proper R&D management systems and prac-tices, which fit local situations of NIEs. This can beaccomplished by combining a careful observation of

2. Literature review
Undoubtedly, R&D performance depends substan-tially on technical leadership (Pelz and Andrews, 1966).Despite the greater number of studies on leadership ingeneral, however, leadership in technological innova-tion has received relatively little systematic attentionfrom academic scholars (Keller, 1992). Farris (1988)argues that technical leadership has been much
discussed thus far, but it is not yet well understood.
More systematic, replicable, and comparative studiesof technical leadership are needed to determine whichinsights are specific to a particular situation and whichare generalizable to many instances of technicalleadership (Farris, 1988).
Theories of leadership outside R&D, though helpful
in some ways in understanding technical leadership,
operate in a different manner. For instance, conven-
tional leadership styles have been found not to be ableto fully explain the difference in research outcomes ofR&D settings in existing studies (Andrews and Farris,1967; Barnowe, 1975). Barnowe (1975) concluded that‘clearly, the better part of variation in researchoutcomes was attributable to factors other thanleadership’ (p. 273). The limited applicability of con-
ventional leadership behaviour stems from the peculiar
nature of both innovation task and R&D professional,which technical leaders must supervise in order toaccomplish their missions.
Innovation tasks, contrary to other operating tasks,
are intrinsically diverse, non-routine, and uncertainabout the relationship between inputs and outputs,take a very long time for fruition, and usually involve a
high risk of failure. Innovation processes are also
plagued by many unforeseen disruptions, delays andsetbacks (Quinn, 1979). To successfully manage aninnovation project, the technical leader, unlike a leader
in charge of a routine job, must have a clear vision tocope with uncertain goals, creativity and persistencethroughout the innovation process, an ability tocooperate and integrate team members with diversebackgrounds and, sometimes, even exploit serendipityor good luck. Successful innovations also require a lotof information and resources from outside of the R&D
team, which must be addressed by the technical leader.
However, conventional leadership behaviour has beenusually confined to internal activities such as taskstructuring activities and human relationships withteam members for task accomplishment and groupmaintenance (Ancona and Caldwell, 1988).
The R&D leader also deals with technical profes-
sionals who have many distinctive characteristics in
terms of their goal orientation, value system, need
structure, and their behavioural patterns (Badawy,1988). They tend not to accept hierarchical control,unless they agree or understand, and frequently showless strong commitment to their organization than totheir profession. They also tend to be chauvinisticabout their technical specialty (Gomez-Mejia et al.,
1990; Miller, 1986; Von Glinow, 1988). Moreover,
since autonomy is important in performing their work,
the individual leader may have problems managingthese professionals.
McCall (1988) suggested that effective technical
leaders tend to affect technical performance ofprofessional groups in two ways. They can take anactive role in stimulating, evaluating and selectingvarious ideas and in setting work goals and objectives
based on their technical competence. On the other
hand, they also need to indirectly affect theirprofessionals by nurturing and maintaining the workclimate of creative tension, which seems to spawnhigh levels of professional productivity (Pelz andAndrews, 1966). For this, they must walk a tightropebetween overcontrol and too much autonomy, makesubtle changes in priorities and sequences, and ensure
as well as channel professional challenges in the work
(McCall, 1988).
Existing leadership research, however, does not
provide a valuable insight into the functions and rolesof R&D leaders. Recently, recognizing the limitationsof earlier leadership studies, different approaches totechnical leadership have been attempted. Farris (1988)classified existing studies of leadership in R&D settings
into three categories: those which studied leadership
directly, those which studied the characteristics oforganizational climate which could be attributable tothe leader, and those which studied the informal rolesof R&D leaders who perform critical functions forproject success.
The third approach, especially, has drawn much
attention in recent studies, because it focuses on
specific activities required for the leader in the
innovation process and thus can offer substantial

product development. From the table, five common
roles could be delineated as representative roles aleader must fulfil to successfully complete the project.Three roles are concerned with the internal activities ofthe R&D team – technical expert, strategic planner,and team builder, while the other two roles are relatedto external activities – champion and informationgatekeeper.
Technical expert. The role of technical expert en-
compasses many functions, including generating andrecognizing good ideas, finding and defining thesignificant problems, providing technical stimulation,and fusing various ideas and solutions into anintegrative framework or model. A consistent empiri-cal result of R&D leadership shows that the technical
expertise of R&D leaders have a direct and positive
impact on technical performance (Farris, 1988). This ispartly because professionals accept authority based onexpertise rather than hierarchy, and partly becausemany critical functions in performing productive R&Dactivities depend on the technical expertise of theleader (McCall, 1988). Existing studies address thetechnical expert role of the leader by the name of
‘technical expert’ (Farris, 1988), ‘expert’ (Chakrabarti
and Hauschildt, 1989), ‘idea generating’ (Roberts andFusfeld, 1981), ‘technical innovator’ (Howell andHiggins, 1990), and ‘engineering manager’ (Clark andWheelright, 1992).
Strategic planner. The conventional role of a project
leader is to set the goals of the project, plan the time
schedule and resource allocation, assign jobs to
individual members, and control and evaluate thewhole project process. However, R&D or innovationproject leaders face difficulties in setting strategic goalsand planning the means to achieve these goals, becauseinnovation projects, by definition, attempt to conquerthe unknown. Therefore the strategic planning role of aleader, which provides a strategic focus to the project
and flexible planning to cope with many unforeseen
contingencies during the innovation process (Barczakand Wilemon, 1989), appears to be critical for thesuccess of innovation efforts. Many prior studies haverevealed the importance of the strategic planner role ofa leader by using various terms such as ‘strategicleader’ (Farris, 1988), ‘strategic planning’ (Fieldmanet al., 1992), ‘pathfinding’ (Beatty and Lee, 1992),
‘planner role’ (Barczak and Wilemon, 1989), ‘project
leading’ (Roberts and Fusfeld, 1981), and ‘programmanager in motion’ (Clark and Wheelright, 1992).
Team builder. The R&D leader by exerting technical
leadership can positively affect team climate, which isconducive to individual creativity. Team cohesivenessor team spirit has been a necessary condition for
building high performance project teams (Barczak and
Wilemon, 1989). Indeed, team building is considered tobe one of the most critical leadership qualities that
determine the performance of interdisciplinary orcross-functional efforts. To provide an atmosphereconducive to teamwork or transcendental effort (Non-aka, 1989), the project leader needs to develop teammembership, resolve conflicts among members in aconstructive way, and foster an environment wherethey have mutual trust and are willing to share
different ideas, information, experiences and
perspectives.
To instill a positive attitude among team members
toward the project and to empower them, R&D leaderscan resort to transformational leadership (Keller,1992). They must articulate their vision and share itwith team members to draw their commitment toaccomplish it. They also stimulate the professional
involvement of team members by emphasizing new
challenges and innovative ways of doing things andencouraging risk-taking behaviour among members.They provide individual support by addressing thepersonal needs for growth and development of eachteam member (Bass, 1985). This role of a leader hasbeen stressed in many studies by means of ‘climatecreator’ (Farris, 1988), ‘climate setter’ (Barczak and
Wilemon, 1989), and ‘exciter’ (Souder, 1987).
Champion. The task of innovation cannot be accom-
plished by only the aforementioned internal activitiesof the leader, because it must involve many interac-tions with external agencies for the supply of newinformation and other required resources. The role of achampion, who actively promotes innovation, builds
support, gets required resources, overcomes resistance
from organizational members, and ensures the im-plementation of the innovation (Howell and Higgins,1990), has long been emphasized as a critical successfactor of technical innovation (Chakrabarti andHauschildt, 1989; Howell and Higgins, 1990; Robertsand Fusfeld, 1981; Schon, 1963).
Without the strong personal commitment and
shrewd political tactics of project champions, technical
innovations, especially radical innovations whichrequire at least a sort of ‘creative destruction’ process,cannot be automatically accepted, nurtured, andimplemented in the existing organization (Howell andHiggins, 1990; Katz, 1993; Kidder, 1981). There arealways many different kinds of organizational resis-tance and reluctance to change or innovation, and the
key ‘antagonists’ must be persuaded, convinced, or,
sometimes even removed by the champion to overcomethe barriers to get resources and support fromrelated organizational units as well as from topmanagement (Markham et al ., 1991). Ancona and
Caldwell (1988) coined this role of technical leader‘ambassador’, while many other studies called it‘champion’ or ‘entrepreneur’ (Chakrabarti, 1974;
Chakrabarti and Hauschildt, 1989; Roberts and
Fusfeld, 1981; Souder, 1987).

Gatekeeper. The other frequently mentioned crucial
function in the innovation process is technical gate-keeping (Allen, 1977; Katz and Tushman, 1981).Innovation process can be conceived as an informationprocessing or creating process (Nonaka, 1989; Tush-man and Nadler, 1986), and requires a great deal ofmarket, organizational, and technical information forcreative transformation. Many bits of information,
however, need a mediating role for an effective
communication: an identification of valuable sources,screening relevant information, and interpreting theimplications of information. Gatekeepers, who occupythe key position in organizational communicationnetworks, keep scanning and collecting informationfrom external sources and disseminating the informa-tion with interpretation to the other internal organiza-
tional members (Allen, 1977).
The R&D project leaders have been found to play an
important role in both internal and external commu-nication (Allen et al., 1988; Barczak and Wilemon,
1991; Katz and Tushman, 1981; Tushman andScanlan, 1981). Many effective project leaders areidentified as linking their teams with outside informa-tion sources, actively monitoring and receiving im-
portant information, relaying this information to
members, and also facilitating communication amongmembers. This can be accomplished through formalchannels such as hierarchical reporting relationships,as well as informal linkages based on personalnetworks. Previous studies also acknowledge the gate-keeper role of the leader with ‘communicator’ (Barczakand Wilemon, 1989), ‘direct market interpreter’ or
‘multilingual translator’ (Clark and Wheelright, 1992),
‘scout’ or ‘guard’ (Ancona and Caldwell, 1988).
Hypothesis 1: The five roles of an R &D leader
mentioned above are positively associated with teamperformance.
Undoubtedly, some of these roles are more impor-
tant in certain situations. Contingency theories of
leadership have found that the effectiveness of theleadership is dependent on the situations the leaderconfronts, such as the task characteristics, nature ofwork groups and group members, and the pastperformance of the group (Jago, 1982). In R&Dsituations, many studies reveal that the leader’s tenurewith the project team, R&D team’s age, and task
uncertainty of the team can be important moderators
of the relationship between the role of leader andproject team performance (Allen et al., 1988; Barczak
and Wilemon, 1991; Keller, 1992).
Leader tenure. The effectiveness of a project leader
can be influenced by the leader’s tenure in the R&Dteam. Only when the leader has been with his team
members long enough, can the leader make a
substantial impact and influence the behaviour ofteam members and team climate, which in turn affectsproject performance. Because it takes a relatively long
time for the technical leader to gain the confidence ofhis R&D professionals who are used to autonomy andnot easily influenced by formal authority, it is nevereasy for the leader to instill his goals and values intohis team members and to change behavioural patternsestablished during the long period of professionaleducation. Even after they have accepted the influence
of the leader, it would take time to bear fruit.
Hypothesis 2: The relationship between the R &D
leader ’s role and team performance is moderated and
reinforced by leader ’s tenure
Team tenure. NIH or Not-Invented-Here syndrome
means ‘the tendency of a project group of stable
composition to believe it possesses a monopoly ofknowledge of its field, which leads it to reject new ideasfrom outsiders to the likely detriment of its perfor-mance’ (Katz and Allen, 1982, p. 7). As project teamsbecome increasingly cohesive and stable enough to feelcomplacent over time, they begin to separate or isolatethemselves from external sources of technical ideas and
influence, and communicate less frequently with out-
side professional groups. NIH syndrome stems fromthe basic tendency that over time individuals try toorganize their work environment in a manner thatreduces the amount of stress and uncertainty they mustface. This individual tendency is reinforced by thesituation where all the project members work togetherand share similar experiences for a certain amount of
time (Katz, 1981). They are more likely to rely on their
existing knowledge, views, experiences, and capabilitiesfor performing their tasks than to resort to outsideinformation and expertise. They become increasinglyimmune to outside technology development trends.
Given the importance of discussing and transferring
technical information and new ideas into the innova-tion process, it is not surprising that Katz and Allen
(1982) found the performance of R&D project teams
declining noticeably after a team’s tenure of five years,together with the communication level among teammembers and outside information sources. This resultimplies that as the team tenure prolongs, it becomesmore crucial for the project leader to make a deliberateeffort to avoid NIH syndrome. The leaders must helptheir team neither to become complacent nor be
isolated from outside information sources. They
should keep challenging team members, facilitatingcommunication, encouraging new approaches, andlinking team members with critical external agencies.On the contrary, since the team building role of aleader intends to foster the cohesiveness and consoli-dation of team members, it is more likely to reinforceNIH syndrome and have a negative impact on the
performance of a long-tenured team. In other words,
the gatekeeper and champion roles of a project leaderbecome more crucial for enhancing team performance,

Hypothesis 3: As the team tenure increases, the
leader ’s roles of gatekeeper and champion are
positively associated with the team performance, whilethe role of team building is negatively associated withthe team performance.
Task characteristics. The role of leaders must also be
contingent to the type of R&D project. Keller (1992)found that different leadership styles are required fordifferent projects. For instance, research projects aremore concerned with innovation and the exploitation
of scientific and technological information from out-
side, while development projects, which generally focuson the improvement of existing technology, require theleader to focus on task allocation and coordinationskills. Indeed, the project leader must satisfy thedifferent requirements of different R&D tasks withdifferent role emphasis.
When the task uncertainty of the R&D team is high,
prior research has found that both internal and
external communication activities must also be highand that project leaders must share their communica-tion activity and gatekeeper role with other teammembers for better team performance (Katz andTushman, 1981; Tushman, 1979; Tushman and Scan-lan, 1981). In an uncertain task environment, highperforming project teams are more likely to rely on a
small number of boundary spanning individuals for
external communication activities, who are not neces-sarily the team leaders. However, this does not meanthat the gatekeeper or communication role of a projectleader becomes weaker, as the R&D project taskbecomes more uncertain. Rather, project leaders mustexhibit aggressive behaviour to process and createmore information together with help from other team
members, as they have to surmount highly complex
and dynamic technical situations in order to accom-plish their innovation projects.
When the R&D project faces greater uncertainty in
market and =or technical environments, it becomes
more critical for the reduction of uncertainty orconfusion among team members to have clear strategicgoals or targets for the project, which used to be very
difficult to formulate under uncertain circumstances.
On the other hand, the team building role of a leadercan be viewed as more important when the team facesa more clearly defined task environment, because it ismore important to have coherent approach or goodteam cohesion for the efficient operation of routineR&D tasks.
Hypothesis 4: When the task uncertainty of the team
is high, the leader ’s roles of gatekeeper and strategic
planner are more positively associated with teamperformance. However, when the task uncertainty islow, the leader ’s role of team builder is more positively
associated with team performance.
3. Research methods
Samples
One hundred and three project teams were sampled
from three government-sponsored and three privateR&D organizations, which were selected in a pair-wisemanner in the areas of electronics, chemistry, andmechanical engineering. In toto , questionnaires were
collected from 570 R&D professionals in 103 projectteams. However, only 87 project teams, comprising 503
professionals, were finally used for further data
analysis, after excluding the teams which respondedto less than four questionnaires.
Measurements
Roles of leader. A multi-item scale was developed to
measure the role of R&D project leader based on the
literature review and pilot interviews with several R&Dproject leaders in both government-sponsored andprivate research institutes. The initial items wereextracted from various studies of the critical functionsof innovation and of the roles of R&D leader (seeTable 1), and then were verified for their relevance to thelocal R&D situation through structured interviews with
R&D project leaders. About half of the team members,
excluding the leader (on average 2.6 per team) wererandomly chosen to rate the scale of leader roles and theindividual scores were aggregated at the team level.
As shown in Table 2, five factors are selected on an a
priori basis from the principal component analysis,
which account for 76% of total variance. There aremany criteria for the extraction of factors, including
eigenvalue, scree-test, a priori criteria, and so on (Hair
et al., 1979). Since this study conceptualized the five
different roles of the leader, the a priori criteria have
been adopted. Each one of the five factors quite wellrepresents the concept of five different roles of theR&D leader, although two of the factors haverelatively low eigenvalues and thus result in lowexplanation powers. All of the factor scores used in
subsequent analyses were created by assigning factor
loading weights to item responses

Table 3 presents means, standard deviations, and a

Further, multipleregression analysis was performed to see whether theserelationships are borne out in a multivariate context.The results of regression analysis in Table 4 confirm

The results also indicate that the four roles of theleader account for 35% of the total variance of R&Dteam performance.
One may argue, however, technical expert represents
53% out of 76% of total variance in the principalcomponent analysis, as Table 2 shows, and thusappears an utmost important role for the R&D leader
with other roles of minor importance. This is not
surprising in the context of R&D activities, sincetechnical innovation itself requires scientific and tech-nical knowledge and deep understanding of thetechnical problems to be solved. Moreover, R&Dpeople themselves are technical professionals, whotend to respect a leader who has excellent technicalexpertise, rather than hierarchical authority (Miller,
1986).
Existing studies in advanced contexts already
recognized the importance of technical expertise forR&D leadership. For instance, Farris (1988, p. 14)asserted that ‘First and foremost is the importance ofthe leader’s technical skills. Much more consistentlyand strongly than in studies of non-R&D situations,the technical skills of technical supervisors are related
to the performance of their groups, and especially to
innovation by the groups.’ McCall (1988) also empha-sizes the importance of technical skill in implementingR&D leadership, since many roles R&D leaders must
perform depend on technical expertise, including
recognizing and communicating good ideas ( gate-
keeper ), influencing work goals ( strategic planner ),
persuading other stakeholders inside and outside theorganization ( championing ).
This does not mean, however, that other roles the
R&D leader must perform are not important for theR&D team performance. Table 4 indicates that leaderroles other than technical expert account for a
significant portion of R&D team performance. Thus
it would be fair to conclude that while technicalexpertise of an R&D leader itself is very crucial for theteam performance, other roles must also be fulfilled bythe leader for the performance of R&D projects,although many may not be successfully performedwithout technical expertise.

This study also found that teamtenure and task characteristics moderated the relation-ship between leader’s roles and team performance. Forinstance, the leader of longer-tenured teams must focus
less on the team builder role in order to avoid possible
NIH syndrome. The team builder role of a leader,however, tends to be more important for the success ofa relatively well-defined R&D project, while, for ill-defined projects, the strategic planner role appears tobe more crucial for the team performance.
These findings have both commonality and differ-
ences compared to those in existing studies. First, the
required roles of an R&D leader in Korea do not seem
to be much different from those found in advancedcountries. Five roles of an R&D leader derived fromthe factor analysis in the study have commonality withthose found empirically in various innovation contexts(see Table 1). This might stem from the fact that theinnovation process basically requires several criticalfunctions to be accomplished in any context (Roberts
and Fusfeld, 1981).
Second, the champion role of a leader, however,
does not appear to be relevant to the success of R&Dprojects in Korea. In advanced countries, there aremany successful heroic stories of innovation cham-pionship (Katz, 1993; Kidder, 1981; Nayak andKetteringham, 1994) and recently many studies havebeen undertaken to reveal the nature of the champion
role in the innovation process (Howell and Higgins,
1990; Markham et al., 1991; Shane, 1994, 1997). There
are a couple of possible reasons to explain theinsignificance of the champion role of the R&D leaderin Korea.
Shane (1998) suggested that the more individualism
and the less power distance and uncertainty avoidanceare valued in a society or organization, the more
prevalent championing activities become. The cultural
characteristics in Korea, however, can be characterizedby lower individualism, relatively higher power dis-tance and uncertainty avoidance, compared to theUSA and other advanced western cultures (Hofstede,1992). Given these cultures, it is not surprising thatmost R&D projects in Korea have been planned andselected from the top management of organizations.
It is hardly conceivable for the project leader to
initiate the R&D project without ap r i o r i formal
consent of top managers. In addition, many R&Dprojects in this study are more likely to be imitativeand incremental in nature and focus less on thecommercialization of new technologies. Performingthese projects may not involve high risk of failure,and neither need strong task coordination with other
organizational units nor, in turn, require the cham-
pion role of an R&D leader.These results offer an implication for the develop-
ment of R&D management theories in a developingcontext. There are basically two ways to extendexisting knowledge and to develop a new theory:formulating an explanation for the observation of realworld facts using the inductive approach and generat-ing predications about the phenomena based ondeductive approaches (Stone, 1978). Research on the
management of innovation in a development context
can benefit from knowledge generated in advancedcontexts for a general conceptual framework and at thesame time generate significantly more creative andrelevant hypotheses when the local situation is takeninto consideration in the research design (Kim andKim, 1985). By combining both the deductive andinductive approaches in an interactive manner, the
R&D management system, which is relevant to
the local situation in developing countries, can beefficiently constructed.
This study, based on a conceptual framework
developed in advanced countries, found both similarand different relationships between the role of an R&Dleader and team performance, compared with thosefound in existing studies. Similarity may represent
fundamental requirements of innovation management,
while dissimilarities can reflect different local R&Dsituations with respect to the level of technologicalcapability, nature of R&D project, social culture, andorganizational structure (Kim and Lee, 1995; Kim andUtterback, 1983).
This study also suggests several managerial im-
plications for R&D leadership and human resource
management in Korea. The R&D project leaders
themselves must be aware of the different rolerequirements of the situations. First, the leaders mustchange their relative role emphasis as their teamevolves along time. In the initial stage, they mustfocus on strategic planner and team builder roles toprovide a clear common understanding of the projectmissions or goals and to establish cohesiveness
among team members. Yet, they must be patient to
see any performance feedback of their leadershipactivities, because it usually takes a considerable timefor their efforts to show measurable effects. However,when their teams have been together for long enoughto suffer from NIH syndrome, they should de-emphasize the role of team building and emphasizemore on interfacing with external agencies and
stimulating or challenging team members to prevent
the tendency towards isolation from the environmentand complacency with the existing programmedactivities.
Second, the leaders must also consider the type of
projects they perform. Fundamental or radical R&Dprojects basically involve more uncertainty in goalsetting and require more activity in processing and
creating scientific, technical, and market knowledge.
Thus they must make more efforts to set clear strategic
162

goals and to design flexible plans to respond to
unforeseen contingencies or serendipity. They shouldalso carry out the technical expert role by suggestingnew ideas and alternative technical solutions forthemselves. However, when they are in charge ofrelatively routine, incremental development tasks,activities for consolidation and empowerment of teammembers must be more emphasized to a greater extent
(Tushman, 1979).
Third, our findings suggest that the diagnostic
activities of the leader role can be used for manypurposes such as selection, performance appraisal,staffing, and design of training programmes and careerdevelopment paths. A suitable R&D project leader canbe selected and evaluated on the basis of a carefulappraisal of the potential leader’s qualification to
perform various roles, in conjunction with the char-
acteristics of the tasks and teams he or she has tosupervise. The results also are helpful in developingdifferent skill training for leaders with different roleperformance profiles (i.e., communication, interper-sonal, team-building, problem-solving, or technicalskills), and to decide a viable career path for them(research specialist, R&D manager, or transfer to the
business division).
Finally, this study is basically cross-sectional in
nature. It surely warrants a longitudinal approach toconclude the causal relationship between the role ofleaders and their team performance. It is alsonecessary for further studies to refine the measure-ment of leader’s roles and R&D team performance.Among the five factors of the roles of R&D leader are
only three factors whose eigenvalues exceed one, as
Table 2 indicates. To improve discriminant andconvergent validities of the measurement, the multi-item scale of the leader’s role can be refined based onthe results of the current study. Another measure-ment approach can be applied for different leaderroles. For instance, sociometric methods can beemployed for the measurement of the role of a
gatekeeper (Allen, 1977; Tushman and Scanlan,
1981), or more qualitative methods such as contentanalysis can be used for the champion role (Howelland Higgins, 1990) and for other leadership roles(Barczak and Wilemon, 1989). In addition, objectiveindicators for team performance should be developedin future studies.
The results of this study should also be extended to
other innovation contexts. Projects for the commercia-
lization of new products or processes may require aslightly different leadership roles for successful im-plementation, and innovation projects aimed at theadoption of new administration systems or informa-tion technologies in the organization might also needdifferent leadership profiles. Future studies can addressthese differences in diverse innovation contexts with
more refined measurements of the role of technical
