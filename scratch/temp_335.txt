Abstract
Purpose –The purpose of this paper is to examine the effects of relationship bonds on the psychological
response and behavior of bank employees based on the job demands –resources theory. Specifically, it
examines the effects of relationship bonds in terms of person –job (P –J) fit, emotional exhaustion, job
satisfaction and boundary-spanning behaviors, all of which comprise the behavioral dimensions of bankemployees. In addition, the study examines how the resiliency of bank employees influences their emotional
exhaustion and determines whether a moderating effect related to emotional exhaustion exists.
Design/methodology/approach –To achieve this aim, data were collected from 365 customer-facing
banking employees in South Korea. Reliability, validity and the hypotheses were verified through structural
equation modeling; any moderating effects were identified using the bootstrap method and the process model.
Findings –Study results showed that financial, structural, internal social and external social bonds –the
bonds pertaining to relationship elements –have positive effects on P –J fit. P –J fit influenced emotional
exhaustion negatively and job satisfaction positively. Furthermore, emotional exhaustion negativelyinfluenced job satisfaction. Job satisfaction had positive effects on service delivery, external representation
and internal influence, the elements comprising boundary-spanning behavior. Finally, resiliency was shown
to lower emotional exhaustion but revealed no moderating effect.Originality/value –First, this study examined relationship bonds, which reference relationship marketing
when introducing organizational resources that influence the psychological and behavioral responses of bank
employees. Second, this study introduced resiliency as a personal resource and clarified the way it applies toan individual ’s psychological response. Third, existing literature has been limited to conducting fragmented
research of the psychological factors that intervene in predisposing factors and job outcomes. This study
makes a unique contribution by establishing a psychological response process.

2. Theoretical background
2.1 The JD –R model
The JD –R model has been designed to describe how burnout and engagement are generated
in employees. The key to the JD –R model is the assumption that specific elements that can
be classified into job demands and resources exist (Bakker and Demerouti, 2007; Demerouti
et al. , 2001; Zablah, Chonko, Bettencourt, Allen and Haas, 2012).
Job demands are related to physical, social, mental and organizational aspects, and
require continuous efforts for job perfor mance. Furthermore, when satisfactory
compensations are not provided, employees ’emotional state is negatively influenced or
stressful elements are created. Conversely, job resources reduce the efforts needed for job
demands and lower the physical and psychological costs. In addition, the resources support
employees ’ability to achieve their job objectives and can enable them to grow in their role,
learn and generate other positive developments (Demerouti et al., 2001). In this study, which
expanded the JD –R model, resources related to the job and the personal resources related to
individual characteristics were shown to exist and effects of resources on emotions were
demonstrated (Bakker and Demerouti, 2008).
Bakker and Demerouti (2007) stated that two types of psychological processes occur
within the JD –R model. The first is related to strain. Job demands prompt strain in job
situations and lead to negative organizational performance. The second is the motivationalprocess. Job resources lead to motivation and help employees accomplish positive outcomes.
In addition to these two psychological processes, job demands and resources generate a
buffer effect toward the motivational and strain processes, respectively. Thus, the effects of
job demands and resources are reduced in each process. Identifying the elements that
constitute the resources in a work environment can be useful in managing the psychological
and emotional states of bank employees. Therefore, it is important to examine the resources
related to a job and the personal resources.
2.2 Relationship bonds
Relationship marketing is related to all marketing activities that successfully establish,
develop and maintain relational exchange (Morgan and Hunt, 1994). The term “relationship
marketing ”was first mentioned by Berry (1983, p. 25) in the service literature and was
defined as that which induces, maintains and improves customer relationships in multi-
service organizations (Hunt et al., 2006). The initial purpose of relationship marketing was to
maintain the commitment relationship between customers and organizations (Hsieh et al. ,
2005) and has been drawing attention in marketing theory and practice as an effective
strategy for organizations to use in forming, maintaining and enhancing its customer
relationships (Ward and Dagger, 2007; Huang, 2008). Although the early form of
relationship marketing focused on the context of the relationship between organizations and
customers (Hsieh et al., 2005), relationship marketing has been expanded to strengthen the
relationship between organizations and employees owing to their significant role in the
relationship between organizations and their customers (Wang, 2014). A previous study580IJBM

interactions through relational exchanges. Similarly, the relationship bonds resulting due to
diverse relationships are mainly divided into financial, structural and social bonds (Liang
and Chen, 2009; Wang, 2014).
Financial bond is the basis of the relationship bonds used by organizations to form
relationships with customers, which strengthens relationships with customers by offering
economic benefits. This refers to the formation of relationships resulting from the provision
of economic benefits, such as discounts and incentives (Hsieh et al. , 2005; Lin et al. , 2003).
The financial bond, which aims to guarantee customer loyalty based on economic benefits,
is the most basic strategy for forming relationships compared to other types ofrelationshipbonds (Berry, 1995; Berry and Parasuraman, 1991). Financial bonds are related
to the economically dependent aspects of various relationships. From the perspective of a
bank employee, financial bonds are related to the efforts of the bank organization to satisfy
the financial aspect through economic incentives, such as wages (Wang, 2014). In other
words, the financial bonds of employees are actualized based on their wage level. When
other organizations provide higher wages and economic support, maintaining the financial
bonds with their organization becomes difficult. Providing economic benefits leads
employees to immerse themselves in the organization (Melancon et al. , 2011). Since the
employees who are satisfied with their economic compensation feel guilty when they leave
the organization, their intention to maintain the relationship becomes stronger (Wang, 2014).
Structural bonds create a customization value for customers (Chiu et al. , 2007). The
customization value appears in two main cases. First, it appears when the relationship withcustomers has been strengthened by the company ’s valuable services, which are difficult to
obtain from other sources, and in resolving the problems of customers. When customers
receive valuable services, it allows them to become more productive and effective by
offering a comprehensive set of services. It also references the provision of innovative
products or services tailored to the demands of customers (Hsieh et al. , 2005). Second,
structural bonds are formed when authority has been granted in the joint decision making
that is performed by customers alongside partners. The authority to make decisions in the
exchange relationships with a partner facilitates the opportunity to generate the
customization value for customers, and they feel greater satisfaction in their relationships
owing to the strengthening of structural bonds (Bolton et al. , 2003). When looking at
structural bonds from the perspective of bank employees, the bank makes an effort to
implement policy, procedures and programs to design work tailored to individuals and offer
additional values customized to employee needs. That is, decision making related to
establishing working hours and adjusting schedules can enhance the customization value
felt by employees in their role (Wang, 2014).
Social bonds are connected to positive interpersonal relationships and play a significant
role in relationship development (Kuenzel and Krolikowska, 2008). In general, social bonds
refer to the degree of mutual sharing of personal closeness and information in interpersonal
relationships (Liang and Wang, 2005; Wang, 2014). Bank employees –the boundary
spanners between the bank organization and its customers –experience interpersonal
interactions both inside and outside organizations. If organizations assist and allow
employees to form suitable social relationships, they can make affective and normative
commitments in their relationship with their organizations (Wang, 2014). In that sense, the
organizations ’efforts to establish social relationships of employees lead to social bond
formation. Internal social bonds refer to the efforts of bank organizations to establish and
maintain relationships between bank employees through communication. Smooth
communication and the relational exchange process among employees form intimacy and
result in reliability (Rodríguez and Wilson, 2002); this, in turn, benefits employees in terms of
both their psychological and emotional states (Wang, 2014).581Effects of

2.3 The relationship between relationship bonds and P –J fit
P–J fit pertains to whether the special requirements of individuals and a job coincide. In
general, such a fit can be identified by determining whether the knowledge, skills and
abilities of an employee coincide with the requirements of a given job (Kristof-Brown, 2000).
P–J fit is mainly divided into two types. The first type is the abilities –demand fit, which
pertains to whether the conditions required to perform a job coincide with the employees ’
abilities or skills. If they possess the ability to perform the job, employees can perform the
duties entailed in the role and achieve a high level of performance. The second type is theneeds –supplies fit. This type of fit is achieved when, for example, a job satisfies the wage
needs of an individual (Yoo, 2013). In the initial research related to P –J fit, two studies –one
focused on abilities –demand fit and the other on needs –supplies fit –were conducted.
However, the two types of fit have since been combined into one general concept, which is
now in use (Cable and Derue, 2002; Scroggins, 2007). The employee seeks to fulfill individual
needs and then endeavors to perform jobs with a high level of compatibility with his or her
individual characteristics. Meanwhile, the organization endeavors to seek employees who
possess the skills and abilities necessary to perform a given job.
Financial bonds refer to the efforts of bank organizations to stimulate the economic
satisfaction of employees through economic incentives, such as wages (Wang, 2014). One
external motive that applies to employees is related to the financial dimension (Melancon
et al., 2011), which exerts influence on behavior by eliciting the employee ’s emotions (Read,
2005). Obtaining economic benefits from an organization constitutes a significant motivator
for employees ( Joseph and Dai, 2010) and is related to their economic reliance on bankorganizations. Thus, bank employees react sensitively when they feel they are receiving
unfair wages compared to the amount of work that is done (Newman and Sheikh, 2012). On
the contrary, when the economic stability of employees has been satisfied due to ample
financial compensation from an organization, the needs –supplies fit is satisfied because the
need of the individual has also been met.
Structural bonds refer to efforts, such as adjusting working hours, to process the tasks of
bank employees in the most convenient and efficient manner and adjust schedules to align
with their needs. This concept encompasses the job policies, procedures and designs tailored
to the respective employees (Wang, 2014). Such flexibility in the working system can assist
employees in immersing themselves in their jobs (Martin and MacDonnell, 2012). Flexibly
adjusting schedules guide employees to perceive the flexibility as effort on the part of their
organizations to help them balance work and life. Since employees demand a balancebetween work and life from their organizations, they perceive flexible work schedules as the
organization ’s effort to take care of its members (Giannikis and Mihail, 2008). Therefore,
when structural bonds, such as a flexible work schedule, have been satisfied, the P –J fit of
employees is enhanced since the needs –supplies fit has been satisfied.582IJBM

personal intimacy and communication among employees through their interpersonal
interactions (Liang and Wang, 2005; Wang, 2014). In interpersonal relationships, social bonds
assist employees in forming an affective commitment (Wang, 2014). Individuals ’wish to
satisfy their social sense of belonging and relational needs is satisfied when they form social
bonds within an organization. Moreover, the relational support in a bank organization
promotes a flow of information and enhances the possibility of resolving any problems arising
from work (Felício et al., 2014). As such, the abilities or skills of employees improve when they
perform tasks. This satisfies both the abilities –demands and the needs –supplies fit.
Finally, external social bonds refer to the support of the bank organization, which enables
the sharing of relational and informational values with customers based on employee –customer
interaction (Biong and Ulvnes, 2011). The relationship formation between employees and
customers plays an integral role in the develop ment of business relationships and is closely
related to positive interpersonal relationships. Bank employees must prioritize the formation of
relationships to achieve positive job performance. The relationship that has been established
through interactions between customers and employees is characterized by mutual trust and
equality (Bolino et al., 2002) and the two sides can achieve mutual understanding by sharing
information (Nahapiet and Ghoshal, 1998). Empl oyees can accomplish their job objectives by
recommending financial products that can reso lve customer issues. The relationships with
customers are essential in performing employee tasks, which satisfies the abilities –demand fit.

H1a. Financial bonds will increase the P –J fit of bank employees.
H1b. Structural bonds will increase the P –J fit of bank employees.
H1c. Internal social bonds will increase the P –J fit of bank employees.
H1d. External social bonds will increase the P –J fit of bank employees.
2.4 The relationships between P –J fit, emotional exhaustion and job satisfaction
Burnout refers to the emotional response to chronic stress; customer-facing employees who
regularly deal with customers experience such burnout (Lewin and Sager, 2009). A previous
study on burnout mentions three types: di minished personal accomplishment,
depersonalization and emotional exhaustion (Maslach and Jackson, 1981). Diminished
personal accomplishment signifies a simultaneous reduction in work accomplishment and
work efficiency. Depersonalization occurs when employees perceive that an important factor
in their job is uncontrollable and random, and is a dysfunctional response to job-related
stress. Finally, emotional exhaustion is a response toward job-related stress that appears
when employees feel emotionally burnt out (Brotheridge and Grandey, 2002; Lewin and
Sager, 2009). Emotional exhaustion is the key element comprising burnout (Boles et al., 1997)
and can be applied to extensive and diverse job situations since it is an indicator used to
evaluate accumulated stress (Gaines and Jermier, 1983).
P–J fit is related to improving job satisfaction, self-efficacy and an individual ’s positive
attitude toward his or her job and organization (Lauver and Kristof-Brown, 2001; Saks and
Ashforth, 2002), and it can have positive effects on the emotional state. When the
abilities –demand fit dimensions needed by employees to perform tasks are satisfied, the
difficulties and pressure involved in job performance are diminished. On the contrary, when
they are not satisfied, the burden experienced by employees related to their job is increased.
Meanwhile, when an employee ’s individual needs, which are met by his or her job, for the
needs –supplies fit dimension are not satisfied, he or she will experience difficulties and
stress. On the contrary, when his or her individual needs and those related to job583Effects of

2.5 The effects and the moderating effect of the resiliency regarding emotional exhaustion
Resiliency refers to the psychological resource through which an individual can overcome
and recover from diverse stressful situations (Luthar et al., 2000; Tugade and Fredrickson,
2004). Previous studies explain resiliency as the psychological characteristic of actively
adjusting and recovering from grave difficulties and hardships in life (Masten and Reed,
2002). Individuals with high resiliency have been found to derive positive outcomes despite
the degradation of their health functions and a stressful situation or environment that
discourages ordinary development (Baumgardner and Crothers, 2009).
In particular, resiliency is important for bank employees who experience conflict situations.
High resiliency helps employees r educe job-related stress (Leach et al., 2005) and maintains their
personal motivation during job performance (Krush et al., 2013). Such resiliency alleviates the
negative effects from stress that employees have already experienced in job-related situations.
Meanwhile, employees with high resiliency ca n not only focus on their work by forming and
maintaining cognitive and sentimental reso urces but also control their stress (Krush et al., 2013).
In addition, resiliency –the personal resource for re ducing the effects of stress –can control
emotions (Fredrickson and Joiner, 2002). When co nsidering the fact that the resource dimension
reduces the psychological costs required in a given job according to the JD –R model, resiliency
directly lowers emotional exha ustion by reducing employees ’psychological costs (Demerouti
et al., 2001). In summary, resiliency itself is a person al resource, reduces emotional exhaustion
and lowers the effects of stress by helping employee make a calm and swift recovery from
stressful situations. Thus, the following hypotheses are established:
H5. The resiliency of bank employees will reduce emotional exhaustion.
H6. As the resiliency of bank employees increases, the effect of P –J fit reducing
emotional exhaustion will be strengthened.
H7. As the resiliency of bank employees increases, the effect of emotional exhaustion
reducing job satisfaction will be weakened.
2.6 Job satisfaction and boundary-spanning behaviors
Boundary spanners not only interact with those outside of the organization, such as
customers, but also influence the employees (Adams, 1976). Boundary-spanning behavior584IJBM

role of boundary spanner (Bettencourt et al. , 2001; Chebat and Kollias, 2000). Previous
studies have mentioned that customer-facing employees play three key boundary spanner
roles (Bettencourt et al. , 2001; Van Dyne et al. , 1994).
The first role is to satisfy customers with a high quality of service; the perception and
satisfaction of service quality are related to customer-facing employees ’behaviors, such as
courtesy, personal attentiveness, respo nsiveness and follow-through on promises
(Parasuraman et al. , 1988). The second role relates to employees ’representation of the
company to outsiders, supporting corporate products and services, and improving the
image and legitimacy of the company (Adams, 1976; Booms and Bitner, 1981; Bowen and
Schneider, 1985). The third role is related to sharing information and distributing
opportunities regarding improvements to meet the varied requests of customers, and ensure
product and service delivery (Schneider and Bowen, 1985). Regarding such boundary-
spanning behaviors, previous studies have identified that attitude factors related to one ’s
job exerted an influence (Bettencourt et al. , 2005).
Job satisfaction refers to employees ’attitude toward their job and organization (Rad and
Yarmohammadian, 2006). Employees ’job attitude improves their self-esteem and loyalty
toward the organization and is directly related to corporate performance measures, such as
an increase in productivity (Brashear et al. , 2004). Job satisfaction is the attitude that
influences one ’s motives and serves as the stimulant that leads to performance. Employees
who are satisfied with their job exhibit higher performance than those who are not. Thus,
employees can perform better when they are satisfied with their jobs since they apply more
effort to achieve their goals (Atmojo, 2012; Schmitz and Ganesan, 2014). Employees should
offer high-quality services to customers to improve their performance and also express
positive sentiments related to their organization. Bank employees with high job satisfaction
provide quality services to customers and improve their performance by expressing positive
sentiments about their bank organization. In addition, the employees with high job
satisfaction prioritize their organization, which leads to altruistic behaviors (Netemeyer
et al. , 1997; Organ and Lingl, 1995). As such, the following hypotheses can be established:
H8a. The job satisfaction of bank employees will increase service delivery.
H8b. The job satisfaction of bank employees will increase external representation.
H8c. The job satisfaction of bank employees will increase internal influence.

3.1 Data collection and survey sample characteristics
This study conducted questionnaire surveys targeting retail bank employees in South
Korea in the Summer 2018. To administer the surveys, a panel from a professional online
survey institute with the largest platform in South Korea was used. The corresponding
survey institute panel is not limited to a certain organization or area and only those who
have volunteered can serve on a panel. In addition, the survey institute induces voluntary
participation from those who joined the panel by providing points online that can
be converted into cash after filling out the questionnaire survey. Prior to conducting the
survey, survey targets were chosen using three steps. First, after several jobs were
suggested, participants were asked to select the one that applied to them. In this stage,
the participants who did not engage with customers were removed. Next, after suggesting
diverse industries related to employees who interact with customers, they chose the

CMB is a systematic error that occurs when measuring constructs and is the bias that
appears when measuring two or more variables using the same method (Billings and
Wroten, 1978; Podsakoff et al. , 2003). Covariance occurs due to a similar procedure when
measuring two or more variables using the same method. When a correlation between the
measurement methods exists, it is difficult to be certain of the correlation between the
measurement variables, even if validity exists (Podsakoff and Organ, 1986).
This study aimed to identify how bank employees ’relationship bonds apply to their
psychological responses and behaviors. To this end, the perception, inclination and attitude
of bank employees were measured based on the self-reported data collected through
the online surveys; the source of their responses was not distinguished; furthermore, the
environment or the time of measurement was uniform. Thus, it was necessary to test
for CMB. To resolve this matter, this study diagnosed CMB by conducting Harman ’s
single-factor test. Harman ’s single-factor test can recognize CMB by identifying the number
of factors needed to explain variance after analyzing all indicators as an unrotated factor
solution in exploratory factory analysis. If variance from CMB exists, only one factor is
explored from the factor analysis or most of the variance from the first factor is explained
(Podsakoff et al. , 2003). This study conducted an unrotated exploratory factor analysis
based on the indicator used to measure constructs. A total of nine factors had eigenvalues
higher than 1 and the variance of the respective factors was confirmed at 32.66, 10.33, 6.40,
5.54, 4.63, 4.06, 3.51, 3.34 and 2.79 percent. After conducting Harman ’s single-factor test, the
factors were not extracted as one and the factor with the greatest variance did not exceed
the majority of the variance.

4.4 Moderating effects of resiliency
It is necessary to determine whether the resiliency of bank employees has a moderating
effect regarding emotional exhaustion. H6evaluated whether the effect of reduced
emotional exhaustion through P –J fit would be strengthened as the resiliency of bank
employees increased. H7tested whether the effects of reduced job satisfaction as a function
of emotional exhaustion would be weakened as the resiliency of bank employees increased.
To identify the hypothesis related to the moderating effect, this study verified the
interaction effect using the bootstrap method and utilizing the process model. The process
model can simultaneously process a moderating effect, a mediating effect and a moderated
mediation effect, which has a combined and controlled moderating effect, and a mediatingeffect based on regression analysis in a single analysis framework; statistical significance is
verified through the bootstrap method (Hayes, 2013; Preacher and Hayes, 2008). Analysis
was conducted using model number 58, which was selected from among the process models
suggested by Hayes (2013). Job satisfaction was inserted into Y and P –J fit was inserted into
X. Moreover, after inserting emotional exhaustion into M and placing resiliency into W, the
analysis was conducted. The moderating effect of resiliency between P –J fit and emotional
exhaustion that was suggested in H6was determined by investigating the presence of a
significant effect from the interaction between P –J fit and resiliency on emotional
exhaustion. Based on the analysis, the interaction effect of P –J fit and resiliency on
emotional exhaustion was not statistically significant ( β¼0.07, CI ¼[−0.02, 0.17], pW0.05).
Thus, H6was not supported. In addition, the moderating effect of resiliency between
emotional exhaustion and job satisfaction, which was suggested in H7, was determined by
examining whether the interaction effect between emotional exhaustion and resiliency had a
significant effect on job satisfaction. The analysis showed that the interaction effect between
emotional exhaustion and resiliency was not significant for job satisfaction ( β¼0.02,

5.1 General discussion
The financial bonds satisfy the economic needs of bank employees and structural bonds
become a job resource for the organization, as they are perceived as the organization ’s effort
to take care of its employees from a policy perspective, which is demonstrated, for example,
by flexible working hours (Giannikis and Mihail, 2008; Wang, 2014). Internal social bonds
streamline job performance by generating an information exchange process to assist bank
employees resolve customer problems (Hu et al. , 2016), and external social bonds enable
bank employees to accomplish their job goals. Also, social bonds satisfy relationship needsby facilitating interaction between individuals (Liang and Wang, 2005). Thus, the internal
and external social bonds of bank employees become job resources and play important
functional roles. The analysis showed that relationship bonds improve the P –J fit.
Conversely, as the psychological resource related to the satisfaction of the conditions
necessary for job performance ability and job performance itself, P –J fit intervened in the job
resource factor and job outcome. The psychological response process was then initiated. P –J
fit lowered emotional exhaustion and improved job satisfaction; emotional exhaustion was
found to inhibit the job satisfaction of employees. In addition, job satisfaction was found to
enhance the boundary-spanning behavior of bank employees. Bank employees ’job
satisfaction improves their behaviors related to external representation, which boosts their
service delivery behavior, the corporate image, products and services provided for people
outside of the company and internal influence behavior, which leads to communication with
colleagues that can enhance service delivery. That is, when employees are satisfied with
their job and have a positive attitude, their efforts to make significant accomplishments in
their job increase (Netemeyer et al., 1997; Atmojo, 2012; Schmitz and Ganesan, 2014) and lead
to their prioritization of the organization and altruistic behavior (Netemeyer et al. , 1997;
Organ and Lingl, 1995).
Meanwhile, since bank employees ’resiliency was found to be a direct factor in lowering
emotional exhaustion, it can be considered a personal resource. There were no moderating
effects related to the reduction of emotional exhaustion owing to P –J fit or any weakening of
negative effects from emotional exhaustion. There can be several reasons for the lack of a
moderating effect of resiliency. First, a moderating effect between P –Jf i ta n de m o t i o n a l
exhaustion was not found because resiliency entails overcoming and adjusting to highly
stressful events (Luthans et al., 2006). Since P –J fit is not the predisposing factor that induces
stress and increases emotional exhaustion, resiliency does not moderate it; it also does not
moderate emotional exhaustion. Therefore, for resiliency, it is appropriate to identify the
moderating effect from the job demand factor on employees ’emotional state. Moreover, the

5.2 Managerial implications
Bank organizations can manage the emotions of employees and induce positive behavior
through their relationship bonds. As such, bank organizations must form financial bonds by
establishing a compensation system that employees perceive as adequate. Structural bonds
constitute another factor, which governs employees ’emotional state. Bank organizations can
immerse employees in their jobs through a flexible working system (Martin and MacDonnell,
2012) and employees can carry out behaviors related to their job through the balance they
strike between work and life. Such an effort by banking organizations not only positively
contributes to employee performance by leading to an increase in productivity, but also
diminishes the expenses incurred due to their inability to manage their emotional state
(Zablah, Franke, Brown and Bartholomew, 2012). Meanwhile, bank organizations must make
efforts to strengthen the internal social bonds within their organization. Bank organizations
should promote the exchange of information between employees, organize communitieswithin the organization to resolve problems related to sales and customers, and establish a
horizontal organizational culture. These efforts will satisfy employees ’social sense of
belonging. This study also found that external social bonds play a role as a job resource. Bank
organizations endeavoring to assist employees in forming good relationships with customers
are ultimately able to manage the emotional state of employees. Bank organizations should be
unstinting in their support of developing an employee manual to manage customer
relationships. Bank organizations should also devise countermeasures against blacklisted
consumers who make demanding requests of employees. The employees who respond to the
demanding requests and blacklisted consumers become mentally and physically exhausted; in
addition, the corporate image may also be negatively affected. Therefore, bank organizations
must create a manual for responding to such customers and occasionally discontinue their
relationship with them.
Bank organizations can enhance P –J fit by improving the relationship bonds of employees
and providing various support systems for regular job education and employee growth.
Regarding P –J fit, bank organizations should not merely satisfy the needs of employees or
improve their job performance ability through wages, but should also endeavor to satisfy both
the abilities –demand and the needs –supplies fit. Bank organizations can also accomplish P –J
fit based on their efforts by using diverse recruiting methods to find the members who possess
suitable skills or abilities required for adequate job performance.
Finally, among the personal inclinations of bank employees, resiliency lowers emotional
exhaustion. Since resiliency is not a fixed individual inclination, bank organizations should
endeavor to improve resiliency by developing job training programs that are designed to
enhance resiliency. Such efforts will increase the boundary-spanning behavior of employees.
5.3 Limitations and future research
First, this study suggested that relationship bonds are the organizational resources related
to a job. In addition, it mentioned resiliency as a personal resource of bank employees.
Regarding the elements related to bank employees ’role, other than those suggested by
this study, a variety of other elements could be at play, such as person –organization fit
(Yoo et al., 2014). Moreover, the elements related to job requirements were not considered in
this study. Since bank employees interact with customers, studies must be conducted after
considering the elements of the requirements that reflect such a characteristic. Therefore,
future studies should suggest various elements from the job requirements and resources,
which reflect the job characteristics of bank employees.592IJBM

effect. As mentioned previously, owing to the characteristics of resiliency, which are
overcoming stressful events and adapting, a moderating effect is expected to appear between
the job requirements and emotional exhaustion, which causes stress for bank employees.
Thus, it is necessary to identify the moderating effect of resiliency between job requirements
and emotional exhaustion by suggesting various job requirements for bank employees.
Third, this study found that the emotional exhaustion of bank employees eventually had a
negative influence on boundary-spanning behavior. Although boundary-spanning behavior is
expected to have a positive effect on the performance aspect of the bank organization, it is
necessary to identify the way it applies to the actual performance of the bank organization. In
that sense, future studies must demonstrate how the boundary-spanning behavior influences
the actual performance of the bank organization; thus, the importance of discussing the
emotional state of bank employees should be emphasized.
Finally, this study targeted bank employees working in Korea; it was conducted to
analyze the perception of ordinary bank employees, but its results are difficult to generalize
since it only targeted bank employees from a certain specific country. For example, Korea is
characterized as being influenced by collectivistic culture and thus a study conducted within
this cultural background could have different results than those resulting from countries
with an individualistic culture. Therefore, it is necessary to approach the generalization of
the results of this study with caution. Further research will be needed to examine any effects
that may be attributed to cultural differences by conducting additional studies that target
bank employees in the other countries.

My company supports my customers to ensure they develop close social relationships.
