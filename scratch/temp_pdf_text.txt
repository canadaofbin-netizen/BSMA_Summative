knowledge sharing: the moderating
effect of leader boundary

Myungsun Kim and Seckyoung Loretta Kim
College of Business Administration, Incheon National University,
Incheon, South Korea
Abstract
Purpose – The research aims to examine the impacts of two different types of goal orientation, i.e. leaning goal
orientation (LGO) and performance-prove goal orientation (PPGO), on employee knowledge sharing, and
whether these relationships are altered by leaders’ boundary spanning behavior (BSB).
Design/methodology/approach – A sample of 170 employees and 93 leaders were collected from South
Korean organizations. Data were analyzed using path analysis.
Findings – The results demonstrated that employees with a high LGO are more likely to engage in knowledge
sharing, while employees with a high PPGO are less likely to show knowledge sharing. Moreover, the findings
showed that highly learning oriented employees tend to engage in knowledge sharing when they are under
leaders who show active BSB.
Practical implications – To encourage knowledge sharing between employees, organizations need to
promote a mastery-structured work environment to help employees develop a stronger LGO. Also,
organizations could benefit from implementing training programs for leaders that promote their BSB.
Originality/value – The research aims to provide a more articulate account for how LGO and PPGO affect
employee knowledge sharing in opposite ways. More importantly, this research provides new insights
regarding the role of leaders’ BSB as a contextual factor in promoting employee knowledge sharing. Using
social learning theory as a theoretical framework, this study indicates how employees with a high LGO learn
the behaviors of boundary spanning leaders and are more willing to engage in knowledge sharing.
Keywords Knowledge sharing behavior, Goal orientation, Leader boundary spanning behavior,
Social learning theory, Interactionist perspective
Paper type Research paper
In a competitive and dynamic business environment, knowledge sharing is critical for
organizations to achieve sustainable competitive advantages (Rao Jada et al., 2019; Yoon
et al., 2020). Knowledge sharing defined as the action of making knowledge available to
others within the organizations such as providing or receiving task information, know-
how, and feedback regarding a product or procedure is widely known to contribute to
performance, innovation, organizational learning, and even long-term success (Yoon et al.,
2020; Wang and Noe, 2010). Recognizing the importance of knowledge sharing as a
primary source of organizational competitive advantages, scholars have continuously
conducted research to explore what promotes knowledge sharing between employees,
which is an essential way to exploit an existing knowledge in the organizations (Rao Jada
et al., 2019).
Interestingly, the major obstacles for organizations to implement successful knowledge
management systems were not the systems but individual employees (e.g. Lee et al., 2015).
Since knowledge is a source of power and competitive advantages for each employee, sharing
knowledge is undoubtedly challenging for them unless they have compelling reasons or
motivations to engage in such behavior (e.g. Kim et al., 2016). As each individual is a primary
entity that initiates knowledge sharing in an organization (Yoon et al., 2020), we focus on

Particularly, we select goal orientation dimensions such as learning goal orientation (LGO)
and performance-prove goal orientation (PPGO) as key predictors of employee knowledge
sharing given their approach component in common (Elliot and Church, 1997), yet differing
tendencies toward knowledge sharing.
Goal orientation refers to a motivational orientation that directs individuals’ attention and
effort on how to approach, interpret, and respond to achievement situations (Vandewalle
et al., 2019). Goal orientation has been known to be a potent antecedent of various individual
attitudes and behaviors across diverse organizational settings (e.g. Stasielowicz, 2019).
As a motivational force of behaviors, goal orientation is expected to influence individuals’
reactions to achievement situations (DeShon and Gillespie, 2005), such as whether to engage
in knowledge sharing at work. Considering the social aspect of most achievement settings in
organizations, this study investigates the relationship between employee goal orientation and
knowledge sharing, through exploring the different dimensions of goal orientation, such as
LGO and PPGO. By empirically testing the effects of two types of goal orientation on
employee knowledge sharing, we contribute to extant literature on goal orientation and
knowledge sharing.
Moreover, we take an interactionist perspective and suggest a comprehensive narrative
that integrates both personal and contextual factors to explain what makes employees to
engage in knowledge sharing. A recent review paper by Vandewalle et al. (2019) called for
more research on the roles of moderator variables in explicating the relationships between
goal orientation and proximal and distal outcome variables, which enhances the potential
predictive power of each goal orientation factor in organizational settings. To respond to such
call, we introduce leaders’ boundary spanning behavior (BSB) as a contextual factor,
referring to leaders’ activities to gain valuable information and knowledge beyond their units
or organization (Salem et al., 2018; Takanashi and Lee, 2019).
Although leaders are the ones who can connect with external parties and actually perform
boundary-spanning activities, little research has explored the role of leaders’ BSB that affects
employees’ attitudes and behaviors (Liu et al., 2018; Marrone et al., 2022; Zhang and Li, 2021).
Given that knowledge sharing requires resources from an individual employee to engage,
leaders’ BSB could be an appropriate situational factor that can change the relationship
between goal orientation and knowledge sharing by providing necessary resources.
Importantly, boundary spanning leaders could act as a strong role model that influences how
goal-oriented employees engage in knowledge sharing. Drawing upon social learning theory
(Bandura, 1986), we expect leaders’ BSB to strengthen the positive relationship between LGO
and knowledge sharing, and to weaken the negative relationship between PPGO and
knowledge sharing. Figure 1 illustrates our hypothesized model.
Theoretical background and hypotheses development
Goal orientation and knowledge sharing
Although knowledge sharing can occur at all different levels of organizations, such as
between individuals, groups, departments, and organizations (Rao Jada et al., 2019; Wang and
Noe, 2010), we focus on the individual level knowledge sharing as any level of knowledge
sharing requires the initiation or the participation of individuals (Yoon et al., 2020). In fact,
knowledge sharing mostly relies on each individual’s willingness to share their knowledge
(Yoon et al., 2020). However, due to their concerns about losing the source of power and
competitive advantages, employees tend to be reluctant to share their knowledge (e.g. Lee
et al., 2015). Thus, prior research has suggested various organizational-, leader-, and
individual factors that predict and promote knowledge sharing (Wang and Noe, 2010).

Building upon the scholarly work on personality trait and motivation as the antecedents of
knowledge sharing (e.g. Kim et al., 2016), we expect goal orientation to serve as a powerful
motivational drive for individuals to engage in more or less knowledge sharing in the
workplace (Lee et al., 2015). Because of its motivational drives, goal orientation guides
individuals to engage in certain types of behaviors, either consciously or subconsciously
(DeShon and Gillespie, 2005). We adopt the most widely accepted conceptualization of goal
orientation that consists of three dimensions: LGO, PPGO, and performance-avoid goal
orientation (PAGO) (Vandewalle et al., 2019). Individuals with LGO focus on developing
competences through mastering new skills in challenging situations. They believe that their
ability can be developed through effort, which is an efficient strategy for improving
performance. In contrast, individuals with PPGO and PAGO are mostly concerned with
demonstrating their ability. Specifically, individuals with PPGO focus on demonstrating
one’s competence and gaining favorable judgments, while individuals with PAGO focus on
avoiding negation of one’s competence and negative judgments from others (Dweck and
Leggett, 1988; Vandewalle et al., 2019).
Given their relevance and different approaching tendencies, we select LGO and PPGO as
key predictors of employee knowledge sharing. Depending on the types of goal orientation,
employees would differ in the extent to which they engage in knowledge sharing because
they tend to hold different beliefs about the possibility of ability development (e.g. Lee et al.,
2015). For example, LGO and PPGO are known to be related to different interpersonal styles
and levels of interest in cooperation (Poortvliet and Giebels, 2012). Similarly, information
sharing with colleagues can be regarded as a positive opportunity for mutual growth for
individuals with a strong LGO; while it could be viewed as risky and problematic for
individuals with a strong PPGO (Vandewalle et al., 2019).
We expect that highly learning oriented employees are more likely to engage in knowledge
sharing. Employees with a high LGO tend to select challenging tasks and skill improvement
goals, and value experiences that provide growth (Vandewalle et al., 2019). As they value
challenging situations or tasks as a chance to develop their competencies by learning new and
diverse information and knowledge, employees high in LGO would actively engage in

their own understanding and encourages them to find a better way to explain the knowledge

In contrast, we argue that employees with a high PPGO would be less likely to engage in
knowledge sharing. Individuals holding a high PPGO assume challenging goals or tasks as a
risk because they fear failing or revealing their inadequate ability to others. In fact, highly
performance goal-oriented individuals are likely to respond to challenging tasks or difficult
situations by avoiding challenges, showing little interest in difficult tasks, and putting little
effort to succeed when facing adversity (Vandewalle et al., 2019). As a result, employees high
in PPGO tend to repeat experienced tasks that might help them prove their competence to
others (Vandewalle et al., 2019). As employees with a high PPGO are mostly concerned about
their performance or outcomes rather than learning itself, they might not perceive any value
for knowledge sharing.
Moreover, knowledge sharing can be costly for employees with a high PPGO because they
can lose their competitive advantages to remain superior to others once their knowledge
becomes available for everyone else in the organization for free. Those employees are sensitive
to the possibility of losing their competitive advantages, as they are motivated to demonstrate
their abilities to others (Zhu et al., 2019). Unless they are confident that they are able to
outperform others at work even after sharing their knowledge, they would not be willing to
share their knowledge with others. Moreover, they may not want to waste their time and effort
for knowledge sharing, which otherwise can be used for other work activities that might bring
them greater personal benefits and rewards by outperforming others (Wang and Noe, 2010).
H1. Employee LGO is positively related to knowledge sharing.
H2. Employee PPGO is negatively related to knowledge sharing.
The moderating roles of leader boundary spanning behavior
BSB refers to actions taken by a focal individual or team reaching out into its environment to
obtain important resources and support that might help accomplishing their objectives (Yao
et al., 2022), and are generally classified into three broad categories: representation, task
coordination, and general information search (Marrone et al., 2022). Importantly, boundary
spanning underlies the survival and success of the team and its organization (Marrone et al.,
2022; Yao et al., 2022). Prior research found BSB as one of significant antecedents of outcomes
such as team knowledge sharing, innovativeness, and overall team effectiveness (Kou, 2021;
Zhang and Li, 2021). Despite its importance, BSB at the individual level has not been studied
sufficiently, especially for those who are in a leader position (Kim et al., 2022).
Using a leadership approach “that creates direction, alignment, and commitment across
boundaries in service of a higher goal” (Cross et al., 2013, p. 81), we expect leaders’ BSB to exert
significant influences over employees’ knowledge sharing. Leaders’ BSB is defined as actions
undertaken by a leader reaching out into the surrounding environment to establish linkages
with external parties, and to obtain important resources and support from the parties (Salem
et al., 2018). Given the vital power and position that leaders have in the workplace, they might
act as key players in connecting with external parties and obtaining valuable resources from in-
and out-group members, thereby exhibiting BSB (Takanashi and Lee, 2019). By serving an
information-gathering function as well as an external representation function, leaders’ BSB can
contribute to employee performance and creativity (Kim et al., 2022; Takanashi and Lee, 2019).
Drawing upon social learning theory, we suggest leaders’ BSB as a critical context that
might interact with goal orientation dimensions (i.e. LGO and PPGO) to affect employee

knowledge sharing. Social learning theory posits that individuals observe and imitate the
behaviors of their role models (Bandura, 1986). In the workplace, employees tend to perceive
their leaders as role models considering their higher position in social hierarchies (Lian et al.,
2022). As a role model, boundary spanning leaders can create a context in which individuals
can “seek or give information, views, and ideas through interaction with other individuals”
(Decuyper et al., 2010, p. 118), and send signal to employees whether it is desirable to share
information and knowledge with others.
As noted, due to the risk of losing competitive advantages coming from their expertise by
sharing knowledge with others, employees tend to be reluctant to engage in knowledge
sharing without an assurance of fair evaluation or an expectation of recognition by their
leaders or the organization for doing it (e.g. Rao Jada et al., 2019). Thus, depending on what
their leaders pursue and expect them to achieve at work (Wang and Noe, 2010), employees
may differ the extent to which they engage in knowledge sharing. Through a social learning
process during which employees repeatedly observe and interact with their boundary
spanning leaders, employees may perceive their knowledge sharing more positively because
it is likely to be promoted by their boundary spanning leaders.
Under the supervision of boundary spanning leaders, employees high in LGO would be
more likely to engage in knowledge sharing. By observing the behaviors of boundary
spanning leaders, i.e. role modeling, employees high in LGO might learn the value of
obtaining critical information or resources through interacting with external actors (Salem
et al., 2018), and thus be more motivated to imitate the behavior of searching for, obtaining,
and sharing information and knowledge at work. Moreover, leaders’ BSB expands the
amount and variety of information available to employees, allowing them to have additional
information or resources for their work (Salem et al., 2018). Since knowledge sharing
consumes resources (Kim et al., 2016; Yoon et al., 2020), employees with a high LGO are more
willing to share their knowledge when leaders provide valuable resources through their
boundary spanning activities. We thus expect the positive relationship between employee
LGO and knowledge sharing to become stronger when leaders highly engage in BSB.
When leaders engage in high levels of BSB, employees high in PPGO may also feel more
comfortable to share their knowledge as this behavior is likely to be encouraged by their
boundary spanning leaders. Due to the possibility of losing their competitive advantages by
sharing knowledge, employees high in PPGO may find it difficult to take any interpersonal
risks inherent in knowledge sharing unless their leaders show enough support and
encouragement. Boundary spanning leaders, who tend to take on new opportunities and
challenges, and frame their own setbacks as learning opportunities, can act as a role model
that encourages employees to perceive the value of interacting with others for obtaining
additional information and resources. Through observing and interacting with boundary
spanning leaders who provide additional resources and support, employees with a high
PPGO may receive signals from their leaders that it is desirable to share their valuable
resources with others, which reduces their fear of failing at work.
H3. Leader BSB moderates the relationship between employee LGO and knowledge
sharing such that the positive relationship is stronger when leader BSB is at high
than when it is at low.
H4. Leader BSB moderates the relationship between employee PPGO and knowledge
sharing such that the negative relationship is weaker when leader BSB is at high
than when it is at low.

Method
Participants and procedures
We collected data from fifteen companies located in South Korea, from industries of mainly
financial services, consulting and IT service. We designed two separate questionnaires: one for
employees and the other for their direct leaders. When making a list of supervisor and
subordinate pairs, our coordinators were informed that supervisor-subordinate dyads should be
in appropriate position to evaluate subordinates’ knowledge sharing and observe supervisors’
boundaryspanning behavior. Participationfor thisresearch wasentirely voluntary. We informed
participants the purpose of this study (in the cover letter) and assured that their responses would
be kept confidential. They were asked to fill out the questionnaires and return them in the sealed
envelopes. In total, 177 employees and 94 leaders returned their responses (89% of response rate).
After deleting incomplete responses, our final sample consisting of 170 employees and 93 leaders
wasusedinthefinalanalysis.Asforleaders,anaverageagewas42.97yearsand86%ofthemare
male. As for employees, an average age was 34.01 years and 64% of them are male.
Measures
On a 7-point Likert-type scale ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 7 (strongly agree), leaders
and employees indicated the extent to which they agree with each statement.
Goal orientation. Goal orientation was assessed by using the 13-item scale from Brett and
VandeWalle (1999). Specifically, LGO was measured by five items (Cronbach alpha 5 0.89),
while PPGO was measured by four items (Cronbach alpha 5 0.88). Sample items are “I am
willing to select a challenging work assignment that I can learn a lot from” for LGO and “I try
to figure out what it takes to prove my abilities to others at work” for PPGO.
Knowledge sharing. Leaders evaluated their employees’ knowledge sharing using the 7-
item scale developed by Srivastava et al. (2006). A sample item includes “This employee
shares their special knowledge and expertise with others” (Cronbach alpha 5 0.97).
Leader boundary spanning behavior (BSB). Leader BSB rated by his or her immediate
subordinate was measured by using the 24-item scale developed by Ancona and Caldwell
(1992). A sample item includes “My leader coordinates activities with external groups”
(Cronbach alpha 5 0.97).
Control variables. We controlled for demographic variables (age and gender) of both
leaders and employees to rule out their potential influences on the study results (Kim et al.,
2017; Li et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2014). We also included PAGO as a control variable (Chen and
Mathieu, 2008; Payne et al., 2007), which was measured by four items from the scale
developed by Brett and VandeWalle (1999) (Cronbach alpha 5 0.82). A sample item for PAGO
is “I prefer to avoid situations at work where I might perform poorly” [1].
Analytical strategy [2]
To test the hypotheses, we conducted a path analysis by using Mplus 8 with maximum
likelihood estimation with robust standard errors (MLR) (Muthen and Muthen, 2012–2017),
which was considered to handle a non-normality issue of the data. To take account of the non-
independence of observations due to individuals’ nesting within leader groups, we employed
“Type 5 Complex” syntax in the analysis (e.g. Liu et al., 2015).

Discussion
As knowledge sharing serves as a fundamental means for achieving organizational
effectiveness, it is imperative to discover what factors affect employee knowledge sharing
(Kim et al., 2016; Rao Jada et al., 2019). We examined two different types of employee goal
orientation simultaneously, i.e. LGO and PPGO, as predictors of knowledge sharing.
According to our findings, employee LGO was positively related to knowledge sharing, while
employee PPGO was negatively associated with knowledge sharing. Further, we tested the
roles of leaders’ BSB as a contextual factor that might alter such relationships and found that
when leaders highly engage in BSB, learning-oriented employees are more likely to engage in
knowledge sharing.
Unlike our expectation, leaders’ BSB did not moderate the relationship between employee
PPGO and knowledge sharing. There might be plausible reasons for this finding; it is possible
that employees with a high PPGO may utilize the additional resources acquired from their
boundary spanningleadersto increase theirin-role performance rather than knowledge sharing.

Theoretical implications
This study suggests several theoretical contributions. First, our research advances extant
literature on the antecedents of knowledge sharing in organizations. Since knowledge
sharing observed at any level of organization primarily arises from each individual’s
willingness and motivation to share, it is critical to understand what factors promote or
hinder knowledge sharing at the individual level (Lee et al., 2015). Scholars have noted the
importance of personal predictors of knowledge sharing such as exchange ideology,
hypercompetitiveness, self-efficacy, and openness to experience (e.g. Yoon et al., 2020). This
study thus focuses on goal orientation dimensions and provides an account for why LGO
prompts knowledge sharing, while PPGO discourages knowledge sharing. Applying goal
orientation theory (Dweck, 1986), this research provides a more articulate account for how
LGO and PPGO affect employee knowledge sharing in opposite ways. By doing so, we
suggest valuable insights into the role of goal orientation in explaining employee knowledge
sharing in organizational contexts.
Taking an interactionist perspective, we further demonstrate the significant role of
a leader’s BSB as a contextual factor in promoting employee knowledge sharing. However,
still more research is needed to explore various factors that might affect individuals’ initiation
to share their knowledge in organizations (e.g. Tan et al., 2016). For example, incentives on
knowledge sharing could be a possible moderator that alters the relationship between
employee goal orientation dimensions and knowledge sharing.
Second, this study contributes to existing literature on leadership and boundary spanning
by demonstrating the significant role of a leader’s BSB that moderates the relationship
between employee goal orientation dimensions and knowledge sharing. Prior studies have
largely overlooked the complex dynamics of BSB undertaken by a leader, rather focused
solely on team boundary spanning (e.g. Zhang and Li, 2021). Moreover, most existing
leadership studies have focused on leaders’ internal activities, such as empowering
leadership and abusive supervision as predictors of employees’ knowledge sharing (Kim

Using social learning theory as a theoretical framework, this study sheds light on how
boundary spanning leaders act as a role model and influence the relationship between
employee goal orientation dimensions and knowledge sharing. Our results demonstrate that
employees with a high LGO learn the behaviors of boundary spanning leaders and are more
willing to engage in knowledge sharing. However, the moderating role of leaders’ BSB in the
relationship between employee PPGO and knowledge sharing was not significant. This
nonsignificant finding implies that employees with a high PPGO might be reluctant to share
their knowledge with others unless leaders explicitly state that knowledge sharing is
essential to be recognized as a high performer. In future research, it would be worthwhile to
examine leaders’ specific behaviors that might promote employees with a high PPGO to
engage in knowledge sharing. Moreover, future research will benefit from demonstrating the
direct effects of boundary spanning leadership on various employee outcomes.
Lastly, we extend the potential application of goal orientation research findings in
organizational settings by providing empirical evidence with employees (Vandewalle et al.,
2019). By investigating the differentiated effects of LGO and PPGO on employee knowledge
sharing, this study improves the understanding of the potential predictive power of each goal
orientation dimension. According to Vandewalle et al. (2019), different from early goal
orientation research that primarily focused on individual behaviors and performance
outcomes, research in the past decade has expanded to include the study of interpersonal
behaviors, which aligns with the contribution of this study for knowledge sharing. Future
research should reveal the underlying mechanisms of how different goal orientation
dimensions influence employees’ knowledge sharing. With a stronger understanding of the
impact of leaders’ BSB as a moderator, we also broaden the scope in which goal orientation
research findings are applied in the context of leadership. In future research, it is worthwhile
to examine the moderating roles of different leader behaviors or leadership in the relationship
between employee goal orientation dimensions and work outcomes.
Managerial implications
This study also suggests practical implications. First, to encourage knowledge exchange
between employees, managers and organizations should be well aware of the importance of
goal orientation that employees are holding and develop interventions to educate them about
the potential benefits of holding a strong LGO. As our findings confirmed the positive effect
of LGO and the negative effect of PPGO on knowledge sharing, managers might consider
adjusting a selection procedure to account for employees with different characteristics and
preferences, especially for the jobs that require and value knowledge sharing. Furthermore,
managers could foster a learning culture that helps employees develop a stronger preference
for developing competence, i.e. LGO (Vandewalle et al., 2019). For example, acting as positive
role models, managers could encourage employees to pursue new and challenging
opportunities, provide useful feedback, and promote psychological safety (O’Keefe et al.,
2013; Vandewalle et al., 2019).
Second, our findings suggest the importance of leader behaviors that match with the
characteristics of employees to promote knowledge sharing. As learning goal oriented
employees exhibit a high level of knowledge sharing in the context of a high level of leader
BSB, organizations should consider matching team members with team leaders properly to
stimulate knowledge sharing (Kristof-Brown et al., 2005; Peng et al., 2022).
Lastly, organizations could benefit from implementing training programs for leaders that
promote their BSB. By adopting participatory and problem-solving approaches, these

Limitations and future research
This study is not without limitations. First, as we used cross-sectional data, common method
bias can be a potential threat to our findings, especially when interpreting the causal
relationships (Podsakoff et al., 2003). Although the common method variance issue may bias
our findings to some extent, the results of interaction effect would be less susceptible to such
bias than are those concerning the main effect significance. Also, we collected data from
different sources, including both leaders and employees, thereby addressing the common
method bias issue to some degree.
Second, as this study only included a limited set of variables, further research should
consider other potential factors that might affect the hypothesized relationships. Through
incorporating more situational and relational variables, researchers would be able to obtain
a more complete picture of knowledge sharing phenomenon in organization settings. For
example, outcome interdependence or perceived motivational climate could be an important
contextual factor that alters the relationship between employee goal orientation and
knowledge sharing (e.g. Zhu et al., 2019).
