Abstract
Purpose –Literature on small and medium enterprises (SMEs) has so far produced limited evidence on how
these firms pursue their organizational flexibility with information and communication technology (ICT) and ad
hoc work practices. The purpose of this paper is to contribute to the extant literature by focusing on how SMEs
use flexible work practices that provide latitude with respect to when employees work, where they work and viawhich communication medium. Specifically, the authors analyze how such practices are related to the conditions
that SMEs face in reference to their competitive environment and their patterns of ICT usage.
Design/methodology/approach –A survey was conducted on 304 Italian SMEs, with the aim of
identifying the contextual dimensions where flexible work is chosen and the different typologies of flexible
work implemented by companies.Findings –Flexible work in SMEs is chosen for different reasons associated to different conditions in the
competitive environments and in ICT usage where SMEs operate. In general, SMEs use flexible work when
they are more capable of improving their external orientation toward suppliers, customers, and the entrancein new markets with ICT. This duality is more likely in the competitive environments where external
orientation and information processing is more needed, namely, environments that are uncertain and complex
for product and breadth of the geographical complexity (scope) covered.
Research limitations/implications –In this paper, the authors offer an analysis on the contextual
characterizations of flexible work practices. Future studies should disentangle more in depth the ways thesecharacterizations are related to different ICT usages.
Practical implications –In uncertain and complex environments, SMEs should increase their external
information processing with ICT and organizational practices that support the latitude of employees involvedin boundary spanning with respect to where, when, and how they work.
Originality/value –This paper offers an interpretation of flexible work as an organizational mechanism used
to cope with uncertain and complex environments where more external orientation is needed. This paper also
shows that there are four different typologies of flexible work implemented by companies, namely, flexible work
for cheaper input costs, flexible work for operational drivers, flexible work for strategic drivers, and flexible workfor individual motivations, and that in some cases the conditions under which they are chosen are different.
Keywords Environmental uncertainty, Small and medium enterprises, Flexible work practices,
ICT-based external orientation

identifying the different typologies of flexible work chosen by SMEs.
Our interest on SMEs is motivated by the fact that SMEs may develop models of flexible
work that differ significantly from those followed by larger organizations, which have been
object of the majority of studies on flexible work so far. Compared to larger enterprises
SMEs face different conditions in relation to their labor contractual framework,
formalization of human resource management practices, degree of unionization, and
geographical scope of their operating units. Furthermore, flexible structures are a source of
SMEs ’strengths over large firms (Terziovski, 2010).
To analyze the linkage between SMEs ’strategic positioning and the use of flexible forms
of work we explore how SMEs use their information and communication technologies (ICT)
in support of their competitive strategies. More specifically, we assess whether the
motivations and the mechanisms at the origin of flexible work can lie in firms ’need to
improve their external orientation (Chung et al., 2014). An externally oriented firm prioritizes
the coordination with partners (e.g. distr ibutors), customers and suppliers, the
reconfiguration of their network of partners and their product development capabilities
over the objectives of internal efficiency. Such strategic posture can be typical of competitive
environments characterized by a high level of uncertainty and complexity in technological
and market conditions. Contingency theory in organizational studies illustrates that
complexity and uncertainty require firms to increase their information processing capacity
and the use of boundary-spanning roles (Leifer and Delbecq, 1978). Flexible forms of work
pursued through the spatial mobility of workers can be a mechanism that SMEs use to cope
with their competitive environment and with the need to have a high degree of coordination
with customers and suppliers. In other words, flexible forms of work can allow SMEs to
cope with the need of external orientation without reliance on hierarchical and bureaucratic
organizational forms.

Theoretical background
Referring to organizational studies on flexible organizations (e.g. Volberda, 1998), this
section illustrates the concept of flexible work in relation to its role in supporting a firm ’s
external orientation as a way to manage flexibility, as the capability to cope with
uncertainty and rapid environmental changes. The general picture emerging from this
analysis is that flexible work and ICT, supporting communication and coordination across
the firm boundaries, can be levers that SMEs use to increase their external orientation in
environments with greater uncertainty and complexity.
Flexible work: definition, cases, and drivers
We define flexible forms of work as the usage of flexible technologies to accomplish tasks
across locational, temporal, and contextual boundaries (Martins et al. , 2004). Flexible work
may be conducted for a variety of reasons and can be described through a spatial, a
temporal, and a contextual dimension. The spatial dimension characterizes where workers
perform their job when they are “on the move. ”The contextual dimension characterizes why
workers perform their jobs outside the firm ’s premise and on the move. This can be related
to a functional need of the enterprise (e.g. an area manager in the sales function or a
technician for a firm in the industrial machinery industry) or it may be related to employees ’
individual requests. The temporal dimension characterizes the time frame when employees
work in mobility ( flexible work may occur with a given periodicity or occasionally).
The literature highlights that most forms of flexible work that occur outside the firm
may find their reason in the firm ’s need for flexibility. Flexible work may address a firm ’s
need to cover a broad geographical scope with limited investments in fixed assets, such as inthe case of sales representatives who are required to develop business opportunities abroad
(Rapp et al., 2012). In a similar way, for some jobs, mobility may address the firm ’s need to
provide technical assistance to sales or after-sales processes (Corso et al. , 2006), such as in
the case of installers and maintenance specialists in sectors like industrial and office
machineries or electricity distribution. In some firms, flexible work may follow the need to
dedicate some human resources in their product engineering area to coordination with
partners in product development activities, or marketing specialists in their coordination
with distributors to adjust the product mix to changes in the demand.
The forms of flexible work illustrated above may represent a mean through which SMEs
deploy some key human resources in the exploration of new markets and technological
opportunities, such as entry into a foreign market and the absorption of external market or
technical knowledge from market or technology partners (Foss et al., 2013). From a strategic
perspective, flexible work based on more direct relationships between the firm and its
partners may allow SMEs a disintermediation from third parties such as dealers (e.g. export
intermediaries) in their access to market knowledge and to the caption of weak signals of
change in market and technological conditions.924JEIM

Another driver of flexible work can be traced back to a firm ’s need to recombine its
resources rapidly in response to changes in the external environment. From this point of
view, flexible work can reflect an attempt to gain agility with contingent employees working
remotely (Sahaym et al. , 2007). In this case, flexible work can be a way to bring new
knowledge to a firm without incurring some of the costs associated with direct employment.
Firms can implement major changes rapidly because they can hire in new skills quickly and
they can shed activities with fewer encumbrances (Kakihara and Sørensen, 2004). Based on
this perspective, the increased level of spatial mobility enabled by ICT can lead to
transformations in industry and value chain structures and to the emergence of new
business frameworks (Lyytinen and Yoo, 2002). For SMEs, this implies that the control
enabled by ICT in flexible work might allow new ways to govern business relationships in
their value chains and in gaining the organizational agility to pursue business opportunities.
Concretely, this may take the form of few flexible workers that develop business
opportunities abroad in lieu of export intermediaries, or the firm ’s capability to take part in
product development projects with external partners due to employees that, with a certain
periodicity, work remotely in the partners ’premises through ICT that support the
coordination among the firm and its partners.
The role of ICT in supporting flexible work
ICT may support flexible work in three ways. The first mechanism relates to the reduction
of communication costs and to its consequent effect of coordination and collaboration across
and within firms. For example, electronic knowledge repositories support knowledge
storage and sharing and may thus facilitate coordination within a team of dispersed
workers. Enterprise 2.0 technologies (as instant messaging tools, social networks, and online
forums and wikis) offer free and easy platforms for communication and interaction.
Their use in organizational routines is facilitated by the fact that these technologies are
often consolidated in the individual routines of employees ’personal lives thanks to the
diffusion of tablets and smartphones (McAfee, 2006).
The second mechanism reflects the role that enterprise information systems such as
enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems, customer relationship management (CRM), and
supply chain management (SCM) tools have in reducing information processing acquisition
costs within the enterprise (Yuan et al. , 2010) and in imposing standardized and codified
work structures on business processes (Neirotti et al., 2011). These technologies allow firms
to codify and standardize business processes that cut across organizational groups and
operational units (Davenport, 1998). The diffusion of cloud and web-based architectures inERP and CRM allow firms to give remote access to business processes also through
devices such as smartphones and tablets. In this regard, the deployment of flexible work
practices exhibits the same technological preconditions of job disaggregation and
offshoring trends (Apte and Mason, 1995).
The third mechanism regards the way ICT supports decision making in organization and
it can be manifested in different ways. First, ICT can support the introduction of a greater
formalization in decision making through the introduction of structured workflow logics.
In this regard, web-enabled group decision support systems combine communication,
computing, and decision support technologies to facilitate unstructured problem solving
and may thus support coordination and decision-making processes across virtual teams of
geographically dispersed workers (Majchrzak and Malhotra, 2003). Enterprise systems
facilitate flexible working by making decentralization of decision making more efficient.
For example, CRM systems can help firms being rapid and effective in responding to some
customers ’highly specific needs. Whereas enterprise systems may lead to greater
delegation of control and decision-making power to flexible workers, some Enterprise

can pursue a greater external orientation without the need to rely on bureaucratic and
hierarchical configurations.

Strategic posture in ICT usage
ICT-based external orientation . The concept of ICT-based external orientation refers to a
firm ’s use of information systems to facilitate and enhance its coordination with customers
and suppliers and to exploit new strategic opportunities that can be available in the externalenvironment (Sambamurthy et al. , 2003). Therefore, it refers to the use of ICT in those
departments that most closely interact with the external environment (i.e. sales, marketing,
logistics, and product development). Firms that are externally oriented in their ICT useoutcomes might be able to respond timely to changes in markets and in customers ’needs
and to enter into new market segments to exploit new business opportunities in rapid times
at a limited cost.
We expect that firms that have developed external orientation through ICT may be more
likely to adopt flexible work practices because they are more active in collaboration with
supply chain partners, and the use of flexible workers can facilitate collaboration and
inter-organizational learning. Inter-firm collaboration requires joint decision making
and richer communication based on social interaction between parties. The spatial mobilityof employees may provide an avenue for the sharing of richer information through
socialization and articulation (Nonaka and Takeuchi, 1995). In this way, in inter-firm
relationships, flexible workers may have a role complementary to ICT. Specifically, the roleof ICT in inter-organizational relationships is to enhance information exchange by creating
process modularity and standard electronic interfaces in supply chain architectures.
The supply chain architectures enabled by ICT allow the exchange of information that isvery generic and tied to operational processes, but it provides little support for the transfer
of idiosyncratic and privileged information that is needed for strategic direction and
innovation (Uzzi and Lancaster, 2003). Knowledge creation and innovation throughinter-organizational relationships occur when the transactions are embedded in social
attachment between parties and in strong inter-firm ties that can be effective for transfer of
complex knowledge. Coherent with this view, more innovative firms usually opt fororganizational structures with decentralized decision making to better access and process
external knowledge during the process of exploiting new strategic opportunities and to
respond to external signals (Foss et al., 2013). In SMEs engaged in product innovation with
their partners, decentralization can take the form of the spatial mobility of some employees
that work within their customers ’operational units for some period of time and are actively
engaged in the decision-making processes during the product development cycle.
These forms of mobility may be driven by the fact that physical distance hinders
coordination and knowledge transfer in product design and engineering activities(Allen, 1977). In other cases, decentralization may take the form of a greater empowerment
for dispersed sales employees in deciding product configurations and the related pricing or
for technical workers engaged in the after-sales services for products with a high degree ofcomplexity, such as industrial machineries. In these cases, the codification of business
processes through ERP or CRM systems allows dispersed workers to access the technical
and market knowledge that is required to accomplish their tasks with autonomy.
Overall, when SMEs develop a high external orientation through ICT, the need for the
mobility of some key roles in the organization can arise in more transactional processes for
those employees involved in operational activities (e.g. in sales or customer services formanaging customer relationships more promptly) or in innovation endeavors for technical

Extent of Enterprise 2.0 usage . The term Enterprise 2.0 refers to those technologies, such as
wikis, social media, and instant messaging tools, which can support lateral communication
and collaboration (McAfee, 2006). Most of these technologies are free and easy-to-use
instruments that have a broad diffusion and can thus offer SMEs a viable avenue (in terms
of usability and costs) to organize and manage knowledge and to support inter-firm
collaborations or intra firm-communication. For example, a dispersed team of salespeoplecan organize a group on a social network such as LinkedIn or Facebook to share experience

their unknown needs.
Compared to CRM and SCM systems, Enterprise 2.0 technologies offer a more effective
way of “hardwiring ”weak ties in lateral and inter-organizational communication
(Schneckenberg, 2009). These mechanisms can allow firms to support the wholeknowledge management cycle, from knowledge creation to knowledge storage and sharing.
Therefore, the broader the use of Enterprise 2.0 technologies in a firm ’s functional

firm can provide to its employees involved in flexible work practices:
H2. The broader the extent of the usage of Enterprise 2.0 tools in SMEs, the greater is
their use of flexible work practices.
Environmental conditions
The environmental factors driving flexible work diffusion can be essentially identified in the
vast literature on the two main factors that demand firms ’agility and flexibility (Volberda,
1998): the geographical complexity (scope covered by firms ’activities); environment
uncertainty (dynamism and product complexity).
Firms with a broader geographical scope are more externally oriented since they must
govern a greater variety of products and processes. They thus face a high complexity in the
distribution of their products/services and in the acquisition of relevant market information.
Environmental uncertainty can be conceptualized as the interplay between product
complexity (i.e. the number of product inputs) and the level of instability in market and
input conditions (dynamism). Firms facing a more uncertain environment need to devote
more resources to coordination with external partners and to environmental scanning, given
the greater complexity of their products and the greater instability in market and
technological conditions.
Geographical complexity (scope) . The geographical scope refers to the geographical extent
of a firm ’s operations. Firms facing a broader geographical scope are abler to acquire
resources and sell their products and services in international markets, implying that these928JEIM

Environmental uncertainty . Organization theory conceptualizes uncertainty in the firm ’s
environment as the interplay of two dimensions: dynamism and complexity (Galbraith,
1974). Dynamism refers to the degree of instability in an industry in relation to market
demand (e.g. changes in customer preference) and technological conditions (e.g. changes inproduct and process technologies). Product complexity is primarily related to the technical
complexity in the number of product components and their interdependencies in the product
architecture and thus can be referred to product complexity (Dale Stoel and Muhanna, 2009).
As the amount of uncertainty increases, the need for information processing increases
and firms must adopt integrating mechanisms, which increase their information processingcapabilities. Uncertain environments require enterprises to deliver offerings at a faster pace
and be ready to reinvent their roles in value chain networks, requiring firms to exchange
and assimilate a broad range of information from external sources (Galbraith, 1974). Mostinformation exchanges go beyond day-to-day operations and regard long-term dynamics
such as market and technology trends, changes in customer preferences, and future product
plans. To ensure the quality of information exchanges, SMEs may use flexible work forenvironmental scanning in the technology portfolios of partners and competitors and for
coordination with partners when a revision of product design is needed because of some
discontinuities in market demand and technological conditions. Therefore, dynamism andproduct complexity might influence SMEs ’needs for flexible work practices as a
decentralization mechanism to improve their external information processing and their
coordination with partners for environmental scanning and product innovation activities.Furthermore, past studies have shown that when products become more complex,
knowledge about interdependencies becomes more tacit (Cusumano and Selby, 1998) and
firms experience a decline in their ability to manage the dependencies among the variousproduct components. An increase in tacit knowledge thus makes it more difficult to effect
change when change is needed. This may imply that flexible workers can be needed for
integration with external partners as a better way to process and transfer knowledge that is
highly tacit and complex:
H4. Environmental uncertainty, seen as the interplay of environmental dynamism
and product complexity, is positively associated with the use of flexible work
practices in SMEs.

Measures
Dependent variables. Use of flexible work practices. We used a dichotomic variable to
distinguish firms that use a certain type of flexible work practice. We classified a firm as onethat had adopted flexible work practices if the following conditions were satisfied:
(1) The firm has at least one employee who works outside the main premises of the firm
at least once a week or when the need occurs. For this criterion, we followed the
definition used by (Corso et al., 2006) for geographically dispersed workers, and we
included employees who are regularly located outside the firm (e.g. sales agents) and
flexible workers who primarily work outside the firm but have their own desk at the
firm (e.g. consultants and maintenance technicians).
(2) During their work outside the firm ’s premises, flexible workers have access to the
firm ’s enterprise systems through the internet. Checking corporate e-mail remotely
was not considered a characterizing attribute. In this way, we were able to capture
those cases in which employees can be engaged in business processes remotely, and
we could restrict our focus to mobility that involves knowledge workers.
We excluded those situations in which employees work only from their home. In accordance
with recent studies on telework (Neirotti et al., 2011), we did not apply any minimum limit to the
normal amount of time spent in flexible work, and we thus also included in the generic categoryof flexible work any employee who works remotely “on-demand ”when a particular necessity
relating to the firm ’s business or the worker ’s private affairs occurs (Kim and Gong, 2016;
Jaakson and Kallaste, 2010). We thus focused on those forms of flexible work that occur on a
regular basis during the week, also including those forms instrumental to the activities of the
firm, even if they do not occur within a predetermined time frame every week.

Use of flexible work practices
Descriptive statistics (Table III) reveal three key findings about the dimensions of flexible

Discussion
The analysis of the contextual dimension of flexible work offers a support to the idea that the
use of flexible work practices occurs in response to SMEs ’need to improve their external
orientation and their need to process external information. Specifically, the data show that the
use of ICT in support of external orientation toward customers, suppliers, and new markets is
often accompanied by the use of flexible work practices motivated by reasons of improving
operational agility such as serving customers more promptly, or achieving a better level ofintegration and collaboration with them in product development (FWT 3.1 and FWT 3.2).
Instead, when SMEs cover a broader geographical complexity, their use of flexible work
mainly aims to bring new competencies into the organization (FWT 4.1 and FWT 4.2), which
is another way through which SMEs may pursue their organizational agility.
Under situations of environmental uncertainty, SMEs tend to use flexible work for
operational reasons related to their coordination needs with partners (FWT 3.1 and FWT 3.2),
whereas they do not show a higher tendency to use flexible work to reconfigure their
competencies (FWT 4.1 and FWT 4.2). The positive correlation between environmental
uncertainty and flexible work for operational reasons is coherent with the fact that in contexts
of dynamism and product complexity, SMEs need to process a wider range of information
(Galbraith, 1974) and to exchange idiosyncratic and tacit knowledge with partners (Malhotra
and Majchrzak, 2005). In these settings, flexible work practices can represent the avenue for
social attachment and coordination that is required for SMEs to achieve knowledge
elaboration and joint decision making.

Conclusions
Implications for theory
The theoretical insights and the empirical findings discussed in this paper contribute to a
more comprehensive view of flexible work that enriches its “classical ”vision of as an
organizational practice triggered by the need of a dispersed workforce to “stay closer ”to
customers or to some firm ’s assets. In this way, we were able to find a link between the use
of flexible work practices and SMEs ’strategic and the competitive scenario, providing four
types of findings, essentially:
(1) the use of flexible work practices involve s a limited percentage of SMEs, and in those
organizations that use this practice it in volves a limited percentage of employees;
(2) SMEs use flexible work practices when they are more capable of using ICT and
Enterprise 2.0 technologies to improve their external orientation;
(3) being a means of external orientation, flexible work practices are more frequent in
environments where more external orientation is needed, namely, uncertainty
industries characterized by more environmental dynamism, product complexity,
and broader geographical complexity; and
(4) the influence of the strategic posture in ICT usage and of environmental conditions
is in some cases different according to the typology of flexible work chosen by
SMEs: flexible work for cheaper input costs, flexible work for operational drivers,
flexible work for strategic drivers, and flexible work for individual motivations.
The rarity of flexible work as an organizational practice in SMEs comes with another rare
condition, i.e., the use of ICT and Enterprise 2.0 technologies to enhance their external
orientation. Flexible work is thus an organizational practice that is complementary to the
usage of ICT in support of market and supply chain relationships.
In highlighting these trends, our study echoes research on the role of ICT in enabling
firms ’greater reliance on market relationships (Malone, 2004), and is consistent with how
scholars viewed ICT in the 1990s: an enabling factor for new forms of industry value chains
that represent “an important ‘bootstrapping mechanism ’of long-predicted shifts from
hierarchical to market forms of organizations, at least in professionalized sectors of the939Flexible work
practices
Downloaded from http://www.emerald.com/jeim/article-pdf/30/6/922/1384627/jeim-04-2016-0090.pdf by University College London user on 30 May 2026
economy ”(Pickering and King, 1995, p. 479). In this view, flexible work can be one of the
intermediary forms between hierarchical and market-based solutions that can provide an
avenue for SMEs ’agility. Flexible workers can be –from an information processing view
(Galbraith, 1974) –a bridge between SMEs and their partners and, from a strategic
perspective, agile and flexible mechanisms to use the external labor market to deploy
specialized competencies when needed.
The view of ICT as a force reshaping work, value chains, and organizations is consistent
with our evidence on flexible work and leads the way to conceptualize many types of flexible
work practices used in SMEs as one of the elements of new organizational forms based on
low bureaucracy and mobility of part of the workforce, aimed at achieving a great
organizational flexibility in complex and uncertain environments. One of the elements found
in this study that could support this view is the limited diffusion that flexible work practices
have among SMEs. Firms using flexible work are likely to operate in dynamic and complex
environments where they address a broader geographical complexity and they take
advantage of Enterprise 2.0 tools and of the support that ICT can give to their market and
supply chain relationships. This support of ICT to external orientation is another rare
attribute in SMEs and may thus suggest that these firms prevalently use flexible work
practice as one of the micro-foundations of new –and yet scarcely diffused –organizational
forms that they develop to address uncertainty and variety in their external environments.
These arguments suggest that by using the tradi tional theoretical position on flexible work
as a workplace innovation that gives employees and firms more flexibility (Chung et al., 2014),
we may risk seeing “the trees and not the forest ”in the rise of new forms of loosely coupled
organizations (Sahaym et al., 2007) or “semi-formal ”organizations (Biancani et al., 2014), thereby
missing the broader implications that flexible technologies have for the reconfiguration of value
chains, firms ’organizational boundaries, and workers ’life in their organizational settings.
Implications for practice
This study shows SMEs ’managers that the technological preconditions needed to deploy
flexible work practices are not complex because they consist of a set of ICT tools –like
Enterprise 2.0 solutions –that are objects of commoditizatio n and consumerization dynamics.
The complexity rather lies in the way firms may design work and relationships with customers
and suppliers to allow new forms of organizational agility. This may primarily explain the
limited diffusion of flex ible work practices in SMEs. Furthermore, SMEs ’managers should be
aware of the fact that flexible forms of work can be chosen given different reasons, namely, for
incurring on cheaper input costs, for operational drivers, for strategic drivers, or for employees ’
motivations. However, they have to take into account that under different conditions each of
these flexible work typologies can be chosen. For e xample, flexible work for strategic drivers is
more likely under high geographical complexity, or flexible work for operational drivers is more
likely under high levels of dynamism and product complexity.
Under uncertain and complex environments, SMEs should increase their external
information processing with ICT and organizational practices that support the latitude of
employees involved in boundary spanning with respect to where, when, and how they work.
Future research
Coherent with the idea that flexible work can be part of a more articulated organizational
form that SMEs adopt to have a greater externa l orientation in a more uncertain external
environment, future research should assess m ore in depth the benefits that flexible work
can generate, and how firms design information systems, practices, and tasks for
dealing with the need of mobility. Furthermore, future research should also focus on
investigating flexible work practice i n other countries in order to verify the
generalizability of the findings.940JEIM
