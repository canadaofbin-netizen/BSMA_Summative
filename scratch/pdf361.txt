Abstract
Purpose  – Social  networking  services  (SNS)  empower  users  with a robust  capability  to connect  with others  
and manage  their  social  relationships.  However,  as the size of users’  social  networks  increases,  coupled  with 
the inherent  boundary-spanning  technical  features  of SNS,  users  are faced  with unprecedented  role stresses.  
This,  in turn,  leads  to maladaptive  lurking  decisions.  This  study  delves  into the mechanism  of this technology-
induced  decision-making  process  among  SNS users.
Design/methodology/approach  – Survey  data were  collected  from  491 Chinese  WeChat  Moment  users.  
The model  and hypotheses  testing  were  conducted  using  SmartPLS  4.0.
Findings  – Our findings  indicate  that both social  network  size and boundary  spanning  have  a positive  
influence  on role conflict  and role overload.  Both  role conflict  and role overload  significantly  contribute  to SNS 
fatigue,  which  further  intensifies  users’  lurking  intention.  Furthermore,  SNS fatigue  fully  mediated  the 
relationship  between  role conflict  and lurking  intention,  and partially  mediated  the relationship  between  role 
overload  and lurking  intention.
Originality/value  – Our study  offers  a fresh  viewpoint  for comprehending  lurking  behaviors  on SNS,  
furnishing  practical  insights  for platform  providers.  Additionally,  it paves  the way for future  research  into the 
deeper  mechanisms  driving  SNS lurking  behaviors,  by providing  a novel  construct  (i.e. boundary  spanning)  to 
distinguish  and measure  the unique  social  environment  of SNS.
Keywords  SNS,  Lurking  intention,  Boundary  spanning,  Role stresses,  SNS fatigue

The pervasive  use of SNS in daily  life has significantly  expanded  and complicated  
people’s  social  networks.  Many  offline  social  connections  are transitioning  online,  with new 
ones being  continually  established.  As of 2015,  the average  number  of friends  per WeChat

(Tencent,  2015). WeChat  has recently  expanded  the maximum  number  of contacts  a user can 
have  from  5,000  to 10,000.  This  enhancement  reflects  the platform’s  commitment  to adapting  
its network  capabilities  to meet  the growing  demands  and needs  of its users.  As users  expand  
their  social  networks  on SNS,  their  social  circles  become  increasingly  varied  and diverse.  
Notably,  WeChat  has become  a hub for most  people’s  social  circles,  encompassing  relatives,  
friends,  supervisors,  colleagues,  customers  and many  others.  When  engaging  in social  
activities  across  these  diverse  circles,  users  must  adapt  to different  roles  and behave  
according  to the expectations  associated  with each role (Zhang  et al., 2011). Consequently,  a 
single  user may juggle  multiple,  and often  conflicting,  roles  within  their  various  social  circles  
on an SNS (Zhang  et al., 2011). For instance,  a user might  be a colleague  or a boss within  a 
work-related  network,  while  simultaneously  being  a parent  or a child  within  their  family  
network.  In traditional  offline  social  environments,  temporal  and spatial  constraints  limit 
users  to playing  a single  role that fits their  specific  situation,  such  as only being  a colleague  
while  at the workplace.  However,  these  constraints  diminished  on SNS (Rui and Stefanone,  
2013), leading  to a blurring  of boundaries  between  various  social  roles.  As a result,  users  
often  must  navigate  multiple  roles  simultaneously  on SNS.  This  phenomenon,  known  as 
boundary  spanning,  is common  among  SNS users  and can lead to increased  role stress,  
including  role conflict  and role overload  (Zhang  et al., 2011; Liu et al., 2020).
Although  current  SNS,  such  as WM and Facebook,  enables  users  to categorize  their  
contacts  into distinct  groups  to mitigate  potential  role stresses,  the effectiveness  of this 
mechanism  is undermined  by its boundary-spanning  nature.  The requirement  for users  to 
select  continuously  and manually  who can view  their  posts  can be an exhaustive  process,  
particularly  as the size and complexity  of their  contact  lists increase.  This  task demands  
considerable  effort  and can lead to user fatigue.  In practice,  many  users  do not rigorously  
organize  all their  contacts  into specific  groups.  Consequently,  lurking  has become  a less time-
consuming  and labor-intensive  coping  strategy  for managing  these  circumstances.  Lurking,  
a type of inactive  online  user behavior  characterized  by non-posting  (Edelmann,  2013), is a 
common  phenomenon  on SNS (Hildebrand,  2017). This  is evidenced  by the fact that more  
than 80%  of the contents  on SNS are created  by <20%  of the active  users,  a distribution  that 
aligns  with Pareto’s  80/20  law (Matei  and Bruno,  2015). Users’  active  participation  is the key 
to maintaining  the prosperity  of SNS,  which  generates  new knowledge  and values  for 
businesses  (Jansen  et al., 2009), the economy  (Asur  and Huberman,  2010) and public  politics  
(Tumasjan  et al., 2010). For SNS providers,  users’  active  participation  significantly  
contribute  to their  sustainability.  For SNS users,  lurking,  as a maladaptive  coping  strategy,  
can reduce  their  short-term  stress  at the expense  of increased  long-term  stress  (Wisniewski  
et al., 2014). As such,  it is crucial  to understand  the underlying  mechanisms  of how to increase  
their  user engagement,  especially  for lurkers,  and to maintain  the well-being  of users.  
Regrettably,  few information  systems  (IS) studies  have  delved  into the mechanisms  of 
lurking  behavior  within  the context-specific  cues of SNS,  particularly  the combined  impacts  
of users’  social  network  size and the boundary-spanning  features  of SNS.ITP

Existing  research  on lurking  has often  justified  the phenomenon  as reasonable,  
attributing  it to personal  traits  such  as being  a loafer  or freeloader  (Curien  et al., 2006), 
users’  low self-efficiency  (i.e. being  a newbie)  (Bogdan  and Bowers,  2007; Preece  and 
Shneiderman,  2009), or the fact that users’  information  needs  can be satisfied  without  posting  
(Preece  et al., 2008; Nonnecke  and Preece,  2001; Nonnecke  et al., 2004). However,  lurking  can 
often  be a maladaptive  coping  strategy  for SNS users,  especially  when  their  extensive  social  
network  size intersects  with the boundary-spanning  technical  feature  (Liu et al., 2020). 
In such  situations,  users  may alleviate  their  short-term  role stresses  at the cost of increased  
long-term  psychological  discomfort.  Contrary  to the behaviors  associated  with contributing  
content,  lurking  behavior  may negatively  affect  users’  self-expression  (Shao,  2009), 
relationship  maintenance  (Lee et al., 2014), impression  management  (Kramer  and Winter,  
2008) and could  even  result  in financial  repercussions  for SNS (Park  et al., 2016).
To bridge  the existing  research  gap, this study  delves  into the technology-driven  decision-
making  process  of users  within  the context  of SNS.  It aims  to answer  the following  research  
questions:  (1) What  are the specific  contextual  cues that lead to maladaptive  lurking  intention  
by users  within  the SNS environment?  (2) How  do these  specific  contextual  cues trigger  
perceived  role stresses  for users  on SNS?  (3) How  do role stresses  influence  the lurking  
intention  of SNS users?  To address  these  questions,  this study  anchored  in the burnout  
theoretical  model  to investigate  the impact  of SNS context-specific  cues on users’  lurking  
intention  from  a role perspective.  Specifically,  we considered  the specific  cues within  the SNS 
context  leveraging  the social  and technical  aspects.  The context-specific  cues of SNS will 
induce  users’  cognitive  pressures  (i.e. role conflict  and role overload)  and emotional  fatigue,  
which  ultimately  lead to their  lurking  intention.
This study  offers  three  significant  contributions  to the burgeoning  literature  on SNS 
lurking.  Firstly,  we examined  the context-specific  cues from  both social  and technical  
aspects.  By integrating  these  two aspects,  we can delve  into the impacts  of role stresses  
engendered  by the distinctive  social  environment  on SNS,  thus expanding  our 
comprehension  of platform-based  features  within  the social  context  of SNS.  Secondly,  we 
identified  boundary  spanning  as a second-order  construct,  comprising  two dimensions:  
parallelism  and reprocessability.  This  provides  a novel  construct  to distinguish  and measure  
the boundary-spanning  activities  on SNS.  Finally,  anchored  in the burnout  theoretical  model  
and integrated  with a role perspective,  this study  stands  as one of the pioneering  efforts  in 
elucidating  the mechanism  behind  the maladaptive  lurking  intention  observed  among  SNS 
users.  This contribution  further  enriches  the SNS lurking  literature,  aiding  in a deeper  
comprehension  of the decision-making  process  behind  users’  lurking  intentions.  The 
integration  of role theory  and the burnout  theory  also theoretically  extends  these  two 
theories.
The structure  of the remainder  of this paper  is as follows.  Section  2 introduces  the relevant  
theoretical  background.  This  is followed  by the presentation  of the proposed  research  model  
and hypotheses  in Section  3. The research  methodology  and data analysis  are detailed  in 
Sections  4 and 5, respectively.  Subsequently,  the research  findings,  contributions,  
implications  and limitations  are discussed  in Section  6. Finally,  Section  7 gives  a 
conclusion  of this study.
2. Literature  review  and theoretical  background
2.1 SNS lurking
Lurking  is a term  associated  with non-posting  behavior  (Edelmann,  2013), defined  as a form  
of inactive  online  user behavior  characterized  by infrequent  posting  (Rafaeli  et al., 2004), 
being  silent  (Leshed,  2005) or a lack of contribution  (Phang  et al., 2015). This phenomenon  is 
prevalent  in both online  communities  and SNS,  as user behavior  online  often  adheres  toInformation

Pareto’s  law (Matei  and Bruno,  2015). Extensive  research  has been  conducted  on this 
phenomenon  within  the context  of online  communities,  identifying  four primary  reasons  for 
lurking:  environmental  influence,  personal  preference,  the relationship  between  the 
individual  and the group  and security  considerations  (Sun et al., 2014). Specifically,  Preece  
et al. (2008)  suggested  the top five reasons  for lurking:  the lack of a need  to post,  the desire  to 
learn  more  about  the group  before  participating,  the belief  that their  silence  is helpful,  
difficulties  with the software  (i.e. poor usability),  and a dislike  of the group  dynamics  or a 
sense  that the community  is not a good  fit for them.
Current  research  on lurking  within  SNS has limitations,  particularly  in distinguishing  the 
fundamental  differences  between  SNS and online  communities  (see Appendix  1). These  
studies  often  overlook  the impact  of changes  in the social  environment,  caused  by technical  
characteristics,  on users’  intentions  to lurk.  Furthermore,  previous  investigations  into the 
lurking  phenomenon  have  yielded  conflicting  viewpoints  without  identifying  its underlying  
mechanism.  Some  researchers  posit  lurking  as a positive  behavior  that should  be encouraged  
(Takahashi  et al., 2002; Edelmann  et al., 2017), while  others  perceive  it negatively  (Mo and 
Coulson,  2010). Interestingly,  users  sometimes  choose  to lurk due to fear of negative  
outcomes  (Ortiz  et al., 2018) or discomfort  in online  social  environments  (Liu et al., 2020). This 
ambiguity  may arise  from  the dual nature  of technology  itself;  technostress  can be both 
positive  and negative,  possessing  a “dark  side”  in addition  to a “bright  side”  (Califf  et al., 
2020; Breward  et al., 2017; Tarafdar  et al., 2014). The objective  of this study  is to conceptualize  
a technostress  process  that encompasses  the negative  components  of technostress,  which  are 
embedded  in users’  maladaptive  lurking  behaviors.  Currently,  the impact  of the SNS distinct  
social  environment  on users’  maladaptive  lurking  intentions  remains  unclear,  particularly  
concerning  the integration  of users’  diverse  social  networks  and the unique  boundary-
spanning  media  features.
SNS differ  from  traditional  online  communities  in several  distinctive  features  (Liu et al., 
2020). Primarily,  SNS are designed  to facilitate  the creation  of social  networks  and establish  
an individual’s  online  presence,  whereas  traditional  online  communities  primarily  aim to 
enhance  one’s  understanding  of a specific  topic  (Zhu and Chang,  2014). Thus,  users  in an 
online  community  are likely  to have  similar  goals,  rules,  and interests,  and therefore  share  a 
common  group  identity,  which  refers  to the common  cognitive  state  of users  (Sun et al., 2014). 
On SNS,  users  form  connections  primarily  because  they are interested  in the individual  
behind  the profile  and wish  to maintain  reciprocal  relationships.  Moreover,  users’  social  
networks  on SNS are highly  diverse,  allowing  them  to assume  as many  identities  as the 
distinct  networks  of relationships  in which  they participate  and play various  roles.  From  a 
technical  perspective,  SNS mix all of a user’s  social  roles  together,  which  can potentially  
impede  their  online  presence  and ultimately  affect  their  level  of online  participation.  As a 
result,  the motivations  for users  to visit and post on SNS differ  from  those  in traditional  
online  communities  (Zhu and Chang,  2014). This  also implies  that the mechanism  of lurking  
on SNS should  differ  from  those  in traditional  online  communities.  To fill this research  gap, 
this paper  aims  to explore  the mechanism  of how the social  environment  of SNS influences  
users’  lurking  intentions.
2.2 Role theory
Role theory  (Kahn  et al., 1964), one of the earlier  models  focuses  on the person’s  role at work  
as a major  source  of occupational  stress.  A role is generally  defined  as a person’s  perception  
of himself/herself  when  he/she  relates  to the surrounding  environment  and people  whom  he/ 
she interacts  with (Hall, 1971). As an example,  an individual  may serve  as a father  in a family,  
as a subordinate  at work,  or as a friend  with some  familiar  people.  Role stresses  or demands  
are seen as coming  from  organizational  and environmental  factors,  which  may adverselyITP

social-psychological  factors  in the contemporary  environment  have  major  effects  on the 
physical  and psychological  well-being  of the person  (Jr., 1968).  This  study  aims  to investigate  
how the unique  context-specific  cues of SNS can induce  users’  psychological  burden  from  a 
role perspective,  subsequently  leading  to lurking  intention  on these  platforms.  Therefore,  
role theory  serves  as a fitting  theoretical  paradigm  to elucidate  the mechanisms  underlying  
this topic.
Given  the distinctive  social  dynamics  of SNS (Zhang  et al., 2011; Liu et al., 2020), we 
employ  a socio-technical  perspective  to analyze  the context-specific  cues.  SNS are socio-
technically  constructed,  where  the social  elements  pertain  to users’  social  networks,  and the 
technical  elements  relate  to the characteristics  of the media.  As depicted  in Figure  1, the 
pervasive  use of SNS in daily  life has significantly  expanded  people’s  social  networks.  Many  
offline  social  connections  are transitioning  online,  and new relationships  are being  formed.  
As the number  of social  connections  on SNS grows,  users’  social  circles  become  increasingly  
diverse,  encompassing  relatives,  friends,  acquaintances,  coworkers  and many  others.  
Concurrently,  SNS offer  users  a unique  technical  environment  where  all social  circles  coexist,  
thereby  blurring  the boundaries  of their  various  social  circles.  In such  circumstances,  
individuals  often  engage  in frequent  boundary-spanning  activities,  which  can lead to issues  
with role pressure.
In this paper,  we adopt  a socio-technical  perspective  to address  SNS context-specific  cues,  
considering  the size of the social  network  as a social  cue and boundary  spanning  as a technical  
cue. Boundary  spanning  refers  to interdepartmental  and external  communication;  it involves  
having  a large  role set and requires  interactions  with many  people,  both inside  and outside  the 
organization,  who have  diverse  needs  and expectations  (Lysonski,  1985; Goolsby,  1992). 
Specifically,  we examine  boundary  spanning  as a second-order  construct,  encompassing  two 
first-order  constructs:  parallelism  and reprocessability . Parallelism  refers  to the degree  to which  
signals  from  multiple  senders  can be simultaneously  transmitted  over the medium  (Dennis  and 
Valacich,  2008). Media  with high parallelism  allow  users  to send and receive  messages  to and 
from  multiple  participants.  High  parallelism  media  allow  users  to send and receive  messages  to 
multiple  participants  (He and Yang,  2016; Tang  et al., 2013). This feature  enables  users  to 
engage  in social  interactions  regardless  of their location.  On the other  hand,  reprocessability  is

Moreover,  having  a large  social  network  size often  results  in diverse  social  circles,  and the 
boundary-spanning  technical  features  can blend  these  circles  together.  Consequently,  users  
need  to frequently  engage  in boundary-spanning  activities  to facilitate  social  interactions  on 
SNS.  When  communicating  across  different  social  circles,  users  must  adapt  their  behavior  
and roles  to meet  the unique  criteria  each circle  uses to evaluate  its members  (Zhang  et al., 
2011). Each  role comes  with its own set of expectations  that users  are expected  to fulfill  (Jr., 
1968).  However,  these  expectations  can vary  greatly  and may even  conflict  between  different  
roles  (Biddle,  1979). This  makes  it challenging  for users  to navigate  the frequent  transitions  
between  roles,  potentially  leading  to discomfort  and cognitive  or emotional  strain.  The role 
transitions  become  more  difficult  when  a person  moves  among  more  segmented  roles  
(Ashforth  et al., 2000). Role conflict  and role overload  are two consequences  of role transitions  
during  these  boundary-spanning  activities  on SNS (Zhang  et al., 2011).
Role conflict  is defined  as the incompatibility  and incongruity  in the expectations  
associated  with a specific  role (Guimaraes  and Igbaria,  1992). As users  engage  in social  
conversations  on SNS,  they often  conduct  boundary-spanning  activities  across  multiple  
roles.  However,  the expectations  from  each role are often  inconsistent  and conflicting.  These  
intertwined  role expectations  can clash  with each other  and cannot  be simultaneously  
fulfilled,  thereby  increasing  the likelihood  of role conflicts.  For instance,  if a doctoral  student  
shares  a travel  photo  on SNS,  his friends  might  “like”  it, whereas  his supervisor  might  
encourage  him to focus  more  on completing  his doctoral  thesis.
Role overload  refers  to the scenario  where  the various  role expectations  placed  on an 
individual  exceed  the resources  they have  available  to manage  them  appropriately  (Bostrom,  
1980). Given  the limited  cognitive  resources  a user can process  within  a specific  timeframe  
(Carpenter  et al., 1994), an increase  in role overload  can lead to individuals  feeling  
overwhelmed  by information  processing  (Ho et al., 2003). For example,  a user may be 
engaged  in multiple  conversations  with diverse  groups  on SNS,  assuming  multiple  roles  
simultaneously,  such  as a friend,  spouse  and colleague.  Therefore,  actively  participating  in 
all SNS conversations  without  making  errors  or adjustments  can pose a significant  challenge  
to the user.
Role conflict  and role overload  can arise  from  diverse  social  networks,  leading  role 
incumbents  to inadequately  manage  their  behaviors  (Peterson,  1995). When  social  
conversations  involve  conflicting  roles  on SNS,  a user must  navigate  the challenge  of 
satisfying  one person’s  expectations  while  fulfilling  another’s.  Moreover,  when  the number  
of overlapping  roles  becomes  excessive,  the social  conversations  can overwhelm  the user.  
Consequently,  role conflict  and role overload  can make  it challenging  for users  to adjust  their  
behaviors  appropriately  on SNS,  causing  them  to exert  more  effort  to meet  others’  
expectations.  This  can lead to users  feeling  emotionally  drained,  a state  referred  to as SNS 
fatigue  (Bright  et al., 2015; Dhir et al., 2018; Karr-Wisniewski  and Ying,  2010). Role theory  
introduces  a new perspective  to explain  how SNS contextual-specific  cues lead to users’  
perceptions  of role stresses,  but cannot  adequately  explain  users’  lurking  intention  because  
of its weakness  in elaborating  on users’  motivational  behavior  (Miles,  2012). Thus,  burnout  
theory  serves  as another  theoretical  basis  for this study  to explain  the psychological  and

2.3 Burnout  theory
Burnout  theory  has been especially  useful  in uncovering  the role of work-specific  role stressors  
in predicting  job burnout  and satisfaction  (Bacharach  et al., 1991; Maslach  and Jackson,  1981; 
Maslach  and Goldberg,  1998; Jackson  et al., 1987; Jackson  and Schuler,  1985). The theory  
suggests  that burnout  occurs  when  certain  valued  resources  are lost, are inadequate  to meet  
demands,  or do not yield  the anticipated  returns.  Certain  behavioral  and attitudinal  outcomes  
are likely  to occur  as a result  of resource  loss and burnout.  The major  outcomes  include  
behavioral  coping  responses;  turnover  intentions;  and erosion  of organizational  commitment,  
job involvement  and job satisfaction  (Kahill,  1988). Specifically,  Maslach  et al. (1996)  provided  a 
general  model  of burnout,  with major  antecedents  and consequences,  which  stated  that work  
overload  and personal  conflict  are the major  antecedents  of burnout,  and the consequences  of 
burnout  are seen most  consistently  in various  forms  of job withdrawal  (i.e. decreased  
commitment,  job dissatisfaction,  turnover  and absenteeism).  This model  introduces  a 
theoretical  framework  that sheds  light on how role conflict  and role overload  lead to significant  
exhaustion  among  SNS users,  which  in turn,  leading  to their lurking  intentions.
According  to Maslach  and Jackson  (1981), burnout  consists  of three  dimensions:  emotional  
exhaustion,  depersonalization  and diminished  personal  accomplishment.  Emotional  
exhaustion  as a form  of strain  is directly  affected  by role conflict  and quantitative  
workload,  whereas  depersonalization  as a form  of defensive  coping  and personal  
accomplishment  as a form  of self-evaluation  are indirectly  affected  by emotional  exhaustion  
(Jackson  et al., 1987). Thus,  this study  employed  the concept  of SNS fatigue  to depict  users’  
exhausted  emotions  on SNS,  which  has been adapted  to the field of information  systems  
research  (Dhir et al., 2018; Zhang  et al., 2016; Ravindran  et al., 2014; Fan et al., 2021). SNS fatigue  
refers  to a subjective,  self-evaluated  feeling  of exhaustion  stemming  from  the use of SNS 
(Zhang  et al., 2016; Ravindran  et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2016; Fan et al., 2021). Existing  literature  
primarily  explores  the precursors  of SNS fatigue,  focusing  on aspects  such as self-efficacy,  
privacy  concerns  and perceived  usefulness  (Bright  et al., 2015), system  feature  overload,  
information  overload  and social  overload  (Zhang  et al., 2016), as well as compulsive  use and 
fear of missing  out (Dhir et al., 2018), among  others.  However,  the existing  literature  exhibits  a 
pronounced  gap regarding  the exploration  of the causes  and consequences  of SNS fatigue,  
especially  from  the perspective  of the various  roles  embedded  within  users’  social  networks.
Interactions  within  the social  environment  of SNS significantly  influence  users’  behavior  
on these  platforms.  Role conflict  and role overload  can pose challenges  for users,  making  it 
difficult  for them  to adjust  their  behaviors  on SNS appropriately.  This can lead to negative  
evaluations  from  others  and a failure  to maintain  their  online  relationships  and images  (Liu 
et al., 2020). The cognitive  stress  from  managing  multiple  roles  can consume  significant  time 
and energy,  leading  to emotional  fatigue  among  SNS users.  Additionally,  experiences  of 
fatigue  may ultimately  drive  a user to withdraw  from  a stressful  environment,  such  as by 
reducing  or suspending  activity  (Ravindran  et al., 2014). Lurking  thus emerges  as a prevalent  
form  of withdrawal  for SNS users  when  dealing  with such  circumstances.  In this context,  we 
specifically  chose  lurking  intention  as a proxy  for lurking  behavior,  given  that lurking  is a 
covert  behavior  that is challenging  to observe.  Furthermore,  in the field of information  
systems,  the notion  that behavioral  intentions  determine  behavior  is widely  recognized  
(Ajzen,  1985). As per the lurking  literature  review  mentioned  earlier,  lurking  intention  is 
defined  as the tendency  to reduce  or even  cease  posting  content  on SNS.

3.1 The influence  of SNS context-specific  cues on role stresses
As SNS continue  to rise in popularity,  users  are increasingly  shifting  their  social  connections  
from  offline  to online  platforms,  while  also forming  new connections.  This  shift has led to a 
continuous  expansion  of their  social  network  size. For example,  the average  number  of 
friends  per WeChat  user is approximately  128, a figure  that is expected  to grow  as more  users  
join the workforce  (Tencent,  2015). WeChat  has recently  expanded  the maximum  number  of 
contacts  a user can have  from  5,000  to 10,000.  This enhancement  reflects  the platform’s  
commitment  to adapting  its network  capabilities  to meet  the growing  demands  and needs  of 
its users.  As the number  of contacts  increases,  the diversity  within  users’  social  circles  on 
SNS also grows.  Notably,  WeChat  has become  a hub for most  people’s  social  circles,  
encompassing  relatives,  friends,  supervisors,  colleagues,  customers  and many  others.  People  
need  to take on different  roles  in different  social  environments  (social  circles),  and each role is 
associated  with a different  set of role expectations  (Zhang  et al., 2011). Role expectations  are 
frequently  inconsistent  and excessive,  with the demands  placed  on the individual  exceeding  
the available  resources  to manage  them  effectively.  Thus.  this interplay  of diverse  role 
expectations  can lead to role stresses  (Zhang  et al., 2011; Liu and Wang,  2018). One form  of 
this stress  is role conflict,  which  arises  from  the incompatibility  between  different  role 
expectations  and the challenges  in reconciling  them  (Guimaraes  and Igbaria,  1992). Another  
form  is role overload,  which  is caused  by an excess  of role expectations  (Bostrom,  1980).

H1b.  Social  network  size positively  affects  users’  perceived  role overload  on SNS.
SNS offer  users  a unique  environment  that spans  across  various  social  boundaries,  bringing  
together  diverse  social  circles  (Hampton  et al., 2011). This allows  users  to engage  in social

3.2 The influence  of role stresses  on SNS fatigue
The social  environment  of SNS increases  the likelihood  of users  engaging  in multiple  
open  conversations  (Zhang  et al., 2011). However,  when  these  conversations  involve  
conflicting  roles,  they overwhelm  the users  faster.  Role conflict  arises  when  the 
expectations  from  different  roles  are contradictory  and cannot  be simultaneously  
fulfilled.  To maintain  relationships  and establish  a positive  online  image,  users  often  
need  to invest  significant  time and energy  to balance  these  conflicting  behaviors.  For 
instance,  a user may be involved  in multiple  social  conversations  with  different  groups  
on SNS at the same  time.  Actively  engaging  in all these  conversations  without  making  
errors  or adjustments  can pose  a real challenge.  Consequently,  users  who frequently  find 
themselves  in situations  of role conflict  may feel emotionally  drained  due to excessive  
energy  consumption  and anxiety  over  their  ability  to manage  their  social  
conversations  properly  (Xiao  et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2021). This  leads  to the following  
hypothesis:
H3. Role conflict  positively  affects  users’  perceived  SNS fatigue.

switch  between  different  roles,  each with its own set of expectations  (Ashforth  et al., 2000). 
This  is particularly  true on SNS,  where  a user can be engaged  in multiple  conversations  with 
diverse  groups  of people  simultaneously  (Zhang  et al., 2011). Given  that people  have  a limited  
amount  of cognitive  resources  available  to process  information  within  a specific  timeframe  
(Carpenter  et al., 1994), an increase  in role overload  can result  in individuals  feeling  
overwhelmed  (Ho et al., 2003). This  is because  role overload  consumes  a significant  portion  of 
a user’s  limited  cognitive  resources,  making  it challenging  for them  to cope (Ho et al., 2003). 
Consequently,  this can lead to a sense  of fatigue  associated  with the use of SNS.  This leads  to 
the following  hypothesis:

3.3 The influence  of role stresses  and SNS fatigue  on lurking  intention
In the context  of role conflict  on SNS,  users  often  find themselves  juggling  two or more  
conflicting  activities.  This  makes  it difficult  for them  to manage  and control  information  
during  social  interactions  (Zhang  et al., 2011). At times,  they may need  to violate  a rule or 
norm  to accomplish  their  desired  tasks,  or they may feel compelled  to suppress  their  genuine  
feelings  and thoughts  to reconcile  the conflicting  activities  (Rutner  et al., 2008). If their  actions  
are approved  by some  but disapproved  by others,  they may experience  anxiety  due to the 
fear of anticipated  sanctions  or negative  reactions  from  others  (Strauman  and Higgins,  1987; 
Levinson  and Rodebaugh,  2013). Individuals  prone  to anxiety  often  avoid  social  interactions  
when  they are linked  with stress  (Kashdan,  2007) and they resort  to safety  behaviors  to 
mitigate  their  anxiety  (Rachman  et al., 2008). Consequently,  out of fear of miscommunication  
in social  interactions,  anxious  users  tend to limit self-disclosure  to prevent  negative  social  
outcomes,  which  is a part of their  safety  behaviors  (Rapee  and Heimberg,  1997; Hong  et al., 
2015). As a result,  lurking  becomes  a safer  alternative  for users  on SNS,  allowing  them  to 
avoid  active  participation  in conversations  (Osatuyi,  2015). This leads  to the following  
hypothesis:
H5. Role conflict  positively  affects  users’  lurking  intention  on SNS.
Users  typically  engage  with a variety  of social  circles  on SNS,  each of which  comes  with its 
own set of roles  and expectations.  However,  when  the number  of roles  from  these  multiple  
networks  becomes  overwhelming,  users  may experience  role overload  (Zhang  et al., 2011). 
This is due to cognitive  limitations  that restrict  the amount  of information  users  can process  
within  a given  timeframe  (Carpenter  et al., 1994). To manage  these  excessive  role 
expectations,  users  are compelled  to invest  more  efforts  and resources  into their  SNS 
interactions.  This  significant  investment  of time and energy  can adversely  affect  their  daily  
lives,  potentially  causing  disruptions  to their  work  and other  important  activities  (Zhang  
et al., 2016; Ravindran  et al., 2014). To mitigate  these  disruptions  and negative  impacts,  users  
may need  to distance  themselves  from  excessive  social  interaction.  In this context,  lurking  
becomes  a viable  solution  for users  to alleviate  the stress  associated  with role overload  on 
SNS.  This  leads  us to the following  hypothesis:
H6. Role overload  positively  affects  users’  lurking  intention  on SNS.
SNS fatigue  is a subjective  and negative  sensation  characterized  by exhaustion,  boredom  and 
burnout,  which  stems  from  excessive  SNS activities  (Zhang  et al., 2016). This is particularly  
prevalent  when  users’  social  interactions  on SNS frequently  involve  role stresses.  To mitigate  
the physical  and psychological  strain  induced  by such  negative  emotions,  individuals  often  
attempt  to alter their  circumstances  (Lavee  et al., 1987). This could  involve  discontinuing  
stress-inducing  behaviors  or adopting  social  avoidance  strategies.  Lurking,  a form  of social  
avoidance  strategy,  can diminish  users’  exposure  to social  activities,  thereby  circumventing  
the additional  social  efforts  and cognitive  resources  required  to manage  SNS fatigue.  Users  of 
SNS are progressively  “de-socializing”  to protect  themselves  from  the negative  emotional  
responses  triggered  by excessive  social  activities.  Consequently,  their  usage  motivation  is 
gradually  shifting  toward  a singular  direction  of merely  consuming  social  content.  This  
observation  leads  to the following  hypothesis:
H7. SNS fatigue  positively  affects  users’  lurking  intention  on SNS.

4. Methodology
4.1 Instruments
The measurement  tools  for this study  were  theoretically  validated  by prior  research,  and we 
adapted  them  for our purposes.  As the original  items  were  in English,  the instruments  were  
first translated  into Chinese  and then back-translated  (i.e. from  Chinese  to English)  (Zhang  
et al., 2016). This process  was performed  by three  professors  proficient  in both languages.  
Any discrepancies  or inconsistencies  in the translation  process  were  resolved  to ensure  the 
final instruments  were  of high quality.  Before  the formal  survey  was launched,  a focus  group  
was assembled.  This  group  consisted  of three  researchers  from  the field of information  
systems  and five graduate  students  who were  experienced  users  of SNS.  Their  task was to 
further  verify  the measurement  items.  Additionally,  we invited  15 SNS users  to participate  in 
a pilot study  to refine  the clarity  of instructions  and questions  in the online  questionnaire.  
To gather  more  detailed  feedback,  the respondents  were  invited  for face-to-face  interviews  to 
share  their  thoughts  on the questionnaire.  After  analyzing  the feedback,  we made  several  
minor  revisions  to refine  the questionnaire.  The final scales  are included  in Appendix  2. All 
items  were  measured  using  seven-point  Likert  scales  ranging  from  “strongly  disagree”  (1) to 
“strongly  agree”  (7).
4.2 Data  collection
Participants  for this study  were  recruited  from  the user base of WeChat,  a cross-platform  
instant  messaging  service  developed  by Tencent  on January  21, 2011.  WeChat  shares  similar  
features  with WhatsApp,  allowing  users  to send  both text and voice  messages.  A key feature  
of WeChat  is WM,  which  serves  as the primary  social  networking  function  within  the app. 
WM is the leading  SNS in China,  boasting  a vast user base and immense  popularity.  Unlike  
other  SNS platforms  such  as Microblog,  Facebook  and Twitter,  WM is primarily  used  for 
communication  among  relatives,  friends,  acquaintances  and other  social  connections  of the 
users.  This makes  it an ideal  platform  for studying  the effects  of SNS context-specific  cues 
from  a role perspective.  An online  random  survey  was conducted  on Sojump,  a professional  
survey  distribution  platform  in China  from  May 23 to June 2, 2017.  Respondents  who 
successfully  completed  and submitted  the survey  were  given  a 5 RMB  monetary  reward.  
In total,  692 WM users  from  mainland  China  completed  our questionnaire.  Because  of the 
length  of the survey,  and to decrease  monomethod  bias, we followed  the best practice  of using  
two attention-trap  questions  to determine  if the respondents  had read all the questions  fully  
and answered  honestly  (Lowry  et al., 2013). After  removing  invalid  responses,  such  as those  
failing  our attention-trap  questions  and those  providing  identical  answers  across  all 
questions,  we were  left with a total of 491 valid  responses  (71%  valid  response  rate).

5.1 Common  method  bias
In our study,  we acknowledged  the potential  for common  method  biases  that could  arise  from  
various  sources,  such  as consistency  motif  and social  desirability,  as is the case with all self-
reported  data (Podsakoff  and Organ,  1986; Philip  et al., 2003). To evaluate  the extent  of this 
common  method  bias, we conducted  statistical  analyses.  Initially,  we performed  a Harmon  
one-factor  test, as suggested  by Podsakoff  and Organ  (1986). This test was applied  to seven  
key variables  in our theoretical  model:  parallelism,  reprocessability,  role conflict,  role 
overload,  SNS fatigue,  lurking  intention  and privacy  concern.  The results  showed  that the 
maximum  covariance  explained  by a single  factor  was 29.064%.  This  indicates  that no single  
factor  could  account  for the majority  of the variance,  suggesting  that our results  are unlikely  
to be significantly  affected  by common  method  biases.  Furthermore,  we considered  the 
measured  latent  marker-variable  approach,  which  is known  to help detect  and control  
common  method  bias (Malhotra  et al., 2006). To implement  this marker-variable  technique,  
we carefully  selected  variables  that were  unrelated  to the phenomena  being  investigated.  
As such,  we chose  “fashion  consciousness,”  defined  as the importance  an individual  places  
on being  fashionably  dressed  (Lumpkin  and Darden,  1982). The results  demonstrated  that 
the path coefficients  and model  fit values  were  in line with the original  estimates  (as shown  in 
Table  2). Consequently,  we concluded  that common  method  bias does not pose a significant  
concern  to our overall  study.

5.5 Robustness  test
Two  additional  tests  were  conducted  to check  for the robustness  of the proposed  research  
model.  First,  social  network  size was treated  as a dichotomous  variable  (low:0  vs high:1).  
Specifically,  to ensure  that the sample  sizes  of the two groups  were  as balanced  as possible,  
users  with a social  network  size of <200  were  assigned  to the lower  group  (N 5 264),  while  
the remaining  users  were  placed  in the higher  group  (N 5 227).  The results  of the model  test, 
as detailed  in Appendix  4, were  consistent  with the findings  from  the hypothesis  testing  
presented  in Figure  4. Second,  by considering  users  with a large  social  network  size who 
infrequently  use the SNS may exhibit  different  behaviors  compared  to those  with a smaller  
social  network  size but more  frequent  usage.  A bootstrap  multigroup  analysis  was 
conducted  to assess  the differences  between  users  who frequently  (hourly,  N 5 321) access  
SNS and those  who access  less frequently  (N 5 170). As indicated  in Appendix  4, users  with a 
high frequency  of SNS usage  and large  social  network  size perceive  a greater  intensity  of role 
overload.  Furthermore,  the frequency  of SNS usage  exacerbates  the relationship  between  role 
conflict  and lurking  intention,  while  simultaneously  undermining  the relationship  between  
role overload  and lurking  intention.  Thus,  the results  confirm  the robustness  of the proposed  
theoretical  model  and provide  evidence  that user characteristics  significantly  impact  the 
lurking  mechanism.

6. Discussion
6.1 Key findings
User-generated  content  is vital for the prosperity  of SNS,  as it generates  new knowledge  and 
value  for various  sectors  including  business  (Lin et al., 2021), finance  (Schniederjans  et al., 
2013; Asur  and Huberman,  2010), decision-making  (Kellner  et al., 2023; Sadovykh  et al., 2015) 
and public  politics  (Tumasjan  et al., 2010). However,  user behavior  on SNS often  adheres  to 
Pareto’s  law (Matei  and Bruno,  2015), with over 80%  of the content  being  created  by less than 
20%  of active  users.  This  study  aimed  to explore  the impact  of context-specific  cues on users’  
lurking  intentions  on SNS.  Our findings  reveal  that the size of a user’s  social  network  and the 
boundary-spanning  technical  feature  can induce  perceived  role stresses,  such  as role conflict  
and role overload.  These  role stresses  can significantly  heighten  users’  SNS fatigue,  which  in 
turn,  amplifies  their  lurking  intention.  Furthermore,  SNS fatigue  fully  mediates  the 
relationship  between  role conflict  and lurking  intention,  and partially  mediates  the 
relationship  between  role overload  and lurking  intention.
Contrary  to previous  studies,  which  largely  suggested  that users  with numerous  social  
connections  are more  likely  to post (Goes  et al., 2014; Stieglitz  and Linh,  2013), our research  
presents  a paradoxical  finding.  We discovered  that a large  number  of social  connections  may 
actually  decrease  a user’s  intention  to post on SNS due to the overwhelming  role stresses  
associated  with managing  such  networks.  SNS are designed  to provide  users  with a robust  
capability  to reach  out to others,  manage  their  social  relationships  and control  their  online  
impressions  (Chen  et al., 2016). Thus,  most  prior  studies  have  focused  on the positive  aspects  
of these  context-specific  cues.  For example,  theories  such  as social  influence  (Posey  et al., 
2010; Venkatesh  et al., 2003; Aral and Walker,  2014) and social  capital  (Wasko  and Faraj,  
2005; Kankanhalli  et al., 2005) suggest  that large  social  networks  provide  users  with 
sufficient  incentives  and potential  social  capital,  thereby  encouraging  them  to contribute  
more  content.  The Media  Synchronicity  Theory  also identified  five capabilities  of media  
(symbol  sets, rehearsability,  transmission  velocity,  parallelism  and reprocessability)  that 
enhance  media  effectiveness  (Tang  and Wang,  2011; Dennis  and Valacich,  2008). However,  
these  studies  often  consider  the effects  of these  context-specific  cues in isolation,  thereby  
overlooking  the overall  effects  when  these  cues are combined.  Our research  offers  a fresh  
perspective  and additional  evidence  to explain  the collective  effects  of these  context-specific

Our results  have  confirmed  that both role conflict  and role overload  can increase  users’

lurkers  and contributors  (Morrison  et al., 2013; Muller,  2012; Tan,  2011; Zheng  et al., 2021). 
The concept  of lurking,  as a passive  usage  behavior  or social  strategy,  has been  largely  
overlooked.  Our study  addresses  this research  gap, offering  new theoretical  and practical  
insights  to better  understand  the lurking  phenomenon  on SNS.
6.2 Theoretical  contributions
Given  the limited  research  to date on lurking  within  SNS,  this study  makes  three  major  
contributions  to our understanding  of the evolution  of SNS users’  lurking  intention.  First,  we 
delved  into the context-specific  cues of SNS from  both social  and technical  perspectives.  
Specifically,  we proposed  that the distinctive  social  environment  of SNS is shaped  through  
the confluence  of social  network  size and boundary-spanning  technical  features.  While  
previous  research  has predominantly  highlighted  the positive  implications  of these  context-
specific  factors  on user behaviors,  our study  offers  a fresh  perspective,  shedding  light  on 
their  potential  negative  effects.  Previous  studies  have  extensively  examined  the positive  
impact  of these  two context-specific  factors  on users’  active  adoption  behaviors,  using  
various  theoretical  lenses  such  as social  influence  theory  (Posey  et al., 2010; Venkatesh  et al., 
2003; Aral and Walker,  2014), social  capital  theory  (Wasko  and Faraj,  2005; Kankanhalli  
et al., 2005) and media  synchronicity  theory  (Tang  and Wang,  2011; Dennis  and Valacich,  
2008). Yet, these  frameworks  tend to address  these  context-specific  cues in isolation.  
In contrast,  our study  offers  an integrated  viewpoint,  evaluating  the influence  of both cues on 
users’  passive  lurking  intention  from  a role perspective.  By integrating  these  two aspects,  we 
can delve  into the impacts  of role stresses  engendered  by the distinctive  social  environment  
on SNS,  thus expanding  our comprehension  of platform-based  features  within  the social  
context  of SNS.  Through  this role perspective,  we also arrive  at conclusions  that challenge  
established  beliefs,  offering  fresh  insights  into the role of platform-based  features  within  the 
SNS social  environment.
Second,  we have  successfully  identified  boundary  spanning  as a second-order  construct  
comprising  two distinct  dimensions:  parallelism  and reprocessability . Through  a comparative  
analysis  between  offline  social  environments  and SNS social  environments,  we have  posited  
that SNS liberate  users  from  the constraints  of time and space,  thereby  fostering  frequent  
boundary-spanning  social  activities  and their  consequent  challenges.  Drawing  from  an 
exhaustive  literature  review,  we have  identified  two pertinent  media  capabilities,  namely,  
parallelism  and reprocessability,  to encapsulate  this unique  social  context  inherent  to SNS.Information

social  roles.  Boundary  spanning  was traditionally  measured  by refining  an instrument  
developed  by Aiken  and Hage  (1972)  and Miles  (1974). The final instrument  was measured

conversations  or informal  written  communication,  e.g. informal  notes,  memos  and the like; (5) 
selected  aspects:  includes  travel  and coordinating  activities.  This  measurement  is not 
suitable  for the context  of SNS because  it is very different  from  traditional  marketing  
organizations.  It would  be useful  to understand  how instrumental  boundary  spanning  
operates  within  the context  of SNS.  As our literature  review  shows,  existing  research  on SNS 
lurking  behaviors  has overlooked  the role of media  contextual  factors.  We have  identified  
parallelism  and reprocessability  as two primary  constructs  that underpin  boundary  
spanning  from  a media  capabilities  perspective.  This  insight  introduces  a novel  
measurement  for distinguishing  and quantifying  the unique  social  environment  of SNS,  
thereby  providing  fresh  insights  into the body  of SNS literature.  In particular,  it highlights  
the influence  of SNS contextual-specific  cues on user behaviors.
Third,  anchored  in the burnout  theoretical  model  and integrated  with a role perspective,  
this study  stands  as one of the pioneering  efforts  in elucidating  the mechanism  behind  the 
maladaptive  lurking  intention  observed  among  SNS users.  We enhance  the current  research  
conceptualization  on the SNS lurking  phenomenon  by emphasizing  that not all lurking  
behaviors  are active  or justified,  particularly  when  examining  role dynamics.  Our empirical  
tests  further  contribute  to the existing  body  of research  on the SNS lurking  mechanism  by 
highlighting  the pivotal  influence  of context-specific  cues (such  as social  network  size and 
boundary  spanning),  maladaptive  cognition  (such  as role conflict  and role overload)  and 
distorted  affect  (i.e. SNS fatigue).  These  insights  significantly  enrich  the SNS lurking  
literature,  facilitating  a deeper  comprehension  of the decision-making  processes  behind  
users’  lurking  intentions.  The integration  of role theory  and the burnout  theory  also 
theoretically  extends  these  two theories.
6.3 Managerial  implications
The results  of this study  provide  compelling  evidence  that both the size of a user’s  social  
network  and the act of boundary  spanning  on SNS can lead to perceived  role conflict  and role 
overload.  As users  form  more  social  connections,  their  networks  become  more  diverse.  
Coupled  with the boundary-spanning  characteristic  of SNS,  this can result  in inevitable  role 
conflict  and role overload.  Consequently,  users  may find it increasingly  challenging  and 
taxing  to navigate  their  social  interactions  effectively,  which  can lead to SNS fatigue  and an 
inclination  to lurk rather  than actively  participate.  The sustainability  of SNS platforms  
heavily  relies  on users  actively  posting  and engaging.  Thus,  these  findings  have  significant  
practical  implications  for SNS providers.  Providers  should  exercise  caution  when  
recommending  new contacts  to users  or sharing  potential  contact  information.  While  
these  recommendations  aim to enhance  user interactions,  merely  increasing  the number  of 
connections  can sometimes  have  adverse  effects,  especially  in terms  of role stresses.  
Furthermore,  SNS providers  should  consider  offering  tools  that are context-aware  and canITP

maladaptive  behaviors.  The ultimate  goal should  be to foster  a healthy  and sustainable  user 
experience  on these  platforms.
6.4 Limitations  and future  research  directions
While  this research  offers  valuable  theoretical  and practical  insights,  our study  comes  with 
several  limitations,  pointing  to intriguing  avenues  for future  research.  First,  our data were  
predominantly  sourced  from  WMs  users  in China.  To gain a broader  understanding,  
subsequent  research  could  extend  data collection  to users  of various  SNS like Facebook  or 
Twitter  in different  countries.  Second,  our cross-sectional  approach  restricts  our ability  to 
delve  deeply  into actual  lurking  behaviors.  A future  field experiment  observing  users’  
longitudinal  social  interactions  on SNS could  provide  insights  into more  objective  behavioral  
and procedural  outcomes.  Third,  our research  focused  on the unique  SNS social  environment,  
primarily  considering  the impact  of users’  social  network  size and the boundary-spanning  
technical  feature.  However,  further  studies  are needed  to delve  into other  specific  contextual  
cues of SNS.  For instance,  understanding  the actual  number  of social  roles  a user assumes  on 
SNS,  and determining  the importance  of different  roles  within  various  social  network  
contexts  would  be beneficial.  This  is especially  relevant  as the stresses  associated  with 
different  roles,  shaped  by varying  organizational  norms  and cultures,  can differ  
significantly.  Lastly,  the impact  of individual  user differences  was not fully  considered  in 
this study.  Such  as users  with large  social  networks  who have  few frequent  interactions  may 
differ  significantly  from  those  with smaller  networks  but more  frequent  interactions.  Future  
research  could  benefit  from  exploring  how different  user characteristics  influence  their  
lurking  intention.
7. Conclusions
SNS empower  users  with the robust  ability  to connect  with others,  significantly  enhancing  
their  management  of diverse  social  connections  and online  personas.  However,  as users’  
social  network  sizes  expand,  coupled  with the inherent  boundary-spanning  feature  of SNS,  
they encounter  unprecedented  role stresses.  These  stresses  can compromise  efficient  
relationship  management  and even  tarnish  their  online  image.  Observing  this paradox,  we 
delved  into the influence  of context-specific  cues in the SNS social  environment  on users’  
lurking  intentions.  Anchored  in the burnout  theoretical  model  and integrated  a role 
perspective,  this study  stands  as one of the pioneering  efforts  in elucidating  the mechanism  
behind  the maladaptive  lurking  intention  observed  among  SNS users.  Our findings  indicate  
that both an increase  in social  network  size and boundary  spanning  significantly  amplify  
role conflict  and role overload.  These,  in turn,  intensify  SNS fatigue,  which  escalates  users’  
intentions  to lurk. This  study  offers  a fresh  viewpoint  for comprehending  lurking  behaviors  
on SNS,  furnishing  practical  insights  for platform  providers.  Additionally,  it paves  the way 
for future  research  into the deeper  mechanisms  driving  SNS lurking  phenomenon.Information
