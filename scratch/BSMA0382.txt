Fostering public sector employees’
citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors through human
resource flexibility and employee
perceptions of normative
public values
Trong Tuan Luu
Swinburne University of Technology, Hawthorn, Australia
Abstract
Purpose – Public sector employees’ contributions play a crucial role in improving public service quality and
promoting the image of public organizations. The aim of this research is to unravel how and when human
resource (HR) flexibility activates citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors among public sector
employees.
Design/methodology/approach – The data were collected from 427 public sector employees and 102
supervisors working for governmental agencies from the districts of a major city in Vietnam. Multilevel
structural equation modeling (MSEM) was employed to analyze the data.
Findings – The positive associations between HR flexibility and the three dimensions of citizen-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors were supported. Harmonious passion demonstrated a mediating path for such
linkages. Employee perceptions of normative public values were found to exert a positive moderating effect on
the positive link between HR flexibility and harmonious passion, as well as their indirect link via harmonious
passion.
Originality/value – This study advances the literature by identifying how and when HR flexibility shapes
citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors among public sector employees.
Keywords Citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors, HR flexibility, Harmonious passion,
Employee perceptions of normative public values
Paper type Research paper
Introduction
Growing attention from academics and practitioners has been given to the role of public
sector employees in engaging citizen-oriented behaviors to deliver, improve and promote
public services (Tantivejakul, 2019). From Bettencourt et al.’s (2005) view, citizen-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors can be defined as individuals’ prosocial behaviors in forms of
internal influence, external representation and service delivery. To fulfill a boundary-
spanning role, organizational members can undertake internal influence behavior by
generating and sharing initiatives, and influencing colleagues to generate initiatives to
improve services (Bayighomog and Araslı, 2019; Bettencourt et al., 2005). Moreover,
employees can engage in external representation by representing the public organization to
citizens and enhancing citizens’ awareness and advocacy of its image (Waymer, 2013). Public
sector employees can also achieve their boundary-spanning role by demonstrating
responsiveness and courtesy in service delivery, which contribute to augmenting citizens’
satisfaction (Nor et al., 2021).

Due to the importance of citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors in improving the
quality of public services (Van Meerkerk and Edelenbos, 2021; Waymer, 2013), it is crucial to
explore its antecedents. de Geus et al.’s (2020) systematic review of public sector employees’
citizenship behaviors revealed a gap in studies of this stream that have focused more on
individual antecedents than on contextual ones. Research also indicated that contextual
antecedents of citizenship behaviors tend to include leadership and organizational justice
(de Geus et al., 2020; Hassan et al., 2017; Tran, 2023) and suggested further inquiries into its
contextual determinants. The current study bridges such a gap by considering the
antecedent role of human resource management (HRM) for citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors as managing human resources (HRs) is one of the crucial ways to foster
citizen-oriented behaviors (Bentein et al., 2021). Since improving customer services
necessitates employees’ broad set of skills and behavioral scripts for adaptation and
flexibility (Ubeda-Garcıa et al., 2017), research has considered the concept of HR flexibility to
explicate partly how organizations can manage changing customer needs (Chang et al., 2013;
Luu, 2016). HR flexibility indicates the degree to which an organization’s HRs (employees)
possess a repertoire of skills and behavioral scripts, and its HR practices can effectively
enable those employees to be adaptive (Way et al., 2015).
Despite the potential role of HR flexibility in fostering client-oriented behaviors, as earlier
discussed (Chang et al., 2013; Luu, 2016), research regarding the relationship HRM and
customer-oriented behavior remains rather quiet about this role of HR flexibility. Insights
into this antecedent effect of HR flexibility are further important since they complement prior
studies that have concentrated on the role of HR practices in promoting citizenship behaviors
through enhancing employee commitment (e.g. high-commitment HR practices) (Chen et al.,
2017), job involvement (e.g. high-involvement HR practices) (Yasir and Majid, 2020) or high
performance (e.g. high-performance work systems [HPWSs]) (Jaiswal and Tyagi, 2020). In
addressing this gap, this study aims to unfold the role of HR flexibility in nurturing citizen-
oriented boundary-spanning behaviors among public sector employees. Drawing on the self-
determination theory (SDT) (Deci and Ryan, 2014), a social environment that can activate
individuals’ psychological needs, particularly for autonomy, competence and relatedness, can
shape their actions (Deci and Ryan, 2014). Thus, HR flexibility, which can equip employees
with skill flexibility, behavioral flexibility and opportunities to use a broad set of skills and
behavioral scripts in working with others and having control over work (Way et al., 2015), can
fulfill such needs among public sector employees and drive them to achieve their boundary-
spanning role. It is crucial to understand the relationship between HR flexibility and citizen-
oriented boundary-spanning behaviors since this encourages public sector practitioners to
build HR practices that can enable employees to develop a broad set of skills and behavioral
scripts necessary for performing new or different work activities and improving public
services.
The SDT further contends that a social context that can fulfill individuals’ needs for
autonomy, competence and relatedness can shape employees’ motivation to autonomously
internalize activities (Deci and Ryan, 2014; Vallerand et al., 2003). Accordingly, as earlier
discussed, HR flexibility, which provides employees with opportunities to satisfy such needs,
may stimulate employees to internalize work activities autonomously and develop
harmonious passion, which is defined as autonomous internalization that drives an
individual to willingly undertake an activity (Vallerand et al., 2003). Moreover, passion has
been reported to link some types of HR practices to employee behaviors such as innovative
work behavior (Jan et al., 2021) or proactive service performance (Chen et al., 2017). These
empirical proofs together with the SDT lens indicate the salience of examining harmonious
passion as a mediation mechanism between HR flexibility and citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors. Investigating the mediating role of this form of autonomous
internalization, the current study can also complement previous studies that have largely

delved into mediation mechanisms such as work engagement (Bal and De Lange, 2015), self-

Furthermore, in light of the SDT, the impact of a situation on employee motivation can be
contingent on contextual or individual factors (Ryan and Deci, 2000). Among individual
contingencies, values are salient contingencies highlighted in the SDT (Graves and Sarkis,
2018) but have been given less attention (Park et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2023). The SDT posits
that, in the face of a specific organizational situation (e.g. HR practices), different values may
affect the process of stimulating employee motivation (Graves and Sarkis, 2018). Research
has reported the potentiality of values in leveraging employees’ perceptions of contextual
factors (e.g. HR practices and leadership), which further strengthens the impact of such
factors (Park et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2023). These suggest the potential moderating effect of
values on the influence of HR practices on employee outcomes. We, hence, postulate that
employee perceptions of normative public values in the work environment may serve as a
moderator that influences employee responsiveness to and reliance on HR flexibility, which
may further fuel their harmonious passion. Normative public values consist of individual
rights (civil rights), representation (responsiveness to all citizens) and equity (fair distribution
of resources) (Pandey et al., 2016). Guided by normative public values, employees are more
inclined to contribute to the public mission (Pandey et al., 2016), which further influences them
to rely on HR flexibility and channel skill and behavioral flexibility into harmonious passion
for serving citizens. By unpacking the moderating role of employee perceptions of normative
public values for the effect of HR flexibility, our study extends the literature that has scarcely
examined the role of normative public values as well as largely focused on moderation
mechanisms such as psychological capital (Cheewakoset et al., 2023), organizational identity
(Xu et al., 2023) or inclusive leadership (Zhang et al., 2023) underlying the effects of flexibility
HRM systems.
In a nutshell, this study develops and validates a research model, in which HR flexibility
influences public sector employees’ citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors through
the mediating path of harmonious passion, as well as employee perceptions of normative
public values play a moderating role for such an effect. This research model contributes to the
extant literature in some respects. First, by unpacking the link between HR flexibility and
citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors among public sector employees, our research
answers the calls of researchers to unravel more contextual factors that shape public sector
employees’ citizen-oriented behaviors (Bentein et al., 2021). Second, our study extends the
relationship between HRM and customer-oriented behavior literature by unfolding the
mediation path of harmonious passion that links HR flexibility to the citizen-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors. Third, by examining the role of employee perceptions of
normative public values as a contingent condition, our study advances our understanding of
conditions through which HR flexibility can be optimally utilized in the workplace.
Literature review and hypothesis development
Theoretical basis of the SDT
The SDT focuses on fundamental human needs (competence, autonomy and relatedness),
motivations and integrated regulation that underpin one’s choices (Deci and Ryan, 2014;
Horwood et al., 2021). It posits that fulfilling these needs determines the quality of one’s
motivation (Gagne and Deci, 2005). These assumptions have been further extended to
contend that it is the extent of autonomous internalization linked with the motivation that
makes a difference (Liu et al., 2011). Building on the SDT, Vallerand et al. (2003) argue that
harmonious passion emerges through the process of autonomously internalizing an activity

into one’s identity, whereby individuals volitionally consider work as crucial to their
identities due to the work characteristics (e.g. the work being meaningful or challenging).
Of particular relevance in current inquiry is a tenet of the SDT in terms of how to promote
autonomous motivation by fulfilling fundamental needs for competence, autonomy and
relatedness (Deci and Ryan, 2014). Partaking in an activity that satisfies these needs is likely
to influence individuals to autonomously internalize the activity into their identity and
develop a harmonious passion (Vallerand et al., 2003). An SDT premise entails the salient role
of contextual factors in impeding or fulfilling basic needs (Deci and Ryan, 2014) and ones’
perceptions of the context are central to their passion (Ho et al., 2018). Building on these SDT
premises, this study next explains how the factor of HR flexibility, which can meet such basic
needs, can facilitate harmonious passion and in turn activate citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors.
HR flexibility and citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors
From Bettencourt et al.’s (2005) view, citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors can be
defined as individuals’ prosocial behaviors that associate their organization with its citizen-
clients in forms of internal influence, external representation and service delivery. To fulfill a
boundary-spanning role, members can engage in internal influence behavior by generating
and sharing initiatives, and influencing colleagues to generate initiatives in relation to service
delivery (Bayighomog and Araslı, 2019; Bettencourt et al., 2005). They represent their
organization to outsiders (including clients) and promote its image and products or services
(Bettencourt et al., 2005). They also demonstrate their boundary-spanning role by providing
service in a courteous, responsible and flexible fashion (Parasuraman et al., 2020).
HR flexibility is defined as the degree to which HRs of an organization possess skill and
behavioral repertoires, as well as the degree to which the necessary HRM practices can be
built and implemented in a timely manner to enhance the flexibility in such HRs (Way et al.,
2015, 2018). HR flexibility is further viewed as a dynamic organizational capability (Sekhar
et al., 2016) encompassing: (1) HRs who possess skill flexibility and behavioral flexibility and
(2) the HR practices employed to effectively maximize such flexibility in HRs (Bhattacharya
et al., 2005).
Guided by the SDT, HR flexibility comprising skill flexibility, behavioral flexibility and
HR practice flexibility can be expected to shape employees’ citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors. Skills flexibility indicates potential alternative uses where an employee’s
skills can be applied (Wright and Snell, 1998). Skill flexibility reflects competence to produce
output streams for current and potential alternative requirements (Bhattacharya et al., 2005).
Employees with skill flexibility anticipate the need for future skills, display learning
motivation for new approaches to tasks and view each event taking place inside the
organization as a learning opportunity (Stokes et al., 2015). Therefore, such employees can
fulfill their needs for competence, control over work (with a broad repertoire of skills) and
relatedness (through motivation to learn with and from others), which can motivate them not
only to fulfill their in-role duties but also to go beyond their roles to contribute to the
organization (Deci and Ryan, 2014). Consequently, employees with skill flexibility can fulfill
their role in delivering services to citizens. Service delivery can also be accomplished since
skill flexibility can enable employees to be responsive and adaptive in serving customers
(Lepak et al., 2003). Furthermore, with learning motivation and a broad repertoire of skills
(Stokes et al., 2015; Way et al., 2018), employees can amplify and use skills to perform beyond
the requirements of their work roles such as generating initiatives for service improvement
(i.e. internal influence) and promoting the organization and its services (i.e. external
representation) as forms of customer-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors.

Employee behavioral flexibility alludes to the degree to which they possess a broad
repertoire of behavioral scripts which they can adapt under various conditions (Bhattacharya
et al., 2005). Rather than adhering to existing service procedures, members high in behavioral
flexibility can adapt and demonstrate appropriate behavioral repertoires in line with
situation-specific demands (Do et al., 2016), as well as respond to previously unknown
situations beyond their work roles (Bhattacharya et al., 2005). Consequently, behavioral
flexibility enables variation to emerge (Do et al., 2016), which is a competence beneficial to
generating service initiatives (i.e. internal influence) and promoting services (i.e. external
representation) in citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors.
HR practice flexibility alludes to the degree to which HR practices can be adapted and
adopted in diverse situations, as well as the pace at which these adaptations and applications
occur (Bhattacharya et al., 2005). HR practice flexibility can provide employees with
opportunities to develop and use a broad set of skills and behavioral scripts for adaptation
(Ubeda-Garcıa et al., 2017). Hence, HR practice flexibility can serve as a contextual factor that
can meet employees’ need for competence, autonomy, and relatedness since such
opportunities not only enhance employees’ skills and behavioral scripts (Way et al., 2018),
control over work and situations in interactions with customers (Do et al., 2016) but also form
connections with others by learning from them (Stokes et al., 2015). Through the SDT lens,
such a contextual factor can motivate them to engage in work duties and contribute to the
organization (Deci and Ryan, 2014). Therefore, working under HR practice flexibility, public
sector employees are motivated to deliver and promote services to citizens (i.e. service
delivery and external representation) as well as use their broad set of skills and behavioral
scripts (Tretiakov et al., 2023) to improve and innovate services (i.e. internal influence).
Additionally, being provided opportunities to enhance and use various skills and behavioral
scripts through HR practice flexibility (Ubeda-Garcıa et al., 2017), employees feel a stronger
bond with the organization, experience more satisfaction of relatedness need and thus
contribute more to the organization through promoting its image (external representation). In
other words, HR practice flexibility can shape behaviors beyond the boundaries of their work,
such as internal influence, external representation and service delivery. Taken together, we
postulate the following hypotheses:
H1. HR flexibility is positively related to public sector employees’ citizen-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors in forms of internal influence (H1a), external
representation (H1b) and service delivery (H1c).
Harmonious passion as a mediator
Passion alludes to an intense desire or inclination toward an activity that individuals are
interested in, find significant, and invest their resources in. Job passion is defined as a job
attitude encompassing an intense liking for an activity (affective element) and the perceived
magnitude and meaning of the activity (cognitive element) (Ho et al., 2011). These elements
are not found concurrently in other job-related attitudes (Ho et al., 2011). Harmonious passion,
a form of work passion, refers to individuals’ view of their work as significant and their
autonomous internalization of the work not due to any pressures, but due to its attributes (e.g.
the challenging nature of the work) (Vallerand, 2015). Employees who are harmoniously
passionate about an activity do not encounter conflict between it and other life facets (e.g.
professional growth goals, values in life and family) (Gong et al., 2020).
In the organizational setting, the SDT and SDT-based research suggest that both
contextual support and individual competence play a pivotal role in influencing an
individual’s extent of harmonious passion (i.e. autonomous internalization) (Deci and Ryan,
2014; Vallerand, 2015). Contextual support offers individuals a sense of volition,
self-determination and freedom from external pressures for thinking or behaving

affecting basic needs (Deci and Ryan, 2014) and work passion (Ho et al., 2018). Hence, by
providing employees with opportunities to develop and use a broad set of skills and
behavioral scripts for adaptation (Ubeda-Garcıa et al., 2017; Way et al., 2018), HR practice
flexibility can serve as contextual support that enables employees to experience self-
determination and autonomously internalize activities, thereby forming harmonious passion.
Additionally, as postulated in the SDT, individuals with high competence and autonomy
(i.e. control over their work) are likely to organize actions based on interests and personal
goals rather than constraints and controls, as well as seek and exploit opportunities for choice
and self-determination (Deci and Ryan, 2014). In this sense, it is posited that employees with a
broad set of skills and behavioral scripts are inclined to experience self-determination and
choice, thereby autonomously internalizing activities and developing harmonious passion.
Specifically, employees, who are high in skill and behavioral flexibility, possess a broad set of
skills and behavioral scripts for adaptation (Stokes et al., 2015). They have positive beliefs
about themselves due to their ability to exhibit appropriate behavioral repertoires under
different situations and produce outputs for potential alternative demands (Bhattacharya
et al., 2005). Besides, with a repertoire of skills for adaptation and flexibility, employees can
have more control over their work (Halbesleben et al., 2014). They also anticipate the need for
future skills, display enthusiasm about learning and build relationships with others for
learning and developing new competence (Stokes et al., 2015). Differently expressed, through
skill and behavior flexibility from HR practice flexibility, employees possess competence and
autonomy in generating appropriate behavioral scripts in serving citizens as well as fulfill
their needs for autonomy, competence and relatedness, which drive them to view their work
as significant and autonomously internalize it, leading to harmonious passion in their work
(Ho et al., 2011).
Though the effect of HR flexibility on harmonious passion has not been established in the
management literature, prior studies have had some indications for the HRM effects on work
passion. Jan et al.’s (2021) study reported the effect of HRM practices on employees’
harmonious passion. Chen et al. (2017) found a positive association between high-
commitment HR practices with harmonious passion for work. In light of the above
reasoning and some evidence, HR flexibility, which comprises skill and behavioral flexibility
and HR practice flexibility (Way et al., 2018), can be expected to nurture public sector
employees’ harmonious passion. The subsequent hypothesis is consequently formulated:
H2. HR flexibility is positively related to public sector employees’ harmonious passion.
As posited in the SDT, with a form of autonomous internalization (e.g. harmonious passion)
(Deci and Ryan, 2014; Vallerand et al., 2003), individuals can self-determine and endorse
activities in a manner that harmonizes with other facets of their lives, which enable them to
concentrate on their activities, to be less prone to depletion of cognitive and affective
resources and to experience positive outcomes such as positive affect, high cognitive
engagement or high task performance (Ho et al., 2011; Horwood et al., 2021; Vallerand, 2015).
With positive affect and high cognitive engagement, harmoniously passionate employees can
effectively engage in behaviors within their roles as well as outside their job descriptions (Ho
et al., 2018). Individuals are also inclined to engage in prosocial behaviors when they
experience positive affect (Kong and Li, 2018). Thus, harmoniously passionate employees are
inclined to promote the organization’s image and public services (i.e. external representation)
as prosocial behavior beyond their work roles. Affective and cognitive resources that
harmoniously passionate members build can also expand their thought-action repertoires (Ho
et al., 2011), which facilitate not only their performance within their roles (e.g. service delivery)
but also their pursuit of unscripted courses of action (Fredrickson, 2001), leading to initiatives
for improving services (i.e. internal influence). Positive affect and high cognitive engagement

stimulate employees to explore links between divergent thoughts, thereby generating novel
solutions for service improvement (Isen, 2000; Liu et al., 2011).
Moreover, since positive affect can be conducive to relation satisfaction and closeness with
others, harmonious passion can contribute to employees’ positive relations with colleagues
(Ho et al., 2018). Such positive relationships can nurture employees’ learning from colleagues’
feedback and inputs as well as collaboration with them, which can help effectively perform
service delivery tasks as well as explore novel solutions to improve service delivery (i.e.
internal influence). Positive interpersonal connections, which fulfill relatedness needs, can
also promote actions beneficial for their organization (Chiniara and Bentein, 2016) such as in
the form of promoting its image and services (i.e. external representation). Furthermore, due
to personally endorsing work activities in a fashion that is in harmony with other facets of
their life (Vallerand, 2015), harmoniously passionate employees are less likely to face conflicts
between roles within and outside their work and invest resources in handling them (Ho et al.,
2018), and hence preserve more resources (e.g. time and energy) to reinvest in improving or
promoting services.
Integrating the above reasoning, it is tenable that harmonious passion leads to generating
service initiatives (internal influence), delivering services effectively and promoting services
(external representation). In juxtaposition with hypothesis H2, we propose the role of
harmonious passion as a mediation path that links HR flexibility to citizen-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors as in the ensuing hypothesis:
H3. Harmonious passion mediates the positive link between HR flexibility and citizen-
oriented boundary-spanning behaviors in the forms of internal influence (H3a),
external representation (H3b) and service delivery (H3c).
Employee perceptions of normative public values as a moderator
Normative public values comprise individual rights (civil rights and property rights),
representation (responsiveness to citizens) and equity (fair distribution of resources) (Pandey
et al., 2016). Guided by these public values, public sector employees place citizens center-stage
and serve them responsibly to build relations with them and gain their trust (Pandey et al.,
2016). Citizens will develop trust in the governmental agencies and the government that care
about their basic human rights such as justice and property ownership (Yousaf et al., 2016).
According to Yang and Pandey (2007), responsiveness and equity are vital in promoting
citizen trust since these can enhance public service quality, satisfy citizens’ needs and
increase their feeling of being equally treated.
The SDT posits that individual self-regulation in response to a situation can be thwarted
or facilitated by individual or contextual contingencies (Ryan and Deci, 2000). Stated
differently, the effects of a situation on employee motivation can be contingent on individual
or contextual factors. In addition to individual contingencies such as personality (Wu and
Zhou, 2023) or independent self-construal (Kong and Ho, 2016) which have been examined in
prior inquiries, values are important contingencies underscored in the SDT (Graves and
Sarkis, 2018) but have been less investigated (Park et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2023). The SDT
postulates that, in the face of a specific situation in the organization (e.g. HRM practices),
different values may influence the process of shaping motivation among employees (Graves
and Sarkis, 2018). Research has given attention not only to the predictive role of perceived
workplace values (Park, 2023) but also to the interaction between values and contextual
factors (Park et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2023). Research has revealed that values may enhance
employees’ perceptions of contextual factors in the organization, which further accentuates
the effects of such contextual factors. For instance, Park et al. (2018) reported that
reform values can further promote public sector employees’ positive appraisal of flexible
work systems, leading to the heightened impact of such systems on their job satisfaction.

Sun et al. (2023) observed the role of power distance value in moderating the influence of
exploitative leadership on employees’ harmonious passion for work. The SDT and such prior
findings highlight the importance of employee perceptions of values as a moderator for
contextual effects, which supports the proposition that employee perceptions of normative
public values in the work environment may play a moderating role in enhancing employee
perceptions of HR practice flexibility, which further influences employees to be more
harmoniously passionate about serving citizens.
Specifically, with high perceptions of normative public values in the work environment,
employees tend to have a better understanding of the public mission and public service goals
of the organization (Pandey et al., 2016). Thus, such employees are more inclined to use their
skills and competence to catch sight of citizen demands and provide better services to
citizens. They, hence, rely more on HR practice flexibility since it enables them to build and
use a broad repertoire of behavioral scripts and skills, with which employees can be
responsive and adaptive to customers’ situation-specific demands and changes in their
demands (Bhattacharya et al., 2005; Lepak et al., 2003), address citizen demands in a timely
and fair manner, and serve them with respect for their civil rights (Pandey et al., 2016). In
other words, the effect of HR flexibility on harmonious passion for serving citizens will be
more pronounced among employees with high perceptions of normative public values.
Additionally, normative public values increase employee bonds with the public
organization, which enhances their motivation to contribute to the organization (Hui et al.,
2021). Therefore, employees with high rather than low perceptions of normative public values
may view HR practice flexibility in a more positive light since a broad repertoire of behavioral
scripts and skills that they acquire can help them make a further contribution to the
organization by providing better services to citizens.
Conversely, with low perceptions of normative public values in the work environment,
employees may be less motivated to improve services for citizens and thus are less inclined to
use skill flexibility and behavioral flexibility for service enhancements. This leads to their less
responsiveness to and reliance on HR practice flexibility, resulting in a less pronounced effect
of HR flexibility on their harmonious passion. From the above reasoning, we can expect
employee perceptions of normative public values as a moderator that accentuates the impact
of HR flexibility on harmonious passion among employees:
H4. Employee perceptions of normative public values moderate the relationship between
HR flexibility and employee harmonious passion such that the relationship is
stronger when employee perceptions of normative public values are higher.
The hypothesis above, together with the postulation on the indirect link between HR
flexibility and employees’ citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors via the mediating
pathway of employees’ harmonious passion, indicates that the indirect relationship between
HR flexibility and employees’ citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors is contingent on
the extent of employee perceptions of normative public values. We expect that HR flexibility
promotes citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors through harmonious passion, which
is more likely to occur under conditions of high perceptions of normative public values. Under
such conditions, employees’ harmonious passion and, in turn, citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors tend to be more influenced by HR flexibility than under conditions of low
perceptions of normative public values:
H5. Employee perceptions of normative public values moderate the relationship between
HR flexibility and employees’ citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors in
forms of internal influence (H5a), external representation (H5b) and service delivery
(H5c) through employee harmonious passion such that the relationship is stronger
when employee perceptions of normative public values are higher.

Research methods
Sampling
The data were garnered from employees who belonged to governmental agencies in a major
city in Vietnam. Six governmental agencies were randomly selected from each of the 24
districts of the city. We approached the heads of these agencies, explicated our academic
purpose and solicited their voluntary participation. From the contact details of employees
and their supervisors supplied by the agencies, the questionnaire link was emailed to them to
invite their participation in tandem with the assurance of the confidentiality and anonymity
of their responses.
Three data collection waves were conducted with a time lag of three months. To mitigate
common method bias (CMB), we collected data for the independent and dependent constructs
in different measurement waves (Podsakoff et al., 2012). The first wave of measurement (T1)
garnered data on HR flexibility and perceptions of normative public values from employees.
In the second wave (T2), the data were collected for harmonious passion from employees. In
the third measurement (T3), supervisors rated employees’ citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors.
At T1, 982 survey links were relayed to employees and 726 complete responses (response
rate: 73.9%) were collected. At T2, 427 complete responses (response rate: 58.8%) were
obtained from employees who partook in T1. At T3, among 144 survey links that had been
distributed to supervisors, 102 complete responses (response rate: 70.8%) were obtained from
supervisors. These participants belonged to 102 agencies. A total of 187 employees (43.8%)
were female. Employees’ mean age was 36.74 years (SD 5 7.52), and their mean tenure with
organization was 7.45 years (SD 5 3.96).
Due to the existence of participants in the sample who failed to respond to survey
questionnaires, it is crucial to test non-response bias (Green et al., 2006). Non-response bias is
presumably nonexistent if there exist no differences in the variables between the survey
waves (Lambert and Harrington, 1990). Following Green et al. (2006), one-way ANOVA was
employed to compare participants between the survey waves, which indicated no differences
in the study variables (p > 0.05). We also found no difference in the study variables by
comparing early respondents (i.e. responding to initial requests) and late respondents (i.e.
responding to follow-up requests after two weeks), demonstrating that non-response bias
was not a concern in the dataset.

Measures
The measurement items (1 5 “strongly disagree”, 5 5 “strongly agree”, unless otherwise
designated) were translated into Vietnamese through the lens of Schaffer and Riordan’s
(2003) back translation approach. HR flexibility was gauged through Way et al.’s (2015) 21-
item instrument (e.g. “Your agency’s current training process enables employees to learn
new/different work activities”). This scale demonstrated an acceptable internal consistency
reliability in Way et al.’s (2015) work of the original scale (Cronbach’s alpha [α] 5 0.82) and
other works (e.g. α 5 0.72 [Way et al., 2018]). Employee perceptions of normative public
values were gauged through three items from Pandey et al. (2016). Employees were asked to
rate the salience of public values including equity (a fair allocation of resources),
representation (responsiveness to all citizens) and individual rights (civil rights, property
rights) in making major decisions about public services in their agency (1 5 “of little
importance”; 5 5 “extremely important”) (e.g. “How important is representation
(responsiveness to all citizens) in making major decisions about public services in your
agency?”). This scale exhibited a strong internal consistency reliability in prior inquiries (e.g.
α 5 0.86 [Luu et al., 2022]). Citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors were examined
through items adapted from Bettencourt et al. (2005), comprising four items for internal
influence (e.g. “Contributes many ideas for citizen promotions and communication”), four
items for external representation (e.g. “Says good things about the organization to others”)
and five items for service delivery (e.g. “Regardless of circumstances, exceptionally courteous
and respectful to citizens”). The internal consistency reliability of the above dimensions of
this scale was reported acceptable in Bettencourt et al.’s (2005) work of the original scale
(α 5 0.94, 0.90, 0.91, respectively) and other works (e.g. α 5 0.80, 0.85, 0.85, respectively
[Bayighomog and Araslı, 2019]). Harmonious passion was assessed through seven items
from Vallerand et al. (2003) (e.g. “My work allows me to live memorable experiences”). The
internal consistency reliability of this scale was found acceptable in Vallerand et al.’s (2003)
work of the original scale (α 5 0.79) and other works (e.g. α 5 0.85 [Khan et al., 2023]). This
research controlled for employees’ age, gender, education and organizational tenure.
Data analytic strategy
By virtue of the nested nature of the data within agencies, this research adopts a 2-1-1 model
(Preacher et al., 2011) with the influence of a level 2 variable (HR flexibility) on level 1 outcome
variables (citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors) mediated by a level 1 variable
(harmonious passion). Multilevel structural equation modeling (MSEM) was conducted
through MPlus 7.2 to appraise indirect effects and mitigate the conflation of the level 1-level 2
nexus (Preacher et al., 2011). Following Hofmann et al. (2000), we first group-mean centered all
level 1 (i.e. employee-level) variables (i.e. harmonious passion, citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors and control variables) to partial out level 2 (i.e. agency-level) variance and
avoid conflating level 1 and level 2 variability. We then centered level 2 aggregates (see
discussion on aggregation later) and added them to model the effect of level 2 variables.
A Monte Carlo 20,000-replication test was employed to test the significance of indirect effects
through confidence intervals (CIs) (Menges et al., 2017). We calculated the interaction term
and plotted the conditional effect of the predictor on the outcome at high (1 SD above the
mean) and low (1 SD below the mean) levels of the moderator.

We assessed between-group variances by computing intra-class correlations 1 (ICC1s), ICC2s
and rwg (Rho-within group agreement) values for HR flexibility and employee perceptions of
normative public values. ICC1s for HR flexibility and employee perceptions of normative
public values were 0.17 and 0.22, respectively, while their ICC2s were 0.69 and 0.73,
respectively. These values were greater than James’s (1982) median value of 0.12 for ICC1 and
Glick’s (1985) ICC2 threshold of 0.60. The rwg average parameter was 0.74 [0.69, 0.82] for HR
flexibility and 0.77 [0.72, 0.86] for employee perceptions of normative public values, exceeding
Klein et al.’s (2000) threshold of 0.70. These variances were supportive of multilevel
assessment.

Conclusion
Our results support hypotheses H1a, H1b and H1c on the positive associations between HR
flexibility and the three components of citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors. We
found evidence for hypothesis H2 regarding the positive link between HR flexibility and
harmonious passion as well as hypotheses H3a, H3b and H3c vis-a-vis the mediation path of
harmonious passion for the links between HR flexibility and citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors. Furthermore, the results voice support for hypotheses H4 and H5,
respectively, on the role of employee perceptions of normative public values as a moderator
that strengthens the effect of HR flexibility on employees’ harmonious passion as well as the
indirect effect of HR flexibility on citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors through
harmonious passion.
Discussion
Research implications
This inquiry contributes to the extant knowledge in various facets. First, the current study
advances the management literature by extending the concept of customer-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors to the public sector as well as identifying the effect of HR
flexibility on citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors of public sector employees.
Albeit citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors have not been explored in the
management literature, our findings are partially consistent with previous studies that
have reported the links between HRM and citizenship behavior in general (e.g. Cesario and
Magalh~aes, 2017; Mostafa et al., 2015). Nonetheless, through unmasking HR flexibility as a
precursor for citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors, this study contributes to the
literature that has not examined HR flexibility as an antecedent but mainly focused on
contextual antecedents around manager/organizational support or fairness such as

2022) or organizational justice (Bettencourt et al., 2005) for customer-oriented boundary-

Second, our results unveil that HR flexibility activates citizen-oriented boundary-
spanning behaviors through fueling their harmonious passion. These findings differentiate
the current study from the extant literature that has concentrated on mediation mechanisms
such as work engagement (Bal and De Lange, 2015), self-efficacy (Luu, 2016) and
employability (Xu et al., 2023) for the effects of flexible HRM systems. The current work
thus contributes to the literature by delving into the mediating role of harmonious passion as
a form of autonomous internalization rather than competence (e.g. self-efficacy and
employability skills) or positive state of mind (e.g. work engagement). Our study also extends
the stream of passion research by adding HR flexibility as a new element to the fledging body
of contextual precursors of harmonious passion, which is confined to group/supervisor
autonomy support, leadership or high-commitment HR practices (Chen et al., 2017; Ho et al.,
2018; Liu et al., 2011; Sun et al., 2023). It also lends further support for the SDT premise in
terms of the pivotal role of a social context (e.g. HR practice flexibility) in stimulating
individuals to autonomously internalize activities.
Third, irrespective of a growing interest among researchers in normative public values
(Luu, 2022; Pandey et al., 2016) as well as the SDT emphasis on the contingent role of values in
the process of shaping motivation among individuals in face of a specific organizational
situation (Graves and Sarkis, 2018), less scholarly attention has been given to the moderation
mechanism of employee perceptions of normative public values behind the HRM effects
(Cheewakoset et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). The current inquiry contributes to the literature by
addressing a call for more inquiries into the moderating role of employee perceptions of
normative public values (Luu, 2022). By examining this moderator, it also distinguishes itself
from prior works that have concentrated on moderators such as organizational identity (Xu
et al., 2023), psychological capital (Cheewakoset et al., 2023), or inclusive leadership (Zhang
et al., 2023) underlying the influence of flexibility HRM systems. It further supports the SDT
premise on the salience of contingencies underlying a contextual effect on activating an
individual’s motivation. The current result indicates that employee harmonious passion is
best realized by public sector employees who (1) work under HR practices that help them
build a broad set of skills and behavioral scripts for flexibility and adaptation and (2) are
exposed to normative public values.
Implications for practice
Our results present some practical implications. Since HR flexibility was found to activate
citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors, HR flexibility should be built in public
organizations. Specifically, the selection process should assess candidates’ skills to adapt and
perform different roles in various situations. Training programs should be flexibly adapted to
arm employees with new skills to perform new or different work aspects in response to
different contingencies. Employees should be trained on how to adapt to deal with diverse
client types, diverse demands and diverse service delivery situations. Through HR flexibility,
employees can develop harmonious passion for serving citizens as well as acquire the
knowledge and skills necessary for the generation of novel solutions for public service
improvement and promotion.
The current study further reveals the moderating effect of employee perceptions of
normative public values underlying the effects of HR flexibility. Hence, public
organizations should provide communication programs to shape or reinforce employees’
awareness of normative public values including civil rights, equity and representation.
When valuing these values and feeling encouraged to enact them, employees are more

Limitations and pathways for future research
Several limitations are acknowledged in the present study. First, its use of self-report data
and a time-lagged research design might engender CMB. While this research found CMB not
to be a major concern through the marker variable test, the interactive effect test and the
multi-source data collection, future research should further diminish CMB through a cross-
lagged approach (Kasl and Jones, 2003). Moreover, the causal relationships of the studied
variables, which could not be derived through a time-lagged approach, should be further
explored through experimental or cross-lagged research designs.
Second, the generalizability of the current findings might be limited by a single cultural
context of the study. Studies of HR flexibility have engaged with some Western contexts such
as North America (Way et al., 2018), Australia, New Zealand (Tretiakov et al., 2023) or Spain
(Ubeda-Garcıa et al., 2017) as well as some non-Western contexts such as India (Sekhar et al.,
2016) or Taiwan (Do et al., 2016). Accordingly, future research is encouraged to delve into the
effectiveness of HR flexibility in more Western and non-Western contexts. Besides, extant
inquiries of HR flexibility have tended to concentrate on some sectors such as IT (Do et al.,
2016; Sekhar et al., 2016; Tretiakov et al., 2023) and hospitality (Ubeda-Garcıa et al., 2017).
More research in settings beyond these sectors as well as in mixed industries should be
conducted to enhance the generalizability of the current findings.
Third, while research has revealed the predictive role of high-performance work systems
or high-commitment HR practices for employee performance outcomes through autonomous
motivation or harmonious passion (Chen et al., 2017; Jaiswal and Tyagi, 2020), future works
should comparatively analyze the effects of HR flexibility versus these two forms of HR
practices on harmonious passion and in turn employee outcomes. Additionally, since our
research model is incapable of accounting for every underlying mechanism for the linkage
between HR flexibility and citizen-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors, future research
should consider other mediators and moderators. When members possess a broad set of skills
and behavioral scripts for adaptation, role breadth self-efficacy may emerge. Therefore,
further research should investigate the mediating role of this variable. Future studies can also
examine skill flexibility and behavioral flexibility as mediation paths underlying the effects
of HR practices. Furthermore, since idiosyncratic deals (i-deals), viewed as individually
negotiated agreements between employees and the employer for work flexibility, can add
further flexibility and adaptability to members (Rofcanin et al., 2017), i-deals may play a
moderation role in the effects of HR flexibility on citizen-oriented boundary-spanning
behaviors on a new research path.
