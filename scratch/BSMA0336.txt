Abstract
Purpose – This study investigated the influence of ambiguous customer expectations and customer 
demandingness, which reflect hindrance and challenge demands, on the boundary spanning behaviors (BSBs) 
of frontline bank employees (FBEs) through person-job fit and work engagement. It also examined the 
moderating effect of customer stewardship between job demands and work engagement.
Design/methodology/approach – Data of 296 FBEs in the retail banking industry in the Republic of Korea 
were collected through an online survey and analyzed.
Findings – Ambiguous customer expectations acted as hindrance demands and negatively affected FBEs’ 
psychological process. Additionally, customer demandingness positively affected FBEs’ psychological process 
as challenge demands. Moreover, person-job fit and work engagement improved the BSBs of FBEs as a 
psychological motivation process. Lastly, customer stewardship buffered the negative influence of ambiguous 
customer expectations on work engagement while strengthening the positive influence of customer 
demandingness on work engagement.
Originality/value – This study contributes to the marketing literature by presenting customer-related 
characteristics influencing the BSBs of FBEs and exploring the psychological response processes. First, this 
study presents empirical evidence that ambiguous customer expectations and customer demandingness play 
the role of hindrance and challenge demands, respectively. Second, it confirms that customer-related 
characteristics affect the BSBs of FBEs through psychological processes. Finally, it enriches the authors’ 
understanding of customer stewardship as a job resource by exploring the role of customer stewardship in 
moderating the relationship between different types of job demands and work engagement.
Keywords Job demands-resources (JD-R) model, Ambiguous customer expectation,
Customer demandingness, Employees stewardship toward customers, Boundary-spanning behaviors (BSBs)

company’s revenue, it is imperative not to underestimate the significance of customers for

generally perceived as very complex by customers. Customers experience reduced cognitive 
resources as they endeavor to understand the banking service and cognitively depleted 
customers have difficulty in grasping the service properly (Mikolon et al., 2015). In this context, 
customers experiencing reduced cognitive resources struggle to comprehend the service, 
leading to ambiguous expectations and higher customer demandingness. FBEs interact with 
customers exhibiting these characteristics. Interestingly, ambiguous expectations hinder FBEs’ 
job performance by reducing satisfaction and clear feedback, while customer demandingness 
requires substantial effort but contributes to personal development and work-related

and customer demandingness) arising from banking services. Identifying the psychological 
resource factor of FBEs is important because it protects the psychological aspects of FBEs 
and helps them perform their job roles (Bakker and Demerouti, 2007; Lee and Han, 2020). 
Thus, the current study proposes customer stewardship as a psychological resource for FBEs

2. Theoretical framework and literature review
2.1 Job demands-resources model
The JD-R model explains how two types of job-related characteristics (i.e. job demands and 
resources) interact in a job situation to influence job motivation and/or stress (Lee and Yoo, 
2022). This theoretical perspective focuses on the positive emotions and motivational potential of 
work-related resources (Bakker and Demerouti, 2007), suggesting that job resources (e.g. 
autonomy, work support, rewards and job feedback) contribute to true welfare at work. In 
contrast, various job demands (e.g. increased occupational burden, inappropriate physical job 
conditions and interplay with customers that generates negative emotions) cause a decrease in 
employees’ energy resources, leading to physical and mental health problems (Yoo et al., 2021). 
Job demands refer to the psychological, physical, organizational and social dimensions of 
work that require physical and/or mental (cognitive and affective) exertion or technique on an 
ongoing basis and are therefore associated with particular physiological and/or mental costs. 
Job demand factors are not always adverse, but they may transform into work stressors when 
excessive exertion is required to satisfy these demands and/or insufficient compensation is 
provided. However, not only do job resources perform a practical role in attaining job 
objectives, they reduce the physiological and mental costs incurred by job demands as well. 
Moreover, job resources encourage individuals to acquire and develop their occupational 
roles, leading to positive growth. Job resources and demands are usually negatively related 
(Bakker and Demerouti, 2007; Lee and Yoo, 2022).
Job demands and job resources are assumed to stimulate different psychological 
processes in the JD-R model (Hakanen et al., 2006): (1) a strain process of exhaustion, wherein 
excessive job demands decrease individuals’ mental and physical resources and may thus 
cause stress and eventually feeble wellness; and (2) a motivational process wherein job 
resources reinforce positive job behaviors and outcomes through work engagement 
(Schaufeli and Bakker, 2004).
Despite the different consequences of job demands and job resources explored in prior 
studies, not all results have been consistent with these domains of work-related peculiarities 
(Olafsen and Frølund, 2018). For instance, cognitive demands, time constraints and 
workloads related to job demands have been previously documented to increase work 
engagement (Bakker et al., 2005; Schaufeli et al., 2008). In relation to these results, a qualitative 
classification that divides job demands into hindrance elements and challenge elements has 
been previously suggested (Crawford et al., 2010; Olafsen and Frølund, 2018; Ventura et al.,

2.2 Customer-related characteristics in banking services: ambiguous customer expectations
and customer demandingness
The perspective of expectations is reflected in a diverse range of fields spanning management 
studies. It has been argued that unfulfilled expectations are important in sectors such as 
product and leader performance satisfaction, or job satisfaction (Coye, 2004). In particular, 
interest in customer expectations in the service sector has consistently surfaced in line with 
increasing attention to improvements in service quality. In the majority of cases, perceptions 
of service quality and/or customer satisfaction are considered a function of the difference 
between the expectations of a customer and their assessment of the real service (Solomon 
et al., 1985; Walker, 1995; Zeithaml et al., 1993). Coye (2004) notes that customer expectations
include the following two principal conceptualizations: (1) the customers’ anticipation of what 
might occur in an imminent transaction or exchange and (2) what the customers ideally want. 
The first conceptualization represents predictive expectations, which are the subjectively 
perceived probabilities of the occurrence of future events. The second conceptualization 
reflects normative expectations that customers wish and/or expect from excellent service 
employees (Coye, 2004; Miller, 1977; Zeithaml et al., 1990, 1993). Accordingly, if customers’ 
expectations for service are ambiguous, the work roles and behaviors of service employees 
tend to be disturbed.
Ambiguous customer expectations are related to customers having uncertain and 
ambiguous requirements, as one of the representative customer-related social stressors 
arising from interactions between service employees and customers (Dormann and Zapf, 
2004; Kim et al., 2012). Furthermore, role ambiguity and uncertainty in goal attainment 
triggered by ambiguous customer expectations is a representative hindrance demand that 
causes individuals to use excessive energy for information processing and problem-solving 
(Crawford et al., 2010). Previous empirical studies have shown that ambiguous customer 
expectations not only negatively affect psychological aspects such as emotional exhaustion 
of frontline employees (Dormann and Zapf, 2004), but also result in deterioration in service 
performance (Kim et al., 2012).
Meanwhile, customer demandingness refers to the level of customers’ countless and 
distinctive demands for obtaining quality, support, satisfaction of special needs and delivery 
of a product or service (Itani et al., 2020; Li and Calantone, 1998). Over the past decade, 
customer demand for service organizations and their employees has continuously grown 
(Agnihotri et al., 2017; Banin et al., 2016; Luu, 2021). Moreover, customers have increasingly 
diverse demands to enhance their perceived experiences and values (Flint et al., 2011; O’Hern

experience higher levels of stress (Agnihotri et al., 2017; Jaramillo et al., 2013), previous studies 
have implied that customer demandingness belongs to job demands. A high level of customer 
demandingness puts additional pressure on FBEs in terms of augmented workload and 
effort, which causes stress (Jaramillo and Mulki, 2008). On the other hand, previous studies 
have confirmed that customer demandingness play a role in improving the ability and 
performance of service employees (Banin et al., 2016; Jaramillo et al., 2013). The conflicting 
roles of customer demandingness imply that it belongs to the challenge demand in that it 
promotes personal growth and development while requiring psychological energy.
2.3 Customer stewardship
The concept of customer stewardship is derived from stewardship theory, which replaces 
traditional control systems based on agency theory (Schepers et al., 2012). Agency and 
stewardship theories are two core theories that are inextricably linked and explain the 
relationship between an organization’s management and its stakeholders (Davis et al., 1997; 
Tosi et al., 2003). Agency theory assumes that external regulations such as compensation 
and/or monitoring are required to prohibit management from behaving opportunistically. In 
contrast, stewardship theory suggests that the interests of managers can be associated with 
those of stakeholders, such that managers’ efforts benefit not only their welfare but others’ 
interests as well (Hernandez, 2008; Schepers et al., 2012). In addition, agency and stewardship 
theories reveal differences in employee perceptions. Agency theory asserts that employees 
(agents) will behave according to their own benefit, which may collide with their 
organization’s interests (Van Puyvelde et al., 2012). On the other hand, stewardship theory 
suggests that employees (stewards) will behave in the best interest of their organization 
ahead of their own benefit (Hernandez, 2012; Singh and Tiwari, 2020).
Based on stewardship theory, Schepers et al. (2012) introduced the concept of customer 
stewardship in the relationship between service employees and customers. Customer 
stewardship refers to a service employee’s sense of moral responsibility and ownership for 
their overall well-being (Boateng et al., 2022; Schepers et al., 2012). For the concept of customer 
stewardship, responsibility and psychological ownership are key motives for aligning the 
interests of service employees and customers (Block, 1996; Davis et al., 1997). Service 
employees who feel responsible for their customers perform role duties in return for 
awareness from customers and the intensification of their occupational beliefs (Hernandez, 
2012). Job-related activities that fail to improve customer well-being and are inconsistent with 
one’s beliefs are rectified through self-regulation (Schepers et al., 2012; Tosi et al., 2003).

Psychological ownership also leads service employees to perceive customer interests as their 
own. Service employees are motivated to prevent exposure to excessive loss or damage when 
they pursue their own interests (Hernandez, 2008; Pierce et al., 2001).
Previous studies related to customer stewardship indicate that customer stewardship can 
share conceptual territory with customer orientation and therefore embrace the concept of 
service to and enhancement of customers (Schepers et al., 2012). However, there are two 
distinct differences between customer stewardship and orientation. The first difference stems 
from the source of the control of work behavior. Customer orientation focuses on the control 
of performance perspectives because it seeks to satisfy customer needs by aiming to achieve 
performance for service employees and organizations (Franke and Park, 2006; Jaramillo and 
Grisaffe, 2009; Jaramillo et al., 2009). In contrast, customer stewardship emphasizes the 
psychological control of service employees because it not only recognizes replying to 
customer needs as an occupational obligation within a formal control system, but considers 
customer well-being as a moral restriction within an informal control system as well (Davis 
et al., 1997; Schepers et al., 2012). The second difference arises from the distinction between the
areas of concern for customers. Customer orientation is a comparatively steady, personal 
surface characteristic that affects job attitudes and behaviors by focusing on satisfying 
customer needs (Flaherty et al., 2009; Licata et al., 2003). By contrast, customer stewardship is 
a more embracing construct that concentrates on the overall well-being of customers rather 
than customer needs (Schepers et al., 2012).
Similar to the common context of stewardship literature, which values pursuing others’ 
interests over self-interests (Block, 1996; Davis et al., 1997), customer stewardship allows 
service employees to form a fundamental belief that puts customer interests ahead of their 
own. This discussion does not imply that customer stewardship has no personal interest in 
service employees. Rather, it implies that serving customer interests is eventually linked to 
the attainment of service employees’ personal interests (Singh and Tiwari, 2020). Customer 
stewardship, in this sense, is more willing to conduct actions that prioritize the interests of 
customers, even in situations where customer interest and self-interest conflict (Davis et al., 
1997; Singh and Tiwari, 2020). Service employees, who regard themselves as holding 
stewardship roles, are responsible for customers (Caldwell et al., 2008; De Ruyter et al., 2009). 
They are perceived as partners for customers and enjoy reputational enhancement, 
reciprocity and autonomy (Block, 1996; Van Puyvelde et al., 2012). In addition, stewardship 
has needs and values that are admired and valued as ends in themselves (Caldwell and Karri, 
2005). Customer stewardship creates intrinsic forms of motivation for service employees 
(Aßl€ander et al., 2016; Singh and Tiwari, 2020). Based on this discussion, we propose that 
customer stewardship is a job resource for FBEs.
2.4 Psychological aspects of FBEs on customer-related characteristics: person-job fit and
work engagement
Person-environment fit signifies the conformance between individuals and particular 
categories of work environments (Bretz and Judge, 1994) and has received a lot of interest 
from both practitioners and scholars interested in interpreting the attitudes and behaviors of 
employees (Bui et al., 2017; Chuang and Sackett, 2005). The concept of person-environment fit 
is broad (Kristof-Brown et al., 2005), and previous studies have reflected it through various 
types of fits associated with the work environment, such as tasks (Wronska et al., 2019), 
groups (Seong and Kristof-Brown, 2012), supervisors (Van Vianen et al., 2011) and customers 
(Yoo, 2016). Among these, person-job fit is a key element that represents person-environment 
fit (Lam et al., 2018; Lee and Yoo, 2022). Person-environment fit is divided into two categories: 
supplementary fit and complementary fit (Bui et al., 2017). Supplementary fit refers to 
possessing similar characteristics between individuals and their work environment, whereas

features, but need each other to form the entirety or replenish what is missing (Boon et al.,

specific characteristics (Tims et al., 2016). The fit of the first characteristic is demand-ability 
fit, which is related to the ability, expertise and techniques of service employees to satisfy job 
requirements. From an occupational perspective, person-job fit improves when service 
employees have the qualifications and techniques to conduct their work. The fit of the second 
characteristic is the needs-supplies fit, which means that the job satisfies service employees’ 
preferences and needs. In terms of employees, person-job fit increases when service 
employees’ needs, such as pay, social relationships and self-actualization, are fulfilled (Lee 
and Yoo, 2022; Tims et al., 2016). While these types of fit were dealt with separately in earlier 
studies (Cable and DeRue, 2002), they have been integrated into a single and overall concept 
of person-job fit in recent studies (Yoo, 2013). In addition, previous studies mention that 
person-job fit belongs to the cognitive dimension in the psychological aspects of an individual 
(Lee and Yoo, 2022).
Meanwhile, work engagement refers to a high standard of devotion and a powerful 
concentration of work conjoined with a condition of positive and affective motivation 
(Schaufeli and Bakker, 2010) and represents the emotional domain in terms of psychological 
aspects (Lee and Yoo, 2022). Work engagement is a core aspect of employees’ psychological 
condition, as described in the J-DR model. The psychological motivation process in the JD-R 
model appears when employees acquire resources for job fulfillment (Bakker and Demerouti, 
2007), and work engagement is a mental response that occurs in the psychological motivation 
process for job resources (Bakker et al., 2014). Work engagement comprises three 
characteristic elements: vigor, dedication and absorption (Bakker and Demerouti, 2008; 
Schaufeli and Bakker, 2004). Vigor is associated with the condition wherein a high standard 
of energy persists even after employees face difficulties. Dedication refers to participation in a 
job while experiencing a sense of challenge, significance and inspiration. Absorption is 
related to the condition wherein an individual is too focused on the task of identifying with the 
task (Schaufeli et al., 2002). Work engagement is the basis for achieving high standards of 
organizational performance and is encouraged to form a high level of work engagement in 
contemporary organizations (Bakker and Demerouti, 2007; Lee and Yoo, 2022).
2.5 Boundary-spanning behaviors
Boundary-spanning employees oscillate between the organization’s external and/or internal 
borders in the fulfillment of their occupational roles (Yoo, 2013). Thus, boundary-spanning 
employees interact not just with internal members but with external customers too (Adams, 
1976). BSBs refer to frontline employees’ activities that originate from their occupational roles 
as boundary spanners for their organization (Bettencourt et al., 2001; Chebat and 
Kollias, 2000).
Previous marketing and management studies have stated that frontline employees 
perform three fundamental boundary-spanning roles (Bettencourt and Brown, 2003; 
Bettencourt et al., 2001; Yoo et al., 2014). The first role is to achieve high customer service 
quality perceptions and satisfaction through frontline employees’ activities, such as 
politeness, personal interest and response and fulfillment of guarantees (Parasuraman 
et al., 1988). The second role is to improve the image and legitimacy of the service organization
by representing it to external stakeholders and supporting products and services (Lee and 
Han, 2020). The third role is to provide various opportunities to exchange intelligence 
internally about changing customer requirements and feasible developments in goods and

3. Hypotheses development
3.1 Ambiguous customer expectations, person-job fit and work engagement
Ambiguous customer expectations can negatively affect the psychological aspects of FBEs, 
because they cause role ambiguity and uncertainty in goal attainment (Dudenh€offer and 
Dormann, 2015). If customers’ expectations of service are not accurately known, FBEs do not 
have a clear understanding of their role in the service process. Because service providers’ 
responses and attitudes toward customers in service situations have a significant influence 
on customers’ perceptions of service (Hartline and Ferrell, 1996), it is the core roles of FBEs to 
make customers highly aware of service quality (Lee and Yoo, 2022). A key way to improve 
service quality is to bridge the gap between customers’ expectations for service and FBEs’ 
understanding of such expectations (Luk and Layton, 2002). Ambiguous customer 
expectations interfere with the role behavior of FBEs because of ambiguous feedback, 
making it difficult to satisfy customer expectations (Dudenh€offer and Dormann, 2015). On the 
other hand, FBEs may revise the service roles or the process based on them understanding of 
what is expected by customers (Luk and Layton, 2002). Therefore, the increased 
understanding of customer expectations influences the job role performance of FBEs 
(Schepers and van der Borgh, 2020). Eventually, due to ambiguous customer expectations, 
FBEs may believe that they and their occupations do not match each other because they 
judge their knowledge and skills to be insufficient to provide services to solve customer 
problems. Meanwhile, if the achievement of occupational goals is uncertain, then FBEs 
cannot satisfy an individual’s needs to be met through the job. Uncertainty of goal attainment 
due to ambiguous customer expectations leads FBEs to expect that they will not achieve the 
desired levels of pay or self-realization through their jobs. In general, pay-for-performance 
systems that connect employees’ salary levels with their previous or future job performance 
and incentives based on individual job performance are used as important organizations’ 
compensation strategy (Gerhart and Fang, 2014; Gerhart and Rynes, 2003; Gerhart et al., 2009; 
He et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2020). These compensation systems serve as motivation for 
employees to achieve better performance (He et al., 2021). In addition, the need for 
achievement has been recognized as an essential psychological need for individuals 
(Albrecht, 2015; Schriesheim and Schriesheim, 1980), whereas ambiguous customer 
expectations hinder the occupational achievement of FBEs. Thus, ambiguous customer 
expectations are perceived as a hindrance demand that obstructs the psychological 
satisfaction of FBEs.

3.2 Customer demandingness, person-job fit and work engagement
Because customer demandingness utilizes the resources of service employees and can 
experience higher levels of stress (Agnihotri et al., 2017; Jaramillo et al., 2013), previous studies 
have implied that customer demandingness belongs to job demands. A high level of customer 
demandingness puts additional pressure on FBEs in terms of augmented workload and 
effort, which causes stress (Jaramillo and Mulki, 2008). However, customer demandingness 
has been confirmed to play a role in improving the ability and performance of service 
employees (Banin et al., 2016; Jaramillo et al., 2013). A high level of customer demandingness 
encourages FBEs to learn more about their customers and develop customized solutions for 
customer problems (Wang and Netemeyer, 2004). Furthermore, customer demandingness 
augments a new set of problem-solving and value-providing abilities, such as enhanced 
improvisation, creativity and FBEs’ efforts (working smart and hard) in service situations, to 
FBEs’ traditional portfolios (Itani et al., 2020; Luu, 2021). Regarding the psychological aspect 
of FBEs, customer demandingness is accepted as a challenging task, and the growth needs of 
FBEs can be satisfied by solving high levels of customer demandingness through the 
application of new knowledge and skills (Agnihotri et al., 2017). We suggest that customer 
demandingness is a challenge that enhances job competency and motivation.
H3. Customer demandingness has a positive influence on person-job fit.
H4. Customer demandingness has a positive influence on work engagement.
3.3 Person-job fit and work engagement
When service employees perceive that their abilities match their job requirements, they 
maintain positive emotions and attitudes, feel happiness and passionately engage in their 
jobs (Lam et al., 2018). Larsen and McKibban (2008) confirmed that individuals wishing what 
they possess and possessing what they wish increase their psychological well-being, such as 
happiness. In the occupational context, demands-abilities fit is associated with individuals 
wishing what they possess, and needs-supplies fit is associated with individuals possessing 
what they wish (Lee and Yoo, 2022). In the same context, the extant literature has confirmed 
that person-job fit has positive effects on service employees (Babakus et al., 2010; Lam et al., 
2018; Yoo, 2013). Lee and Yoo (2022) demonstrated that person-job fit, which belongs to the 
cognitive aspect, has a positive influence on the emotional aspect and leads to a motivational 
process. Based on these discussions, we suggest the following hypothesis:
H5. Person-job fit has a positive influence on work engagement.
3.4 Work engagement and boundary-spanning behaviors
Previous studies related to the JD-R model show that work engagement leads to high 
outcomes for individuals and organizations because it improves the fulfillment of in- and 
extra-roles (Bakker and Demerouti, 2008; Bakker et al., 2004; Gierveld and Bakker, 2005; 
Salanova et al., 2005; Xanthopoulou et al., 2007). Therefore, it can be predicted that FBEs with 
a high level of work engagement will make efforts to perform their job roles. FBEs who are 
strongly motivated obtain satisfaction from job achievement and thus work harder to excel

3.5 Moderating role of customer stewardship
We propose that customer stewardship buffers the negative effect of customer-related 
hindrance demands on work engagement. Hindrance demands, such as ambiguous customer 
expectations, accumulate stress and interfere with work roles and behaviors in reaching work 
performance (Dudenh€offer and Dormann, 2015; Kim et al., 2012). Even if FBEs experience 
high hindrance demands, having access to sufficient job resources may buffer the negative 
effects of hindrance demands because job resources increase the willingness to devote effort 
to work (Meijman and Mulder, 1998; Tadi  
c et al., 2015). For example, customer stewardship 
can provide the energy required for customers’ ambiguous expectations through the 
motivation to pursue their interests. Moreover, as customer stewardship leads FBEs to focus 
more on the welfare of their customers, they may be less concerned with the uncertainty of 
goal achievement because of ambiguous customer expectations. This suggests that customer 
stewardship moderates the relationship between ambiguous customer expectations and 
work engagement. Meanwhile, we argue that customer stewardship boosts the positive effect 
of customer-related challenge demands on work engagement. When faced with complex or 
challenging problems at work, access to sufficient job resources improves one’s job 
competency and the prospect that one’s work effort will lead to positive outcomes. Thus, 
challenge demands have the highest motivational potential when combined with job 
resources (Tadi  
c et al., 2015; Widmer et al., 2012). This can improve work engagement. In the 
context of FBEs, customer demand as a challenge demand leads to engagement in work 
because of the ability to develop customized solutions. At the same time, customer 
stewardship stimulates FBEs’ work engagement by an immersion in customer interests. 
Accordingly, we propose the following hypotheses:
H9. The negative influence of ambiguous customer expectations on work engagement
will be weaker when customer stewardship is high.
H10. The positive influence of customer demandingness on work engagement will be
stronger when customer stewardship is high.

5.4 Moderating effects of customer stewardship
We used the PROCESS macro, based on the bootstrapping method suggested by Hayes 
(2013), to test the moderating effects of customer stewardship on the relationships between 
customer-related characteristics, which reflect hindrance and challenge demands, and work 
engagement. Because customer-related characteristics were established to influence work 
engagement through person-job fit in our research model, we used the PROCESS macro 
(conditional process analysis) model 5 to confirm the interaction effect of customer 
stewardship and the indirect effect of person-job fit. In order to perform the analysis, 
bootstrap resamples were set to 5,000 and the bootstrap confidence intervals was set to 95%.

expectations and customer demandingness) as X, work engagement as Y, person-job fit as 
M and customer stewardship as W (Hayes, 2013). We set demographic variables as control
variables. Previous studies have suggested that demographic factors such as gender, age, 
work experience and education can affect frontline employees (Lee and Yoo, 2022; Levy and 
Sharma, 1994; Franke and Park, 2006). Therefore, we used demographic factors of FBEs as 
control variables to rule out the possibility that these would influence the relationship 
between variables. The PROCESS macro model 5 provides results in two steps.

6.1 Theoretical implications
We contribute to the marketing literature by presenting customer-related characteristics that 
influence the BSBs of FBEs and explore psychological response processes. The first 
contribution of the current study is that ambiguous customer expectations and customer 
demandingness were specified as customer-related characteristics affecting the BSBs of 
FBEs. Previous studies have emphasized the importance of frontline employees’ BSBs 
(Bayighomog and Araslı, 2019; Bettencourt and Brown, 2003; Bettencourt et al., 2005), which 
led scholars to explore antecedents that influence BSBs of frontline employees in services 
sector. To this end, previous studies have examined the influence of individual-related factors

such as role stressors and personality characteristics (e.g. Bettencourt and Brown, 2003; 
Chien et al., 2021), or have focused on organizational factors such as relational factors within 
organization (e.g. Lee and Han, 2020; Yoo, 2013), organizational justice (e.g. Bettencourt et al., 
2005) and leadership (e.g. Bayighomog and Araslı, 2019). However, these studies have not 
confirmed how customer-related characteristics affect BSBs of FBEs. Frontline employees in 
service sector are expected to interact with customers to identify and satisfy customer needs 
(Kim and Yoon, 2012; Singh, 2000). In the process, customers increase or inhibit frontline 
employees’ ability to perform their job roles due to individual customer factors (H€ 
uttel et al., 
2019). Previous studies have shown that customer-related characteristics affect not only the 
attitudes of employees related to work but also job behaviors (Stock and Bednarek, 2014; Yoo 
and Arnold, 2016). Moreover, because banking services are highly customized, customers not 
only have heterogeneous characteristics from each other (H€ 
uttel et al., 2019), but also typically 
engage in a high level of interaction with FBEs before being purchased (Chan et al., 2010). 
Therefore, customer-related characteristics are particularly important to FBEs in the 
banking services sector. We contribute to the bank marketing literature by identifying the 
influence of customer-related characteristics on BSBs of FBEs in the banking services sector. 
Second, this study presents empirical evidence that ambiguous customer expectations 
and customer demandingness, which are customer-related job demands, play the role of 
hindrance and challenge demands, respectively. Previous studies have expanded the JD-R 
model by identifying that job demands are classified as hindrances and challenges and have 
different effects on motivation (Crawford et al., 2010; Olafsen and Frølund, 2018; Ventura 
et al., 2015), whereas attempts to classify customer-related job demands into hindrances and
challenges have been deficient. Our results support the notion that ambiguous customer 
expectations are hindrance demands that inhibit the motivational process of FBEs and play a 
role in reducing the BSBs of FBEs by negatively affecting cognitive and emotional aspects. 
In addition, we find support for our findings that customer demandingness is a challenge that 
enhances the motivational process by positively affecting the cognitive and emotional 
aspects of FBEs and that it is an antecedent in improving the BSBs of FBEs. The findings of 
this study expand the theoretical scope of the JD-R model by producing empirical evidence 
that ambiguous customer expectations belong to hindrance demands and customer 
demandingness belongs to challenge demands.
Third, our findings provide an understanding of the role of job resources in the 
relationship between different job demands such as hindrances and/or challenges and work 
engagement. Previous studies conducted from the JD-R perspective have suggested that job 
demands and resources are not only negatively associated but also have a buffering effect on 
each other’s psychological processes (Bakker and Demerouti, 2007; Bakker et al., 2014). 
However, it has been identified that the influence on the motivational process in the extended 
JD-R model that divides job demands into hindrances and challenges depends on the type of 
job demands (Van den Broeck et al., 2010; Crawford et al., 2010), and thus the moderating roles 
of job resources may also be different. We demonstrate that customer stewardship as a job 
resource buffers the effect of hindrance demands on work engagement and reinforces the 
effect of challenge demands on work engagement. We enrich the understanding of customer 
stewardship as a job resource by exploring its role in the relationship between different types 
of job demands and work engagement.
6.2 Managerial implications
Because our findings indicate that specific factors related to customers affect the BSBs of 
FBEs, it is necessary to discuss practical strategies to improve their BSBs based on these 
factors. First, ambiguous customer expectations are negatively related to the psychological 
aspects of FBEs and diminish BSBs. Therefore, financial service organizations must

establish practical solutions to reduce ambiguous customer expectations. Increasing the 
social capital between FBEs and customers is one way to lower ambiguous customer 
expectations. The increase in social capital between FBEs and customers expands shared 
interpretations and information about each other (Lee and Yoo, 2022; Nahapiet and Ghoshal, 
1998). Shared information about customers not only gives FBEs an understanding of the 
customer’s situation, but also allows FBEs to, in reverse fashion, suggest requisitions and/or 
solutions to customers. Eventually, FBEs’ social capital for customers can be utilized as a way 
to deal with ambiguous customer expectations.
Second, customer demandingness is positively related to the psychological aspects of 
FBEs and intensifies BSBs. FBEs cannot completely exclude job demands in their job 
performance, and it is important to identify challenge demands in the jobs of FBEs because 
challenge demands among job demands are positively related to work engagement. For FBEs 
to achieve personal development and performance in a job environment with high customer 
demand, it is necessary to design jobs to increase the empowerment of FBEs. If FBEs are not 
empowered to respond to customer requests, they cannot address customer demandingness 
and may lead to increased job stress. Additionally, it is necessary to strengthen their 
capabilities through regular job training and education, thereby enabling them to meet the 
level of customer demandingness. Although customer demandingness acts as a training 
factor that increases the abilities of FBEs (Banin et al., 2016; Jaramillo et al., 2013), FBEs that 
do not have a certain level of ability cannot handle customer demandingness. Therefore, 
FBEs that lack job performance capabilities increase job stress because of customer
demandingness.
Third, customer stewardship acts as a job resource by buffering the negative influence of
hindrance demands on work engagement and improving the positive influence of challenge
demands on work engagement. Therefore, practical solutions should be implemented to
increase FBEs’ customer stewardship. Customer stewardship being reflected in 
organizational values is a representative solution to improve FBEs’ customer stewardship. 
Organizational values influence the behavior of FBEs and direct them to follow the 
organization’s spirit and philosophy (Boateng et al., 2022). Financial service organizations are 
required to reflect customer stewardship in organizational values and infuse organizational 
values into FBEs. For example, financial service organizations can make customer 
stewardship reflective and prominent in the moral code within the organization, placing it 
at the forefront of FBEs’ minds. Financial service organizations must also infuse in their 
FBEs a sense of responsibility and ownership in their FBEs. Managers can achieve this by 
periodically communicating the customer vision of the organization to FBEs.
6.3 Limitations and future research
Our findings represent a framework for investigating the role of customer-related 
characteristics and their effect on the BSBs of FBEs from the perspective of the JD-R 
model. Despite these contributions, the current study has several limitations. First, while the 
JD-R model assumes a psychological dual process (i.e. strain and motivational processes), this 
study included only the motivational process in the research model. Reflecting on only one of 
the dual processes in the JD-R model may provide a partial or insufficient understanding of 
job demands and resources. Therefore, future studies should investigate the role of job 
demands and resources in the strain process.
Second, we presented customer-related characteristics as antecedents of the BSBs of 
FBEs. However, despite the existence of various aspects that affect the BSBs of FBEs, we 
failed to consider other factors. In particular, the characteristics of financial services (e.g. 
service complexity and degree of contact with customers) were not controlled or considered 
even though they could affect the degree of relationship with customers. In future studies, an

Third, we conducted our investigation in a single culture (i.e. Asia), which may restrict the

Fourth, this study is not free from endogeneity concerns. Rutz and Watson (2019) 
emphasized that there is no perfect solution to endogeneity and suggested relying on theory 
as a way to mitigate endogeneity concerns. In the context of managing endogeneity concerns, 
researchers may include control variables to address omitted variable bias (Hill et al., 2021), 
but it may be implausible to identify and measure all possible control variables. Therefore, 
researchers should rely on sound theoretical reasoning beyond simply including control 
variables (Rutz and Watson, 2019). The purpose of our study is to identify the effects of 
customer-related characteristics on psychological processes and frontline employees’ BSBs in 
banking services. This study adopted the theoretically extended JD-R model as a framework 
to understand these objectives and related concepts. All variables set in this study were based 
on theoretical reasoning. Moreover, Hill et al. (2021) proposed the use of the latent variable 
method (SEM) and the resolution of the CMV to solve measurement error, which is a source of 
endogeneity. We utilized covariance-based SEM for analysis and addressed the potential 
problems of CMV through statistical techniques. Nevertheless, this study cannot be 
completely free from endogeneity concerns because there is no perfect solution to 
endogeneity.
