Discussion and implications
Discussion
Boundary spanners operate at the boundary or periphery of a service firm, 
managing communication, negotiating and resolving issues, and advocating 
the organization’s image (Bettencourt et al., 2005). However, hardly any 
research has examined the antecedents to boundary-spanning behaviors in 
the hospitality industry, despite it being a highly relevant field. Taking a social 
exchange and role theory perspective, the current research addresses the gap in 
the literature by investigating the organizational supportive antecedents and 
psychological constructs as mechanisms of boundary-spanning behaviors. 
With most of the hypotheses supported, the results demonstrated that the 
more organizational support customer-contact employees perceived, the more 
job satisfaction and organizational commitment they demonstrated which, in 
turn, led to boundary-spanning behaviors. A closer look at the findings 
uncovers several valuable insights.
First, all three dimensions of perceived organizational support lead to 
boundary-spanning behaviors, via job satisfaction and organizational commit­
ment, with varied influences, corroborating the existing findings in the general 
marketing field that management interventions/supervisor support (Ackfeldt 
& Wong, 2006), internal communication (Malhotra & Ackfeldt, 2016), and 
training (Johlke et al., 2002) promote boundary-spanning behaviors of high- 
contact employees. Overall, such findings harmonize with (1) social exchange
