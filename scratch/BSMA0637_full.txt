Radical green innovation has emerged as an effective strategy for firms to achieve
sustainable development in the volatility, uncertainty, complexity, and ambiguity
(VUCA) environment. Although the importance of external knowledge acquisition has
been recognized, the mechanism of how proactive boundary-spanning search affects
radical green innovation is still blurred. Drawing on the resource-based view and
dynamic capabilities theory, this research discusses the impact of two types of proac-tive boundary-spanning search on radical green innovation and considers the mediat-
ing role of organizational resilience and the moderating role of big data analytics
capability. Based on data collected from 294 manufacturing firms in China, the results
show that both value-chain-based proactive search and industry –university-based
proactive search positively influence radical green innovation. Furthermore, organiza-tional resilience fully mediates the relationship between value-chain-based proactive
search and radical green innovation, and partially mediates the relationship between
industry –university-based proactive search and radical green innovation. Moreover,
we demonstrate that big data analytics capability moderates the relationship
between industry –university-based proactive search and radical green innovation.
This research clarifies not only the mechanism of proactive boundary-spanning
search driving radical green innovation by highlighting the mediating role of organiza-
tional resilience and the moderating role of big data analytics capability, but also pro-
vides theoretical and managerial implications for manufacturing firms and other
stakeholders to carry out radical green innovation.

1|INTRODUCTION
In order to achieve long-term and sustainable development, the top
priority of firms is to carry out green innovation (Bhatia, 2021 ;D u
et al., 2018 ; Huang & Li, 2017 ; Tang et al., 2018 ; Zhang & Zhu, 2019 ).
However, the new normal of volatility, uncertainty, complexity, and
ambiguity (VUCA) and the sudden outbreak of the COVID-19 crisishave brought challenges to firms in green innovation (Su et al.,
2022 ;
Zhang et al., 2022 ; Zhao et al., 2021 ), resulting in low productionefficiency (Do et al., 2022 ). Facing the double pressure of external
environmental impact and environmental protection (Zhang
et al., 2020 ), firms must carry out radical green innovation. Radical
green innovation refers to the process in which firms develop newadvanced environmental technologies by changing the original tech-
nological trajectory and fundamentally or subversively increasing the
environmental benefits of existing products and processes (Daiet al.,
2015 ). It is not only an inevitable choice for firms to break
through technical bottlenecks (Su et al., 2022 ), but also conforms toReceived: 1 January 2023 Revised: 29 August 2023 Accepted: 6 September 2023

the innovation trend and market demand sustainability (Xin
et al., 2022 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ). Therefore, how to drive firms to
implement radical green innovation has emerged as a significant topic
that requires to be addressed immediately.
The resource-based view suggests firms are required to pursue
valuable, rare, and inimitable resources to build competitive advantage
(Barney, 1991 ). From this perspective, existing research has analyzed
the mechanism of internal heterogeneous resources on radical greeninnovation (Guo et al.,
2020 ; Su et al., 2022 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ; Zhao
et al., 2021 ; Zhou et al., 2023 ) and found that rich resources provide
strong support for radical green innovation. Compared with incremen-tal green innovation, radical green innovation requires more novel,complex, and extensive heterogeneous knowledge resources (Dai
et al.,
2015 ; Kiefer et al., 2019 ; Liao & Zhang, 2020 ; Martínez-Ros &
Kunapatarawong, 2019 ). It is difficult for firms to realize radical green
innovation only based on their own knowledge reserves, and firms
must search for external resources across borders (Wang et al., 2020 ).
Although some scholars found that external resource acquisition canaffect radical green innovation in a beneficial way (Bhatia,
2021 ; Chen
et al., 2014 ; García-Morales et al., 2012 ; Zhang et al., 2020 ), the con-
nection between proactive boundary-spanning search and radicalgreen innovation has received little attention. Proactive boundary-
spanning search, as a new way for firms ahead of competitors to
search for valuable knowledge that has not been generally recognized(O'Cass et al.,
2014 ; Yang et al., 2021 ), can facilitate firms to anticipate
and respond to changes in the green market environment. Moreover,
firms can benefit from “first-mover advantage ”by actively pursuing a
proactive boundary-spanning search (Katila & Ahuja, 2002 ) and taking
the lead in the green market (Wang et al., 2023 ). However, diversified
and heterogeneous knowledge resources increase the search cost and
innovation difficulty of firms (Katila & Ahuja, 2002 ; Sofka &
Grimpe, 2010 ; Yang et al., 2021 ). Furthermore, scholars emphasized
that knowledge acquisition from different search objects, such as
value-chain partners and industry –university partners, will lead to dif-
ferent impacts on firm innovation (Duong et al., 2022 ; Köhler
et al., 2012 ; Marchi, 2012 ). Specifically, knowledge acquisition from
value-chain partners can help firms deeply understand green market
demand and the latest trend (Ben Arfi et al., 2018 ; Demirel &
Kesidou, 2019 ), and knowledge acquisition from industry –university
partners can help firms quickly tackle the key core of green technolo-
gies (Ma et al., 2018 ). Thus, based on the search object, it is worth
classifying proactive boundary-spanning search into value-
chain-based proactive search and industry –university-based proactive
search and exploring how two types of proactive boundary-spanningsearch affect radical green innovation.
Under the new normal of VUCA, firms are facing a challenging,
fast-paced, and dynamic business environment (Long et al.,
2023 ).
This means that successful implementation of radical green innovationdepends not only on proactive boundary-spanning search but also on
firms' ability to adapt to external environments; otherwise, firms may
suffer innovation crises and operational disruptions (Akgün &Keskin,
2014 ; Bode & Macdonald, 2017 ). Thus, to effectively identify
and respond to external shocks, firms need to build resilience (Doet al., 2022 ; Duchek, 2020 ). Dynamic capabilities theory points out
that in a complicated and turbulent external environment, if heteroge-neous resources that build competitive advantages for firms are diffi-
cult to meet the needs of the competitive environment (Eisenhardt &
Martin,
2000 ), it will lead to core rigidity and constrain the sustainable
development of firms (Song et al., 2020 ). Organizational resilience, as
a kind of dynamic capability to withstand external environmental
shocks and quickly recover from them (Williams et al., 2017 ), is a key
source of long-term competitive advantage for firms (Teixeira &
Werther, 2013 ). Many scholars argue that firms with high resilience
are generally more proactive, flexible, and preventive (Jia et al., 2020 ;
Parker & Ameen, 2018 ; Williams & Shepherd, 2016 ; Yao et al., 2021 ),
as resilience can help firms timely identify emergencies and effectively
respond to uncertainties and shocks in the innovation process (Do
et al., 2022 ). This shows that firms can form organizational resilience
to adapt to the external environment through proactive boundary-
spanning search and thus promote radical green innovation. Even
though previous research on organizational resilience has identifiedseveral antecedents, including environmental shocks (Duchek,
2020 ;
Williams & Shepherd, 2016 ), organizational learning (Do et al., 2022 ;
Linnenluecke, 2017 ), social capital (Jia et al., 2020 ; Ma & Zhang, 2022 ;
Yu et al., 2023 ), and green supplier integration (Ji et al., 2020 ). It is still
unclear how proactive boundary-spanning search drives organiza-
tional resilience. Moreover, although extensive research has empha-sized that organizational resilience facilitates technological innovation(Do et al.,
2022 ; Kantur & Say, 2015 ; Richtnér & Löfsten, 2014 ;
Yang & Hsu, 2018 ) and environmental performance (Ji et al., 2020 ), it
has not been adequately explained how firms can enhance organiza-tional resilience by two types of proactive boundary-spanning search
to handle the challenges associated with radical green innovation.
Thus, whether and how organizational resilience mediates the impactof value-chain-based proactive search and industry –university-based
proactive search on radical green innovation needs to be further
explored.
In addition, with the rapid development of digital technologies,
big data has become a strategic resource that nurtures green innova-
tion (Al-Khatib,
2022 ; Xin et al., 2022 ). However, proactive boundary-
spanning search is only a means of acquiring knowledge; how toeffectively utilize the searched knowledge and resources mainly
depends on firms' internal capabilities (Feng & Sheng,
2023 ; Wang
et al., 2020 ). Dynamic capabilities theory suggests that firms' innova-
tion depends on their capabilities to effectively manage resources
(Helfat & Peteraf, 2003 ). As a kind of dynamic capability, big data ana-
lytics capability is becoming increasingly prominent in the digital econ-omy (Awan et al.,
2021 ; Gupta & George, 2016 ) and plays a vital role
in firms' sustainable development (El-Kassar & Singh, 2019 ; Sahoo
et al., 2023 ). Big data analytics capability is beneficial for firms to iden-
tify useful knowledge from voluminous data and orchestrate tangibleand intangible knowledge into specific knowledge resources required
for radical green innovation (Mikalef et al.,
2019 ; Wamba et al., 2017 ).
If firms have a high level of big data analytics capability, their effi-ciency in identifying and integrating external knowledge will improve
(Lin & Kunnathur,
2019 ; Mikalef & Krogstie, 2020 ; Song et al., 2018 )XUE and WANG 1835

and thus facilitate firms to conduct radical green innovation. Unfortu-
nately, previous studies mostly focused on factors such as resourceorchestration capability (Wang et al.,
2020 ), knowledge integration
capability (Sheng, 2017 ; Yang et al., 2021 ), absorptive capacity (Flor
et al., 2018 ; Pacheco et al., 2018 ; Wang et al., 2022 ), governmental
networking, and environment turbulence (Bao et al., 2021 ), moderated
the relationship between boundary-spanning search and green inno-
vation, and ignored the moderating role of big data analytics capabil-ity. Therefore, drawing on dynamic capabilities theory, it is

big data analytics capability and clarifying the impact mechanism ofvalue-chain-based proactive search and industry –university-based

To fill the above research gaps, drawing on the resource-based
view and dynamic capabilities theory, we investigate the influence oftwo types of proactive boundary-spanning search on radical green
innovation and explore the mediating role of organizational resilience
and the moderating role of big data analytics capability. This study hasthree main contributions as follows. First, we divide proactive
boundary-spanning search into value-chain-based proactive search
and industry –university-based proactive search and find that two
types of proactive boundary-spanning search are important drivers of
radical green innovation. Hence, our study expands the literature on
proactive boundary-spanning search and provides new drivers forachieving radical green innovation. Second, there are few studiesregarding organizational resilience as a mediator to link external
knowledge acquisition and green innovation. Grounded on dynamic
capabilities theory, we find the importance of organizational resilienceby revealing the different mediating roles it plays in the relationships
between value-chain-based proactive search, industry –university-
based proactive search, and radical green innovation. Third, consider-ing the digital economy, we identify the moderating role of big data
analytics capability in the relationship between value-chain-based pro-
active search, industry –university-based proactive search, and radical
green innovation, which can help us better understand the role of bigdata analytics capability in radical green innovation and contribute to
the big data analytics capability literature.
This paper is structured as follows. First, we develop the research
hypotheses. Second, we explain the methodology. Third, we provide
the data analysis and results. Finally, we discuss the results and pro-
pose the research implications and limitations.
2|RESEARCH HYPOTHESES
2.1 |Proactive boundary-spanning search and
radical green innovation
According to the resource-based view, firms with the best resources
and capabilities may gain comparative and competitive advantages
in environmental sustainability (Grant, 1991 ; Serrano-García
et al., 2021 ; Tariq et al., 2017 ). Compared with incremental green
innovation, radical green innovation has higher complexity,uncertainty and dual externalities (Dai et al., 2015 ; Martínez-Ros &
Kunapatarawong, 2019 ), and a greater demand for heterogeneous
resources. Therefore, firms are required to expand the scope of
knowledge and increase the stock of heterogeneous knowledge
resources (Pacheco et al., 2018 ; Srinivasan et al., 2021 ). Proactive
boundary-spanning search, as the behavior of a firm to search for
heterogeneous knowledge across borders ahead of its competitors
(O'Cass et al., 2014 ), can help firms obtain knowledge and informa-
tion needed for radical green innovation (Flor et al., 2018 ). The
knowledge includes not only green needs from customers (Ben Arfi
et al., 2018 ), environmentally-friendly raw materials from suppliers
(Cao et al., 2021 ), and the latest trends from partners and competi-
tors (Marchi, 2012 ), but also involves advanced technologies from
universities and research institutes (Hu et al., 2021 ), the green
development policies from governments (Zhang et al., 2022 ), and
the green market prospects of management consultancies (Yu
et al., 2020 ). Therefore, the essence of radical green innovation is a
continuous process of external knowledge acquisition, and firms canacquire diverse knowledge through communication and cooperation
with partners, such as customers, suppliers, other industries, universi-
ties, research institutes, governments, and management consultancies.
Existing research only explored the impact of proactive
boundary-spanning search on radical green innovation from the per-
spective of search timing (Wang et al.,
2023 ). However, it cannot
accurately represent the influence of knowledge from differentsources on radical green innovation. Firms need to deeply understand
the nature and effect of knowledge search to successfully implement
innovation (Katila & Ahuja,
2002 ; Köhler et al., 2012 ). Hence, the
degree of heterogeneity among various knowledge sources should
not be ignored. Considering search objects, we divide proactive
boundary-spanning search into value-chain-based proactive searchand industry –university-based proactive search. Value-chain-based
proactive search is the activity by which firms ahead of competitors
search knowledge from suppliers, customers, and other industries.While industry –university-based proactive search is the activity by
which firms ahead of competitors search knowledge from universities,
research institutes, governments, and management consultancies.
Value-chain-based proactive search is important for firms in
emerging economies such as China to achieve radical green innova-
tion, enabling firms to quickly identify innovation opportunities and
commercialize new ideas (Zhang et al.,
2017 ). It will help firms ahead
of competitors to prioritize green demand and market information
(Ben Arfi et al., 2018 ; Demirel & Kesidou, 2019 ) and develop new
green products, thereby facilitating radical green innovation. First,value-chain-based proactive search helps firms take the lead in new
markets and identify customer needs or potential market opportuni-
ties (Hoppmann et al.,
2021 ). This search improves firms' understand-
ing of current and potential customer needs and preferences, helpsfirms gain a deeper understanding of customer product usage and
substitution patterns (Ciampi et al.,
2021 ), and provides solutions for
potential problems with new products (Zhang et al., 2017 ). Thus, by
acquiring knowledge from value-chain partners, firms can develop
green products to meet market needs (Sidhu et al., 2007 ; Wang1836 XUE and WANG

et al., 2023 ), increase the updating speed of green products (Bode &
Macdonald, 2017 ), and then facilitate radical green innovation. Sec-
ond, value-chain knowledge is usually regarded as the source of ideas
for green innovation (Köhler et al., 2012 ; Srinivasan et al., 2021 ). Spe-
cifically, value-chain-based proactive search enables firms to conductactive market research or design green products to meet customer
needs (Wang et al.,
2019 ; Zhang et al., 2017 ), which will reduce the
risk of radical green innovation failure and increase market acceptanceof green products (Duong et al.,
2022 ; Sheng, 2017 ), thereby achiev-
ing radical green innovation. Third, value-chain partners can continu-
ously exchange information, develop capabilities, and learn from eachother, which provides firms with better information on demandchanges and new product launches (Sofka & Grimpe,
2010 ). In this
way, by conducting value-chain-based proactive search, firms can tar-
get the correct market segments, make appropriate strategic decisionsin product R&D, production, and marketing (Flor et al.,
2018 ;
Marchi, 2012 ), and reduce the risk of entering the green market and
the risk of green innovation failure, thus enhancing radical green inno-vation. Based on this, we propose the following hypothesis:
H1a. Value-chain-based proactive search has a positive
impact on radical green innovation.
Since the acquisition of heterogeneous resources provides more
possibilities for firms to enhance green innovation (Hu et al.,
2021 ),
we think that industry –university-based proactive search may have a
positive impact on radical green innovation. Specifically, on the one
hand, industry –university-based proactive search can help a firm
ahead of its competitors master core key technologies (Ma
et al., 2018 ) and develop advanced green materials and cutting-edge
green technologies (Duong et al., 2022 ). Moreover, this search can
improve firms' understanding of cutting-edge frontier technology,
help firms master intangible knowledge that is difficult to copy, and
make use of it before competitors (Long et al., 2023 ). Thus, by acquir-
ing knowledge from universities, research institutes, governments,and management consultancies, firms can identify not only informa-
tion about green development policies and subsidies to discover new
applications of green technology knowledge in other fields(Marchi,
2012 ; Wang et al., 2020 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ), but also exca-
vate new environmental information and skills to fill their knowledge
gaps and promote joint research on key core technologies (Liu, 2021 ;
Ma et al., 2018 ; Yang et al., 2021 ), thereby promoting radical green
innovation. On the other hand, knowledge from industries and univer-
sities is recognized as an important source of green technology(Köhler et al.,
2012 ; Srinivasan et al., 2021 ). Industry –university
knowledge can enhance not only the heterogeneous knowledge
reserve of firms and upgrade and update firms' existing green innova-tion capabilities (Zhang & Liu,
2014 ), but also help firms focus their
search on the most possible areas to reduce trial-and-error costs (Hu
et al., 2021 ). In addition, industry –university knowledge enables firms
to find the best resource allocation scheme, help firms break throughexisting technology trajectories (Duong et al.,
2022 ; Pacheco
et al., 2018 ), and create new green technologies, skills, and practices(Snihur & Wiklund, 2019 ; Zhou et al., 2023 ), thus facilitating radical
green innovation. Based on this, the following assumption isproposed:
H1b. Industry –university-based proactive search has a
positive impact on radical green innovation.
2.2 |Proactive boundary-spanning search and
organizational resilience
The word “resilience ”first appeared in the fields of ecology, psychol-
ogy, and engineering (Home & Orr, 1997 ; Meyer, 1982 ). With the
increasing complexity of the external environment and the uncertainty
of competitive markets, scholars have begun to introduce resilienceinto the management field (Carden et al.,
2018 ; Dutta, 2017 ; Williams
et al., 2017 ). At present, most research on organizational resilience is
based on dynamic and static perspectives. Under the dynamic per-spective, organizational resilience is a capability formed by the long-
term, continuous, and dynamic experience of firms, which can help
firms be more immune and better adjust to environmental shocks(Linnenluecke,
2017 ; Ortiz-de-Mandojana & Bansal, 2016 ). Under the
static perspective, organizational resilience is a firm's characteristic
that maintains active adaptability in today's challenging environment(Gao et al.,
2017 ) and recovers, survives, and even further develops in
the face of a crisis (DesJardine et al., 2019 ). In this study, we focus on
the former perspective and consider organizational resilience as a kind
of capability that can help firms be alert to changes, cope with the dis-turbance of uncertain events in crises, and gradually achieve self-
regulation, immunity, and recovery. According to dynamic capabilities
theory, in order to survive and develop, firms need to continuouslyacquire heterogeneous knowledge to ensure that resources can
dynamically match environmental changes (Teece et al.,
1997 ). In this
way, firms can better cope with unexpected shocks in a complex andchangeable environment, thereby achieving sustainable development(Eisenhardt & Martin,
2000 ; Helfat et al., 2009 ). Proactive
boundary-spanning search, as a behavior of leading competitors to
gain heterogeneous knowledge across organizational and technologi-cal boundaries, can promote the evolution and development of orga-
nizational resilience in uncertain environments. It provides resources
for firms to dynamically match environmental changes, enabling firmsto rapidly identify and respond to potential crisis events, thereby
recovering from them (Ji et al.,
2020 ; Ortiz-de-Mandojana &
Bansal, 2016 ). Therefore, we propose that proactive boundary-
spanning search is beneficial to organizational resilience.
Value-chain-based proactive search can help firms ahead of com-
petitors better understand the changes in value-chain demand(Duchek,
2020 ) and choose value-chain knowledge that best matches
the current external environment for decoding and absorption, which
helps firms dynamically match market changes. Thus, value-
chain-based proactive search can enhance organizational resilience infour aspects. First, since firms need knowledge and information to
generate creative and innovative ideas to respond to potentialXUE and WANG 1837

environmental accidents or problems (Cheng & Lu, 2017 ), value-
chain-based proactive search can facilitate the transfer of valuableinformation and knowledge by communicating between firms and
customers, suppliers, and other industries (Ji et al.,
2020 ). Good inter-
action, communication, and cooperation make it easier for firms toidentify and utilize potential resources, facilitate more effective
resource allocation and knowledge transfer among partners, and form
reasonable and effective risk sharing (Jia et al.,
2020 ), thereby alleviat-
ing risks and enhancing organizational resilience. Second, value-
chain-based proactive search is an advantage in resolving conflicts
between firms and value-chain partners (Ben Arfi et al., 2018 ;D u
et al., 2018 ), which in turn facilitates firms to improve their effi-
ciency in dealing with environmental issues. In this way, firms can
build organizational resilience. Third, value-chain-based proactive
search is an interactive process, and its advantages originate fromthe practice of building mutual understanding and solving problems
together (Jia et al.,
2020 ). By building partnerships with other value-
chain partners, firms can quickly perceive risks when crisis eventsoccur (Bao et al.,
2021 ), orchestrate external resources in a short
time to solve emergencies (Barasa et al., 2018 ; Sheng, 2017 ), and
then enhance organizational resilience (Cheng & Lu, 2017 ;Y u
et al., 2023 ). Forth, value-chain-based proactive search enables firms
to prepare for and adapt to crises in advance, launch various emer-
gency plans (Bode & Macdonald, 2017 ), minimize the impact of the
external environment, and foster organizational resilience, therebyaccelerating organizational resilience. Thus, we proposed the follow-
ing hypothesis:
H2a. Value-chain-based proactive search has a positive
impact on organizational resilience.
Industry –university-based proactive search can help firms grasp
the development trend of green technology more quickly (Hu
et al.,
2021 ) and understand the latest government policies and sup-
port information ahead of competitors, thus reducing trial-and-errorcosts and building organizational resilience (Zhang et al.,
2022 ). First,
in order to withstand and recover from external environmental
shocks, firms need to generate radical ideas (Richtnér &Löfsten,
2014 ). Through industry –university-based proactive search,
firms can actively commercialize innovative achievements (Do
et al., 2022 ; Wang et al., 2022 ), enhance product added value and
competitive advantages (Flor et al., 2018 ; Yang et al., 2021 ), and then
cope with turbulent market environments and promote organizational
resilience (Kantur & Say, 2015 ). Second, industry –university-based
proactive search is a learning process, and its advantages come from
joint R&D and joint solutions of key core technologies (Long
et al., 2023 ). By establishing communication and learning relationships
with universities, research institutes, governments, and managementconsultancies, firms can acquire not only knowledge to strengthen
organizational resilience and accumulate sufficient experience to cope
with future emergencies (Barasa et al.,
2018 ; Dutta, 2017 ), but also
identify problems in time, make up for deficiencies, and quickly
recover to a better state than before the crisis (Duchek, 2020 ),thereby boosting organizational resilience. Based on this, the follow-
ing assumption is proposed:
H2b. Industry –university-based proactive search has a
positive impact on organizational resilience.
2.3 |Organizational resilience and radical green
innovation
Since incremental green innovation is not enough to cope with the
turbulent environment, current research believes that radical greeninnovation, as an innovation with both attributes of radical innovation
and green innovation (Shang et al.,
2020 ), should be taken as a key
strategy for firms to realize sustainable development in thepost-epidemic era (Cui & Wang,
2022 ). Radical green innovation
technology is more difficult, more complex, and more uncertain than
incremental green innovation (Liao & Zhang, 2020 ) and can signifi-
cantly enhance the green competitive advantage of firms (Kiefer
et al., 2019 ). Therefore, to realize radical green innovation, firms need
not only to subvert the original business form and achieve higher-levelinnovation based on customer consumption patterns (Bhatia,
2021 ),
but also need to consider ecological and environmental benefits (Tariq
et al., 2017 ). Dynamic capabilities theory emphasizes that firms must
deploy their resources and match their capabilities with the changingexternal environment to ensure competitiveness (Teece,
2018 ). Orga-
nizational resilience, as an important way for firms to timely respond
to external environmental shocks (Duchek, 2020 ; Hillmann &
Guenther, 2021 ), can help firms recover from crises and leapfrog
development (Dutta, 2017 ). Therefore, we propose that organizational
resilience can effectively facilitate radical green innovation.
First, organizational resilience can help firms capture small infor-
mation and threats in complex environments (Duchek, 2020 ), identify
potential risks in advance, take proactive early warning measures toreduce the cost of green innovation, and then shorten the R&D cycleof green products (Cheng & Lu,
2017 ). In this way, firms can optimize
environmental management practices and mitigate the negative
impact of innovation risk and crisis (Akgün & Keskin, 2014 ), thereby
promoting radical green innovation. Second, organizational resilience
can help not only firms recombine their reserves according to environ-
mental changes and optimize production processes in real time duringoperations (Ji et al.,
2020 ), but also reduce the use of natural
resources and environmental pollution and timely recover renewable
resources (Jia et al., 2020 ). As a result, firms can reduce the negative
impact of their products and operations on the natural environment
(Huang & Li, 2017 ), achieve the coordination of economic develop-
ment and environmental protection, and then enhance radical greeninnovation. Third, organizational resilience can help firms identifypotential development opportunities and new growth and quickly
enter potential markets. In this way, firms can detect not only changes
in customer green demand (Ben Arfi et al.,
2018 ) and track innovation
trends in their industry (Parker & Ameen, 2018 ), but also introduce
green technologies, materials, and knowledge and upgrade and iterate1838 XUE and WANG

the attributes and R&D processes of existing green products (Bode &
Macdonald, 2017 ), thus realizing radical green innovation. Based on
this, we propose the following assumption:
H3. Organizational resilience has a positive impact on
radical green innovation.
2.4 |The mediating role of organizational
resilience
According to the resource-based view, external knowledge acquisition
is the core process to help firms develop organizational resilience and
innovation advantages (Wang & Ellinger, 2011 ). Since firms' internal
resources are limited, firms must access external resources across bor-ders to find alternatives and solutions (Yang et al.,
2021 ; Zhang
et al., 2017 ). As a result, firms that pay more attention to proactive
boundary-spanning search can seize market demand and enhancegreen innovation capabilities. Under the VUCA environment, although
firms can acquire heterogeneous knowledge needed for radical green
innovation through proactive boundary-spanning search, heteroge-neous resources are not sufficient to cope with external environmen-
tal shocks (Do et al.,
2022 ). Firms also need to foster organizational
resilience by proactive boundary-spanning search to identify and dealwith dynamic and complex business environments, thus promotingradical green innovation.
Specifically, by conducting value-chain-based proactive search,
firms can improve their sensitivity to changes in the demand and per-formance of environmental protection products in the green market
(Bode & Macdonald,
2017 ). In this way, firms can identify and solve
problems in green innovation (Cheng & Lu, 2017 ), such as poor com-
munication across departments, delayed information feedback, low
organizational efficiency, and decreased customer satisfaction
(Marchi, 2012 ), thus fostering organizational resilience. Moreover,
organizational resilience can help firms predict future disasters moreaccurately (Parker & Ameen,
2018 ) and buffer the negative impacts of
crises through the reconfiguration of internal and external resources.
Accordingly, firms can improve not only their early warning ability andhelp them identify customers' green needs and potential green market
(Jia et al.,
2020 ; Yao et al., 2021 ), but also promote them to effectively
explore new solutions and timely develop new green products tomeet market needs (Akgün & Keskin,
2014 ; Yu et al., 2023 ), thus
enhancing radical green innovation. Therefore, we propose the follow-
ing hypothesis:
H4a. Organizational resilience mediates the effect of
value-chain-based proactive search on radical greeninnovation.
Industry –university-based proactive search is beneficial for firms
to acquire industry –university knowledge ahead of competitors, and
rich knowledge is conducive to firms accurately identifying the devel-
opment trend of advanced green technologies (Hu et al.,
2021 ;J iet al., 2020 ). In this way, firms can timely respond to crises and build
organizational resilience (Linnenluecke, 2017 ). When facing emergen-
cies, firms with organizational resilience can quickly identify potential
crises, develop temporary solutions to cope with sudden crises
(Kendra & Wachtendorf, 2003 ), and provide creative solutions for cri-
ses (Cheng & Lu, 2017 ). Thus, firms can timely capture and prevent
not only the emergence of potential threats (Bode &
Macdonald, 2017 ), but also break through existing technology trajec-
tories, promote R&D of green technologies by optimizing resource
reserves (Ji et al., 2020 ), and quickly respond to the opportunities and
challenges brought by crises (Do et al., 2022 ; Ortiz-de-Mandojana &
Bansal, 2016 ), thereby realizing radical green innovation. Hence, we
develop the following assumption:
H4b. Organizational resilience mediates the effect of
industry –university-based proactive search on radical
green innovation.
2.5 |The moderating role of big data analytics
capability
Big data analytics capability is the information processing capability of
firms to transform resources into competitive advantages throughdata management, data infrastructure, and data talent (Mikalefet al.,
2019 ; Wamba et al., 2017 ). The resource-based view holds that
innovation is an activity that firms effectively acquire, manage, and
apply heterogeneous resources to create value (Grant & Baden-Fuller,
2004 ). In the area of digital interconnection, big data analytics
capability enables firms to use big data resources to evaluate, process,
and generate value (Gupta & George, 2016 ; Shan et al., 2019 ), which
can help firms enhance internal procedures, operations, and functions
and better identify opportunities and apply resources (Singh & Del
Giudice, 2019 ). Thus, we argue that firms with a high level of big data
analytics capability can better capture the direction of green products,adapt more quickly to changing environments, and apply green tech-
nology knowledge and green innovation resources (Al-Khatib,
2022 ),
thereby enhancing radical green innovation.
On the one hand, with a high level of big data analytics capability,
firms can expand and deepen their relationships with customers, sup-
pliers, and other industries. In this way, the scope of knowledgesearch will be expanded (Mikalef & Pateli,
2017 ), and the degree of
resource management will be improved (Wamba et al., 2017 ). Thus,
firms can develop, deploy, and reallocate more green innovationresources by boosting internal processes, business operations, and
financial and capital effectiveness (Awan et al.,
2021 ), and then
enhance radical green innovation. Moreover, with a high level of bigdata analytics capability, firms can better analyze the demand direc-tion of green products in a fast-paced business environment (Shan
et al.,
2019 ; Xin et al., 2022 ) and accurately predict consumers' green
behavior and green consumption patterns (Sahoo et al., 2023 ;X u
et al., 2016 ), thereby boosting radical green innovation. On the con-
trary, with a low level of big data analytics capability, firms may find itXUE and WANG 1839

difficult to discover and use value-chain knowledge during innovation
(Dubey et al., 2021 ). This may hamper firms from grasping green
demands in a rapidly changing and complex environment (Al-
Khatib, 2022 ), thereby restricting radical green innovation. Thus, when
the level of big data analytics capability is high, value-chain-based pro-active search has a stronger positive effect on radical green innova-
tion. Based on this, we propose the following hypothesis:
H5a. Big data analytics capability positively moderates
the relationship between value-chain-based proactive
search and radical green innovation.
On the other hand, with a high level of big data analytics capabil-
ity, firms can analyze and evaluate more knowledge acquired from
universities, research institutes, governments, and management con-sultancies. Rich industry –university knowledge will help firms better
cope with the risks associated with radical green innovation and
increase their success. Thus, with a high level of big data analyticscapability, firms can effectively identify green technology knowledge
in industries, universities, and research institutions (Akhtar
et al.,
2018 ; Lin & Kunnathur, 2019 ) and accurately assess the feasibil-
ity and potential risks of green innovative solutions (El-Kassar &
Singh, 2019 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ), so as to boost radical green innova-
tion. Moreover, with a high level of big data analytics capability, firmscan better monitor the development trend of green technology andtrack the use and reuse of green materials (Sahoo et al.,
2023 ), which
will help firms optimize resource usage and minimize waste, thereby
enhancing radical green innovation (Riggs et al., 2023 ). On the con-
trary, with a low level of big data analytics capability, firms may be
unable to identify the reliability and authenticity of external informa-
tion, leading to the risk of information leakage (Papadopouloset al.,
2017 ). Due to the increased risks of radical green innovation, it
will be difficult for firms to break the existing trajectory of green tech-
nology and engage in radical green innovation (El-Kassar &Singh,
2019 ; Mikalef & Krogstie, 2020 ). Accordingly, when the level ofbig data analytics capability is high, industry –university-based proac-
tive search has a stronger positive effect on radical green innovation.Hence, we make the following assumption:
H5b. Big data analytics capability positively moderates
the relationship between industry –university-based pro-
active search and radical green innovation.

3|METHOD
3.1 |Data collection
The manufacturing industry, as an important pillar industry in China, is
also the industry with serious pollution. Under the strategy of “carbon
peaking and carbon neutrality, ”greater demands are being put for-
ward for the green development of manufacturing firms (Shang
et al., 2020 ). However, faced with the impact of the environment and
the dual pressures of external turbulent and environmental protection
(Zhang et al., 2020 ), how manufacturing firms can successfully carry
out radical green innovation has become an urgent problem to besolved. In order to obtain a typical sample, we selected manufacturingfirms located in Guangdong, Jiangsu, Shaanxi, Yunnan, and Zhejiang.
Specifically, Guangdong is located in the Pearl River Delta, and Jiangsu
and Zhejiang are located in the Yangtze River Delta. These provincesreflect a high level of economic development. Shaanxi is located in the
northwest of China and belongs to the Silk Road Economic Belt, with
a medium level of economic development. Yunnan is located in thesouthwest of China and has a relatively low level of economic devel-
opment. Manufacturing firms in the above provinces vary in their
levels of radical green innovation, representing different levels of eco-nomic development and culture.

3.2 |Measurement

measurement items and invited managers to propose modifications toinappropriate items. Based on managers' feedback, we revised those
unclear and ambiguous items, making sure that each item was under-
standable and relevant to the firms' actual situation. Finally, with thehelp of two professors who have research experience abroad, we
translated the questionnaire back into English. The final questionnaire
is composed of two parts: the background information of the surveyfirms and the content of the survey. The main variables were mea-
sured using a Likert 5 scale, and 1 –5 points are assigned from strongly
disagree tostrongly agree .

3.3 |Reliability and validity analysis
First, the reliability of the scale was evaluated by calculating
Cronbach's alpha and composite reliability (CR), and the results are
shown in Table 2. The values of Cronbach's alpha and CR are greater
than 0.7 (Fornell & Larcker, 1981 ; Nunnally, 1978 ), which shows that
the scale has good reliability. Second, by referring to existingliterature, expert interviews, and pre-surveys, the content validity of
the questionnaire was ensured. Third, by conducting confirmatoryfactor analysis, the factor loading of each variable is greater than 0.5
and average variance extracted (AVE) is greater than 0.5, which
demonstrated a high level of convergent validity. As for thediscriminant validity, it can be seen from the results in Table
3that
the fitting values of the five-factor model are good ( x2/df=2.592,
RMSEA =0.074, CFI =0.913, TLI =0.901) and significantly better
than other factor models. In addition, as shown in Table 4, the square
root of AVE for each variable is larger than the correlation coefficient
between the corresponding variables, indicating that the scale hasgood discriminant validity.
3.4 |Nonresponse bias and common method
To assess nonresponse bias, early and late respondents were divided
into two categories based on the return order of the questionnaire,and the t-test was analyzed on 50 early and 50 late respondents
(Dennehy et al.,
2021 ). The results showed that there are no signifi-
cant differences in firm scale, firm age, and R&D intensity betweenthe two groups ( p> .05). As a result, this study has no significant non-
response bias. At the same time, the study used two methods to test
common method bias. First, Harman's single-factor test was used toput all the items of key variables together for factor analysis. Theresults showed that the maximum factor variance was 37.502%, less
than 40% (Hair et al.,
2016 ), indicating that there is no serious prob-
lem of common method bias. Second, as indicated in Table 3, confir-
matory factor analysis was conducted to obtain the one-factor fitting
model ( x2/df=9.494, RMSEA =0.170, CFI =0.514, TLI =0.468).
Compared with other factor models, the one-factor model has theworst fit, further indicating that the problem of common method bias
is not serious.

5|DISCUSSION, CONCLUSION, AND
LIMITATION
5.1 |Discussion
Combining resource-based view and dynamic capabilities theory, this
study explores the relationship between two types of proactive
boundary-spanning search and radical green innovation, and investi-
gates the mediating effect of organizational resilience and the moder-ating effect of big data analytics capability.
First, the results show that both value-chain-based proactive
search and industry –university-based proactive search have positive
effects not only on radical green innovation but also on organizational
resilience. The findings support the conclusion of Ben Arfi et al.
(
2018 ), who argued that different types of external knowledge, such
as knowledge gained from universities, research institutions, firms,
and consumers, have different effects on green innovation. Our
results are also partially consistent with the research of Zhang et al.(
2017 ), who confirmed that boundary-spanning demand-side search
guides firms to develop advanced technologies to meet green needs,
and expand the research of Yang et al. ( 2021 ), who suggested that
moderate proactive boundary-spanning search can help firms developgreen innovation. According to the search object, we further divide
proactive boundary-spanning search into value-chain-based proactivesearch and industry –university-based proactive search. This is
because, as the diversity of externally available knowledge increases,it becomes more difficult for firms to search for and integrate knowl-
edge (Köhler et al.,
2012 ; Marchi, 2012 ). Boundary-spanning search
based on different search objects is conducive to improving thesearch efficiency of firms and reducing search costs and innovation
risk (Duong et al.,
2022 ; Zhang et al., 2017 ). We find that it is benefi-
cial for firms to dynamically match the changes of the external envi-ronment and cultivate organizational resilience through proactive
boundary-spanning search. This is consistent with Duchek (
2020 ),
who suggested that internal and external knowledge resources areparticularly significant for building organizational resilience. Our find-ing also extends the study of Cheng and Lu (
2017 ), who proposed that
the ability of firms to actively acquire knowledge from partners can
ensure the survival and development of firms, thus forming organiza-tional resilience.
Second, our results indicate that organizational resilience posi-
tively influences radical green innovation. Specifically, organizationalresilience can guide firms to identify, withstand, and recover from
innovation crises by dynamically matching heterogeneous resources
with a competitive environment. Previous research studies on organi-zational resilience and firms' innovation mainly regarded innovation as
a single dimension, which may obscure the impact of organizational
resilience on different innovations, especially radical green innovation.We prove that organizational resilience is crucial for firms to realizeradical green innovation. Therefore, our result supports the argument
of Kantur and Say (
2015 ) and Richtnér and Löfsten ( 2014 ), who
believed that organizational resilience plays an important role in cop-ing with innovation risks and improving firms' creativity. Taking radical
green innovation into account, our finding is partially consistent with
the conclusion of Ji et al. (
2020 ), who emphasized that organizational
resilience can promote the sustainable development of firms.
Third, our results reveal that organizational resilience mediates
the relationship between value-chain-based proactive search,industry –university-based proactive search, and radical green innova-
tion. Specifically, organizational resilience fully mediates not only the
relationship between value-chain-based proactive search and radical
green innovation, but also partially mediates the relationshipbetween industry –university-based proactive search and radical
green innovation. This is consistent with the research of Do et al.
(
2022 ), who suggested that organizational resilience plays a mediat-
ing role between organizational learning and innovation. We find
that value-chain-based proactive search has an indirect effect on
radical green innovation, and organizational resilience plays a fullymediating role. The possible reason is that green market demand
changes faster in the VUCA environment, and knowledge obtained
by value-chain-based proactive search, such as green group thinking,expectations, consumption preferences, and competitors' conditions,is more susceptible to the external environment. Hence, value-
chain-based proactive search affects organizational resilience, and
organizational resilience further affects radical green innovation. Ourresearch strengthens the opinion that external knowledge acquisition
is conducive to building organizational resilience and explains how1846 XUE and WANG

firms can leverage heterogeneous knowledge to facilitate radical
green innovation.
Fourth, our results show that big data analytics capability plays a
moderating role in the relationship between industry –university-
based proactive search and radical green innovation. The finding sup-ports the results of Al-Khatib (
2022 ), Mikalef et al. ( 2019 ), and Wamba
et al. ( 2017 ), who proposed that big data analytics capability can
accelerate the transformation of existing knowledge into firm perfor-mance and competitive advantage. The finding is also consistent with
Xin et al. (
2022 ), who argued that firms can acquire complementary
green resources and knowledge more effectively with a high level ofbig data analytics capability. This is because most industry –university
knowledge obtained from universities, research institutes, govern-
ments, and management consultancies is theoretical and standardized
(Köhler et al.,
2012 ). In other words, industry –university knowledge
has greater technical difficulty and a longer technological cycle. If
firms want to apply this kind of knowledge frequently, they must rely
on big data analytics capability. With a high level of big data analyticscapability, firms can effectively transform and apply industry –
university knowledge to better carry out radical green innovation.
While we find that big data analytics capability does not moderate thelink between value-chain-based proactive search and radical green
innovation, which is partially opposite with Ciampi et al. (
2021 ), who
found that big data analytics capability can strengthen the relationshipbetween firms and other stakeholders and help firms create bettervalue. One possible explanation is that most knowledge acquired from
customers, suppliers, and other industry firms is value-chain-based
knowledge, which has a greater impact on maintaining sustainablecompetitive advantage. This also explains the research of Ciampi et al.
(
2021 ), who argued that big data analytics capability spurs innovation
to enhance firms' competitive advantage. Moreover, the synergybetween knowledge provided by value-chain partners and the existing
knowledge base is the strongest (Ben Arfi et al.,
2018 ). Thus, knowl-
edge from value-chain partners is more actionable, which makes iteasier for firms to commercialize. As a result, value-chain-based pro-active search can drive radical green innovation regardless of the level
of big data analytics capability. Therefore, big data analytics capability
has no significant moderating effect on the relationship betweenvalue-chain-based proactive search and radical green innovation.
5.2 |Theoretical implications
This study contributes to the literature in several ways. First, prior
studies on proactive boundary-spanning search mainly focused on
search timing (Wang et al., 2023 ; Yang et al., 2021 ) and paid less
attention to search objects. Although scholars suggest that where tosearch for valuable knowledge is crucial for firms to innovate(Duong et al.,
2022 ; Köhler et al., 2012 ; Zhang et al., 2017 ), the
impact of different search objects on radical green innovation is still
unclear. By considering search objects, we distinguish two types ofproactive boundary-spanning search, namely, value-chain-basedproactive search and industry –university-based proactive search, to
deeply understand proactive boundary-spanning search. Thus, thisstudy enriches the dimensions of proactive boundary-spanning
search. Furthermore, despite the fact that existing studies have con-
centrated on the factors driving green innovation (Bhatia,
2021 ;
Zhang et al., 2020 ), the literature on radical green innovation with
high technology is still limited. From search object and proactive
behavior perspectives, this study regards value-chain-based proac-tive search and industry –university-based proactive search as key
drivers of radical green innovation. Hence, these findings further
supplement not only the research on proactive boundary-spanningsearch by considering search objects, but also offer a novel view-point to comprehend the drivers of radical green innovation.
Second, our results unfold the “black box ”in the relationship
between value-chain-based proactive search, industry –university-
based proactive search, and radical green innovation by investigating
the mediating role of organizational resilience. Although existing
research has focused on the direct effect of external knowledgeacquisition on radical green innovation (Chen et al.,
2014 ; Zhang
et al., 2020 ), it is still unclear what mechanism and how value-
chain-based proactive search and industry –university-based proactive
search affect radical green innovation. Due to the uncertainty of the
external environment, it is increasingly common for firms to face
shocks (Linnenluecke, 2017 ; Williams & Shepherd, 2016 ). Therefore,
this study emphasizes the necessity of organizational resilience. Basedon dynamic capabilities theory, we explore how organizational resil-
ience affects the relationship between value-chain-based proactive
search, industry –university-based proactive search, and radical green
innovation. The results show that organizational resilience plays a fully
mediating role between value-chain-based proactive search and radi-
cal green innovation, and plays a partial mediating role betweenindustry –university-based proactive search and radical green innova-
tion. This study elucidates not only the role of organizational resil-
ience, but also provides new insights for us to understand howdifferent types of proactive boundary-spanning search affect radicalgreen innovation.
Third, this study highlights the role of big data analytics capability
and uncovers that the relationship between industry –university-based
proactive search and radical green innovation is moderated by big
data analytics capability. Although some scholars confirmed that
big data analytics capability contributes to resource acquisition andintegration (Al-Khatib,
2022 ; Gupta & George, 2016 ), there is little
research on how big data analytics capability can moderate the
relationship between value-chain-based proactive search, industry –
university-based proactive search, and radical green innovation.
Drawing on dynamic capabilities theory, we find that big data analyt-
ics capability positively moderates the relationship between industry –
university-based proactive search and radical green innovation. Thisresult expands not only the moderating factors that influence the
relationship between industry –university-based proactive search and
radical green innovation, but also uncovers how big data analysiscapability can strengthen the impact of industry –university-basedXUE and WANG 1847

proactive search on radical green innovation and expands the applica-
tion field of dynamic capabilities theory.
5.3 |Managerial implications
Our results provide some managerial implications for manufacturing
firms, universities, and policymakers. First, we find that organizationalresilience can help firms actively evaluate and digest heterogeneous
knowledge and dynamically match changes in the external environ-
ment, so that firms can successfully carry out radical green innovation.Hence, in order to survive in the volatile environment, especiallyunder the new normal of VUCA, manufacturing firms need to
strengthen their early warning and response capabilities for crisis
events. Specifically, firms need not only to smooth informationexchange channels and establish a real-time warning system to help
firms monitor early crisis signals and timely detect abnormal events,
but also to strengthen employees' crisis awareness and improveemployees' emergency management capabilities. In this way, firms can
quickly develop detailed emergency plans in the face of emergencies.
Moreover, we find that with the help of big data analytics capability,manufacturing firms can efficiently integrate and apply resources and
improve radical green innovation. Therefore, we suggest manufactur-
ing firms should pay close attention to the development of big datatechnology and the cultivation of big data talents. Specifically, firmsneed not only to strengthen R&D intensity in the field of big data and
actively develop or purchase big data technology products, but also
need to increase training in business intelligence, data mining, andbasic statistics and recruit talents with good technical and manage-
ment skills in big data analytics, hence optimizing firms' ability to use
big data to support decision-making.
Second, we find that proactive boundary-spanning search is a
critical driver for promoting radical green innovation. It is suggested
that effective external knowledge acquisition can alleviate resourcebottlenecks in the innovation process, which is vital for manufacturingfirms to realize radical green innovation. Therefore, as an important
objective of proactive boundary-spanning search, universities should
actively participate in green cooperation activities betweenmanufacturing firms and other stakeholders. Specifically, on the one
hand, universities can actively develop green invention patents
through funds established by firms. This can guide universities notonly to participate in green innovation through industry –university
cooperation, but also to introduce and cultivate high-tech talents for
firms through joint training. On the other hand, universities can regu-larly hold academic seminars to promote resource integration and
information sharing and facilitate radical green innovation. In this way,
universities can grasp the development trend of green technologythrough in-depth communication with other stakeholders, therebyaccelerating joint research on key technologies and the transforma-
tion of scientific research achievements.
Third, our research also has practical implications for policy-
makers. Although the cost and risk of radical green innovation are rel-
atively high, it can bring greater technological leaps and potentialsocial benefits to firms. Thus, as the maker of laws, regulations, and
policies, the government should increase its support for green innova-tion and actively create a better policy environment for sustainable
development. The government can strengthen financial support for
green development and encourage firms to invest more resources inradical green innovation through tax deductions and exemptions and
government subsidies. Meanwhile, to better encourage radical green
innovation, the government should improve the market mechanism,optimize the technology exchange environment, and strengthen the
protection of green intellectual property by enforcing stricter regula-
tions. In this way, managers will increase investment confidence andguide manufacturing firms to carry out radical green innovation.
5.4 |Limitations and future research
The limitations of this study offer a number of options for further
research. First, we selected manufacturing firms as research objects,which is an important pillar industry in China. Under the strategy of
“carbon peaking and carbon neutrality goals, ”the sustainable devel-
opment of manufacturing firms is supervised and supported by vari-ous ministries and commissions. Thus, our research results may have
certain industry limitations, and the generalization needs to be further
tested. Future research should collect relevant data from differentcountries or industries to improve the generalizability of the research.Meanwhile, although our research took firm size as a control variable,
we found that it has no impact on radical green innovation. Therefore,
future research can explore this relationship in other industries.Besides, our research does not consider the business model, and
future research should discuss the differences between various busi-
ness models in terms of radical green innovation.
Second, this research only reveals the mediating role of organi-
zational resilience and the moderating role of big data analytics
capability. Since radical green innovation is a result of many factors(Su et al.,
2022 ), it is necessary to seek other potential variables that
may influence the relationship between value-chain-based proactive
search, industry –university-based proactive search, and radical green
innovation. Some scholars emphasized that effective resource man-agement also contributes to sustainable development (Cui &
Wang,
2022 ; Long et al., 2023 ). Thus, future research can consider
the mediating role of green knowledge integration and environmen-tal resource orchestration. Meanwhile, due to the complex and
changeable external environment, the impact of external environ-
mental variables on radical green innovation should not be ignored.Future research can explore the moderating role of environmental
competitiveness, market attractiveness, and the institutional environ-
ment, which can help us better understand the benefits and risks ofradical green innovation.
Third, proactive boundary-spanning search is not a perfect search,
and excessive search may lead to the risk of distraction. Specifically, in
order to occupy the green market, firms may spend more time,energy, and financial resources to acquire and learn heterogeneous
knowledge. This will distract managers' attention, causing firms to be1848 XUE and WANG

unable to timely match new green knowledge with changes in the
dynamic environment, thereby hindering the sustainable developmentof firms to a certain extent (Yang et al.,
2021 ). Therefore, future
research should consider the possible negative effects of two types of
proactive boundary-spanning search and explore how firms can avoidsuch risks in radical green innovation.
