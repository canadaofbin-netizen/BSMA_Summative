Organizational inertia ABSTRACT  
In the present study, we introduce CEO curiosity as a new personality dimension of CEOs to explain corporate 
strategy. Prior research has implied that CEO curiosity may play a critical role in firm innovation. However, 
exploration linking curiosity at the executive level to organizational innovation is surprisingly lacking. To this 
end, we aim to examine the impact of CEO curiosity on firm innovation. Based on a sample of 563 Chinese firms, 
we found that CEO curiosity positively influences firm innovation via boundary-spanning search, while orga-
nizational inertia weakens both the direct effect of CEO curiosity on boundary-spanning search and its indirect 
effect on firm innovation. With this study we contribute to both the upper echelons theory and the micro -
foundation of innovation and offer new insights into the study of curiosity by unveiling the understudied but 
important role of CEO curiosity in firm innovation.   
1.Introduction 
In a rapidly changing business environment, CEOs face more changes 
than ever before, and grappling with the unfamiliar has become the 
norm. In this context, curiosity, defined as the desire to acquire new 
knowledge and information that leads to exploratory behaviors (Ber-
lyne, 1966 ; Harrison et al., 2011 ; Litman, 2005 ; Mussel, 2013 ), has been 
recognized as an increasingly important personality trait for CEOs to 
succeed in such challenging times (Gutschi and Klarner, 2021 ; Yu, 
2021 ). Many CEOs have emphasized the importance of curiosity and 
believe it is a shared personality of successful CEOs (Canning, 2013 ). For 
example, when Michael Dell, the chief executive of Dell, Inc., was asked 
what attributes CEOs will need most in turbulent times, he replied, “I 
would say curiosity. ” He noted that curiosity can inspire leaders to 
continually seek out the fresh ideas and approaches needed to keep pace 
with change and stay ahead of competitors. Alan D. Wilson, chief ex-
ecutive of McCormick & Company, noted, “Business leaders who are 
always broadening their horizons and knowledge, and who are naturally 
curious, are more likely to succeed ” (Berger, 2022 ). 
Although CEO curiosity has been highlighted as increasingly 
important for corporate success in business practice, there has been little 
exploration of the link between executive curiosity and firm-level 
strategy (Garrison et al., 2008 ; Gutschi and Klarner, 2020 , 2021 ), 
particularly the impact of CEO curiosity on firm innovation (Jeraj and 
Antoncic, 2013 ; Yu, 2021 ). Innovation is critical for firms to gain and maintain competitive advantages and superior performance (Ahuja 
et al., 2008 ; Cho and Pucik, 2005 ; Rubera and Kirca, 2012 ; Schumpeter, 
1934 ); research based on upper echelons theory (UET) has argued that 
CEOs are the most powerful actors in firms and that their psychological 
characteristics affect their information processing, thereby significantly 
impacting firm decisions and outcomes, including firm innovation (Chen 
et al., 2015 ; Hambrick, 2007 ; Hambrick and Mason, 1984 ). In the pre-
sent study, we focus on CEO curiosity, given that previous literature has 
implied that CEO curiosity is likely to be a key driver of corporate 
innovation. 
Specifically, curious individuals are likely to be stimulated by novel, 
complex, or ambiguous notions, which subsequently motivates a wide 
range of exploratory behaviors (Litman et al., 2005 ). Additionally, 
curious individuals can associate cues reflecting novelty and challenge 
with opportunities to learn, grow, and innovate (Kashdan et al., 2004 ), 
generating innovative ideas and perspectives (Celik et al., 2016 ; Hardy 
et al., 2017 ). The most distinctive characteristics of innovation activities 
include their novelty, uncertainty, and complexity (Holmstrom, 1989 ); 
thus, curiosity is likely to be central to the intrinsic motivations that 
drive innovative behavior (Schiefele et al., 1992 ). Further, empirical 
evidence has demonstrated that curiosity is a positive predictor of cre-
ative problem solving and performance (Hardy et al., 2017 ), worker 
innovation (Celik et al., 2016 ), and learning and exploration (Syed et al., 
2020 ; Van Dijk and Zeelenberg, 2007 ). Curious leaders are likely to 
catalyze the curiosity of their followers (Harrison, 2011 ; Zacher, 2016 )

Despite these insights, little is known about the impact of CEO cu-
riosity on firm innovation, which may lead to the omission of a key 
variable in examining the determinants of innovation outcomes. Build -
ing primarily on UET, we attempt to fill this gap in the present study by 
focusing on CEO curiosity as a critical antecedent of firm innovation. 
Specifically, we argue that CEO curiosity is an important factor in 
facilitating firm innovation and indirectly influences firm innovation 
through boundary-spanning search. Curious CEOs are intrinsically 
willing to explore and learn a wide range of new knowledge, which 
subsequently leads employees to seek knowledge from different sources 
(Vera and Crossan, 2004). Additionally, we argue that search activities 
that cross organizational or knowledge boundaries can promote the di-
versity of an enterprise’s knowledge base and significantly positively 
affect firm innovation (Katila and Ahuja, 2002). Considering that 
existing UET literature has emphasized that the effect of CEOs is con-
strained by organizational factors (Hambrick and Finkelstein, 1987), we 
further posit that the extent of the effect of CEO curiosity may be 
moderated by organizational inertia, a key organizational factor that 
researchers have investigated as a pivotal moderator in many previous 
studies (Li and Tang, 2010; Zhang et al., 2022). 
With this study we make several contributions. First, we contribute 
to UET by studying the role of CEO curiosity in firm innovation. CEO 
curiosity, a common and increasingly important psychological charac -
teristic of top executives, has been suggested to affect firm decisions and 
outcomes significantly (Garrison et al., 2008; Gutschi and Klarner, 2020, 
2021). However, previous researchers of firm innovation have paid little 
attention to the role of CEO curiosity. Our research builds a direct link 
between the two, and in this way, we respond to calls for additional 
research on the microfoundation of innovation (Choudhury and Haas, 
2018; Davis and Aggarwal, 2020; Yildiz et al., 2021). Second, we shed 
new light on how CEOs influence firm innovation by investigating the 
mediating role of boundary-spanning search, which helps open the 
“black box” frequently criticized in UET studies (Cho and Hambrick, 
2006; Hambrick, 2007; Lawrence, 1997; Neely et al., 2020). Third, we 
identify the boundary condition for the effect of CEO curiosity by 
examining the moderating role of organizational inertia. Finally, we 
enrich the growing body of research on curiosity in the workplace (Celik 
et al., 2016; Harrison, 2011; Lievens et al., 2022; Mussel, 2013) by 
examining the impact of executive-level curiosity on firm-level out-
comes, a hitherto understudied yet important topic (Gutschi and 
Klarner, 2020, 2021; Jeraj and Antoncic, 2013). 
2.Theory and hypotheses 
2.1. CEO curiosity 
Curiosity refers to the desire for new knowledge that motivates 
exploratory behaviors such as information seeking, learning, and 
thinking (Berlyne, 1966; Harrison et al., 2011; Litman, 2005; Mussel, 
2013). As an important, stable individual difference variable (Celik 
et al., 2016; Mussel, 2013), curiosity plays a distinct role in human 
behavior and development (Gutschi and Klarner, 2020; Hardy et al., 
2017) and has been a focal point of study in psychological scholarship 
(Harrison, 2011; Lievens et al., 2022). For example, research has shown 
that adolescents with high curiosity actively engage in the exploration of 
new things and feel a higher level of positive well-being than those with 
low curiosity (Jovanovic and Brdaric, 2012). Additionally, curiosity 
helps older adults maintain cognitive function, mental health, and 
physical health (Sakaki et al., 2018). Curiosity is also associated with 
numerous other positive outcomes such as longevity (Swan and Car-
melli, 1996), individual creativity (Hardy et al., 2017; Schutte and 
Malouff, 2020), better learning outcomes (Kang et al., 2009), high levels 
of engagement and low levels of emotional exhaustion (Garrosa et al., 2017), and overall life satisfaction (Kashdan and Steger, 2007). 
In recent years, an increasing number of firms such as Dell, Nike, 
Disney, Target, and General Electric have embraced curiosity as a value, 
and curiosity is interwoven into crucial organizational tasks (Lievens 
et al., 2022); as such, the role of curiosity in the workplace has received 
increasing research attention. For example, researchers have found that 
curiosity facilitates newcomer adaptation (Harrison et al., 2011) and 
positively affects worker innovation (Celik et al., 2016) and employee 
job performance (Mussel, 2013). Additionally, curiosity at the leader 
level may catalyze the curiosity of followers (Harrison, 2011), increase 
team creativity (Evans, 2020), and spark the creation of opportunities 
(Arikan et al., 2020). However, CEO curiosity, which plays a key role in 
firm strategy (Gutschi and Klarner, 2021), has been largely overlooked 
in research (Garrison et al., 2008; Gutschi and Klarner, 2020, 2021), 
especially the impact of CEO curiosity on firm innovation, because cu-
riosity is likely to be an upstream contributor to creativity and innova -
tion (Celik et al., 2016; Kashdan and Fincham, 2002; Loewenstein, 
1994), and the potential intermediary mechanisms and boundary con-
ditions of any such relationship are yet unexamined. 
In the present study, we focus on the strategic significance of CEO 
curiosity, defined as a CEO’s desire to acquire new knowledge that 
stimulates exploratory behavior, information seeking, and learning. 
Curious CEOs may have an intrinsic interest in a wide range of new 
knowledge in areas such as technology and marketing and obtain 
appropriate data and make decisions based on this knowledge (Jeraj and 
Antoncic, 2013). 
2.2. CEO curiosity and firm innovation 
Innovation helps firms grow and gain long-term competitive 
advantage (Ahuja et al., 2008; Cho and Pucik, 2005; Schumpeter, 1934). 
Given the significant role of CEOs in shaping firm innovation outcomes 
(Elenkov et al., 2005; Hambrick, 2007), scholars drawing on UET have 
investigated the influence of several CEO characteristics on firm inno-
vation, including hubris (Tang et al., 2015a), overconfidence (Hirsh -
leifer et al., 2012), narcissism (Kashmiri et al., 2017), and proactiveness 
(Kiss et al., 2022). Despite a growing body of research on the role of 
executive personality in firm innovation, relatively little is known about 
the effect of CEO curiosity on firm innovation. Based on evidence from 
previous research on curiosity, we argue in the present study that CEO 
curiosity is positively associated with firm innovation. 
Individuals with high levels of curiosity are open to new knowledge 
and ideas and are likely to conquer situations that are appraised in terms 
of novelty, complexity, and ambiguity (Mussel, 2013). Innovative ac-
tivities are characterized by novelty, uncertainty, and complexity 
(Holmstrom, 1989), and the CEO is the key decision-maker of firm 
strategies (Hambrick, 2007; Hambrick and Mason, 1984); a CEO driven 
by curiosity is more likely to guide a firm to carry out innovative ac-
tivities than a CEO who lacks such a quality. Additionally, in view of the 
fact that corporate innovation activities often require a large amount of 
cross-integration of knowledge from various fields (Chen et al., 2011), 
the diverse knowledge base that curious CEOs and their employees form 
through extensive knowledge-seeking behaviors provides the novel raw 
material needed to recombine ideas and generate creative solutions 
(Celik et al., 2016; Hardy et al., 2017), thus facilitating corporate 
innovation. 
Moreover, curious CEOs inherently enjoy the process of discovery, 
learning, and thinking (Mussel, 2010); they usually engage in complex 
and divergent thinking (Celik et al., 2016; Schmeichel et al., 2003) and 
can link cues that reflect novelty and challenge with opportunities to 
innovate (Kashdan et al., 2004). Motivated by a unique curious 
perspective (Harrison et al., 2011), these CEOs are likely to identify 
problems in a company’s production and operation and tend to view 
challenging situations more creatively, thus enhancing more innovation 
and positive changes within a company (Gino, 2018). In addition, CEOs 
with high levels of curiosity will learn more new skills and, accordingly, Z. In addition, curiosity is an important factor in determining the

H1. CEO curiosity is positively related to firm innovation. 
2.3. Mediating effect of boundary-spanning search 
Boundary-spanning search refers to information-gathering activities 
that extend beyond organizational boundaries or original knowledge 
bases (Rosenkopf and Nerkar, 2001). Boundary-spanning search can 
bring heterogeneous knowledge resources and inspiration to enterprises, 
promote the updating of their existing knowledge bases, and serve as an 
important means to overcome the shortage of local search (Rosenkopf 
and Almeida, 2003). With today’s knowledge economy and technolog -
ical advancements, the external environment enterprises face is chang -
ing rapidly, and local searches may hinder enterprises from keeping up 
with environmental changes (Rosenkopf and Nerkar, 2001). Therefore, 
boundary-spanning search is essential for enterprises to achieve dy-
namic matching with the external environment (Katila and Ahuja, 2002; 
Laursen and Salter, 2006). In the present study, we argue that 
boundary-spanning search plays a mediating role in the relationship 
between CEO curiosity and firm innovation. 
Curious CEOs enjoy exposure to new things and have a thirst for new 
knowledge; particularly, they demonstrate a strong tendency to seek 
knowledge and information in unfamiliar areas because the acquisition 
of new knowledge makes them feel satisfied and happy (Litman, 2010). 
According to UET, the CEO, as the primary decision-maker of an en-
terprise, plays a guiding role in the learning activities of the firm 
(Hambrick, 2007; Vera and Crossan, 2004). Thus, the more curious 
CEOs are, the more likely they are to drive their firms to cross organi -
zational boundaries or existing knowledge pools to search for new 
knowledge about technologies and markets. Further, CEOs’ curiosity 
drives them to find ways to improve their organizations (Garrison et al., 
2008). To better adapt to changes in the environment, curious CEOs are 
likely to push companies to conduct cross-border searches and subse -
quently absorb, reorganize, and integrate new knowledge to provide 
novel ideas for promoting enterprise development. Indeed, research has 
suggested that companies led by curious founders search extensively for 
a wide variety of external knowledge (Hahn et al., 2019; Lievens et al., 
2022). 
Through boundary-spanning searches, enterprises acquire more 
complementary knowledge and resources, thereby fostering the inte-
gration and reorganization of new and original knowledge as well as 
providing novel and rich knowledge sources for enterprises to solve 
problems and discover new opportunities (Rosenkopf and Nerkar, 
2001). Additionally, boundary-spanning search is beneficial for enter -
prises to better grasp market changes and competitor dynamics (Wil-
helm and Dolfsma, 2018), helping enterprises avoid falling into “core 
rigidity” and “capability trap” problems (Dahlander et al., 2016). Thus, 
guided by market information and competitor information, companies 
can adjust their business models, improve their production technologies, 
and innovate their products and services based on the acquired market 
and technological knowledge, thus fostering increased innovation 
(Katila and Ahuja, 2002). Hence, we propose the following: 
H2. The positive relationship between CEO curiosity and firm inno-
vation is mediated by boundary-spanning search. 2.4. Moderating effect of organizational inertia 
UET states that the extent to which CEO characteristics matter to 
firm decisions and outcomes depends on the level of managerial 
discretion; when the level of managerial discretion is high, the charac -
teristics of executives are more likely to be reflected in firm strategy and 
outcomes (Hambrick, 2007; Hambrick and Finkelstein, 1987). Accord -
ing to Hambrick and Finkelstein (1987), organizational factors are 
important determinants of this discretion because the internal organi -
zation defines the degree of compliance of an organization to various 
possible actions and subsequently empowers the CEO to implement 
these actions. Based on these insights, we intend to examine the 
moderating role of organizational inertia in the relationship between 
CEO curiosity and boundary-spanning search and in the indirect effect of 
CEO curiosity on firm innovation, given that organizational inertia is an 
important organizational factor that may determine the scope of 
managerial discretion (Finkelstein and Hambrick, 1990; Hambrick, 
2007; Li and Tang, 2010). 
Organizational inertia refers to the property of an organization 
maintaining its existing structural state (Hannan and Freeman, 1977); 
highly inertial organizations change their core features much more 
slowly than changes in environmental conditions because of their 
standardized routines or formally defined roles and control systems 
(Hannan and Freeman, 1984; Kelly and Amburgey, 1991). As a result, an 
executive seeking change may be severely constrained by the ingrained 
culture of a highly inertial organization. This means that even though 
curious CEOs have a strong tendency to guide their organizations to seek 
new knowledge across domains, it is difficult for them to easily change 
organizational activities when they are constrained by a high degree of 
inertia. As Johnson (1988) noted, failure often occurs even when man-
agers are aware of the need to change. Increasing inertia constrains a 
CEO’s discretion. Therefore, we propose the following: 
H3. Organizational inertia weakens the positive relationship between 
CEO curiosity and boundary-spanning search. 
According to the notion that organizational inertia moderates the 
relationship between CEO curiosity and boundary-spanning search, and 
considering that boundary-spanning search is positively related to firm 
innovation, it is logical to suggest that organizational inertia also 
moderates the strength of the mediating mechanism for boundary- 
spanning search in the relationship between CEO curiosity and firm 
innovation—a moderated mediation model (Edwards and Lambert, 
2007). As previously mentioned, the relationship between CEO curiosity 
and search across boundaries is weaker when organizational inertia is 
high. Hence, the indirect effect of CEO curiosity on firm innovation via 
boundary-spanning search may also be weaker when the level of orga-
nizational inertia is high. Taken together, we propose the following: 
H4. Organizational inertia moderates the mediating effect of 
boundary-spanning search on the relationship between CEO curiosity 
and firm innovation such that the indirect effect of CEO curiosity on firm 
innovation performance via boundary-spanning search is weaker when 
the level of organizational inertia is high.

3.Method 
3.1. Sample and data collection 
We collected the data for the present study through a questionnaire 
survey of Chinese firms located in Jiangyin City, Jiangsu Province, 
involving biopharmaceutical, machinery manufacturing, and new ma-
terial industries. Before the formal research, we comprehensively 
explained the purpose, method, and requirements of this research to the 
individual in charge of the Jiangyin Bureau of Industry and Information 
Technology and invited him to help us contact the relevant enterprises Z. 4participating in the questionnaire survey. The questionnaire comprised 
three parts: (a) instructions for filling out the questionnaire, (b) basic 
information about the enterprise, and (c) main variable questions. To 
avoid the impact of common method bias on the research results, we 
implemented three measures. First, we adopted multisource data 
collection: the respondents for the questionnaire survey were each firm’s 
CEO, chief technology officer (CTO), and chief financial officer (CFO). 
Each completed a survey separately, unaware of the questions given to 
the others. Specifically, the CEOs provided information about their own 
curiosity, age, gender, and shareholding ratio and basic information 
about their company such as its industry and number of employees; the 
CTOs provided information about boundary-spanning search; and the 
CFOs provided information about the firms ’ innovation and organiza -
tional inertia as well as other financial data. Second, the questionnaire 
included a clear statement that the data of this research are confidential 
and completed anonymously. Third, the questionnaire did not relay the 
names of variables, the purpose of the study, or the connotation of the 
questions, and we asked the respondents to answer truthfully. 
We surveyed a total of 602 enterprises, excluding 39 sample enter -
prises with incomplete questionnaires, for a total of 563 valid samples 
retained and a valid recovery rate of 93%. Among these 563 valid 
samples, small and medium-sized enterprises comprised the majority, 
among which enterprises with fewer than 100 employees accounted for 
47.8%, enterprises with 100–500 employees accounted for 45.5%, en-
terprises with 500 to 1000 employees accounted for 3.7%, and enter -
prises with more than 1000 employees accounted for 3%. The age 
distribution was wide, with 30.4% of enterprises established for less 
than 10 years, 51.7% of enterprises established for 11–20 years, 13.5% 
of enterprises established for 21–30 years, and 4.4% of enterprises 
established for more than 30 years. 
To test for possible nonresponse bias, we conducted a wave analysis 
(Hsieh et al., 2008 ) and compared the indicators of key constructs be-
tween the early and late respondents based on whether the question -
naire collection interval was greater than 15 days. The t-test results of 
each key variable were as follows: firm innovation (p 0.152), CEO 
curiosity (p 0.836), boundary-spanning search (p 0.443), and 
organizational inertia (p 0.612). There was no significant difference 
between the two sets of sample data, indicating that nonresponse bias 
was not present. 
3.2. Measures 
To ensure the reliability and validity of the measurement instrument, 
we used the mature scales developed and validated by prior research and 
translated and back-translated the measurement scales (Brislin, 1970 ). 
We used the following specific measurement instruments: 
3.2.1. Dependent variable: firm innovation 
We measured firm innovation by adopting a three-item scale used in 
many previous studies (e.g., Dyer and Song, 1997 ; Song et al., 2006 ). 
Response options ranged from 1 ‘‘strongly disagree ’’ to 6 ‘‘strongly 
agree. ’’ Sample items included the following: “The overall performance 
of our new product development program has met our objectives ” and 
“From an overall profitability standpoint, our new product development program has been successful. ” In the present study, the scale ’s reliability 
was 0.957. 
3.2.2. Independent variable: CEO curiosity 
We used a five-item scale developed by Litman and others (e.g., 
Litman, 2008 ; Litman and Jimerson, 2004 ; Litman and Spielberger, 
2003 ) to measure CEO curiosity. Response options ranged from 1 
(“strongly disagree ”) to 6 (“strongly agree ”). Sample items included the 
following: “I enjoy exploring new ideas ” and “I enjoy learning about 
subjects that are unfamiliar to me.” The scale ’s reliability for the present 
study was 0.953. 
3.2.3. Mediating variable: boundary-spanning search 
Drawing on the scale that Sidhu et al. (2007) developed, we used four 
items to measure boundary-spanning search. Response options ranged 
from 1 (“strongly disagree ”) to 6 (“strongly agree ”). Sample items 
included the following: “We are well aware of technological and tech-
nical developments within our industry ” and “In our company, there is 
close surveillance of advancements in product and process technologies 
in supplier industries. ” In the present study, the scale ’s reliability was 
0.948. 
3.2.4. Moderating variable: organizational inertia 
We adopted a four-item scale that Huang et al. (2013) used to 
measure organizational inertia. Response options ranged from 1 
(“strongly disagree ”) to 6 (“strongly agree ”). Sample items included the 
following: “Our company has difficulty identifying how other firms 
solve problems ” and “Our company rarely observes changes in the 
external environment. ” The scale ’s reliability for the present study was 
0.967. 
3.2.5. Control variables 
Following previous innovation literature (e.g., Chen, 2022 ; Chen and 
Nadkarni, 2017 ; Kiss et al., 2020 ), we controlled for several firm, CEO, 
and industry variables to remove the interference of other possible 
influencing factors. First, we controlled for four firm-level factors: firm 
size, firm age, debt ratio, and research and development (R&D) invest -
ment. We measured firm size as the natural logarithm of the number of 
employees in the firm and firm age by the number of years since the 
firm’s founding. We captured the debt ratio by the ratio of the firm’s total 
liabilities to total assets. We measured R&D investment by the natural 
logarithm of the sum of R&D expenditures of the firm. Second, at the 
CEO level, we controlled for age, gender, and shareholding ratio. We 
used the natural logarithm of CEO age to measure CEO age and included 
CEO gender as a dummy variable in the model. We captured CEO 
shareholding ratio by the ratio of the number of shares owned by the CEO 
to the total number of shares. Third, at the industry level, we included 
industry sector dummies in our model to control the industry effect (e.g., 
software, machinery manufacturing, and new materials). Additionally, 
we controlled whether a firm was a high-tech firm, coded as 1, or a 
non-high-tech firm, coded as 0.

4.1. Common method bias testing 
As mentioned, we reduced potential common method bias in the 
present study by using multisource data collection, having participants 
fill out the questionnaire anonymously, and concealing variable names 
in the questionnaire. We further adopted the Harman single-factor test 
to examine the potential problem of common method variance; the re-
sults of this test showed that the first principal component obtained 
without rotation was 40%, which is below the significant level of 50% 
(Fuller et al., 2015 ; Hair et al., 1998 ), indicating no significant common 
method bias present. 
4.2. Confirmatory factor analyses 
We conducted confirmatory factor analyses using AMOS 20.0 to 
evaluate the validity of the key variables. We first examined a four-factor 
model, in which we included CEO curiosity, boundary-spanning search, 
organizational inertia, and firm innovation. The results indicated that 
the hypothesized four-factor model fit the data well: χ2(96) 219.159, 
CFI 0.989, TLI 0.986, and RMSEA 0.048 (Hair et al., 1998 ). In 
addition, all factor loadings were significant, providing evidence for 
convergent validity. 
To test discriminant validity, we compared the priori four-factor 
model (curiosity, boundary-spanning search, organizational inertia, 
and firm innovation) with the one-factor and three-factor models. The 
three-factor model was a combination of CEO curiosity and boundary- 
spanning search into one factor, given that a correlation analysis 
showed that their correlation was the highest among the four constructs 
(r 0.478, p D0.001). The model comparison results suggested that the 
four-factor model provided a significantly better fit than did the one- 
factor model (χ2[104] 7212.231, CFI 0.341, TLI 0.239, RMSEA 
0.349), and the three-factor model (χ2[101] 2163.851, CFI 0.809, 
TLI 0.773, RMSEA 0.191). Hence, the discriminant validity of the 
four constructs was supported.

Table 2 shows the regression results for hypothesis testing. In H1, we

To test the mediating role of boundary-spanning search in the rela-
tionship between CEO curiosity and firm innovation (H2), we adopted

5.1. Theoretical implications 
With our study we contribute to both UET and the microfoundation 
of innovation by introducing CEO curiosity as a critical antecedent for 
explaining variations in firm innovation management. UET suggests that 
top executives ’ psychological characteristics can greatly affect firm 
behavior and outcomes (Hambrick, 2007 ; Hambrick and Mason, 1984 ). 
Existing research affiliated with this theory has demonstrated that CEO 
characteristics such as hubris (Tang et al., 2015a ), overconfidence 
(Hirshleifer et al., 2012 ), humility (Zhang et al., 2017 ), and narcissism 
(Kashmiri et al., 2017 ) are significantly related to firm innovation. 
Although prior works have provided meaningful insights, recent UET 
scholars have emphasized that only a few extant studies associate CEO 
personality with firm innovation, advocating for further research to 
explore the impact of various CEO characteristics on firm performance 
(Wang et al., 2016 ; You et al., 2020 ), particularly some potential key 
CEO characteristics (Zhang et al., 2022 ). Given that prior literature has 
conceptually and empirically demonstrated the importance of CEO cu-
riosity in guiding the innovation process (Evans, 2020 ; Yu, 2021 ; 
Zacher, 2016 ), focusing on the impact of CEO curiosity may help explain 
why innovation varies so greatly among companies led by CEOs with 
different levels of curiosity and may help investors and policy makers 
better understand firm-level innovation activities. In explaining how 
CEO curiosity, a previously underexplored dimension of managerial 
psychology, positively influences a firm’s innovation, we advance a 
theoretically important and novel lens to understand the determinants 
of firm innovation. As such, we contribute to UET and advance research 
on the microfoundation of innovation (Choudhury and Haas, 2018 ; 
Davis and Aggarwal, 2020 ; Yildiz et al., 2021 ). 
Further, we provide new insight into how CEOs influence firm 
strategy by identifying the mechanism through which CEO curiosity 
may affect corporate innovation. UET studies have yielded a wealth of 
evidence that top executives ’ characteristics affect organizational out-
comes (e.g., Wang et al., 2016 ; You et al., 2020 ), but these studies have 
often been criticized for their lack of attention to the process mecha -
nisms behind the relationship between the executive and firm outcomes 
(Cho and Hambrick, 2006 ; Hambrick, 2007 ; Lawrence, 1997 ; Neely 
et al., 2020 ). Although some researchers have attempted to show how 
these associations occur (e.g., Chen et al., 2023 ; Zhang et al., 2022 ), the 
processes that mediate CEO characteristics and strategic choices are still 
not well understood or empirically tested. Studies have demonstrated 
that top executives ’ long-term orientation and risk-taking propensities 
are major behavioral mechanisms that affect firm innovation (Chen 
et al., 2023 ; Ling et al., 2008 ; Tang et al., 2015a ; Zhang et al., 2022 ). However, implementing innovation is not accomplished by simply tak-
ing risks and building long-term orientation; it also requires diverse 
knowledge and skills (Amabile and Pratt, 2016 ). Using survey data, we 
demonstrated that boundary-spanning search is the mechanism that 
links CEO curiosity to firm innovation, and this finding may provide a 
new perspective for understanding how CEO traits are linked to corpo -
rate innovation. In this way, we have responded to calls for research that 
addresses the “black box” of how CEO characteristics affect organiza -
tional outcomes (Cho and Hambrick, 2006 ; Hambrick, 2007 ; Lawrence, 
1997 ; Neely et al., 2020 ), adding to both the conceptual and practical 
contributions to UET (Neely et al., 2020 ). 
Moreover, we have highlighted the moderating effect of organiza -
tional inertia as an important internal organizational factor determining 
managerial discretion, weakening the impact of CEO curiosity on 
boundary-spanning search and the indirect effect of CEO curiosity on 
firm innovation performance via boundary-spanning search. As an 
initial attempt to explore CEO curiosity, it is important to identify 
essential factors that moderate the relationship between CEO curiosity 
and firm actions and outcomes to gain a deeper understanding of the 
strategic implications of CEO curiosity. In this way, we help advance 
research on CEO curiosity and upper echelons studies, not simply when 
the organization reflects more or less the cognitive values and psycho -
logical biases of top executives (Hambrick and Mason, 1984 ) but also its 
practical implications (Tang et al., 2015b ). 
Finally, we have extended the research on curiosity by linking 
executive-level curiosity to firm-level outcomes. Curiosity has been a 
focus of psychological literature, and existing research has suggested 
that it is associated with many generative outcomes such as increased 
well-being (Gallagher and Lopez, 2007 ; Jovanovic and Brdaric, 2012 ), 
health (Sakaki et al., 2018 ), and levels of engagement and emotional 
exhaustion (Garrosa et al., 2017 ). Given the emergent importance of 
curiosity at work for individuals and organizations, in recent studies 
researchers have investigated the role of curiosity in the workplace 
(Lievens et al., 2022 ). For example, some authors have suggested that 
curiosity is positively related to newcomer adaptation (Harrison et al., 
2011 ), worker innovation (Celik et al., 2016 ), and employee job per-
formance (Mussel, 2013 ), and others have demonstrated that leader 
curiosity helps motivate team-level creativity (Evans, 2020 ). However, 
research on the role of curiosity in the workplace is still lacking (Har-
rison, 2011 ; Lievens et al., 2022 ). Moreover, CEO curiosity plays a 
beneficial role in shaping firm strategy (Gutschi and Klarner, 2021 ), yet 
we know little about its strategic implications. By elucidating the posi-
tive effect of CEO curiosity on firm innovation, we have echoed the call 
to focus on CEO curiosity (Garrison et al., 2008 ; Gutschi and Klarner, 
2020 , 2021 ), particularly the influence of CEO curiosity on firm inno-
vation (Jeraj and Antoncic, 2013 ; Yu, 2021 ), thus highlighting the role 
of CEO-level curiosity in organizations. By investigating the mediating 
and moderating effects, we have provided new insights into the pro-
cesses and conditions that explain the role of curiosity, which re-
searchers have insufficiently explored in previous studies (Celik et al., 
2016 ; Mussel, 2013 ). 
5.2. Managerial implications 
The results of the present study have practical implications for firms 
and executives. Innovation is key for firms to maintain a competitive 
advantage (Ahuja et al., 2008 ; Garud et al., 2013 ). Although innovation 
activities can generate excess returns and contribute to high growth, 
they, unlike conventional activities such as mass production and mar-
keting, involve exploring new and untested approaches, often with 
longer investment cycles and a greater likelihood of failure (Holmstrom, 
1989 ). It is because of these characteristics of innovation activities that 
CEOs tend to invest less in innovation and focus more on daily projects 
with fast and more certain financial returns (He and Tian, 2013 ). As a 
result, innovation in organizations is often postponed until a crisis oc-
curs (Celik et al., 2016 ; Van de Ven, 1986 ). Curious CEOs, however, are Table 3

Moreover, collecting and analyzing the curiosity traits of competi -

for organizations to consider when making strategic decisions. 
In short, curiosity is a key personality trait for leaders (Wright and 
Quick, 2011) because of the ever-increasing speed of change and in-
formation, and firms and CEOs must value curiosity because curiosity 
will only continue to grow in importance (Lievens et al., 2022). 
5.3. Limitations and future direction 
Although in the present study we took the lead in exploring the 
impact of CEO curiosity on firm innovation and its mediating mecha -
nisms and boundary conditions, there remain limitations, primarily re-
flected in the following aspects. First, the samples of this study are 
mainly from Jiangyin City, Jiangsu Province, which may affect the 
external validity of the research conclusions. Future research can 
expand the scope of the survey and increase the sample’s size and di-
versity to ensure the universality of the research conclusions. Second, 
although we adopted a multisource data collection method, because this 
study is cross-sectional, it cannot accurately reflect the causal relation -
ship between variables. Therefore, it is necessary for future research to 
further reveal the causal relationship between variables through 
multitime-point and multistage tracking research methods. 
Additionally, future research can further explore other potential 
mechanisms and boundary conditions by which CEO curiosity affects 
corporate innovation. For example, CEO curiosity may promote firm 
innovation by improving employees’ sense of psychological security or 
eliciting employee curiosity to extend current knowledge about the 
connection between CEO curiosity and firm innovation. Further, 
although CEO curiosity is likely to be a critical factor driving corporate 
innovation based on existing curiosity research and the theoretical 
analysis and empirical tests of this present study, future research is 
needed to further examine the explanatory variances of different per-
sonality traits on corporate innovation. 
6.Conclusion 
The present study is one of the first empirical studies to explicitly test 
the relationship between CEO curiosity and firm innovation as well as its 
intermediary mechanisms and boundary conditions. Using a sample of 
563 Chinese firms, we found that CEO curiosity positively influences 
firm innovation via boundary-spanning search, whereas organizational 
inertia negatively moderates the impact of CEO curiosity. Examining 
CEO curiosity, a new key dimension of executives’ psychological char-
acteristics, demonstrates great promise for advancing UET research and 
the firm innovation literature. We hope that the present findings will 
spur additional research on the curiosity traits of executives.
