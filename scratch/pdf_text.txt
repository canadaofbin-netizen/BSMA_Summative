Applicant attraction to ﬂexible work
arrangements: Separating the inﬂuence of ﬂextime
and ﬂexplace

1University of Baltimore, Maryland, USA
2Texas A&M University, College Station, Texas, USA
3Independent Contractor, Worthington, Ohio, USA
Flexible work arrangements (FWAs) are widely implemented for organizational purposes
including recruitment. Theoretically, these arrangements alter temporal and physical
boundaries around work. However, the time and place dimensions are frequently
confounded in research, making the separate and joint effect of each on various outcomesunclear. To determine the relative importance of FWA dimensions as anticipated
resources, this study experimentally manipulates discretion over when (ﬂextime) and
where (ﬂexplace) one is expected to work on anticipated organizational support (AOS)and organization attraction. Prospective employees ( N=130) participated in a 3 93
within-subject experiment in which they rated nine hypothetical organizations that varied
in ﬂextime and ﬂexplace. Results indicated main effects for both ﬂextime and ﬂexplace on
both AOS and organization attraction with ﬂextime having the stronger impact. Althoughthe combination of a high level of both ﬂextime and ﬂexplace yielded the highest ratings of
AOS and organization attraction, the interaction between ﬂextime and ﬂexplace was not
statistically signiﬁcant, suggesting ﬂextime and ﬂexplace have independent effects onrecruitment outcomes. Relationships between ﬂextime and ﬂexplace and organizational
attraction were slightly stronger for individuals who prefer to integrate their work and
non-work roles. Managerial implications and directions for future research are discussed.

DOI:10.1111/joop.12095726Today, more organizations are recognizing that employees seek employment opportu-
nities that offer resources to fulﬁl work and non-work role demands (Mantell, 2011; Worldat Work, 2009). One way organizations recognize employees’ limited resources is to giveemployees more control over work boundaries by offering them ﬂexible work
arrangements (FWAs). These arrangements alter when and where work demands can
be addressed, reducing work –non-work conﬂict (Hobfoll, 1989; Voyandoff, 2005).
Offering FWAs signals employers’ awareness and support of employees’ multiple rolesand demands (Grover & Crooker, 1995). Job seekers are likely to perceive FWAs asanticipated resources and take them into consideration during recruitment. As a result,FWAs can be used as a tool to meet organizational recruitment goals (Kossek & Friede,2006).
Many forms of FWAs exist, such as telework, ﬂextime schedules, compressed work
weeks, and ﬂexible shift work (Baltes, Briggs, Huff, Wright, & Neuman, 1999; Kossek,
Hammer, Thompson, & Burke, 2014). Multiple studies have shown that FWAs areassociated with a number of positive outcomes for current employees (Baltes et al. , 1999;
Gajendran & Harrison, 2007; Kossek & Michel, 2011), as well as prospective employees(Casper & Buffardi, 2004). Theoretically, FWAs offer employees some degree of controlover the temporal and/or physical boundaries around which they work (Voyandoff,2005). Thus, FWAs vary on two independent but related dimensions: Discretion over
work time anddiscretion over work place (Allen, Johnson, Kiburz, & Shockley, 2013;
Butler, Grzywacz, Ettner, & Liu, 2009). If it is possible for employees to work ‘anywhere’,
it is also likely that they can work ‘anytime’. Thus, organizations that offer discretion overwhere work is conducted also tend to offer discretion over when work is conducted(World at Work, 2011), but not always.
Flexible work arrangement researchers do not always differentiate between the
multiple dimensions of discretion that are inherent within speciﬁc policies. This raisesdoubts about the scientiﬁc evidence supporting the individual policies, as it is impossibleto know the relative desirability and inﬂuence of each dimension of discretion when they
are studied together. As a result, practice is not informed by good science. By
systematically varying multiple levels of each FWA dimension, we reveal the effect ofFWA dimensions as anticipated resources to recruitment outcomes. New insights are alsogained about the relative importance of temporal versus physical work boundaries,providing valuable information for the design and marketing of FWAs.
We conducted a within-subject full-factorial vignette experiment, following many of
the best practices recommended by Aguinis and Bradley (2014). We systematically varythe two focal independent variables ﬂextime and ﬂexplace to be one of the three levels.
By having each participant rate each vignette, we can estimate the effect of two
practical increases in discretion over when and where work is conducted. We test theincreases from no discretion to some discretion and from some discretion to unlimiteddiscretion. This design permits an examination of three levels of each FWA dimensionrelative to the others, as well as all corresponding combinations of ﬂextime andﬂexplace.
Our study contributes to the research literature by addressing a number of limitations
previously documented. Speciﬁcally, two recent reviews (Allen & Shockley, 2009;
Casper, Eby, Bordeaux, Lockwood, & Lambert, 2007) identiﬁed only one study of work
schedules using an experimental design. As a result, the large majority of conclusionsmade about FWAs are based on correlational data that assume linear relationships. This isproblematic because compared to other research designs, experiments have the highestlevel of internal validity and permit causal inferences. Researchers have noted thatFlexible work arrangements 727examining different levels of ﬂexibility is critical to pinpointing the degree of ﬂexibility
that offers positive outcomes (Christensen & Staines, 1990), calling for ‘a more nuancedapproach to how we assess FWA’ (Allen & Shockley, 2009, p. 279) to determine therelative importance of the various levels of FWA dimensions.
Flexible work arrangements
Flexible work arrangements are ‘a mutually beneﬁcial arrangement between employeesand employers in which both parties agree on when, where and how the employee willwork to meet the ﬁrm’s needs’ (Kossek et al. , 2014, p. 2). Although there are variations of
the traditional workday, we use the 8 am –5 pm Monday through Friday work week as a
comparison. Two of the most widely implemented and therefore studied FWAs in the
United States and Europe are ﬂextime and ﬂexplace (Matos & Galinsky, 2012; Shockley &
Allen, 2007; Stavrou, 2005). In fact, the number of wage and salaried employees given aﬂextime option has increased dramatically in recent years (Galinsky, Bond, Sakai, Kim, &Giuntoli, 2008; Matos & Galinsky, 2012). As Galinsky et al. note, ﬂexibility is no longer just
a perk to be offered but a competitive tool that organizations can use to meet theirstrategic recruitment objectives. Organizations seeking to maximize talent attraction andretention frequently utilize FWAs to gain a competitive edge (Barney, 1991; Kossek et al. ,
2014; SHRM, 2013; World at Work, 2011).
Theoretical foundations
Theoretically, the reason why prospective employees are likely to ﬁnd FWAs attractive isbecause they signal to applicants that the organization cares about employees (Grover &Crooker, 1995; Spence, 1973), and such arrangements extend more control to employeesfacilitating their ability to meet work and non-work demands. According to signallingtheory, prospective applicants interpret observable information conveyed by an
organization as signs or signals of less observable characteristics (Spence, 1973). In the
context of recruitment, applicants infer the extent to which an organization is ‘family-friendly’ based on the types of policies offered (Casper & Harris, 2008; Grover & Crooker,1995).
The appeal or attractiveness of FWAs is often explained by Hobfoll’s (1989, 2001)
conservation of resources theory and Voyandoff’s (2005) resources and demands model.According to these theories, people are motivated to conserve resources (Hobfoll, 1989,2001) because they rely on them to meet environmental demands (Voyandoff, 2005).
Resources have been deﬁned as ‘anything perceived by the individual to help attain his or
her goals’, and discretion is one of the ways that resources have been operationalized inthe research literature (Halbesleben, Neveu, Paustian-Underdahl, & Westman, 2014,p. 1338). Likewise, FWAs have been described as ‘boundary-spanning resources’(Voyandoff, 2005) as they facilitate the ability to perform the work role in the non-workdomain and quickly transition between roles.
In a recruitment context, applicants rather than employees evaluate FWAs and
contemplate what it will be like to work for that organization. We propose that FWAs can
be conceptualized as anticipated resources and that consistent with resource and
demand theories, they will be motivated to conserve and retain such resources to reducestrain resulting from multiple role demands. When evaluating a given organization,applicants will consider their current resources as well as anticipated resources offered bythe organization. Based on resource theories, applicants will be more attracted to728 Rebecca J. Thompson et al.organizations they can anticipate more resources from rather than less. Further, FWAs
may signal to potential applicants that if they were to work for this company, they cananticipate being able to meet both work and non-work demands with some ease andtherefore experience less work –non-work conﬂict, because they will have ﬂexible work
boundaries.
While it is generally accepted that FWAs are desirable (Cook, 2009), the value of
these resources is determined by the individual as well as his or her personal context(Halbesleben et al. , 2014). In other words, resources are not organized hierarchically in
terms of importance; rather, their attractiveness is relative to the individual and his orher unique demands. Some researchers have begun to speculate that in addition to theresources themselves, various aspects or dimensions of the resources also matter. Forexample, Payne, Cook, and Diaz (2012) recently demonstrated that the convenience of
the childcare resource was related to time-based family interference with work,
absenteeism, and turnover intentions. Correspondingly, in this study, we differentiateFWAs that permit discretion over when work is conducted from those that permitdiscretion over where work is conducted. It is unclear whether potential applicantsﬁnd the option to alter temporal boundaries more or less desirable than the option toalter physical boundaries. Further, it has not been empirically tested whether it is moredesirable to have discretion over both time and place and, if so, how much of each typeof discretion is preferred. From a practical perspective, it is important to determine the
relative effectiveness of each FWA dimension offered to identify their unique
contributions. Next, we describe the nature of each dimension individually andtogether.
Flextime
Discretion over when employees work, the days and the hours, is a core dimension onwhich FWAs can vary. Operationally, a ﬂextime schedule includes ‘core time’ in which
employees are required to work (e.g., 9 am –3 pm, Monday –Friday) and ‘ﬂexible time’,
corresponding to the hours employees can choose to conduct work (e.g., 6 am –9 am,
3p m –6 pm, Monday –Friday; Baltes et al. , 1999; Kossek et al. , 2014). Thus, ﬂextime
increases the time periods employees are available for non-work demands by givingemployees some discretion over their start and stop work times each day. Researchsuggests that work and non-work demands have increased, competing for the ﬁniteresource of time (Jacobs & Gerson, 1998) making the ability to control when one works allthe more valuable.
Consistent with Shockley and Allen (2007), we use the term ﬂextime to refer to the
extent to which employees can alter temporal boundaries around work. The underlyingconstruct is discretion or freedom to make changes to work time which is conceptuallycontinuous, ranging from no discretion (i.e., set hours on set days) to unlimited discretion(no set hours/days).
Flexplace
Discretion over where employees conduct work is another core dimension on which
FWAs can vary. Traditionally, employees ‘go to’ work, such that they leave home toconduct their work at an alternative physical location (e.g., ofﬁce building). Here,employees can access information they need to do their work and communicate withimportant constituents (supervisors, coworkers, clients, etc.). When organizations permitFlexible work arrangements 729employees to work from alternative locations, they give employees more options as to
where they can conduct their work.1We use the term ﬂexplace to refer to the extent to
which employees can alter physical boundaries around work. Like ﬂextime, ﬂexplace is acontinuous construct ranging from no discretion to unlimited discretion over working
away from the main worksite (cf. Bailey & Kurland, 2002). Operationally, this might be
conveyed as how frequently (number of days a week/month) employees are permitted towork in an alternative location.
Flexplace is frequently studied and implemented in practice as telework or
telecommuting (Shockley & Allen, 2007) whereby ‘employees perform tasks elsewherethat are normally done in a primary or central workplace, for at least some portion of theirwork schedule, using electronic media to interact with others inside and outside theorganization’ (Gajendran & Harrison, 2007, p. 1525; see also the deﬁnition of telework in
the US Telework Enhancement Act of 2010; US Congress, 2010; section 6501). According
to these deﬁnitions, telework alters where work is conducted. Note that the deﬁnition
does not involve altering when work is conducted.
The confounding of ﬂextime with ﬂexplace
Theoretically, discretion over time is independent of discretion over place (Allen et al. ,
2013; Shockley & Allen, 2007). Correspondingly, FWA policies can take a variety of
forms, with organizations extending ﬂextime without extending ﬂexplace and vice
versa. However, in practice and in research, ﬂextime and ﬂexplace are sometimesextended together or combined into one policy. For example, telework policies thatpermit employees to work at an alternative location on non-traditional days/timesextend discretion over both temporal and physical work boundaries (Standen, Daniels,& Lamond, 1999). The organizations that tend to offer ﬂexplace policies (i.e., telework)also tend to offer ﬂextime policies (World at Work, 2011). Sixty-nine of Fortune
Magazine’s 100 Best Companies to Work For in 2014 offered both ﬂexible scheduling
and telecommuting (T. Russell, personal communication, 2014). It is not clear,
however, whether employees are permitted to utilize both policies at the same time.This study tests the extent to which having discretion over both time and place isattractive.
Perhaps because ﬂextime and ﬂexplace are frequently extended together, research
on FWAs has become a bit muddled. Some researchers conceptualize telework within agiven study as discretion over both place and time (e.g., Major, Verive, & Joice, 2008;
Pearlson & Saunders, 2001; Stavrou & Kilaniotis, 2010). For example, teleworkers have
sometimes been referred to as ‘anytime, anywhere workers’ (e.g., Lister & Harnish,
2011). Also, authors (e.g., Overmyer, 2011) tout ‘ﬂexibility in scheduling work’ (p. 10)as a recognized employee beneﬁt of telework without explicitly acknowledging thatsuch a beneﬁt would require ﬂextime as well. Similarly, research studies indicate thatteleworkers report that telework ‘provides ﬂexibility in your daily schedule’ as a waythat teleworking assists with dependent care situations (Major et al. , 2008, p. 75).

We argue that it is phenomenologically different to have the option to alter time and
not place, or place and not time, as well as the option to alter both. In the next section, we
elaborate on the various combinations of ﬂextime and ﬂexplace and how eacharrangement differs.
Flextime without ﬂexplace: A ﬂextime without ﬂexplace arrangement permits
employees to alter temporal but not physical boundaries around work. In thisarrangement, employees can come to the worksite earlier or later than a traditional start
time, allowing them to tend to non-work demands during traditional work hours. For
example, an employee might take a pet to the veterinarian’s ofﬁce for an 8-amappointment, take the pet home, and begin his/her workday at the main worksite at10 am, working later in the day to fulﬁl an 8-hr workday. Many non-work demands canonly be attended to during traditional work hours (e.g., child’s doctor’s appointment at2 pm on Tuesday). Compared to a traditional work schedule, employees with traditionalﬂextime schedules have wider ranges of time to devote to non-work demands. Based onresource theories, we predict that higher levels of ﬂextime will be rated as signiﬁcantly
more supportive and attractive than lower levels of ﬂextime.

Flexplace without ﬂextime: When employees have discretion over where they work
but not when they work, they have the option to alter their physical but not their temporal
boundaries. Employees are expected to be working and available via various forms of
communication technology during the organization’s mandated working hours. This
arrangement allows employees to conduct their work in more convenient andcomfortable locations. Not having to report to a central work location can result inboth ﬁscal and temporal beneﬁts such as eliminating commute time and decreasing theamount of money spent on transportation and work clothes. By altering the physicalboundary surrounding where work is conducted, employees can more quickly transitionfrom their work role to a non-work role.
Flexplace without ﬂextime means employees do not have the ability to attend to non-
work demands that arise during the organization’s mandated work hours. For example,
employees cannot supervise children/elders or take care of home chores while alsoconducting work tasks. Flexplace with ﬂextime: Flexplace with ﬂextime means employees have discretion over

Thus, ﬂextime augments thebeneﬁts of ﬂexplace by allowing for more ﬂuid transitions between roles thataccommodate the employer as well as the employee. For instance, an employee may

Conservation of resource theorists speculate that some resources typically occur
together which Hobfoll (1988) refers to as ‘resource caravans’. A combination of a highlevel of ﬂextime and a high level of ﬂexplace results in an arrangement with the mostdiscretion over temporal and physical boundaries, but we propose that the
combination is synergistic, not just additive. Human resource researchers have
speculated that policies that are ‘properly conﬁgured’ result in better outcomes,because bundles of practices ‘simultaneously exploit the potential for complementa-rities or synergies among such practices’ (Huselid, 1995, p. 636). Like otherresearchers (cf. Shockley & Allen, 2010), we conceptualize ﬂextime as an enhancingmoderator of the ﬂexplace –outcome relationships. In other words, ﬂextime augments
the value of ﬂexplace.

Does the attractiveness of FWAs depend on individual differences?
Theoretically, FWAs are most desirable to those who anticipate a need or preference
for using these policies. Correspondingly, the relationship between discretion overwhen and where one works and recruitment outcomes may depend on somedemographic and individual differences. An examination of moderators of theserelationships reveals important conditions to be considered when implementing suchpolicies.
Sex
Although both men and women are interested in ﬂexible workplace policies(Linkow, Civian, & Lingle, 2011), the likelihood of using a policy and the nature ofthe policy used differ between men and women (Bond, Galinsky, Kim, & Brownﬁeld,2005; Hill, Martinson, Hawkins, & Ferris, 2003; Hill et al. , 2008). Researchers
examining differences in sandwiched generation couples propose that wives mayselect jobs that offer more ﬂexibility than their husbands (Hammer & Neal, 2008);perhaps, because women tend to anticipate more work –family conﬂict than men
(Burley, 1994; Cinamon, 2006). Therefore, it seems likely that men and women may
be differentially attracted to organizations that offer FWAs. We hypothesize thatsex will moderate the relationships between ﬂextime/ﬂexplace and organizationattraction.

Preference for integration
Preference for integration describes the extent to which individuals maintainboundaries between their various work and non-work roles (Ashforth et al. , 2000;
Kossek, Lautsch, & Eaton, 2006; Kreiner, 2006). Some individuals prefer to separate
their work and non-work roles, whereas others prefer to integrate or blur the
boundaries between work and non-work, allowing for more overlap of roles andtasks. Shockley and Allen (2010) found that individuals with a higher level of a ‘needfor segmentation’ (the opposite of preference for integration) used FWAs lessfrequently than those with less of a need for segmentation. Anticipated caregiving demands
A third individual difference that may alter the attractiveness of FWAs is caregivingdemands. Individuals caring for dependent children and/or elders are likely to seek workarrangements that give them discretion over temporal and physical work boundaries tobetter manage these demands. Indeed, researchers have indicated that to attract and retainemployees with multiple dependent care responsibilities, employers should ﬁnd ways toimplement and maximize ﬂexibility (Hammer & Neal, 2008). Compared to applicants who
do not anticipate having caregiving demands, we hypothesize that individuals who do
anticipate having caregiving demands will have a stronger preference for organizationsthat offer FWAs.

Flextime versus ﬂexplace
Based on the premise that FWAs signal organizational support to future employees (cf.Greenberg, 1990; Grover & Crooker, 1995; Meyer, 1979) and previous research hasshown that ﬂexible beneﬁts are perceived as supportive (Casper & Buffardi, 2004;Rhoades & Eisenberger, 2002), we posited and found that applicants rate organizationsthat offer more discretion over when work is conducted as signiﬁcantly more attractivethan organizations that offer less or no discretion over work time, because theseorganizations are perceived as signiﬁcantly more supportive.
Likewise, participants also rated organizations offering more discretion over where
work is conducted as signiﬁcantly more supportive and attractive. Interestingly,organizations offering the most discretion over where work is conducted were rated assupportive and attractive as the organizations offering a moderate amount of discretionover work place. Thus, participants in this study did not perceive an appreciabledifference between a moderate and high amount of ﬂexplace. There are two possibleexplanations for this. First, despite how we intended to operationalize a high level ofﬂexplace, we realized after the study that the high ﬂexplace condition did not explicitly
indicate that individuals could work away from the main worksite as many days as desired.
Instead, the description simply read ‘work from home via technology such as a computer’.In contrast, the moderate level of ﬂexplace read ‘work from home via technology such as acomputer up to 2 days a week’. The potential ambiguity over how frequently one couldwork from home in the high condition may have contributed to the lack of a signiﬁcantdifference between moderate and high levels of ﬂexplace.
An alternative interpretation of the lack of differences between high and moderate
levels of ﬂexplace is that discretion over how frequently one can work away from the main
worksite may have a curvilinear relationship with recruitment outcomes, such that being
able to work in an alternative location as frequently as desired does not result inappreciable gains over having the opportunity to do so periodically. Additional researchparsing out different levels of discretion over how frequently one works away from themain worksite (e.g., 3 days a week, 1 day a month) is needed to determine whether this istrue or if there is a minimum amount of ﬂexplace necessary to have an inﬂuence.Theoretically, dramatic changes in perceptions and/or behaviours in response to smallchanges in one’s circumstances have been explained in mathematics using catastrophe
theory (Zeeman, 1976). Thus, to the extent that researchers continue to ﬁnd that making
ﬂexplace an option (regardless of how much discretion is extended) results in positiveoutcomes, catastrophe theory may help to explain this phenomenon.
A comparison of participant ratings of ﬂextime versus ﬂexplace revealed that
respondents rated ﬂextime slightly higher than ﬂexplace, suggesting that applicants valuediscretion over temporal boundaries a little bit more than discretion over physicalboundaries. Similarly, Allen et al. ’s (2013) meta-analysis recently revealed that ﬂextime
had stronger effects on employee work interference with family than ﬂexplace. Perhaps,
participants anticipated some of the potential downsides to working away from the main
ofﬁce including fewer opportunities for face-to-face networking and mentoring or theyworried about how a lack of physical boundaries between work and non-work can blurthe boundaries between corresponding roles resulting in more work –non-work conﬂict
(Hill, Miller, Weiner, & Colihan, 1998).740 Rebecca J. Thompson et al.Flextime and ﬂexplace
Because the combination of ﬂextime and ﬂexplace offers employees the most control overtheir work boundaries, and ﬂexplace beneﬁts can be enriched by ﬂextime, it waspredicted that the relationship between ﬂexplace and recruitment outcomes would be
enhanced by higher levels of ﬂextime. Although the high –high combination was
perceived as the most supportive and attractive as conveyed by the mean ratings for thatexperimental condition, the combination of the two had additive, rather than synergistic,effects on the focal recruitment outcomes. In other words, the inﬂuence of ﬂexplace wasnot dependent upon ﬂextime. Participants in this study did not consider ﬂextime whenrating the supportiveness and attractiveness of ﬂexplace. Each FWA dimension had itsown unique inﬂuence, and the addition of the other dimension did not enrich nordiminish the effect of the other. The potential synergies that occur when employees have
discretion over both temporal and physical work boundaries may not be apparent to
applicants and therefore not have the expected effect on recruitment outcomes. Instead,the synergies may manifest in employee outcomes like lessened work –non-work conﬂict
and related psychological outcomes.
Moderating effects
We also tested the extent to which individuals differ signiﬁcantly in their attraction to
organizations offering differing levels of ﬂextime/ﬂexplace based on individual and
demographic differences. We found that individuals with a strong preference forintegrating their work and non-work roles were signiﬁcantly more attracted toorganizations offering ﬂextime and ﬂexplace than individuals with a preference forsegmenting those roles. These ﬁndings suggest that individuals vary in their preferencesfor managing work and non-work demands, and these preferences play a role in howattractive organizations are as potential employers. This is consistent with recent researchexamining boundary management styles, which suggests there are multiple ways that
employees can manage work and non-work roles and that employers can more creatively
support employees’ management of these roles through customized work arrangements(Kossek & Lautsch, 2012).
Contrary to popular press articles that tout FWAs as policies geared more for women
than men (Smithson & Stokoe, 2005), both men and women found the organizationoffering the most discretion over time and place as the most attractive. This may reﬂectnew generations of workers who are attempting to distribute household chores andcaretaking more equitably, thus anticipating non-work demands that may interfere with
work demands constrained by traditional temporal and physical boundaries.
Contrary to expectation, the relationships between FWAs and recruitment outcomes
did not depend on anticipated caregiving demands. Perhaps, the respondents in this studyhave not thought much about childcare/eldercare responsibilities or do not fullyappreciate how difﬁcult these demands can be. Additionally, researchers may want toconsider measuring additional outcome variables such as anticipated work –non-work
conﬂict (e.g., Westring & Ryan, 2011).
Theoretical implications
Much of the research on FWAs has studied them as human resource beneﬁts asimplemented in practice, ignoring the theoretical constructs underlying these beneﬁtswhich help to explain why they are desirable and advantageous. We emphasize discretionFlexible work arrangements 741is a continuous construct, suggesting that there is a full range from none to unlimited
discretion that can be extended to employees. Theoretically, more discretion and controlis better and more desirable (Karasek, 1979). Consistent with this, our data indicated thatincrementally more ﬂextime was rated as signiﬁcantly more attractive. Similarly, some
discretion over where work could be conducted was rated as signiﬁcantly more attractive
than no discretion. Future research is needed to test whether alternative increments ofdiscretion result in similar trends. Some researchers have noted that having too muchdiscretion can be paralysing, a phenomenon called the paradox of choice (Schwartz,2004).
We proposed that applicants perceive FWAs as anticipated resources when evaluating
the attractiveness of a given organization. Consistent with Conservation of ResourcesTheory (Hobfoll, 1989, 2001), applicants were attracted to organizations that offered
more rather than less resources. Consistent with Spence’s (1973) signalling theory,
participants perceived organizations that offered FWAs as more supportive, and theseratings help to explain why the organizations were rated as attractive.
Researchers examining the effects of FWAs on employees have argued for the
distinction between availability anduseof FWAs (Butts et al. , 2013; Kelly & Moen, 2007;
Kossek, 2005; Kossek et al. , 2006). We propose that when studying applicants, it is
important to gather information on how the FWA is communicated, and the extent towhich applicants perceive the arrangement is ﬂexible. Applicants’ ratings of perceived
discretion over work time and place
4were positively related to the experimentally
manipulated dimensions in a theoretically consistent manner (.46 for ﬂextime and .63 forﬂexplace). These ratings of perceived discretion showed the same pattern of relationshipswith the outcome variables as the experimental variables, suggesting that to the extentthat policy and perceptions are aligned, positive outcomes will result. Alignment ofpolicies and perceptions sometimes break down when organizations communicatemixed or inconsistent messages to prospective employees.
Applied implications
Whereas the overall percentage of companies that offer FWAs continues to rise, manyorganizations have cut back on other forms of workplace ﬂexibility (e.g., reduced hours,career ﬂexibility; Matos & Galinsky, 2014). These trends suggest that organizations areinterested in offering policies with maximum effectiveness for employees and employers(Kossek et al. , 2014). The current study begins to assess the value of including
information about speciﬁc FWAs in recruitment materials, which is a very common
practice for attracting new talent (World at Work, 2011). By understanding the relative
attractiveness of each dimension of FWAs, organizations can determine which policieswill have the biggest impact.
The actual level of discretion extended to employees through various FWAs is not
uniform across all organizations. For example, some organizations adhere to theprototypical ﬂextime schedule which includes core hours, whereas other organizationsdo not have core hours. Additionally, the number of core hours varies across organizations(Baltes et al. , 1999). Some organizations permit telework only on an ad hoc (as needed)

The relatively strong effects of ﬂextime and ﬂexplace on the two recruitment
outcomes in this study suggest that it is beneﬁcial for organizations to offer these policiesas well as to market them when recruiting new employees. The consistency in the pattern
of relationships for both dimensions across two recruitment outcomes is quite
compelling. Organizations that offer FWAs in addition to the other beneﬁts are likely tohave a competitive edge in recruiting new employees.
Some jobs simply cannot be conducted at alternative times or places (e.g., bus driver).
Organizations that are only able to alter one dimension of FWAs (discretion over time orplace) are still likely to have an advantage in recruitment over organizations that do notoffer discretion over either. Alternatively, if organizations can only implement one FWAdue to limited resources, then based on the results obtained here, they may want to offer
ﬂextime as it had slightly stronger effects than ﬂexplace.
Limitations and directions for future research
To isolate the inﬂuence of ﬂextime from ﬂexplace, we chose to conduct a trueexperiment, manipulating levels of each FWA dimension independently. This calculateddecision was made in an effort to maximize internal validity recognizing a trade-off inexternal validity. Consistent with other recruitment studies, we asked participants to rate
descriptions of hypothetical organizations. This level of a control provided a low-cost
approach enabling us to demonstrate preliminary evidence of the unique and possiblejoint effects associated with perceptions of ﬂextime and ﬂexplace on job seekerassessments. The effects we revealed are likely an underestimate of real-world effectsgiven our experimental paradigm. Future research in this area should seek to replicatethese ﬁndings in naturally occurring recruitment contexts and extend our research to non-recruitment-related outcomes.
Second, the participants in this study were undergraduate students who will eventually
be going on the job market. College students are frequently the target of recruitment efforts
by organizations. Additionally, this age group is most likely to search job vacancies on Web-based platforms (e.g., Monster.com; Dineen & Noe, 2009). Thus, these participants arelikely to evaluate organizations based on vacancy characteristics such as FWAs. Using asample of individuals seeking employment maximizes task salience (cf. Graham & Cable,Flexible work arrangements 7432001); thus, the participants were likely to be primed to notice and interpret differences in
the organizational descriptions (Casper & Buffardi, 2004). Correspondingly, we expectour results to generalize to similar samples of potential job applicants.
Organizations that offered a high level of both ﬂextime and ﬂexplace were rated as the
most attractive and supportive, yet the combination of the two did not yield
supplementary higher ratings of support or attractiveness. There may be a thresholdbeyond which additional discretion does not lead to better outcomes. Alternatively, theparticipants in this sample may not have been able to fully anticipate the synergy that mayoccur when these two beneﬁts are combined as they may not have had the opportunity toactually experience both ﬂextime and ﬂexplace together. Correspondingly, applicantswith more job experience may be able to more critically evaluate the pros and cons ofFWAs. Additional research is warranted with a sample that has actually used FWAs.
There may also be generational differences in preferences for FWAs, with younger
generations preferring more discretion over temporal and physical boundaries betweenwork and non-work (Twenge & Campbell, 2008), while older generations may be lessaccustomed to this blending. Younger generations of workers may even expect FWAs. Infact, there is anecdotal evidence to support this (Overmyer, 2011).
To keep the task manageable for study participants, respondents were only asked to
rate positive organizational outcomes associated with offering FWAs. Future researchshould also examine positive and negative individual and organizational outcomes. Many
have speculated that altering boundaries between work and non-work can lead to more
work, work –non-work conﬂict, and burnout (Hill et al. , 1998). However, the extent to
which applicants anticipate such possibilities is not clear and likely depends on theirexperience using such policies as well as family demands and resources.
The current study provides initial experimental evidence for the inﬂuence of ﬂextime
and ﬂexplace on recruitment outcomes. Speciﬁcally, we isolated the effects of three levelsof each FWA dimension, revealing their independent inﬂuence on AOS and organizationattraction. It was also determined that these relationships were stronger for individuals
who prefer to integrate rather than segment their work and non-work roles. It is hoped
that the ﬁndings obtained here serve as the impetus for future research to avoidconfounding ﬂextime and ﬂexplace so that science can better inform practice.
