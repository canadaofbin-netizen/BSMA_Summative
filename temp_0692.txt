Abstract  
Boundary-spanning behavior has a ttracted considerable interest in recent years. Studies on this type of behavior have focused on 
its positive outcomes from the pers pective of social networks. For decades, research has consis tently demonstrated that the bo und-
ary-spanning behavior produces a wide array of positive results  for teams and organizations. Ho wever, scholars have found that  such 
behavior has negative outcomes for individuals. Using the conse rvation of resources theory (COR), we examined the dou-
ble-edged-sword effect of boundary- spanning behavior on creati vity at different levels, as well as its mediating mechanism an d 
boundary conditions. 
To test the proposed theoretical model, we applied multi-wave a nd multi-source research design. The data were collected from 
dyads of employees and supervisors in a company. At time 1, the  boundary-spanning behavior, role stress, and role breadth 
self-efficacy were measured. These variables were rated by the employees. Approximately a month  later, we asked the supervisor s to 
rate the employees’ creativity. These variables were assessed b y mature scales. A total of 536 employees (90.32%) and 111 lead ers 
(82.22%) responded to our survey. Confirmatory factor analyses and average variance extracted were conducted to assess the dis -
criminant validity and convergence validity of the key variable s. Multilevel structural equation modeling was used to validate  the 
hypothesis, and Monte Carlo simulation pro cedures using open-so urce software R were conducted to test mediation effects. 
Results showed that at the tea m level, boundary-spanning behavi or had a significantly positive effect on team creativity (β = 0.18, 
p < 0.05). However, at the individual level, boundary-spanning b ehavior had a significantly negative effect on employees’ creat ivity 
(β = –0.02, p < 0.05). At the individual le vel, boundary-spanning behavior h ad a significantly positive effect on role stress (β  = 0.05, p 
< 0.01) and role stress had a sign ificantly negative effect on cr eativity (β = –0.34, p < 0.001). The mediation effect of role stress was 
significant (β = –0.02, p < 0.05, Monte Carlo = 20000, 95% CI = –0.03, –0.006). This evi dence would indicat e that the bound-
ary-spanning behavior had a negative effect on individual creat ivity via role stress. Results a lso showed that the product ter m be-
tween the boundary-spanning behavior and role-breadth self-effi cacy was significant (β = –0.08, p < 0.01). The role-breadth 
self-efficacy moderated the rela tionship so that the mediating effect of role stress was stronger for employees with low role- breadth 
self-efficacy.  
The study illustrated the double-edged-sword effect of boundary -spanning behavior on creativity at different levels as well as  the 
mediating mechanism and boundary conditions regarding the negat ive effect of boundary- spanning behavior on individual creativ ity, 
thereby enriching the literature on boundary-spanning behavior.  Furthermore, this study identified the boundary conditions of COR, 
which broadens the scope of research on this theory. 
Key words  boundary spanning behavior; crea tivity; role stress; role-brea dth self-efficacy 
 
1  Introduction 
1.1  Questions 
In the current context of China’s efforts to push forward the 
new era of comprehensive deepening reform, enterprises are 
faced with a changing environment, which requires enterprises to pay close attention to the external environment at all times , 
gather information and resources  from outside the company to 
deal with volatile situations and to win in the fierce competit ion. 
So companies are increasingly, and urgently, encouraging em-
ployees to engage in boundary-spanning behavior. Bound-
ary-spanning behavior refers to the behavior of an individual 
taking information from outside t he organization and transmit-ting it to people inside the or ganization (Katz & Tushman, 
1983). Boundary-spanning behavior includes two levels, team and individual, which have been  studied respectively in the 
literature (Marrone, Tesluk, & Carson, 2007). However, team boundary-spanning behavior is ba sed on the individual level 
first and then aggregate to t he team level (Chan, 1998). The 
two levels of boundary-spanning behavior are interrelated, individual boundary-spanning beh avior is the basis of team 
boundary-spanning behavior (Liu & Li, 2014). In addition, according to the concept of boundary-spanning behavior, boundary-spanning behavior in volves both employees and 
teams and has an impact on both, with the employee being the perpetrator of the boundary-spanning behavior, the team is the  Acta Psychologica Sinica 
 receiver of boundary-spanning behavior. Does boundary-span-
ning behavior have the same eff ect on the perpetrator and the 
receiver of the act? Previous studies have not systematically answer this question yet. There fore, it is of great significanc e to 
study the boundary-spanning behavior at both team and indi-vidual levels in order to fully understand the effects of bound -
ary-spanning behavior on the outcome variables at different levels. 
Related research shows that boundary-spanning behaviors at 
the team level can improve t eam creativity (Xu & Qu, 2014), 
but at the individual level, we still know very little about wh at 
and how employees’ boundary-spanning behavior affects indi-vidual creativity. In many cas es, boundary- spanning behavior 
is an out-of-role behavior beyond the duty of role norms, and implementing boundary-spanning b ehavior spends a lot of ad-
ditional resources such as time and energy of employees (Xu, 
2019). Even in roles that require employees to span boundaries,  
employees also expend a lot of time and energy paying close attention to the external enviro nment and obtaining information  
that is useful to the team (Katz  & Tushman, 1983). The litera-
ture on creativity suggests that adequate resources are neces-sary to enhance individual crea tivity (Amabile, 1988). So from 
a resource perspective, boundary -spanning behavior can ham-
per creativity. Although it has b een recognized that there may 
be negative effects of boundary -spanning behavior (Marrone et 
al., 2007), no further research has been done to explore how th e 
negative effects of boundary- spanning behavior arise? And under what conditions? 
Based on the conservation of resources theory and previous 
studies (Hobfoll, 1989, 2001; Hobfoll, Halbesleben, Neveu, & 
Westman, 2018), this study constructed and empirically exam-ined the effects of boundary-spa nning behavior on creativity at  
both team and individual levels, and further explore how and 
under what conditions these effects occur. Conservation of resources theory (COR) suggests that the loss of individual 
resources can lead to stress that can adversely affect the indi -
vidual (Hobfoll, 1989; Hobfoll, H albesleben, Neveu, & West-
man, 2018). According to the conservation of resources theory, 
we think that at the team leve l, boundary-spanning behavior 
can obtain resources for the team and promote team creativity, but at the individual level, employees’ boundary-spanning be-
havior can consume their resources, leading to role pressure, 
thus reducing the individual creativity of the employee. In ad-dition, the literature on conservation of resources theory sug-
gests that self-efficacy influences the individual stress-pro-
ducing process (Hobfoll, 2001; Hobfoll, Freedy, & Geller, 1990). Based on this, we further examined the moderating ef-
fect of role-breadth self-effi cacy on the above relationships. 
1.2  Boundary-spanning behavior and creativity 
Hobfoll (1989) was the first to develop the conservation of 
resources theory. The theory holds that individuals always 
strive to acquire, preserve, and protect resources. Hobfoll (19 89) 
defined resources as “the individual’ characteristics, conditio ns, 
and energies, that make individual feel valuable or the means 
by which individual acquires th em.” These include material 
resources (e.g., plant), conditi onal resources (e.g., seniority , 
position), personal characteristic s (e.g., self-efficacy, and I Q), 
and capability resources (e.g. , time, and knowledge). These 
resources are valued by individuals because they help them 
achieve their goals. 
The literature on creativity s uggests that adequate resources are necessary to enhance individual creativity (Amabile, 1988).  
Inadequate resources are importa nt barriers to individual crea-
tivity (Amabile, 1988). Without s ufficient resources (such as 
time and knowledge), individuals will not be able to discover problems, find new ways to solv e them and find new ideas (de 
Jonge, Spoor, Sonnentag, Dormann, & Van Den Tooren, 2012). According to the conservation of resources theory, the acquisi-
tion, preservation, and conserva tion of resources are essential  
for the survival and development of individuals (Hobfoll, 1989;  
Hobfoll et al., 2018). Not only th e individual but also the tea m, 
in order to maintain their own survival and development, will 
continue to access resources . Team boundary-spanning behav-
ior is an important way for a team to acquire resources. At the  
team level, boundary-spanning be havior aims to achieve the 
team goals (Choi, 2002). In the process of interaction with the  
outside, employees will consciously acquire useful information and resources for the team. Often, these resources are scarce within the team, and they are heterogeneous and novel com-pared to resources within the  team. And heterogeneous, innova-
tive resources are necessary to enhance the team’s creativity 
(Zhang & Ge, 2016). In addition, boundary-spanning behav-ioral teams are proactive in r eaching out to the organization’s  
top leaders for support and co mmitment (Ancona & Caldwell, 
1992), which is important for enhancing the team’s creativity because of the high risks associated with creative activities, so 
it’s easy to fail (Zhu, Xu, & Zhang, 2018). Obtaining the sup-
port of the leader can free the team’s worries, and make the team feel comfortable to engage in creative activities, thus im -
proving the team’s creativity. Empirical studies have shown that team’s boundary-spanning b ehavior improves team crea-
tivity (Xu & Qu, 2014). 
But at the individual level, first of all, both in-and 
out-of-role boundary-spanning behavior, boundary-spanning employees need to pay close attention to the external environ-
ment for a long time, from the external complex and rapidly 
changing environment to obtain useful information for the team,  
this information needs to be translated into knowledge that 
team members can understand and then passed on to team 
members (Katz & Tushman, 1983), a complex process that consumes a lot of time and effo rt. The empirical study shows 
that there is a significant positive correlation between employ -
ees’ boundary-spanning behavior and resource loss (Xu, 2019). Second, both in-and out-of-role boundary-spanning behavior, 
as long as employees span boundary, means that employees 
need to maintain contact with a wide range of stakeholders (Ancona & Caldwell, 1992), deal with a wide variety of differ-
ent types of people and comple x interpersonal relationships. 
Too much external interpersonal activities will interfere with employees' attention, resulting in employees can not focus on 
creative work, which has a negative impact on individual crea-
tivity. Empirical studies have shown that boundary-spanning 
behavior reduces employees’ en gagement at work (Ramrajan, 
Bezrukova, Jehn, & Euwema, 2011). Finally, for out-of-role 
behavior, on the one hand, employees generally need to engage in out-of-office behavior (Paul, Scott, & Sarah, 2011), which 
usually takes up employees' downtime, leading to employees 
don't have enough time to recover from work, and the restora-tive experience is an important factor in boosting employee 
creativity (Eschleman, Madsen,  Alarcon, & Barelka, 2014). On 
the other hand, employees need to balance their in-role tasks with their out-of-role behaviors, which takes a lot of time and   ZHU Jiqiang et al. : The cross-level double-edged-sword effect of boundary-spanning behavior on creativity  
 effort. Individual resources such as time and energy are limite d, 
and the loss of individual re sources by boundary-spanning be-
havior will lead to the reduction of individual resources spent  
in creative activities. This is not conducive to the promotion of 
individual creativity. In conclu sion, we propose the following 
hypothesis: 
Hypothesis 1: At the team lev el, team boundary- spanning 
behavior promotes team creativit y. But at the individual level,  
employees' boundary-spanning behavior can reduce their indi-vidual creativity. 
1.3  The mediating effect of role stress 
Conservation of resources theory holds that both actual and 
potential resource losses trigge r an individual's stress and th at 
after stress, the individual take s steps to relieve it (Hobfoll , 
1989; Hobfoll et al., 2018). According to this theory, we pro-pose that employees' boundary-spanning behaviors consume 
their time, energy, and other re sources, thus resulting in role  
stress and have a negative impact on individual creativity. Rol e 
stress is a stress that occurs when an individual fails to fulf ill 
his or her role expectations effectively. It includes three asp ects: 
role conflict, role ambiguity, an d role overload (Kahn, Wolfe, 
Quinn, Snoek, & Rosenthal, 1964) . Role conflict refers to an 
employee's inability to meet the conflicting demands or expec-
tations of multiple roles (Kim,  Murrmann, & Lee, 2009; Rizzo, 
House, & Lirtzman, 1970). Role ambiguity refers to an em-
ployee's lack of clarity or understanding of his or her role an d 
inability to achieve clear role expectations (House & Rizzo, 1972; Rizzo et al., 1970). Role overload is when the expecta-
tions of a role exceed the lim its of one's capacity (Singh, 199 8). 
Role stress tends to occur in boundary-spanning activities 
(Wang, Liu, & Liu, 2017). Both in- and out-of role require the 
employee to span boundary. As long as the employee imple-
ments the boundary-spanning behavior, the employee needs to contact with different external stakeholders, and need to play 
different roles in interacting with different external stakehol ders. 
Each role has different, and sometimes even conflicting de-
mands on the employee (Rakaraja n et al., 2011), and the fre-
quent boundary-spanning activity of the employee requires 
constant switching between diff erent and possibly conflicting 
roles. On the one hand, this is easy to cause the role of confl ict; 
on the other hand, the employees will have confusion and not 
know clearly their own positioni ng, resulting in role ambiguity . 
If boundary- spanning behavior is extra-role behavior, on the 
one hand, in addition to the role tasks within the team, bound-
ary- spanning employees also need to take on multiple tasks such as getting information from outside, processing informa-
tion, and passing information to others. This increases the 
workload of the employees, and consumes a lot of time, energy, and other resources, leading to role overload (Deng, Liu, &Mao,  
2018). Empirical studies show tha t the extra-role helping be-
haviors that consume employees' time and energy tend to cause role stress (Chen, Chen, & Luo, 2020). On the other hand, the 
task work within the role and the boundary-spanning behavior 
outside the role may not be cons istent with the requirements of  
the employees, which easily leads to role conflict. 
According to the conservation of resources theory, measures 
will be taken to prevent further resource loss when an individ-ual feels stress (Hobfoll, 1989; Hobfoll et al., 2018). A creat ive 
activity consumes a lot of em ployee’s resources. When the 
boundary-spanning employees due to excessive consumption of resources, resulting in the employees do not have enough re-sources to invest in creative activities, in order to prevent f ur-
ther loss of resources, the employees will reduce the investmen t 
in creative activities, choose re laxation to restore resources (de 
Jonge et al., 2012), and wait unt il resources are restored befo re 
you get back to work. Empirical  studies have s hown that role 
stress reduces employee’s engagement (Wang, Han, & Yang, 
2014). In addition, role stress is often accompanied by emo-
tional exhaustion (Wang & Zhang, 2016). Employees in emo-
tional exhaustion often lack the motivation to work (Wan, 2011)  
and are not motivated to engage in creative activities. Empiric al 
studies have shown that role stress reduces employee creativity  
(Çekmecelioglu & Günsel, 2011). Based on the above analysis, we propose: 
Hypothesis 2: Employees' boundary-spanning behavior re-
duces employees' individual creativity by increasing their role  
stress. 
1.4  The moderating effect of role breadth self-efficacy 
Conservation of resources theor y holds that individuals with 
more resources are less susceptible to resource loss and identi -
fies self-efficacy as an impor tant individual trait resource 
(Hobfoll, 2001) and an important factor of maintaining the individual resource pool (Hobf oll, 2001, p. 349), influencing 
the process by which loss of resources leads to stress (Hobfoll , 
2001). Role breadth self-efficac y is a kind of important 
self-efficacy, and it is close ly related to boundary-spanning 
behavior and role stress. T herefore, we propose that 
role-breadth self-efficacy moderates the relationship between boundary-spanning behavior and role stress. Role-breadth 
self-efficacy refers to an emplo yee's perception of his or her 
ability to perform tasks that are broader, more proactive, and beyond the call of duty (Hwang, Han, & Chiu, 2015). It is a 
form of self-efficacy that em phasizes the employee's confi-
dence to perform broader out-of-role behaviors (Galperin, 2012). 
Boundary-spanning behavior is a very challenging and 
complex activity (Ma rrone et al., 2007) and  the implementation 
of boundary-spanning behavior cons umes a great deal of time 
and effort. Role-breadth self-efficacy (Hobfoll, 2001), as an 
important personal trait resour ce, can help employees to resist  
the resource depletion caused by boundary-spanning behaviors. 
According to the conservation of resources theory (Hobfoll, 
2001), employees with a high role - breadth self-efficacy have 
more resources. Those with more resources are less sensitive to  
resource loss and less prone to stress. Specifically, employees  
with high role- breadth self-efficacy were more confident in their ability to manage relationships with stakeholders than 
employees with low role-breadth self-efficacy (Marrone et al., 
2007), and this self-confidence can help employees to reduce the resources consumption caused by boundary-spanning be-
havior, thereby reducing the negative effects of bound-
ary-spanning behavior, thus red ucing role stress. Employees 
with high role-breadth self-efficacy were more confident of 
their ability to perform tasks  beyond their assigned duties (Li ao 
& Liang, 2015) and more confident of performing extra-role behaviors (Galperin, 2012), they  have a high ability to cope 
with setbacks (Hartog & Belschak, 2012), and they can cope 
with difficulties (Liao & Liang,  2015). Therefore, in the face of 
the challenges and complexiti es of boundary- spanning behav-
ior, such as conflicting role demands, role ambiguity, and role  
overload, they have the confidence to play a variety of roles and to be able to address these issues. therefore bound- Acta Psychologica Sinica 
 ary-spanning behavior is less likely to cause role stress. Base d 
on the above analysis, we propos e the following assumptions: 
Hypothesis 3a: Role breadth sel f-efficacy moderates the re-
lationship between boundary-spanni ng behavior and role stress. 
The higher the role breadth self-efficacy of employees, the 
weaker the positive effect of boundary- spanning behavior on role stress. 
Furthermore, when the role stress of employees is reduced, 
the initiative and enthusiasm o f employees to engage in crea-
tive activities are increased. In particular, employees with hi gh 
role-breadth self-efficacy are more motivated to be active and 
innovative (Parker, Williams, & Nick, 2006), and more actively 
engaged in creative activities. Combining Hypothesis 2 and 
Hypothesis 3a, we propose the fo llowing first stage moderated 
mediation mode l hypothesis: 
Hypothesis 3B: Role breadth s elf-efficacy moderates the 
mediating effect of role stress on employees' boundary- span-
ning behavior and individual creativity. The higher the em-ployee's role breadth self-efficacy, the weaker the mediation o f 
the role stress is.

2.2  Measurement 
Boundary-spanning behavior. Us ing a scale developed by 
Faraj and Yan (2009), employees were asked to rate the extent 
to which they had received information and resources from 
outside the team. Using A seven- point scale, 1 to 7 represented  
“very inconsistent” to “very consistent”. A total of 4 measure-
ment items, a typical measurement item is “I will contact with 
important people outside the tea m to obtain information and 
resources for the team”. Following Marrone et al (2007), the 
average value of each employee's boundary-spanning behavior 
is regarded as the boundary-spann ing behavior at the team level . 
The reason for averaging, rather than variance or maximum or 
minimum, is that the variance a pproach is suitable for explor-
ing variables that make up a diverse team, such as age diversit y. 
The maximum or minimum method is suitable for situations 
where the team outcome depends entirely on the individual in 
the team (Barrick, Stewart, N eubert, & Mount, 1998). In this 
study, team’s boundary-spanning behavior is mainly concerned 
with the number of boundary- spanning behavior, not the diver-
sity of boundary-spanning behavior, so from our theoretical 
focus point of view, it is not suitable for the use of variance  
method. The number of the team 's boundary-spanning behavior 
is not decided by an individual's boundary-spanning behavior in the team, but decided by the team members  as a whole, so 
the team's boundary-spanning beha vior is not suitable to use th e 
maximum or minimum method. In the same way, team creativ-
ity is aggregated using the mean method. Cronbach's α is 0.80 
for employee boundary-spanning behavior and 0.79 for team’s 
boundary-spanning behavior.

4  Discussion 
From the perspective of resource, this study explores the ef-
fects of boundary-spanning behavior on team creativity and individual creativity, as well as the mediating mechanism and 
boundary conditions. The results show that team bound-ary-spanning behaviors improve t eam creativity at a team level,  
but at the individual level,  employee boundary-spanning be- Acta Psychologica Sinica 
 haviors negatively a ffect individual creativity by increasing 
role stress. Compared with the employees with high role-breadth self-efficacy, the employees with low role-breadth  
self-efficacy were more likely t o have role stress after imple-
menting boundary-spanning behaviors . Thus the negative effect 
on individual creativity is eve n stronger. Scholars and practi-
tioners should be alert to the adverse effects of bound-ary-spanning behavior and minimize their negative effects while giving full play to their positive effects. 
4.1  Theoretical contributions 
The theoretical contributions o f this study are mainly re-
flected in the following three as pects: First, previous studies  
have either examined the effects of boundary-spanning behav-
ior from the perspective of beh avior receiver or perpetrator 
(Ancona & Caldwell, 1992; Song, Wang, & Zhang, 2017), this study integrates these two perspectives and explores the posi-
tive and negative effects of boundary- spanning behavior at 
both team and individual levels . More importantly, when fo-
cusing on the literature on the relationship between bound-
ary-spanning behavior and creativity, we found that current 
research focuses on the impact of boundary-spanning behavior on behavioral receiver (team cre ativity) (Xu & Qu, 2014), this 
study focuses on the impact of boundary-spanning behavior on 
the behavior actor himself (indi vidual creativity), that is, we  
shift the research perspective of the relationship between 
boundary-spanning behavior and creativity from the behavior 
receiver to the behavior actor himself, expanding from the team  
level to the individual level an d helping scholars and practiti o-
ners to more fully understa nd boundary-spanning behavior. 
Second, previous studies have focused on the positive ef-
fects of boundary-spanning behavior from a learning and net-
work perspective (Liu, Jiang,  Chen, Pan, & Lin, 2018). Em-
pirical studies such as Xu and Qu (2014) showed that team boundary-spanning behavior impr oves team creativity through 
team learning. Liu and Li (2014) examined the mediating role 
of network-centeredness and found that employees’ bound-ary-spanning behavior promoted their task performance by 
enhancing their network-centere dness. Different from previous 
studies, this study examines the negative impact of bound-ary-spanning behavior from the resource perspective. The re-
sults show that boundary-spanning behavior has a negative 
impact on individual creativity by  increasing role stress. The 
results of this study show that in addition to the positive imp act 
of boundary-spanning behavior, but a lso have a neg ative impact 
on employees, thus expanding the boundary-spanning behav-ior-related research. 
Finally, the research enriches conservation of resources the-
ory and finds the specific boundary condition of the resource preservation theory-The role-brea dth self-efficacy. Halbesleben , 
Neveu, and Paustian Underdah l (2014) called people to pay 
attention to the interaction of multiple resources and empha-sized the role of focusing on e nvironmental factors. In respons e 
to the call of Halbesleben et al. (2014), the present study ex-
plored the moderating effect of the intrinsic psychological characteristic resources (role-breadth self-efficacy) on the 
process of resource depletion, w hich further enriched the theor y 
of resource conservation. 4.2  Practical implications 
Previous studies on boundary-spanning behavior and crea-
tivity have shown that boundary-spanning behavior can pro-mote team creativity (Xu & Qu, 2014), but this study shows that boundary-spanning behavior  can not only promote team 
creativity, but also hinder individual creativity, and this sug -
gests that the contribution of boundary-spanning behavior to team creativity may come at the expense of individual creativ-
ity. That is, the individual sa crifices the individual to achie ve 
the greater good. Therefore, in practice, when teams enhance 
team creativity through members’ boundary-spanning behav-
iors, we need to be alert to the negative effects of bound-
ary-spanning behaviors on employees. On the one hand, we need to take measures to minimize the negative effects; on the 
other hand, we should encourage and reward the team spirit of 
the employees. For example, when building a team, try to choose employees with a high sense of self-efficacy. And more 
praise should be used to manag e employees to improve their 
sense of self-efficacy. Because employees with a high sense of self-efficacy have less adverse effects when they engage in 
boundary-spanning behavior. In addition, team leaders can 
publicly praise and reward employees who engage in bound-ary-spanning behavior, and make other members feel that the 
implementation of boundary-spanni ng behavior is recognized 
and supported by the team. On the  one hand, it can reduce the 
role stress and the negative in fluence of the boundary-spanning  
behavior. On the other hand, it is also an affirmation and reco g-
nition of the dedication of the team that the employees sacri-ficed themselves to achieve, thus encouraging the employee to 
make more efforts to contribute to the team. 
4.3  Research limit and future research 
First of all, although this stu dy adopts a multi-stage and 
multi-source design to reduce the effect of common method 
bias to a certain extent, it s till can not reach the conclusion  of 
causality, in particular, boundary-spanning behavior and role 
stress were collected at the same time point. Future research 
may consider using the experiment a l  w a y t o  t e s t  t h e  r e l a t i o n s  
between the variables, in order to obtain a reliable causal con -
clusion. In addition, the team c reativity is measured by the wa y 
that team leaders evaluate each member’s individual creativity,  
rather than by the team as a whol e, which is also the shortcom-
ing of this study, future resear ch will allow team leaders to 
directly evaluate the overall cr eativity of the team, and furth er 
verify the conclusions of this paper. 
Second, in many cases, boundary-spanning behavior may 
not be voluntary, but rather orch estrated by the team to adapt to 
the external environment (Ancona & Caldwell, 1992), so it can 
be divided into active and passive behaviors. The effects of 
these two different types of boundary-spanning behavior may be different. Future studies coul d continue to explore the posi -
tive and negative effects and mechanisms of different types of 
boundary-spanning behavior. In addition, this study only fo-cused on the stress mechanism. A t the individual level, in addi -
tion to role stress, there may be other mechanisms that lead to  a 
positive impact on creativity. For example, if bound-ary-spanning behavior employees have rich sources of infor-
mation, employees can easily access a wealth of even new 
ideas, which may promote indivi dual creativity. Future research  
will continue to explore the dual and even multiple mechanisms 
by which boundary-spanning behaviors have both positive and 
negative effects on creativity at the individual level. 
Finally, this study is conducte d in China, and whether the 
conclusions can be extended to other countries remains to be further tested. China is a country with high collectivism 
(Hofstede, Hofstede, & Minkov, 2010), and Chinese employees  ZHU Jiqiang et al. : The cross-level double-edged-sword effect of boundary-spanning behavior on creativity  
 are more likely to sacrifice their own interests for the sake o f 
the collective good, so they are more likely to take the initia tive 
to span boundaries in order to improve team performance. In 
contrast, the United States is  a country with a higher tendency  
to individualism (Hofstede et al., 2010). American workers are more concerned with their own interests and less likely to sac-
rifice their own for the colle ctive good, so they are less like ly to 
span boundaries on their own in itiative. That is, employees 
from different cultural backgrou nds may have different possi-
bilities and motivations for boun dary-spanning behavior. Future  
studies can collect data in different countries at the same tim e 
for comparative studies and may draw interesting conclusions.
