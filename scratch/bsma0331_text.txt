Prompted  by the relationship  management  literature  which has appeared  
recently  in marketing,  we posit  that the performance  of sellers  in ongoing  buyer ­
seller relationships  is influenced  significantly  by the manner  in which sellers  
interact  with buyers,  which we refer to as "boundary  behavior."  Although  a 
growing  literature  has drawn attention  to the importance  of relationship  
management,  no research  has investigated  the extent  to which,  if any, relation ­
ship management  leads to improved  performance  in terms  of "hard"  measures,  
such as sales. We fill this gap by examining  how managerially  controllable  
boundary  behaviors  influence  sellers ’ sales performance.  We further  examine  
how situational  factors moderate  these effects,  and consequently  illuminate  
contexts  in which  the actions  of boundary  spanning  personnel  are most critical.  
The findings  are likely to be of interest  to managers  who operate  at the 
organization  boundary,  and who are therefore  concerned  with the effective  
deployment  of these important  resources.

I. INTRODUCTION
The purpose  of this study is to examine  how the manner  in which sellers  
interact  with buyers,  in ongoing  industrial  buyer-seller  relationships  (hereafter  
"business  relationships"),  influences  sellers ’ performance.  The study  is motivated  
by the basic premise  that the success  of business  relationships  is influenced  by 
both the product  exchanged  and the nature of the interactions  (hereafter  
"boundary  behavior")  that take place  between  buyers  and sellers.
This premise  is supported  by research  which  has appeared  recently  in the 
marketing  literature  which underscores  the importance  of cooperation  and 
reciprocity  in ongoing  relationships  (e.g. Anderson  and Narus 1990; Anderson  
and Weitz 1989; Arndt 1979; Dwyer, Schurr and Oh 1987; Levitt 1983).  
Presumably,  improved  relationships  lead to improved  performance  of sellers  in 
terms of "hard"  measures  such as increased  share of buyers ’ business,  but no 
empirical  research  has addressed  this question.
In this study we seek to address  this void by assessing  the influence  of 
managerially  controllable  boundary  behaviors  on sellers ’ performance  across  a 
variety  of business  relationships.  Further,  we examine  how contextual  factors  
(environment,  buying  situation,  and age of relationship)  moderate  this relation ­
ship. The implications  of such moderating  effects  are particularly  important
1

because  boundary  behavior  involves  an investment  of human  resources.  To 
effectively  allocate  these limited  resources  it is essential  that managers  know  
under  what circumstances  the boundary  behavior > performance  linkage  is 
more,  or less, strong. The main objective  of this study  is, therefore,  to address  
this question.
In developing  our model  of seller  performance,  we draw  upon  extant  theory  
and empirical  evidence  in the areas of (1) marketing  channels  and industrial  
buyer behavior,  (2) negotiation  behavior,  (3) group behavior,  and (4) or­
ganizational  information  processing  to identify  boundary  behaviors  which are 
likely to lead to enhanced  performance  of sellers. These behaviors  relate  
primarily  to how effectively  boundary  activities  are coordinated,  and how 
effective  these  behaviors  are in creating  favorable  perceptions  among  organiza ­
tion members.  We further  identify  characteristics  of the environment  and buying  
situation  which  are expected  to moderate  these  effects,  principally  through  their  
influence  on decision-making  risk. Finally,  we examine  the moderating  effects  
of age of the relationship.
The model  is tested  by surveying  buyers  in a broad  cross-section  of business  
relationships.  Organization  informants  evaluate  a particular  supplier  ("target

regularly  uses alternative  suppliers.  Accordingly,  assessments  of target  suppliers ’

IL CONCEPTUALIZING  BUSINESS  RELATIONSHIPS
A. Defining  Performance  in Business  Relationships
In keeping  with tradition  in marketing,  we define  performance  in business  
relationships  from the perspective  of the seller. We examine  two quantitative  
and one qualitative  measure  of sellers ’ performance.  The first quantitative  
measure  is the share of business  suppliers  derive from buyers,  taking into 
consideration  the number  of competitive  suppliers  used. The second  quantitative  
measure  consists  of buyers ’ estimates  of their allocations  to target suppliers,  
assuming  such allocations  were  unconstrained  by contractual  agreements,  supply  
limitations,  switching  costs, and so on. This measure  is intended  to draw out 
buyers ’ "idealistic"  purchases  which  may not be reflected  by the current  status.
In addition  to these quantitative  measures,  we also assess  buyers ’ trust in 
sellers. Trust  is regarded  as a critical  attitude  in ongoing  relationships  because  
it captures  the degree  to which  one party  is willing  to rely on another,  knowing  
it will incur losses  if the trusted  party  fails (Deutsch  1958; Rotter  1971; Schurr  
and Ozanne  1985). Buyers  do not normally  "hedge"  by placing  simultaneous  
orders  with multiple  sellers;  they trust that sellers  will deliver  as promised.  
Where  trust in a seller  is high relative  to competitive  sellers,  a buyer  is likely  to 
rely more  on that seller  (i.e., place  more  business  with that seller).  We therefore
4

--- PAGE 15 ---
5
expect  that trust will be highly  correlated  with our quantitative  measures.
B. Relationships  as Emergent  Groups
Organizations  are related  through  exchanges  (boundary  behaviors)  which  
occur among  a subset  of members  (boundary  spanners)  belonging  to both the 
buying  and selling  organizations.  As a result  of their ongoing  interaction,  these  
boundary  spanners  also comprise  a separate  group  (Schopler  1987; Van de Ven 
1976) or "social action system"  (Van de Ven 1976) which is the conduit  of 
information  and resource  exchanges  between  the two organizations  (hereafter  
this group is referred  to as the "boundary  group"). In a very real sense the 
boundary  group  is the relationship.  Johnston  and Bonoma  (1981),  in a related  
vein, developed  this notion  in connection  with the buying  center:
The buying  center  exists as a communication  network  that does 
not necessarily  derive its configuration  nor operation  from the 
formal  organization,  but rather  from the regularized  patterns  of 
communication  that reflect the individuals  involved  and their  
relationships  (p. 146).
Although  the emergent  group concept  has been recognized  by previous  
researchers  investigating  interorganizational  relationships  (Schopler  1987; Van 
de Ven 1976),  its major  implication,  that our understanding  of such relationships  

--- PAGE 16 ---
6
can be enhanced  by examining  group processes , has not been reflected  in 
empirical  research.  We therefore  draw, in part, upon group  theory  to develop  
our investigation  of boundary  behaviors,  recognizing  that it is the conjunction  of 
these  behaviors  as both organization  spanning  and group  actions  that is a unique  
characteristic  of interorganizational  relationships.
In research  on group behavior,  group effectiveness  is usually  defined  in 
accordance  with two broad  sets of criteria. The first is the effectiveness  with 
which  groups  accomplish  external  tasks  (Johnson  and Johnson  1987;  Shaw  1981).  
For boundary  groups, the overriding  external  task involves  consummating  
economic,  i.e., product,  exchanges.  Outcomes  of such exchanges  are evaluated  
from  both buyers ’ and sellers ’ perspectives.  Specifically,  buyers ’ assessments  are 
expected  to follow  principally  from  their  evaluations  of sellers ’ product  offerings.  
We acknowledge  the primary  importance  of the offering,  but in this study we 
wish  to examine  how  boundary  behavior  affects  performance  after  accounting  for 
the value afforded  by the offering . Accordingly,  we include  as a covariate  the 
buyers ’ evaluation  of seller ’s offerings.
The second  set of group  effectiveness  criteria  includes  the cohesiveness  and 
adaptiveness  with which  group  members  work  to achieve  external  tasks,  i.e., how 
well groups  deploy  their  human  resources  independent  of external  task (Bradford  
1964; Egan 1976; Martin 1983). This type of effectiveness  is important  in 

--- PAGE 17 ---
7
predicting  how  well groups  are likely  to perform  when  faced  with tasks  which  are 
not routine,  practiced,  or highly procedurized.  Business  relationships  are 
characterized  by varying  levels  of future  uncertainty,  and thus boundary  groups  
require  varying  levels of cohesiveness  and adaptiveness.  We expect  buyers  to 
adjust  their dependence  on sellers  (i.e., allocation  of business  to those sellers)  
partly  in accordance  with their  perceptions  of how  effectively  the boundary  group  
is likely  to deal with future  contingencies.  For buyers  this is a simple  matter  of 
risk balancing;  the greater  the perceived  risk associated  with a particular  seller,  
the less the dependence  on that seller. Accordingly,  it is this latter  concept  of 
group effectiveness  (relating  to cohesiveness  and adaptiveness)  which is our 
focus,  and which  motivates  our investigation  of boundary  behavior  as an antece ­
dent to performance.
Activities  which  promote  group  cohesiveness  and adaptiveness  are frequently  
referred  to as group  maintenance  functions  (Ancona  and Caldwell  1988;  Dimock  
1986). Maintenance  functions  relate to interaction  among  boundary  group  
members  (Van de Ven 1976),  that is, the flow of information  which  influences  
their  knowledge  and perceptions.  Much  of what  we know  about  group  processes  
has been  gained  through  the "experiential  learning"  approach  to group  research,  
wherein  participant-observers  have learned  effective  group  skills in an environ ­
ment in which external  goals are de-emphasized  (Bradford  1964; Gibb 1964).

--- PAGE 18 ---
8
Group  researchers  frequently  identify  group  structure  as an explanatory  factor  
for group effectiveness  (Dimock  1986; Johnson  and Johnson  1987). Group  
structure  is frequently  defined  in terms  of the pattern  of interaction  among  group  
members  (Dimock  1986;  Johnson  and Johnson  1987). Generally,  group  resear ­
chers  have found  that when  interaction  among  group  members  is extensive,  that 
is, when  all (or nearly  all) members  are engaged  in meaningful  interactions  with 
each other,  group  cohesiveness  and adaptiveness  is enhanced  (Bradford  1964;  
Egan 1976; Gibb 1964). Based  upon findings  from group  research,  we include  
boundary  group  structure  variables  among  the explanatory  variables  in our model  
of business  relationships  (Figure  III-1).
C. Boundary  Group  as Coordination  Mechanism
A broad range of literature,  including  negotiation  theory (Pruitt 1981),  
transaction  cost theory  (Williamson  1979), and the "relationship"  literature  in 
marketing  (Anderson  and Narus 1984, 1990; Dwyer,  Schurr and Oh 1987;  
Frazier,  Spekman  and O’Neal 1988), converges  on the notion  of coordinative  
behavior  (also referred  to as "collaborative,"  or "cooperative"  behavior)  as a 
central  desideratum  of ongoing  relationships.  Coordinative  behavior  is a very 
general  phrase  referring  to parties ’ commitment  to, and behaviors  directed  
toward,  achieving  mutually  rewarding  outcomes  (Dwyer,  Schurr  and Oh 1987;

--- PAGE 19 ---
9 
Pruitt  1981;  Van de Ven and Walker  1984). This is in contrast  with competitive  
behavior,  wherein  parties  perceive  that achievement  of their  own goals  comes  at 
the expense  of their partners ’ (i.e., outcomes  are zero sum). Over and above  
specific  actions  taken to achieve  coordination  in the narrow,  logistical  sense,  
coordinative  behavior  includes  actions  which  show  that partners ’ are committed  
to achieving  mutual  benefit. Coordinative  interorganizational  behavior  and 
cohesive  group  behavior  are achieved  simultaneously  in the boundary  group.
Business  relationships  are seldom  circumscribed  by detailed  contracts  which  
spell out responses  to every contingency,  and most managers  are averse to 
pursuing  such contracts  (Macaulay  1963). Boundary  group members,  as 
boundary  spanners,  may be expected  to experience  relatively  high levels  of role 
conflict  and role ambiguity  as they strive  to reconcile  differences  in organization ­
al goals which inevitably  arise (Lysonski  1985). Therefore,  for relationship  
stability,  coordinative  behavior  is necessary  from  two viewpoints:  (1) integrating  
organizational  goals,  and (2) promoting  the psychological  well-being  of boundary  
group members,  who in turn influence  the perceptions  and attitudes  of other  
organization  members.  Anderson  and Narus  (1990)  found  coordination  to have  
a positive  influence  on trust in channel  relationships,  and research  on industrial  
buyer behavior  has shown  that industrial  buyers  strongly  prefer  to use a col­
laborative  problem-solving  approach  in dealing  with sellers  (Day, Michaels  and

--- PAGE 20 ---
10 
Perdue  1988; Dion and Banting  1988; Perdue,  Day and Michaels  1986). No 
research,  however,  has investigated  the influence  of coordinative  behavior  on 
sales-related  performance.
Specific  coordinative  behaviors  relate to the manner  in which boundary  
group  members  provide  information  about  themselves  and their organizations,  
as well as the manner  in which  they acquire  information  about their partners  
(Pruitt  1981). We distinguish  coordinative  behavior  from coordinative  mechan ­
isms . such as formal  rules and procedures  (Ruekert  and Walker  1987),  on the 
basis of the former ’s ability  to convey  motives  and intentions  in addition  to 
providing  specific  information,  or heuristics,  for coordinating  activities.  Sellers ’ 
coordinative  behaviors  comprise  the second  set of explanatory  variables  in Figure  
III-1.
D. Boundary  Spanning
In order to effectively  coordinate  activities,  related  organizations  must  
process  information  across  their respective  boundaries.  Accordingly,  boundary  
group  effectiveness  is likely to be influenced  by its capacity  as an information  
processing  unit. Daft and Lengel  (1986) suggest  that organizations  process  
information  for two reasons:  (1) to reduce uncertainty,  and (2) to reduce  
equivocality  (ambiguity).  Uncertainty  means  the absence  of information,  and

--- PAGE 21 ---
11
corresponds  to the amount  of information  that must be processed,  whereas  
equivocality  refers  to the existence  of multiple  or conflicting  interpretations,  and 
relates  to the ability  of the information  channel  to deal  with  complex  information  
and/or  subtle  differences  in meaning.
We assume  organizations  have both information  processing  requirements,  
and accordingly  define  information  capacity  of the boundary  group  in terms  of 
its ability  to accommodate  both  kinds  of information  processing  needs.  The third  
set of explanatory  variables  in Figure  III-1 relates  to the information  capacity  
of the boundary  group.
E. Context  Effects
The boundary  group approach  to business  relationships  is "micro,"  when  
viewed  from the perspective  of the organization  as a whole. From  the buyer ’s 
perspective,  a seller  represents  but one element  in a larger  environment.  The 
boundary  group  perspective  focuses  on interpersonal  processes,  independent  of 
larger organizational  considerations.  Assuming  interpersonal  processes  are 
influential  in establishing  boundary  group norms and behaviors,  then the 
evolution  of boundary  groups may not necessarily  "fit" the needs of the 
organization  as viewed  from a larger  perspective.  For example,  buyers  facing 
rapid technological  change  or other dynamic  elements  in their output  market  

--- PAGE 22 ---
12
generally  require  more  adaptive,  responsive  business  relationships  than  do buyers 
facing  relatively  stable  output  markets  (Ruekert  and Walker  1987; Walker  and 
Ruekert  1987). But what will result  if the boundary  group  is not adaptive  or 
responsive?
Stated  differently,  the foregoing  question  asks to what extent,  if any, the 
influence  of boundary  behaviors  on sellers ’ performance  is contingent  on the 
context  of the business  relationship.  In addition  to buyers ’ output market  
environment,  characteristics  of the product  may also be expected  to moderate  
the boundary  behavior > performance  linkage  because,  from  the buyer ’s point  
of view, different  buying  situations  may be more, or less, critical  to buyers ’ 
overall  profitability.  Finally,  we expect age of the relationship  to have a 
moderating  effect,  because  over time buyers  and sellers  tend to form enduring  
beliefs  which  are not necessarily  related  to their current  interaction  (Anderson  
and Weitz  1989). Figure  III-1 therefore  incorporates  the moderating  influence  
of buyers ’ environment,  product  characteristics,  and age of relationship.
F. Domain  of Study
Researchers  emphasize  that voluntary  exchanges  between  organizations  may 
be placed  along  a continuum  ranging  from "discrete"  ("market")  on one end to 
"relational"  on the other  end, depending  on the parties ’ level of incentive  for, or 

--- PAGE 23 ---
13
commitment  to, an ongoing  relationship  (Dwyer,  Schurr  and Oh 1987; Macneil  
1980; Ruekert,  Walker  and Roering  1985; Williamson  1979). It is only in 
instances  in which exchange  is at least moderately  "relational"  that our 
framework  is relevant.  Williamson  (1979)  conceptualizes  such relationships  as 
those  requiring  investment  of significant  idiosyncratic  human  capital,  in the form  
of relationship  specific  training  and leaming-by-doing.  We place  this study  in the 
relational  domain  by examining  only business  relationships  which  (1) have been  
ongoing  for one year or longer,  and (2) involve  a product,  in terms  of potential  
impact  on buyers ’ profitability,  of at least moderate  importance.  We therefore  
expect  at least a moderate  level of relationship-specific  human  capital  in our 
sample  of business  relationships.
G. Summary
We define  sellers ’ performance  in business  relationships  in terms  of the share  
of business  sellers  obtain  (or might  obtain)  from  buyers,  as well as buyers ’ trust 
in them. Traditionally,  marketers  have focused  on the marketing  mix as the 
principal  factor  explaining  sellers ’ performance.  Recent  marketing  literature  has 
emphasized  the importance  of the "human"  element  in business  relationships,  
which  we view from the perspective  of the boundary  group.
Boundary  groups  emerge  as a result of ongoing  interaction,  and form the 

--- PAGE 24 ---
14
primary  mechanism  of coordination  in business  relationships.  Coordination  
entails  the processing  of information,  not only for specific,  logistical  purposes,  
but also as the basis  for boundary  group  members ’ perceptions  and motivational  
inferences.  Three  sets of boundary  behavior  variables  are expected  to influence  
sellers ’ performance:  (1) boundary  group structure,  (2) sellers ’ coordinative  
behaviors,  and (3) boundary  information  capacity.  Boundary  group structure  
refers to the interaction  pattern  in the boundary  group. Sellers ’ coordinative  
behavior  refers to sellers ’ information  giving and information  seeking  style.  
Boundary  information  capacity  refers  to the amount  and complexity  of informa ­
tion which  the boundary  group  is able to process.
The environment,  buying  situation,  and age of the relationship  are expected  
to moderate  the influence  of boundary  behavior  on sellers ’ performance  through  
their effects  on decision-making  uncertainty,  decision-making  importance,  and 
belief  formation,  respectively.

III. MODEL  OF SELLERS ’ PERFORMANCE
A. Dependent  Variables
1. Share  Ratio  - Actual. Actual  share ratio refers  to the ratio of sellers ’ 
actual  share  of buyers ’ business  divided  by their  "expected"  share,  where  expected  
share is the inverse  of the number  of suppliers  used by buyers ’ for the focal 
product. A share ratio of greater  than 1.0 thus indicates  a share premium,  
whereas  a ratio of less than 1.0 indicates  a share  penalty.
Buyers  are expected  to spread  their business  among  a set of suppliers  such 
that their dependence  on each represents  a balanced  risk. Buyers ’ perceptions  
of risk associated  with sellers  are expected  to follow  from their perceptions  of 
the (1) uncertainty,  and (2) significance  of the outcomes  from  dealing  with those  
sellers  (March  and Shapira  1987). Where  uncertainty  is lower,  the significance 
of outcomes  must therefore  be greater  for a given level of perceived  risk. The 
more  effectively  coordinated  the business  relationship,  the less uncertainty  buyers  
will associate  with an inherently  unpredictable  future;  buyers  will be confident  
that they will be able to adapt. Factors  such as price, quality,  delivery,  and 
service  being  equal,  sellers  in better  coordinated  relationships  are expected  to 
receive  more of buyers ’ business.

3. Trust. Trust refers to the degree  to which  buyers  believe  sellers  are 
reliable  and may be counted  on to act in buyers ’ interests  (Schurr  and Ozanne  
1985). As has been noted in the literature,  trust implies  a willingness  to put 
oneself  at risk, because  there is always  the possibility  that the trusted  party  may 
not act in one’s interests  (Deutsch  1958; Rotter  1971; Rempel,  Holmes  and 
Zanna  1985). Trust is strongly  linked  to ongoing  exchange  because  it is not 
possible  to specify  in advance  all possible  contingencies  which can affect a 
relationship.  One party  in a relationship  must  inevitably  act on the expectation  
that the other  will respond  as desired,  and thus must  trust the other  (Blau 1974).  
Trust  has been emphasized  in recent  marketing  channels  literature  as an

--- PAGE 27 ---
17
important  factor  in relationship  continuity  (Anderson  and Weitz  1989;  Anderson  
and Narus  1990).
B. Explanatory  Variables
1. Seller ’s Vertical  Involvement.  Seller ’s vertical  involvement  refers  to the 
degree  to which interaction  with the buyer is hierarchically  dispersed  in the 
seller ’s organization.  Boundary  group  members,  acting  as representatives  for the 
organization  as a whole,  are accountable  to others  in the hierarchy  (upward  and 
downward)  for their actions  as organization  representatives.  Where  sellers ’ 
hierarchical  representation  is greater,  buyers  will perceive  less seller account ­
ability  outside  the boundary  group,  and consequently  less likelihood  of agree ­
ments  with  boundary  personnel  being  modified,  or countermanded  (Pruitt  1981).  
Where  the perceived  risk of modification  or abrogation  of agreements  is less, 
buyers  will associate  less uncertainty  with boundary  group  decisions,  and will be 
more  likely  to increase  their  dependence  on sellers  by increasing  share. We thus 
hypothesize  that seller ’s vertical  involvement  will be positively  associated  with 
share.
Seller ’s vertical  involvement  is also expected  to be positively  related  to 
buyer ’s trust. Group  researchers  have consistently  reported  that the level  of trust 
in groups  is influenced  positively  by the extent  to which  all group  members  are 

--- PAGE 28 ---
18
"connected,"  i.e., all engaged  in ongoing  interaction  (Bradford  1964; Gibb 1964;  
Golembiewski  and McConkie  1975;  Martin  1983). The distinction  between  par­
ticipants  and observers  in groups  actualizes  this concept.  An overarching  group  
objective  is to achieve  a condition  in which all members  are participants  
(Bradford  1964). Observers  lower  the level  of group  trust  because  it is uncertain  
whether  or not they have "bought  in" to the group  norms  and commitments,  and 
thus it is unclear  whether  they are motivated  to support  group  decisions  (with  
which  they may not fully agree).
The boundary  group is embedded  in a larger system comprising  the 
respective  parent organizations.  From the buyer ’s perspective,  there are 
"observers"  in the seller  organization  to the extent  that the boundary  group  fails 
to subsume  it entirely  ("complete"  trust may be viewed  as a goal, not fully 
attainable  in the small  group  sense).  Seller ’s vertical  involvement  is one measure  
of the degree  to which  the boundary  group  subsumes  seller  organizations,  and 
is therefore  expected  to correlate  positively  with the perceived  level of "par­
ticipants"  in the seller organizations.  We therefore  hypothesize  that seller ’s 
vertical  involvement  will be positively  related  to buyer ’s trust.
2. Seller ’s Lateral  Involvement,  Seller ’s lateral  involvement  refers to the 
degree  to which interaction  with the buyer  is functionally  dispersed  within  the 

--- PAGE 29 ---
19
seller  organization.  Business  relationships  typically  require  the coordination  of 
activities  involving  a number  of functional  areas (Walker  and Ruekert  1987).  
The more extensively  representatives  of these functional  areas are involved  in 
interaction  with the buyer, the greater  is the expected  level of participative  
coordination  (McCann  and Galbraith  1981; Walker  and Ruekert  1987).
Participative  coordination  refers  to mutual  resolution  of conflict,  or potential  
conflict,  among  the parties  whose  activities  must be coordinated.  This is in 
contrast  to hierarchical  coordination,  which refers to imposition  of higher  
authority,  usually  through  required  adherence  to formal rules or operating  
procedures  (Walker  and Ruekert  1987). Participative  coordination  is expected  
to lead to fuller understanding  of, and more adaptive  solutions  to, problems  
which  may cut across  functional  areas. Effective  coordination  on the part of the 
seller will result in fewer problems  adversely  affecting  the buyer, and should  
result  in greater  perceived  competence  and enhanced  confidence  in sellers  (i.e., 
lower  perceived  risk associated  with those sellers). Other  factors  being equal,  
buyers  are more  likely  to shift  business  toward  sellers  in which  they  perceive  less 
risk. Accordingly,  we hypothesize  the level of sellers ’ lateral  involvement  to be 
positively  related  to share.
Following  from  the same  argument  advanced  for vertical  involvement,  sellers ’ 
lateral  involvement  is expected  to be positively  related  to the perceived  level of

20
"participants"  in the seller organization.  Where  the level of participants  is 
higher,  trust  is expected  to be correspondingly  higher.  We therefore  hypothesize  
that sellers ’ lateral  involvement  will be positively  related  to buyers ’ trust.
3. Buyer ’s Vertical  and Lateral  Involvement.  Buyer ’s vertical  involvement  
refers to the degree to which interaction  with the seller is hierarchically  
dispersed  within  buyer ’s organization.  Buyer ’s lateral  involvement  refers  to the 
degree  to which  interaction  with the seller is functionally  dispersed  within  the 
buyer ’s organization.  Buyers ’ vertical  involvement  is expected  to relate  primarily  
to the level of approval  of, and organizational  commitment  to, boundary  group  
agreements,  whereas  lateral  involvement  is expected  to relate  primarily  to coor­
dination  of functionally  interdependent  activities.
More  extensive  vertical  involvement  within  the buyer  organization  will result  
in greater  opportunity  for responsible  members  to directly  negotiate  with the 
seller  regarding  scope  of activities,  mutual  responsibilities,  terms  and conditions  
of payment,  remedies  and recourse,  etc. More  extensive  lateral  involvement  will 
result  in greater  opportunity  to coordinate  functional  assignments,  set schedules,  
establish  liaison,  etc. We expect  both of these  measures  to be positively  related  
to the level of coordinative  effort,  and we assume  that, on average,  such efforts 
are effective.  Accordingly,  both  variables  are expected  to exert  a direct  influence

on coordination  effectiveness.
Further,  owing  to bounded  rationality,  individual  boundary  group  members  
within  buyers ’ organizations  are not likely  to have familiarity  with all details  of 
the business  relationship.  Where  these  individuals  perceive  extensive  boundary  
group involvement,  therefore,  they are likely to conclude  that others  in their  
organization  have "signed  off* in their respective  areas. Consequently,  they are 
likely  to have greater  generalized  confidence  in dealings  with that seller,  even  
pertaining  to areas  outside  of their direct  responsibility.  Therefore,  in addition  
to their  direct  impact  on coordination  effectiveness  in the logistical  sense,  greater  
vertical  and lateral  involvement  also operate  through  their influence  on the 
perceptions  of boundary  group  members.
In sum, greater  vertical  and lateral  involvement  of buyers  is expected  to lead 
to (1) more  effective  coordination  with sellers,  as well as (2) perceptions  which  
enhance  buyers ’ general  level of confidence  in sellers. Accordingly,  we 
hypothesize  that buyers ’ vertical  involvement  and buyers ’ lateral  involvement  will 
positively  influence  share.
Buyers ’ vertical  involvement  and lateral  involvement  are also expected  to 
positively  influence  buyers ’ trust. As buyers ’ involvement  with sellers  becomes  
more extensive,  greater  opportunities  are available  for members  of seller  
organizations  to demonstrate  trustworthy  behavior.  In the process  of interaction

display  trust. Seemingly  insignificant  interactions  such as returning  phone  calls 
promptly,  anticipating  and responding  to others ’ concerns  without  being  asked,  
and making  the effort  to confirm  commitments,  all build trust (Golembiewski  
and McConkie  1975). Based  on the assumption  that exchanges  are, on average,  
used propitiously  to this end, greater  interaction  will enhance  the trust of both 
parties. Empirical  support  for this conclusion  has been found in marketing  
channels,  where  greater  channel  member  participation  in decision  making  was 
found  to positively  influence  trust (Dwyer  and Oh 1987).

4. Disclosing,  Disclosing  refers to the extent,  as perceived  by buyers,  to 
which  sellers  provide  information  regarding  their (sellers ’) plans,  programs,  and 
objectives.  In business  relationships  it is imperative  that each party  have a suffi­
cient understanding  of the other ’s business  so as to permit  adaptive  problem  
solving. In support  of this, Anderson  and Weitz  (1989)  have found  that when  
channel  members  keep their partners  well informed  as to the details  of their  

--- PAGE 34 ---
24
business,  partners  are likely to place greater  confidence  in them, coordinate  
more effectively  with them,  and be more  willing  to rely on them.
Business  relationships  involve  a process  of ongoing  negotiation,  that is, a 
continual  resolution,  or prevention,  of conflict. Pruitt (1981) stresses  the 
importance  of information  exchange  in achieving  coordinative  agreements.  In 
simulation  of buyer-seller  bargaining  Pruitt  and his associates  have found that 
providing  explicit  information  about goals, values,  and priorities  consistently  
leads to increased  levels of "integrative  agreements"  (Carnevale,  Pruitt and 
Seilheimer  1981; Pruitt and Lewis 1975; Pruitt and Lewis 1977). Integrative  
agreements  are those which  entail  a high level of mutual  satisfaction  with the 
outcomes.  Greater  disclosing  by the seller  is expected  to lead to more effective  
coordination  because  it provides  buyers  with a rich data base for the formulation  
of adaptive  solutions  to conflict  which  are perceived  to be based  on the genuine  
needs  of the seller  (Pruitt  1981). Because  disclosing  has been shown  to lead to 
coordination  effectiveness,  we hypothesize  that disclosing  will have a positive  
influence  on share.
In small groups,  disclosing  is risk-taking  behavior  because  it opens the 
discloser  to possible  rebuff  or ridicule  (Egan  1976;  Golembiewski  and McConkie  
1975;  Martin  1983). Analogously,  disclosing  in business  relationships  may entail  
giving  information  which  is potentially  damaging  to the seller. Examples  of such 

--- PAGE 35 ---
25
disclosures  would  be the revealing  of plans  and goals,  which  if passed  on to com­
petitors  could be used to sellers ’ detriment.  When such disclosing  is done  
because  it is in the best interests  of the relationship,  it is perceived  as a trusting  
action  on the part of sellers,  and should  in turn enhance  buyers ’ trust. In 
support,  Anderson  and Weitz (1989)  found that the level of communications  
concerning  plans,  programs,  expectations,  goal setting,  and performance  evalua ­
tion was positively  related  to trust in channel  relationships.  We therefore  
hypothesize  that disclosing  will have a positive  influence  on trust.
5. Initiating.  Initiating  refers to the degree  to which  sellers  overtly  seek 
relevant  information  about buyers ’ business  operations  and market  needs.  
Initiating  is a relationship-specific  form  of intelligence  gathering.  If all relevant  
and important  information  needed  to effectively  coordinate  activities  in a 
business  relationship  were  voluntarily  offered,  then  initiating  would  not be neces ­
sary. However,  owing  to bounded  rationality,  buyers  are unlikely  to know  all of 
the information  which is either useful, or necessary,  for sellers ’ optimal  
fulfillment  of their obligations  in the business  relationship.  The degree  to which  
sellers  actively  seek this information  is likely to be predictive  of the degree  to 
which they actually  possess  such information.  The degree  to which sellers  
possess  needed  information  is, in turn, likely  to positively  influence  coordination  

--- PAGE 36 ---
26
effectiveness,  and share.
Initiating  behavior  is also expected  to positively  influence  buyers ’ perceptions  
of sellers ’ motivation  to perform  in their (buyers ’) interests.  Where  sellers  show  
high initiation,  they are likely to be perceived  as "intrinsically"  motivated  (i.e., 
motivated  by concerns  for the mutual  welfare  of the parties),  and consequently  
trusted  more  by buyers (Rempel,  Holmes  and Zanna  1985).
6. Signaling.  Signaling  refers  to the degree  to which  sellers  communicate  
their  intended  actions  in the relationship.  Signaling  by sellers  is the most direct  
form of coordinative  behavior  because  it (1) clarifies/confirms  planned  actions,  
and (2) provides  an opportunity  for buyers  to modify  planned  actions  before  they 
are (potentially)  erroneously  enacted. Accordingly,  we expect signaling  to 
positively  influence  coordination  effectiveness,  and share.
Signaling  also complements  initiating  by providing  feedback  on information  
gained  from  buyers.  In small  group  settings  this has been  described  as "reflecting  
accurate  empathy"  (Egan 1976), or "clear signaling"  (Golembiewski  and 
McConkie  1975). Signaling  closes the loop begun  with initiating.  Signaling  
presents  an opportunity  for sellers  to confirm  that they have attended  to sellers ’ 
needs  and expectations,  and that those  needs  and expectations  will be responded  
to. In relationships,  partners  must not only be empathetic  with their counter ­

--- PAGE 37 ---
27
parts,  they must actively  convey  this (Egan  1976). In group  research,  signaling  
has been shown  to be a trust-building  behavior  (Egan  1976; Golembiewski  and 
McConkie  1975); accordingly  we hypothesize  that it will positively  influence  
buyers ’ trust in business  relationships.

7. Frequency.  Frequency  is defined  as the inverse  of the average  time 
between  interactions  between  buyer and seller. Other factors  being equal,  
frequency  relates  positively  to the amount  of information  processed  in the 
boundary  group,  and to the ability  of boundary  interaction  to reduce  uncertainty  
(Daft  and Lengel  1986). On balance,  greater  uncertainty  reduction  should  lead 
to greater  coordination  effectiveness  (Anderson  and Weitz 1989),  and greater  
share.
The finding  of a positive  relationship  between  communication  frequency  and 
trust has been  fairly  robust,  both within  the marketing  literature  (Anderson  and 
Nanis 1990; Anderson  and Weitz 1989) and the literature  on group  behavior  
(Bradford  1964;  Loomis  1959; Golembiewski  and McConkie  1975). In general,  
the more frequently  individuals  communicate,  the more they are perceived  by 
their partners  to be motivated  in the interest  of the relationship,  and the more  
they are trusted.  Accordingly,  we hypothesize  that frequency  will have a positive  
influence  on buyers ’ trust.
8. Richness.  Richness  is defined  as the proportion  of interactions  between  
the buyer  and seller  which  are conducted  face-to-face.  Face-to-face  communica ­
tion is a richer  medium  than impersonal  modes  (Daft and Lengel  1986). In 
contrast  to quantity  of information,  richness  refers to the ability to process  

--- PAGE 39 ---
29
ambiguous  information  (Daft and Lengel  1986). Rich communication  media  
(e.g., face-to-face  meetings)  are most effective  in dealing  with ambiguity.  
Organizations  are expected  to have both types of information  processing  needs  
(Daft and Lengel  1986; Huber  and Daft 1987), and we assume  both types of 
information  must be processed  for effective  coordination  in business  relation ­
ships. We further  assume  that, due to considerations  of cost and logistics,  most  
business  relationships  are characterized  by sub-optimal  levels of face-to-face  
communication  (sub-optimal  in an information  processing  sense,  not cost/benefit  
sense).  Accordingly,  increasing  the proportion  of face-to-face  communication  is 
expected  to produce  a more balanced  fit with the actual  information  load, and 
enhance  coordination  effectiveness.  We therefore  hypothesize  that richness  will 
positively  influence  share.
Richness  is expected  to have a positive  influence  on buyers ’ trust because  
face-to-face  interaction  has the capacity  to convey  non-verbal  cues and voice  
inflections  which can substantially  influence  individuals ’ perceptions  of others  
(Johnson  and Johnson  1987; Shaw 1981). Indeed,  some researchers  consider  
face-to-face  interaction  to be a necessary  condition  for group development  
(Bonner  1959; Homans  1950). Considering  the extent  to which face-to-face  
interaction  and non-verbal  information  processing  has been emphasized  in the 
trust literature,  we expect  richness  to have a positive  influence  on buyers ’ trust.

1. Environmental  Dynamism  - Share.  Environmental  dynamism  refers  to the
perceived  frequency  of change  in market  forces  in buyers ’ environments  (Achrol  

--- PAGE 41 ---
31
and Stern 1988). Environmental  dynamism  has been found to be positively  
related  to decision-making  uncertainty  in marketing  channels  (Achrol  and Stern  
1988). This finding  is in general  agreement  with the literature  which has 
investigated  the effects  of environmental  uncertainty  (Duncan  1972; Huber  and 
Daft 1987; Leblebici  and Salancik  1981; Thompson  1967).
Where decision-making  uncertainty  is high, the relative  importance  of 
participative  coordination  is expected  to be greater  than when  decision-making  
uncertainty  is low, because  more flexible  and innovative  approaches  to the 
resolution  of interdepartmental  conflict  are likely to be required  (Walker  and 
Ruekert  1987). Therefore,  the greater  the environmental  dynamism,  the more  
important  the coordination  enhancing  effects of sellers ’ and buyers ’ lateral  
involvement  are expected  to be.
Sellers ’ and buyers ’ vertical  involvement  both relate to the dispersion  of 
decision-making  authority  represented  in the boundary  group. We expect  the 
responsiveness  of the boundary  group  as a decision-making  unit to be enhanced  
by greater  vertical  involvement,  because  accountability  to members  outside  the 
boundary  group is lessened  (more  key decision  makers  belong  to the group).  
Where  environmental  dynamism  is high, the responsiveness  of the boundary  
group  is likely  to be a more important  consideration  than when  environmental  
dynamism  is low, because  the boundary  group  will be called  upon  to make  more  

--- PAGE 42 ---
32
decisions,  more quickly  (Huber  and Daft 1987; Walker  and Ruekert  1987).  
Accordingly,  we hypothesize  a greater  influence  of vertical  involvement  (buyers ’ 
and sellers ’) when  environmental  dynamism  is high.
Sellers ’ coordinative  behaviors  are also expected  to have  a stronger  influence  
on performance  under conditions  of high environmental  dynamism.  Where  
decision-making  uncertainty  is high,  the need  to make  more  innovative  decisions,  
and consequently  the need for more extensive  knowledge  of sellers,  is greater  
than under  conditions  of low decision-making  uncertainty  (Walker  and Ruekert  
1987). Sellers ’ disclosing  gives buyers ’ a basis from which  to formulate  more  
adaptive  solutions  to problems,  and is therefore  expected  to be more  instrumen ­
tal in achieving  coordination  effectiveness  under  conditions  of high, versus  low, 
decision-making  uncertainty.  Likewise,  the coordination  enhancing  effects  of 
sellers ’ initiating  and signaling  should  have a greater  impact  when decision ­
making  uncertainty  is high.
Environmental  uncertainty  has typically  been examined  as an antecedent  of 
boundary  spanning  activity  (Katz  and Kahn 1966; Schwab,  Ungson  and Brown  
1985; Thompson  1967), although  there is evidence  that organization  structure  
may influence  boundary  spanning  more than environmental  uncertainty  (Leifer  
and Huber  1977). However,  no research  has investigated  the moderating  effect  
of environmental  uncertainty  on boundary  spanning  as a predictor  of perfor ­

decision  enhancing  capacity  of each exchange.

3. Environmental  Diversity  - Share. Environmental  diversity  refers  to the 
degree  of heterogeneity  in buyers ’ output  markets  for the focal  product.  Where  
buyers ’ customers  are diverse  with respect  to demographics,  industry,  and 
preferences  for product  features,  buyers ’ strategic  behavior  is likely  to approxi ­

Under  such  conditions,  greater  interfunctional  conflict  and need  for coordination  
(within  buyer  organizations)  is expected  because  managers  must contend  with,  
and adapt to, a broader  array of unfamiliar  situations  (Walker  and Ruekert  
1987). On average,  this adaptation  is likely  to also complicate  the coordination  
task on the part of sellers,  as they are called  upon  to make  modifications  in their  

--- PAGE 46 ---
36

argue that, due to such added complexities,  heterogeneous  environments  in 
channels  require  more  decentralized  decision  structures  and more  participative  
decision  making.  Accordingly,  we hypothesize  that the coordination  enhancing  
effects  of sellers ’ lateral  involvement  and buyers ’ lateral  involvement  will be 
relatively  more  important  under  conditions  of high environmental  diversity  than 
under  low diversity.
Greater  vertical  involvement  in the boundary  group  means  greater  decision ­
making  authority  and less accountability,  thus enabling  it to respond  to a broader  
range  of situations  than  when  vertical  involvement  is lower. Where  buyers  serve  
a diverse  set of customers,  with diverse  needs  (which,  in turn, face a diverse  set 
of environments),  buyers  will be presented  with (1) more frequent  decision ­
making  situations,  and (2) more  varied  decision-making  situations.  Environmen ­
tal diversity  is, therefore,  expected  to increase  the importance  of the decision ­
making  responsiveness  of the boundary  group.
Sellers ’ coordinative  behaviors  are also expected  to assume  greater  impor ­
tance under conditions  of high environmental  diversity. Specifically,  sellers ’ 
disclosing,  initiating,  and signaling  will have  greater  impact  when  the number  and 
complexity  of problems  are greater,  and the potential  adverse  impact  of sub­
optimal  solutions  is greater.  Under  such conditions  the risk reducing  potential

5. Product  Importance  - Share  and Trust. Product  importance  refers  to the 
perceived  impact  of the product  on buyers ’ profitability.  Product  importance  is 
expected  to be directly  related  to the magnitude  of decision-making  outcomes,

value  of the product  is greater,  or the product  otherwise  has greater  potential  to 
affect  buyers ’ profitability  (via impact  on quality,  productivity,  etc.), buyers  are

those  decisions  to a greater  degree  than for products  of lesser  importance.

vertical  and lateral  involvement  (Johnston  and Bonoma  1981). We expect  the 
same effects,  on average,  to obtain  in business  relationships.  No research,  
however,  has examined  the moderating  effect  of product  importance  on boundary  
behavior  as a predictor  of performance.

Coordination  and risk reduction  are therefore  expected  to be more strongly  
predictive  of performance  in more  important  situations.  It follows  logically,  then,

IV. METHOD
A. Measures
Descriptions  of the measures  used in the study appear  in Appendix  A. 
Scales used to measure  these constructs  were either adapted  from previous  
measures  or developed  specifically  for this study. In developing  these  measures  
a thorough  literature  review  was first conducted.  Next, a pool of items was 
developed  taking  into consideration  extant  measures,  the domain  of the construct  
as defined  herein,  and the context  of the study. Preliminary  questionnaires  were  
then  developed  and pre-tested  via personal  interviews  with purchasing  managers  
and purchasing  agents. Successive  rounds  of two or three interviews  were  
conducted,  each round incorporating  refinements  to the instrument  based on 
prior rounds.  This process  was continued  until feedback  from the interviewees  
indicated  that the instrument  was clear, answerable,  meaningful,  and relevant,  
and that further  continuation  of the process  was unlikely  to result  in substantive  
improvements.  The process  comprised  four rounds  and a total of ten interviews 
(averaging  about  45 minutes  each).
In the early stages  of questionnaire  development,  three  changes  were made  
to the original  plans regarding  study variables.  First, the emphasis  of the 
variable  disclosing  was shifted  toward  sellers ’ tendencies  to reveal  potentially
50

--- PAGE 61 ---
51
damaging  or embarrassing  information,  rather  than straightforward  information  
regarding  plans, objectives,  and so on (as originally  planned).  This latter  
information  was viewed  by interviewees  as "normal-course-of-business,"  and thus,  
expected.  In contrast,  disclosing  "less-than-glamorous"  information  emerged  as 
a more powerful  construct  in the context  of relationships.  Second,  informants  
were asked to evaluate  a target product  (rather  than product  or service,  as 
originally  planned).  Early  interviews  revealed  that (1) a "product"  focus  allowed  
informants  to more easily  select  a "target"  offering,  and (2) attempts  to elicit a 
mix of target products  and services  would be likely to confuse  informants.  
Informants  were, however,  asked to classify  the target  product  as (1) capital  
goods,  (2) parts  or components,  (3) raw materials,  (4) operating  or maintenance  
supplies,  or (5) other. We then used this information  to infer the size of the 
"service"  component  of the target product. Specifically,  we regarded  "capital  
equipment"  and "parts  and components"  as "high  service"  products,  whereas  "raw 
materials"  and "operating  or maintenance  supplies"  were classed  as "low service."  
This classification  served  as the basis for testing  moderating  effects  of product  
type. Third,  an additional  dependent  measure,  satisfaction  with the relationship , 
was incorporated.  The purpose  of this addition  was to assess  buyers ’ affect at 
a very global  level. Accordingly,  our measure  tapped  buyers ’ overall  feelings  
toward  suppliers  (including  their  wishes  that other  suppliers  were  "like this one,"  

--- PAGE 62 ---
52
and their general  disposition  toward  continued  relationships;  see Appendix  A). 
We hypothesized  that our global  satisfaction  measure  would  be influenced  in the 
same manner  as trust, but sought  a more global  indicator  of affect  toward  the 
supplier.  Analyses  and discussion  which follow  reflect  these three changes  to 
prior  plans.
B. Data Collection
Data  were collected  via a national  mail survey. The sampling  frame  for the 
study  was the master  membership  list of the National  Association  of Purchasing  
Management  (NAPM),  from  which  a random  sample  of 800 names  and business  
addresses  was obtained  (NAPM  provided  this sample). NAPM  members  
typically  belong  to their firm ’s purchasing  department  and have the title of 
"Purchasing  Manager"  or "Purchasing  Agent." Along  with the study question ­
naire, each of the 800 informants  was sent a personalized  cover letter and 
postage-paid  business  reply envelope. No gift or monetary  incentive  was 
provided.  One week after the initial  mailing  a postcard  reminder  was sent to 
every  informant,  again  requesting  their participation  in the study. Three  weeks 
after the initial  mailing  a replacement  questionnaire,  modified  cover  letter,  and 
return  envelope  were mailed  to those informants  who had not yet responded.  
Copies  of the first cover letter, postcard,  and second  cover letter appear  in

--- PAGE 63 ---
53
Appendix  B. Appendix  C contains  the study  questionnaire.
Thirty-five  of the 800 questionnaires  were returned  unanswered  for one of 
the following  reasons:  (1) the address  was undeliverable,  (2) the addressee  was 
no longer employed  at that address,  or (3) the addressee  was no longer  
employed  in a purchasing-related  position.  Consequently,  these  informants  were  
deleted  from the sample. From  the remaining  765 informants,  a total of 454 
usable  responses  was obtained  for a response  rate of 59.3%,  which  compares  
quite favorably  to response  rates typically  obtained  in organizational  surveys.  
Further,  a comparison  of the early  and late responses  (specifically,  the first 20% 
received  versus  the last 20% received)  showed  no significant  differences  in the 
means  of any of the study  variables,  indicating  that nonresponse  bias is unlikely  
to be a problem.
Informants  were asked  to select  their firms ’ most recent  product  purchases  
meeting  the following  three conditions:  (1) the product  was at least moderately  
important,  (2) they  purchased  the product  from  two or more  competing  suppliers,  
and (3) they were very familiar  with their firms ’ relationships  with these  
suppliers.  These instructions  were intended  to ensure that informants  had 
adequate  knowledge  of the relationship  and that the relationship  was of 
sufficient  importance  that the effects  hypothesized  in our model  could  reasonably  
be expected.  Informants  were instructed  to select  one supplier  of the product,

thereafter  directing  their responses  to the "target  product"  and "target  supplier."  
So as to obtain  variance  on the dependent  measures,  half of the informants  were  
asked to select as the target supplier  the one with the largest  share of their  
business  for the target  product,  and the other half were asked to select the 
supplier  with the smallest  share.

V. FINDINGS  AND DISCUSSION
A. Main  Effects
1. Actual  and Predicted  Share  Ratio. On balance,  our findings  suggest  that 
boundary  behavior  has little ability  to predict  sellers ’ shares. Specifically,  since  
none  of the regression  coefficients  for model  4.1 were  significant,  hypotheses  1 A, 
IB, IC, ID, 4A, 4B, 4C, 7A, and 7B, relating  to the main  effects  on actual  share  
ratio, were not supported.  Likewise,  none of the regression  coefficients  for 
model  4.2 were significant,  thus failing  to support  hypotheses  2A, 2B, 2C, 2D, 
5A, 5B, 5C, 8 A, and 8B, relating  to the main effects  on predicted  share ratio.  
In sharp contrast  to these findings,  it appears  that boundary  behavior  is quite  
effective  in explaining  buyers ’ trust and satisfaction  (discussed  below). In light 
of these latter  findings,  the former  are surprising  because  trust in, and satisfac ­
tion with, a seller  might,  on their own merits,  reasonably  be expected  to lead to 
rewarding  that seller with greater  business. Parenthetically,  we tested this 
supposition  by regressing  both actual  share ratio and predicted  share ratio on 
trust and satisfaction,  and neither  variable  was significant  in explaining  sellers ’ 
shares  (actual  or predicted).  It is also important  to bear in mind that product  
rating was non-significant  in regressions  for sellers ’ shares (both actual and 
predicted).
76

--- PAGE 87 ---
77
These  results  suggest  that other  factors,  not addressed  in the study,  underlie  
buyers ’ failure  to "reward"  those  suppliers  which  they trust, and with which  they 
are satisfied,  and whose  product  they rate highly. Clearly,  buyers  may choose  to 
reward  suppliers  in ways other than increasing  shares  (e.g., paying  a premium  
price, "trading  up" to high-margin  goods, accepting  seller-preferred  deliv-  
ery/service  schedules,  and so on). Also,  non-performance  factors  may influence  
sellers ’ shares  (e.g., sellers ’ location,  complementary  offerings,  reciprocity,  and 
so on). On the other hand, it may be that control  mechanisms  necessary  to 
effect  "performance-share"  matching  are not in place in many  organizations,  or 
that momentum  and convenience  tend  to perpetuate  buying  patterns  even  though  
disconfirming  evidence  is available.  The concluding  section  of the dissertation  
discusses  these issues  more fully.
Notwithstanding  the foregoing  discussion,  which  addresses  omitted  variables  
which might be responsible  for the "non-findings"  relating  to sellers ’ shares,  
methodological  limitations  must  also be considered.  It should  be noted  that the 
study  employed  a single  informant  in each organization.  Consequently,  it is not 
possible  to fully assess  the construct  validity  of measures  employed  in the study,  
particularly  convergent  validity  (Campbell  and Fiske 1959; Phillips  1981). Had 
multiple  methods  (multiple  informants)  been utilized  for each organizational

disclosing,  and signaling,  which  are all of roughly  equal  influence  (see full sample

the less-than-glamorous  aspects  of their businesses,  make  overt efforts  to keep

these  things  as well or to as great  a degree.  These  findings  should  be of interest

prior expectation  of a positive  relationship  between  these variables  and 
satisfaction  was not supported.  In regression  model  4.6 (final  satisfaction  model)  
coefficients  of product  rating,  disclosing,  initiating,  signaling,  and frequency  are

coefficient  of richness  is non-significant,  failing  to support  our expectation  (Table

influenced  by product  rating,  disclosing,  initiating,  signaling,  and communication  
frequency.  Product  rating  and initiating  appear  to have the greatest  influence  
on satisfaction,  followed  by disclosing  and signaling,  followed  by frequency  (see 
full sample standardized  parameter  estimates,  Table IV-10). In general,  
satisfaction  with the relationship  and trust  appear  to be influenced  similarly,  with 
the exception  that communication  frequency  has a positive  overall  effect on 
satisfaction  (but not trust). On balance,  then, it appears  that sellers  can employ  
essentially  the same communication  strategy  to enhance  both buyers ’ trust and 
their overall  satisfaction  with the relationship.  The single  point of difference  
appears  to be that increasing  communication  frequency  may be more effective  
in enhancing  satisfaction.
The above discussion  must be qualified,  however,  by noting the cross ­
sectional  nature  of the study. This is an important  limitation  which  significantly  
tempers  our ability  to make  causal  attributions.  It is possible,  for example,  that 
more frequent  communication,  as well as greater  disclosure,  initiating,  and 
signaling  tend to occur  in those  relationships  which  are characterized  by greater  
trust  and satisfaction  (i.e., reverse  causation).  Furthermore,  although  our a priori  
reasoning  was that greater  richness  would  lead to greater  trust and satisfaction,  
it is possible  that low trust and satisfaction  ("crisis  levels")  could lead to buyer  
and seller engaging  in greater  face-to-face  discussions  (to "fix" the problem).

we observed.  Consequently,  our findings,  both in support  of and contrary  to a 
priori  reasoning,  should  be interpreted  cautiously.

Our findings  suggest  that when  age of the relationship  is low, communication  
frequency  has a greater  positive  influence  on trust. Similarly,  age of the 
relationship  also appears  to moderate  the influence  of communication  frequency  
on satisfaction.  Apparently,  communication  frequency  is more  influential  in the 
early stages  of a relationship,  when  initial  perceptions  are being  formed.  Later  
on perceptions  may become  "calcified"  and relatively  resistant  to modification.  
Sellers  may be particularly  interested  in this finding  because  it is easy for them  
to distinguish  among  customers  according  to age. Therefore,  it should  prove  
useful  for them  to design  communication  programs  separately  for different  age 
groups,  with more "intensive"  communication  strategies  directed  to the newer  
customers.
Our findings  also suggest  that environmental  diversity  moderates  the 
influence  of both initiating  and product  rating  on buyers ’ trust. When  environ ­
mental  diversity  is low, initiating  appears  to have a greater  influence  on trust 
than when  environmental  diversity  is high. As noted,  this finding  runs counter  
to our hypothesis.  Since  our definition  of environmental  diversity  focuses  on the 
level of diversity  in buyers ’ output  markets  (customers),  it is logical  to expect  
that, in instances  of high diversity,  initiating  behavior  on the part of sellers  
(efforts  to keep abreast  of buyers ’ customers  and competitive  environment)  
would  have a greater  influence  on buyers ’ trust. However,  the opposite  was

--- PAGE 95 ---
85 
found  in our sample,  i.e., the influence  of initiating  on trust appears  to be weaker  
when  environmental  diversity  is high. In search  of possible  explanations  for this 
finding,  we first note that the influence  of product  rating  on buyers ’ trust was 
also moderated  by environmental  diversity,  but in the opposite  direction.  That  
is, product  rating  was found  to have a greater  positive  influence  on buyers ’ trust 
for the high diversity  group. Collinearity  between  product  rating  and initiating  
could,  therefore,  lead to the unexpected  finding  regarding  initiating.  However,  
the correlation  between  product  rating  and initiating  is only .41 (Table  IV-2),  
and this, when considered  with the relatively  stable subgroup  parameter  
estimates  (Table IV-7), suggests  that collinearity  is unlikely  to have been  
responsible.  Another  possible  explanation  for this finding  is that buyers ’ facing  
diverse markets  produce  a wider range of products,  have more suppliers,  
purchase  a wider  variety  of inputs,  and thus, on average,  attach  less importance  
to a single  product/supplier  than do buyers  facing  less diverse  markets.  This 
possibility  was investigated  and rejected.  No significant  difference  in the level 
of target product  importance  was found between  the high diversity  and low 
diversity  groups;  this is confirmed  by the very low correlation  (.06) between  
environmental  diversity  and target  product  importance.  Finally,  buyers  in firms  
facing  very diverse  markets  (serving  numerous  customers  unrelated  to the target  
product  via the value chain)  may view initiating  (attempts  by suppliers  to "find

--- PAGE 96 ---
86
out" about  their customers  and competitive  situation)  as rather  irrelevant.  This 
explanation  is, however,  highly  speculative,  and further  research  would  appear  
to be necessary  before  drawing  any such conclusion.
Our failure  to find other  moderating  effects  is not offered  as evidence  that 
the other  moderator  variables  are unrelated  to boundary  behavior.  For example,  
buyers ’ levels of trust and satisfaction  may tend to vary systematically  across  
specific  product  categories,  within  our broad  classes  of product  type. According ­
ly, the variance  due to specific  category  effects might have overwhelmed  
boundary  behavior  effects,  thus leading  to our finding  that product  type has no 
moderating  influence.  Further,  dimensions  such as complexity  and intangibility  
may be the most significant  product-related  factors bearing  on boundary  
behavior.  Our four broad  classes  of product  type may not have cleanly  tapped  
differences  on these dimensions.  A second  example  concerns  environmental  
dynamism,  where,  again  no moderating  effects  were  observed.  It is possible  that 
under  conditions  of high dynamism,  a great  deal of boundary  behavior  "noise"  is 
introduced  (that is, excessive,  ineffective  communication),  stimulated  by the 
turbulent  environment.  The presence  of such "noise" would be likely to 
confound  the moderating  effect  of dynamism  on the influence  of communication  
frequency,  because  the relative  efficiency  of communication  may  differ  across  the 
high/low  dynamism  conditions.  Clearly,  a number  of other  explanations  might

--- PAGE 97 ---
87
be offered  for these,  and other,  hypothesized  moderating  effects  which  we failed  
to observe.  Accordingly,  we again  note that our findings  should  be interpreted  
cautiously.
C. Summary
in summary,  our findings  suggest  that, in addition  to product  rating,  the 
boundary  behaviors,  (1) disclosing,  (2) initiating,  and (3) signaling,  have,  overall,  
a positive  influence  on buyers ’ trust. Furthermore,  when the age of the buyer ­
seller relationship  is low, communication  frequency  has a significant,  and 
significantly  greater,  positive  influence  on trust than  when  age of the relationship  
is high. Overall,  the boundary  behaviors,  (1) disclosing,  (2) initiating,  (3) 
signaling,  and (4) frequency,  appear  to have a positive  influence  on buyers ’ 
satisfaction.  Age of the relationship  also moderates  the influence  of communica ­
tion frequency  on buyers ’ satisfaction;  when age is low the influence  of 
communication  frequency  on satisfaction  is stronger  that when age is high.  
Figure  V-1 presents  a revised  model  of the influence  of boundary  behavior  on 
buyers ’ trust and satisfaction,  based  on the findings  of the study. Our study  thus 
extends  prior research  by identifying  specific  communication  behaviors  which  
lead to trust and satisfaction.  Managers  should  also be interested  because  these  
boundary  behaviors  represent  actionable  interaction  strategies.  This finding  

--- PAGE 98 ---
88
points  to the importance  of boundary  behavior  in the early  stages  of relationship  
development,  and suggests  to managers  that newer  customers  could  usefully  be 
targeted  with more intensive  communication  strategies.
Finally,  it should  be noted  that environmental  diversity  appears  to moderate  
the influence  of initiating  on buyers ’ trust. When  diversity  is high, the effect  of 
initiating  on trust  appears,  unexpectedly,  to be weaker  than  when  diversity  is low. 
Given  the unexpected  direction  of this effect,  and lack of compelling  explanation  
for it, it seems  premature  to offer  recommendations  without  further  investigation.

VI. CONCLUSION
We have examined  how boundary  behavior  influences  buyers ’ trust in, and 
satisfaction  with, suppliers.  We have also examined  the influence  of boundary  
behavior  on the share  of business  received  by suppliers  in competitive  situations.  
Further,  we have  examined  how  effects  of the environment,  buying  situation,  and 
age of the relationship  moderate  these influences.
In sum, boundary  behavior  is quite  effective  in explaining  variance  in buyers ’ 
levels of trust and satisfaction,  but is ineffective  in explaining  variance  in the 
amount  of business  received  by suppliers.  The age of the buyer-seller  relation ­
ship moderates  the influence  of communication  frequency  on both trust and 
satisfaction.  In each case communication  frequency  has a stronger  (positive)  
influence  when age of the relationship  is relatively  low, thus pointing  to the 
importance  of maintaining  an "open"  channel  of communication  in the early  
stages  of relationships.  This finding  should  be important  to managers  who have 
"key" account  responsibility;  age of the relationship  may be a variable  which  is 
overlooked  by many  of them and we provide  evidence  that newer  relationships  
may benefit  most from intensive  communication  strategies.  Environmental  
diversity  was found  to moderate  the influence  of initiation,  but, surprisingly,  in 
the opposite  direction  from that which  we had predicted.  As noted earlier,  it

Of particular  interest  are the "non-findings"  relating  to sellers ’ shares of 
business.  This area would seem to be a particularly  fruitful  one for future  
research.  Prior  research  on market  share  has concentrated  heavily  on consumer  
markets,  primarily  retail grocery  (e.g, Guadagni  and Little 1983; Lattin and 
Bucklin  1989). Recently,  this research  has benefitted  from scanner  data which  
facilitates  investigation  at the level of the individual  consumer.  In the sparse  
literature  on competition  in business  markets,  however,  no prior studies  have 
investigated  sellers ’ shares,  or their antecedents,  at the individual  account  level.  
It appears  that an understanding  of buyers ’ allocation  behavior  in competitive  
supplier  situations  would  fill an important  void in our knowledge  of industrial  
buyer  behavior.  Our initial  attempt  at filling  this void suggests  several  avenues  
that should  be pursued  in future  research.
First,  although  we defined  sellers ’ performance  in terms  of share,  follow-up  
research  should examine  the notion of "reward,"  as seen from the buyer ’s 
perspective.  Rather  than attempting  to explain  share in isolation  from other  
reward  factors,  it will probably  prove  more useful  to examine  the behavior  of a 
set of variables  which captures  this notion. In addition  to share, suggestions  
advanced  earlier  include  (1) price  premium,  (2) favoring  high-margin  items,  and 
(3) accepting  seller-preferred  delivery/service  schedules. Second,  reward  

--- PAGE 104 ---
94
structures  are likely to vary by industry,  therefore,  future research  should  
examine  them by industry.  Third, "non-performance"  factors  such as sellers ’ 
location,  complementary  offerings,  and reciprocity  are likely  to have an effect  on 
the manner  in which  buyers  reward  sellers. These  factors  were not considered  
in this study. Therefore,  a model  predicting  sellers ’ rewards  might  include  both 
a set of "performance"  antecedents  (those in this study) and a set of "non ­
performance"  antecedents.  Fourth,  proceeding  in a somewhat  different  direction,  
the issue of performance-reward  matching  (control)  is raised  by the findings  of 
our study. We did not assess  whether  buyers  in our sample  had in-place  supplier  
rating  systems. Such systems  range from very informal  methods  to elaborate,  
computer-based  "cost-ratio"  plans  (Hutt  and Speh 1989). It would  be interesting  
to examine  whether  share,  under  any of these  systems,  is differentially  predicted  
by supplier  evaluations.  To date, no study has addressed  this question.

Ferris  Industries,  Inc., a firm based in Houston,  Texas. He is the author  of
