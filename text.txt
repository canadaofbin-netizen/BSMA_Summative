This exploratory study examined the practice of public relations in Zimbabwe by surveying and understanding 
public relations roles typically practiced by Zimbabwean PR practitioners. Through the lens of systems theory, 
the research investigated whether the practiced public relations roles allow organizations to perform all three 
boundary spanning functions such as output, throughput, and input. The results confirmed prior theoretically 
and empirically derived typology of roles (technician, manager, strategist) from the African continent. However, 
the observed structures of public relations roles deviate from previous findings, indicating that Zimbabwean PR 
practitioners ’ professional roles may differ from those in neighboring countries.   
1.Introduction 
Amplified cultural, economic, and professional globalization has re- 
introduced the concept of public relations roles into the scholarly 
agenda, as research on international public relations roles becomes a 
necessary and crucial function in further understanding the practice of 
public relations in different countries (Tindall & Holtzhausen, 2011 ). 
Increasingly, scholars have called for and offered more culturally diverse 
public relations research. Creedon and Al-Khaja (2019) highlighted the 
need "to move the discipline ’s scholarship from global isolation to cul-
tural inclusivity" (p. 84) through broadening research outreach. This 
exploratory study looks at the practice of public relations in Zimbabwe, 
an understudied African country with an active public relations industry 
and a long history of the profession. Specifically, the current study 
contributes to the ongoing discussion about whether the structure and 
performance of public relations roles differ across national and cultural 
borders and how it defines the profession globally (Vercic, Grunig, & 
Grunig, 1996 ). 
Previous research showed that Zimbabwe has had an active chapter 
of the International Public Relations Association (IPRA) and its own 
Institute of Public Relations since 1966 and has PR practitioners working 
in several sectors (Oksiutycz & Nhedzi, 2018 ). Yet, its inclusion in public 
relations studies is lacking (Ngondo, 2019 ). In fact, of the 54 African 
countries, only a handful of nations have been studied in connection 
with public relations: Kenya (e.g., Kiambi & Nadler, 2012 ); South Africa 
(e.g., Holtzhausen, Petersen, & Tindall, 2003 ; Holtzhausen, 2005 ; 
Rensburg & Botha, 2014 ; Tindall & Holtzhausen, 2011 ); Nigeria (e.g., Molleda & Alhassan, 2006 ; Ukonu, Anyadike, & Okoro, 2018 ); Ghana (e. 
g., Wu & Baah-Boakye, 2009 , 2014 ); Uganda, Mauritius, Guinea, and 
Tanzania (Van Heerden & Rensburg, 2005 ), and most recently 
Zimbabwe (Muchena, 2017 , 2018 ; Ngondo, 2019 ; Oksiutycz & Nhedzi, 
2018 , among others). 
This study widens the research coverage of the African continent by 
surveying Zimbabwean PR practitioners to explore their professional 
roles and understanding of public relations. Further, this study con-
tributes to the recognition of cultural diversity in the practice and theory 
of public relations, as urged by Dutta and Elers (2020) . Similar to 
Azionya, Oksiutycz, and Benecke (2019) , who asserted that their 
research offered a new way of representing marginalized voices in 
public relations education and industry, this current research follows 
suit. First, it extends and clarifies previous work on public relations roles 
in Africa by Steyn (1999) , Tindall and Holtzhausen (2011) , and Van 
Heerden and Rensburg (2005) , whose research included participants 
from countries such as Uganda, Nigeria, Kenya, South Africa, Mauritius, 
Guinea, and Tanzania. In so doing, it confirms previous theoretically and 
empirically derived typology of roles, yet observes a variation in the 
structure of those roles in comparison to the earlier findings on the Af-
rican continent. Second, the study offers a unique insight into the public 
relations activities and the composition of the public relations labor 
force in Zimbabwe and adds to the kaleidoscope of understandings of 
global public relations with its specific focus on Zimbabwe. 
In what follows, the manuscript briefly discusses public relations 
research conducted in Africa, provides background on the public re-
lations practice and education, and gives an overview of public relations

1.1. Public Relations Research in Africa 
The public relations profession and related phenomena have been 
studied in several African countries. As part of an emerging agenda, 
most research on public relations in Africa seeks to understand the 
profession in general, models of practice, professional public relations 
roles, as well as examine various cases of strategic communication 
campaigns. Previously, researchers examined public relations education 
in an emerging democracy such as Ghana, aiming to understand how 
future PR practitioners are shaped in different socio-political contexts 
(Thompson, 2018). Wu and Baah-Boakye (2014) also studied profes -
sionalization of public relations in Ghana and concluded that the field is 
moving toward professionalism as practitioners become involved in 
conducting more summative and formative research for their organi -
zations and clients. Similarly, in Ghana and Kenya, Kiambi and Nadler 
(2012) examined Grunig and Hunt’s (1984) four Western-centric public 
relations models (press agentry, public information, two-way asym -
metrical, and two-way symmetrical), and two international models - the 
personal influence model and the cultural interpreter model (Grunig, 
Grunig, Sriramesh, Huang, & Lyra, 1995). Among the six public re-
lations models, the cultural interpreter model and the personal influence 
model emerged as the most frequently used in Ghana and Kenya (Kiambi 
& Nadler, 2012; Wu & Baah-Boakye, 2009). 
In Nigeria, Ukonu et al. (2018) found that the public relations 
practice follows what they called a British paternalistic model, or gov-
ernment influence model, tied to the history of the British colonial rule. 
Ukonu et al. (2018) concluded that the profession was undergoing an 
identity crisis, in which different industries were using the classic four 
public relations models differently. Yet, Nigerian public relations was 
linked more to the British style (government influence) but informed by 
American pedagogical practices. According to Ukonu et al. (2018), this 
dichotomy has made it difficult for Nigerian practitioners to evolve and 
construct their own public relations identity. 
South Africa has been the site of most research on public relations 
conducted on the continent. For example, Holtzhausen et al. (2003) 
studied the application of symmetric principles among South African PR 
practitioners and found that they developed their models of public re-
lations practice based on the socio-political and cultural environments. 
Scholars examined South African public relations through the lens of 
communication management (Rensburg, 2014), strategic communica -
tion (Steyn, 2009), financial communication (Rensburg & Botha, 2014), 
cultural intermediaries (Benecke et al., 2017), and public relations ed-
ucation (Azionya et al., 2019). Scholars also explored public relations as 
activism contributing to social dialogue and social change (Benecke & 
Oksiutycz, 2015), and studied public relations roles predominantly 
occupied and practiced by South African practitioners (Steyn, 1999; 
Tindall & Holthausen, 2011; Van Heerden & Rensburg, 2005). Addi-
tionally, South Africa was included in the Global Capability Framework 
study (Gregory & Fawkes, 2019), which aimed to describe the scope and 
the roles of the profession on an international level to provide practi -
tioners a standardized understanding of public relations competencies 
and best practices. 
These disparate findings from the African continent accentuate the 
need to understand public relations’ practice diversity, especially from 
the understudied countries such as Zimbabwe. The nations on the Af-
rican continent vary significantly, and it is important to consider how 
public relations practices and uses may differ based on a country or a 
sub-region to help build a scholarly agenda for future inquiries. 1.2. Background on Zimbabwe Public Relations 
Zimbabwe is a landlocked country in Southern Africa and is bordered 
by such nations as Botswana, Zambia, Mozambique, and South Africa. It 
has a population of a little over 14 million people, 16 official languages, 
including English, with Harare as the capital city (Central Intelligence 
Agency, CIA, n.d.). A former British colony, Zimbabwe gained its inde-
pendence in 1980. Zimbabwe’s economy heavily relies on mining and 
agriculture. Like the United States, Zimbabwe is a member of several 
international organizations such as the United Nations, the International 
Monetary Fund, the World Bank, and the World Trade Organization (US 
Department of State, n.d.). Zimbabwe is a host to such global companies 
as Coca-Cola, Nestle, Chevron, Price Waterhouse, British Petroleum, 
Barclays Bank, and British American Tobacco, among others, presenting 
a fertile market for the public relations industry. 
According to Mawarera (2014), Zimbabwe’s public relations practice 
can be traced back to the mid-1950s. By the mid-1960s, the industry had 
been actively developing with the founding of the Rhodesia Public Re-
lations Institute in 1966, renamed to Zimbabwe Institute of Public Re-
lations (ZIPR) after Zimbabwe’s independence from Great Britain 
(Oksiutycz & Nhedzi, 2018). 
1.2.1. Public Relations Education in Zimbabwe 
Although professional organizations such as IPRA and ZIPR have 
existed since the late 1960s, formal public relations education and 
training is still in its infancy in Zimbabwe. To date, no Zimbabwean 
university offers public relations as a full qualification. In contrast, all 
three public universities in Zimbabwe offer accredited undergraduate or 
postgraduate degrees in marketing (Oksiutycz & Nhedzi, 2018), where 
PR practitioners often receive their education. 
Most PR practitioners in Zimbabwe receive education via on-the-job 
training, postgraduate diplomas offered by ZIPR (Muchena, 2017), or 
certificates offered by South African companies such as Oracle Seven 
Investments, who solicit Zimbabwean PR professionals to undergo 
10-day training programs that cover topics such as media relations, 
speech writing, social media management, and public relations man-
agement (B. Tonhodzayi, Group PR and Corporate Affairs Manager at 
Zimpapers, personal communication, November 27, 2019). 
Similar to other countries’ experiences, PR practitioners in 
Zimbabwe are often transplants to public relations from different dis-
ciplines, predominantly journalism and media studies (Muchena, 2017). 
In Zimbabwe, ZIPR is among the few organizations offering specialized 
courses in public relations, consistently expressing concern regarding 
limited opportunities for formal training. ZIPR offers its diploma pro-
gram in-person or online, which is taught by practitioners. The diploma 
covers public relations theory and practice, media relations, public 
speaking, speech writing, information technology in public relations, 
grooming, and personal branding, etc. (ZIPR LinkedIn, 2019). However, 
this program is not accredited by the Zimbabwe Council for Higher 
Education (ZIMCHE) and, therefore, cannot qualify as formal education, 
and its courses are heavily rooted in media studies (Muchena, 2017). 
Muchena (2018) noted how public relations education is often 
included as a module in marketing, English, communication, media 
studies, or human resources and customer care, but not as a standalone 
degree. This trend is reflected in how the public relations industry and 
its labor force are constructed. Zimbabwean PR practitioners have var-
ied educational backgrounds and operate either as independent de-
partments or attached to other branches within the company with 
combined functions in marketing and sales (Muchena, 2017, 2018). 
In recent years, the Zimbabwean government charged higher edu-
cation to reflect industry trends (Matambo, 2018). In 2018, the Mar-
keters Association of Zimbabwe (MAZ), in partnership with Bindura 
University of Science Education, started offering a two-year professional 
diploma called Public Relations and Corporate Reputation Management. 
The diploma-granting program, developed with local and international 
industry experts in marketing and public relations, aimed to address the P.S. Ngondo and A. 1.3. Public Relations Research in Zimbabwe 
Despite its public relations’ history, Zimbabwe is a relative 
newcomer to the public relations scholarly agenda. The available 
research on Zimbabwe public relations addresses such issues as corpo -
rate social responsibility (CSR), social media use, and public relations’ 
strategic management function. Masuku and Moyo (2013) investigated 
CSR in Zimbabwe as an "invention of PR" (p. 113). The authors inter-
viewed PR professionals and found that "the management function of PR 
can play a positive role in society, while CSR demands to reduce the 
self-interest of organisations in their preferences and actions within" (p. 
121). Further, the Zimbabwean practitioners believed that CSR could 
augment corporate identity and bolster corporate citizenship as an in-
vention of public relations. Interestingly, Kakava, Mbizi, and Manyeruke 
(2013) found that over half of Zimbabwean companies prefer to engage 
in philanthropic CSR with short-lived benefits to the community rather 
than contributing toward long-term sustainable development. Masuku 
and Shava (2014), however, showed how the public relations depart -
ment at Nestle Zimbabwe used CSR to enhance its reputation via ac-
tivities such as sponsoring music festivals, sports events, and initiating 
health and education programs. 
Ngondo (2019) investigated the use of social media and other digital 
technologies among Zimbabwean PR practitioners and found that the 
emergence of new media changed the way Zimbabwean communication 
professionals communicate and handle external and internal commu -
nication. Blogging and social media became the routine responsibilities 
of Zimbabwean PR practitioners. The practitioners also aspired to 
become primarily responsible for monitoring and managing all aspects 
of the organization’s digital communication (Ngondo, 2019). As argued 
by Ngondo (2019), these findings stress the importance for public re-
lations to continue to set itself apart, specifically from marketing, in the 
African region. 
Muchena (2017, 2018) investigated the role public relations played 
in private and public organizations in Zimbabwe and observed that the 
practice’s perception differs from the actual practice, a gap particularly 
pronounced in public institutions. Muchena (2017) interviewed practi -
tioners to examine whether they perceive public relations as a strategic 
management function. In parallel with the understanding of the 
technician-manager role dichotomy, Muchena (2017) examined the 
technical and strategic communicative functions of public relations and 
concluded that Zimbabwean practitioners generally understand public 
relations as a strategic management function in line with the Western 
definitions of public relations. However, in his later study, Muchena 
(2018) found that public relations is practiced as a technical function 
dominated by such activities as news release writing and event planning. 
To address this gap, Muchena (2018) called for more studies on Zim-
babwean public relations practice to develop its own body of literature 
that "advances local theory and practice relevant to the Zimbabwean 
situation" (p. 59). This study of public relations roles in Zimbabwe re-
sponds to the call for more diversified research from the African conti -
nent and Zimbabwe. It is the first attempt to understand public relations 
roles in this nation. 
2.Literature Review 
The objective of this study is to explore the existing public relations 
roles typically practiced by Zimbabwean professionals. Described as patterns of behavior that determine everyday professional activities and 
functions of PR practitioners, public relations roles reflect different po-
sitions and ranks PR practitioners occupy in organizations (Grunig & 
Hunt, 1984). For this reason, this research investigated whether a public 
relations function in organizations is performed at all three levels: the 
organizational or macro level, functional or meso level, and imple -
mentational or micro level (Steyn, 1999), and whether it is reflected in 
how public relations roles manifest themselves in Zimbabwe. 
2.1. Boundary Spanning and Public Relations Roles 
The systems theory perspectives on public relations argue that an 
organization is a system separated from its external environment by a 
boundary through which information flows (Dozier & Broom, 2006). 
One of the key PR professionals’ tasks is to span this boundary with “one 
foot in the organization and one foot outside” (Grunig & Hunt, 1984, p. 
9) to allow the information to circulate from the organization to the 
environment and from the environment to the organization to ensure 
informed decision making. PR practitioners also span the boundaries to 
reach different publics inside and outside of the organization. 
Organizations as systems can be closed and open, as well as some -
thing in between. Closed systems organizations are isolated, and their 
boundaries are considered impenetrable, discouraging information ex-
change. For this reason, organizations that operate as closed systems are 
not as quick and flexible to address emerging issues and are usually 
reactionary to crises (Cutlip, Center, & Broom, 2006). Public relations 
function in closed systems is confined to one-way communication. Or-
ganizations as open systems regard the environment as essential for their 
survival and continuous success. These organizations facilitate infor-
mation exchange through the boundary spanning function afforded by 
public relations. The role of public relations in open systems is focused 
on two-way communication and relationship building (Cutlip et al., 
2006; Grunig, Grunig, & Dozier, 2002). 
For an organization to survive and thrive in an environment, the 
information flow process needs to include such steps as input, output, 
and throughput (Cutlip et al., 2006; Grunig et al., 2002). The input 
happens in the form of research, environmental scanning, and messages 
to the organization. For example, PR professionals use formal and 
informal research to collect information about key publics and issues 
and deliver them back to the organization. In addition, understanding 
the attitudes and behaviors of key publics is essential to organizations as 
they plan their goals and strategies (Mehta & Xavier, 2009). This in-
formation input function corresponds to the strategist role (Steyn, 1999, 
2009). 
The process through which information from the environment is 
handled is known as throughput. During the throughput stage, the input 
data (information) is organized and managed, and possible solutions to 
issues are identified and formulated. This information throughput 
function corresponds to the manager role (Steyn, 1999, 2009). Impor -
tantly, via the throughput function, information output is prepared. 
Finally, output describes the process through which information 
flows back from the organization into the environment in the form of 
organizational messages, including news releases, annual reports, social 
media posts, etc. This information output function corresponds to the 
technician role (Steyn, 1999, 2009). To evaluate the impact of outputs, 
organizations may seek feedback from the publics that is delivered to the 
organizations as input, creating a feedback loop essential for two-way 
communication. Therefore, this process is circular and characterizes 
an open system (Grunig & Hunt, 1984). 
Based on the systems theory, public relations roles can be concep -
tualized along the lines of functions associated with activities necessary 
to carry out the process of information input, throughput, and output. 
Broom (1986) described the function of public relations as "gathering, 
assimilating, interpreting and disseminating intelligence about the 
environment" (p. 2), providing a meaningful link between the systems 
theory perspectives on public relations (Grunig et al., 2002) and the P.S. Ngondo and A. 2.2. Public Relations Roles 
The concept of ’roles’ refers to PR practitioners’ everyday activities 
and is key to understanding public relations. Public relations roles are 
the behavioral patterns of individuals in organizations practicing public 
relations. These roles set individuals apart and define expectations from 
the organizations’ perspectives. Public relations roles were first intro-
duced by Broom and Smith (1979) as four types: expert prescriber, 
communication facilitator, problem-solving process facilitator, and 
PR/communication technician. Dozier (1983) empirically tested these 
roles and observed a strong link among three out of four roles suggesting 
that expert prescriber, problem-solving process facilitator, and 
communication facilitator represent different functions and re-
sponsibilities of a PR practitioner at a manager level (see also Cutlip 
et al., 2006; Dozier & Broom, 1995), submitting that PR practitioners 
can be described as performing either a technician role or a manager role 
within the organization. Importantly, Dozier (1983) warned that the line 
between technicians and managers is a blurry one and that the terms do 
not describe completely separate categories, but rather predominant 
roles. 
The research on public relations roles has been conducted in 
different parts of the world and different contexts, highlighting the 
public relations practice’s multiformity. For example, Beurer-Züllig, 
Fieseler, and Meckel (2009) investigated the typologies of communica -
tors in Europe, taking into consideration differences in cultural and 
national contexts. They identified five distinct types of European 
communication professionals: negotiators, brand officers, policy advi-
sors, internal communicators, and press agents. The authors concluded 
that these five roles fall "in line with Dozier’s and Broom’s 
manager-technician dichotomy,” with two out of five roles representing 
manager roles and three out of five are in essence technician-level roles 
(Beurer-Züllig et al., 2009, p. 159). 
Arguing that social media is one of the essential duties that should be 
reflected in today’s public relations roles, Neill and Lee (2016) studied 
and identified seven social media roles: social media technician, social 
listening and analytics, online media relations, policymaker, employee 
recruiter, internal social media manager, and policing. Although these 
roles emerged in the context of social media, "five of these new roles 
were correlated with the traditional public relations manager role, but 
only three of them (…) with the communication technician role" (p. 13). 
Seeking to understand the public relations roles of South African 
practitioners, Steyn (1999, 2009) introduced the role of a strategist to 
the original binary conceptualization of the technician-manager roles. 
Specifically, Steyn (1999) argued that the three public relations roles of 
strategist, manager, and technician correspond to three functions of PR 
practitioner as a boundary spanner: input, throughput, and output. Ac-
cording to Steyn (1999), these roles and functions are performed at three 
levels: the organizational or macro level, functional or meso level, and 
implementational or micro level (Steyn, 1999). Van Heerden and 
Rensburg (2005) later, empirically, verified the existence of these public 
relations roles among practitioners in seven Southern and Eastern Af-
rican countries. However, in their study, the role of technician and 
manager were combined as responsibilities between these two functions 
were indistinct. In contrast, the strategist’s role emerged as a strong 
indicator of the growing importance of the input function in South 
Africa’s public relations practice. 
Similarly, Tindall and Holtzhausen (2011) examined the roles of 
strategic communication professionals in South Africa. Their research 
discovered four roles specific to South Africa: strategist, media 
specialist, liaison, and cultural interpreter. Interestingly, the strategist 
role emerged as the most unified, while the role of media specialist 
represented a blend between the roles of communication technician and 
media relations. Tindall and Holtzhausen’s (2011) findings demon -
strated that political and cultural environments shape public relations roles’ context and content. Their study also recommended a differenti -
ated approach to accept the notion that roles are not mutually exclusive 
and can be performed cross-functionally. 
Different taxonomies and typologies of public relations roles exist 
depending on the applied theoretical framework. However, many 
scholars find consistent support for Dozier’s general binary conceptu -
alization of public relations roles: manager and technician, or different 
combinations of the two. While presenting important cultural particu -
larities in performing public relations roles in different countries and 
contexts, studies find that those unique and culture-specific roles fit 
under the umbrella of the manager and/or technician (Beurer-Züllig 
et al., 2009; Steyn, 2009). Since this study is informed by the systems 
theory perspective to investigate how the boundary spanning function of 
public relations in the organizations is performed at different levels 
(macro, meso, micro), we chose to focus on two traditional public re-
lations roles of manager and technician (Dozier & Broom, 2006), and the 
role of strategist proposed by Steyn (1999). 
2.2.1. Micro level: Public relations technician 
The role of the public relations technician is performed at the micro 
or program/implementational level (Steyn, 1999). This role is associ -
ated with the output function of PR practitioners as boundary spanners 
(Steyn, 1999). As conceptualized by Dozier (1984) and Broom and Smith 
(1979), the communication technician is responsible for program 
implementation by preparing and disseminating public relations tactics, 
such as brochures, news releases, speeches, fact sheets, social media 
posts, photographs, etc. Mehta and Xavier (2009) argued that the notion 
that public relations technicians are still focused on production and 
dissemination endures in the digital age. Such continuance was exem -
plified by Neill and Lee (2016) when they identified three social media 
roles that focused on meso level activities such as social media techni -
cian, policymaker and policing. 
2.2.2. Meso level: Public relations manager 
The role of the public relations manager is performed at the meso or 
functional level. In this capacity, PR practitioners are responsible for 
developing communication policies and programs, managing informa -
tion input, determining what and how it should be communicated to the 
publics, identifying, managing, and anticipating threats coming from 
the external and internal environments. In the age of social media, 
public relations managers are also held accountable for program out-
comes, including social media campaigns. This role is associated with 
the throughput function of PR practitioners as boundary spanners when 
information is shared internally with the relevant decision-makers to 
help develop an overall corporate communication strategy (Dozier & 
Broom, 2006). 
2.2.3. Macro level: Public relations strategist 
The role of the public relations strategist is performed at the macro or 
organizational level. Steyn (1999) argued that public relations managers 
often act as strategists when dealing with the dominant coalition. Public 
relations strategists are involved in research, social listening, and 
decision-making at the macro level to understand the external and in-
ternal environments and determine the approach an organization takes 
toward communication with stakeholders. Steyn (1999; 2002) also 
argued that PR practitioners’ role at the macro level also defines how 
public relations is practiced at the meso and micro levels and what 
public relations model is predominantly practiced. This role is associ -
ated with the input function of PR practitioners as boundary spanners - 
when PR professionals assume responsibility for developing an overall 
communication strategy for the organization based on information input 
coming in the form of research, media monitoring and environmental 
scanning, social listening, issue tracking. Public relations strategists use 
this input to identify strategic stakeholders, detect and anticipate 
changes in the environment, identify issues affecting the publics’ atti-
tudes and opinions toward the organization. P.S. Ngondo and A. 3.Method 
Consistent with prior research on public relations roles (Beur-
er-Züllig et al., 2009 ; Li, Cropp, Sims, & Jin, 2012 ; Neill & Lee, 2016 ; 
Steyn, 1999 ; Van Heerden & Rensburg, 2005 ), the researchers admin -
istered previously developed and tested survey instrument (Steyn, 1999 ; 
Van Heerden & Rensburg, 2005 ). Data for the study was collected online 
using Qualtrics, because online distribution provides access to a 
worldwide population, as well as to specialized and hidden populations, 
such as PR practitioners in Zimbabwe. It also provides convenience, an 
opportunity for immediate feedback, and tailored questions (Wimmer & 
Dominick, 2006 ). 
3.1. Study Population and Sampling Procedure 
Due to this study ’s exploratory nature and lack of formalized 
knowledge about the public relations industry in Zimbabwe, partici -
pants were recruited through a combination of purposive and snowball 
sampling methods, starting with professionals personally known to one 
of the researchers who is from Southern Africa. The study population 
included self-identified media, public relations, communication, and 
marketing professionals. Previous studies showed that various educa -
tional paths and experiences could lead to a career in public relations 
(Oksiutycz & Nhedzi, 2018 ). In many countries with developing econ-
omies, PR practitioners may wear mismatched job titles while per-
forming public relations duties (2018 , Muchena, 2017 ). For this reason, 
before taking part in the survey, all participants had to answer a screener 
question asking, "In your current role, do you practice public relations 
(PR) or PR-related activities in Zimbabwe?" Those who answered "yes" 
moved forward with completing the questionnaire while those who 
answered "no" were thanked for their time, and the survey was 
terminated. 
Low response rates, traditionally associated with international 
research (Kent, Taylor, & Turcilo, 2006 ), were neutralized by offering 
respondents a $15 incentive to cover Internet and mobile data usage 
costs. Internet and/or mobile data access is available in most areas in 
Zimbabwe, so access was not a challenge. Upon completing the ques-
tionnaire, participants were redirected to a separate page to claim 
compensation to safeguard anonymity. In addition, the four-step dis-
tribution process was used, and invitation emails were sent to all po-
tential participants once a week over four weeks. The follow-up 
correspondence was conducted per Dilman ’s (1978) recommendations, 
who argued that this method allows researchers to maximize the response rate. The researchers received 347 total responses (N 347), 
with 242 surveys usable for analysis. This number exceeded Van Heer-
den and Rensburg ’s (2005) response rate of 151 East African partici -
pants and Steyn ’s (1999) response rate of 103 South African 
participants. 
3.2. Instrument 
This study utilized a survey instrument previously developed by 
Steyn (1999) and re-designed by Van Heerden and Rensburg (2005) to 
capture the performed public relations roles: technician, manager, and 
strategist. The survey consisted of 36 multi-layered questions. The 
measurement instrument developed by Steyn (1999) was included fully 
as a reliable, previously tested tool. Specifically, the measurement for 
the construct of the technician role included six items (α .885), the 
construct of the manager role consisted of five items (α .909) and the 
construct of the strategist role had 10 items (α .938). All items were 
measured on a 5-point Likert scale from strongly disagree to strongly 
agree. For example, respondents had to indicate their level of agreement 
or disagreement with such statements as "A current activity of the PR 
department in our organization is to develop public relations strategy 
that supports corporate strategy." Statement responses were coded so 
that the higher value indicated the presence of the concept, meaning 
that a PR practitioner indeed engaged with the specified activity (see 
Table 1 for a complete list of items). 
In addition, the survey asked questions about the participants ’ pro-
fessional, educational, and socio-economic backgrounds. Specifically, 
the researchers sought out such information about the participants as 
gender, age, level of education and major, type of employer, industry, 
and working experience in public relations. To ensure that the instru -
ment is appropriately localized and contextualized, the researchers 
consulted with three Zimbabwean PR practitioners to check terminology 
related to job titles, professional organizations, education levels, etc. 
The instrument was in English as it is one of the official languages 
spoken throughout Zimbabwe and used in education and business. 
3.3. Data Analysis 
To answer the first research question, principal component analysis 
(PCA) was performed on 21 survey items that represented various public 
relations activities associated with three types of public relations roles. 
PCA was used because it explores the overall variability of the data and 
helps generate a minimum number of components that account for the 
maximum number of variance in the data (Tabachnik & Fidell, 2007 ). To 
answer the second research question, composite variables of public re-
lations roles (technician, manager, strategist) were created to run a 
correlation analysis and calculate the Pearson product-moment corre -
lation coefficient. In addition, preliminary analyses were performed to 
ensure there was no violation of the assumptions of normality, linearity, 
and homoscedasticity. The results of the analyses are reported in the 
next section.

4.3. Confirming Public Relations Roles in Zimbabwean Context 
The first research question asked about how Zimbabwean PR prac-
titioners define their roles and activities and what distinct roles are 
being enacted. The 21 survey items that represented public relations 
activities in organizations and designed to explore professional public 
relations roles were used to study public relations roles in Zimbabwe. 
The authors of the scale (Steyn, 1999 , 2002 ; Van Heerden & Rensburg, 
2005 ) suggested that the scale captures three dimensions of public re-
lations roles – technician, manager, and strategist. To explore this 
structure with the Zimbabwean sample, the items were subjected to 
principal components analysis (PCA).

factor analysis was assessed. All items were deemed suitable with 
acceptable communality values (F.50). The examination of the corre -
lation matrix revealed the presence of many coefficients of .3 and above.

value of .6 (Kaiser, 1974). Overall factorability of the data was good 
(MSA .931 and x2 [df 210] 3255.041, p D.000), and univariate 
MSA values were also acceptable (i.e., all items are greater than .60).

aid in the interpretation of these three components, oblimin rotation was 
performed. The rotation solution revealed the presence of simple 
structure (Thurstone, 1947 ), with all three components displaying 
strong loadings and all variables loading substantially on only one

4.4. Understanding Public Relations and Public Relations Roles in 
Zimbabwean Context 
To capture a baseline of how PR professionals in Zimbabwe under -
stand and view public relations, an official definition of the concept 
adopted by PRSA was offered to study participants, "Public relations is a 
strategic communication process that builds mutually beneficial re-
lationships between organizations and their publics" (PRSA, 2020 , para. 
4). The respondents were asked to indicate their agreement or 
disagreement with the definition. The results showed that participants, 
in general, supported the offered definition (M 4.16; SD 1.23). The 
overwhelming number of respondents (n 208; 87.5%) indicated that 
they either strongly agree (n 121; 50.8%) or agree (n 87; 36.6%) 
with the PRSA ’s definition. The remainder of the participants (n 30; 
12.5%) expressed different levels of disagreement and/or indifference 
with the statement: 10.9% (n 26) strongly disagreed, 0.8% (n 2) 
disagreed and 0.8% (n 2) neither agreed nor disagreed. 
The relationship between public relations roles (technician, man-
ager, strategist) and view of public relations (agreement with PRSA ’s 
definition) was investigated using Pearson product-moment correlation 
coefficient (see Table 3). The analysis showed a small correlation be-
tween the view on public relations and the technician role, r .157, n 
213, p D.022. Similar results with modest effect sizes were obtained for 
the association between the view of public relations and manager role (r .257, n 212, p D.000) as well as the strategist role (r .225, n 
205, p D.001). 
5.Discussion 
This research aimed to help advance the knowledge base of public 
relations theory and practice with regard to Zimbabwe and offered the 
opportunity to widen the research coverage of the African continent. In 
so doing, the study explored views on public relations held by Zim-
babwean professionals and the types of public relations roles typically 
performed. First, this research investigated whether the boundary 
spanning function of public relations and the associated public relations 
roles are performed at all three levels: 1) the strategist role at the 
organizational or macro level (input), 2) the manager role at the func-
tional or meso level (throughput), and 3) the technician role at the 
implementational or micro level (output). Second, the study responded 
to the call to examine whether the structure of public relations roles 
differed across national and cultural borders or if they were universal. 
Third, the study explored the relationship between public relations roles 
enacted by Zimbabwean professionals and their understanding of the 
field. In addition, this paper provided a description of public relations 
education and background on the public relations industry and its pro-
fessionals in Zimbabwe. The discussion of findings and contributions to 
the literature are presented below. 
5.1. Boundary Spanning and Public Relations Functions in Zimbabwean 
Organizations

spanning function plays a pivotal role in organizations, allowing them to 
remain open, responsive, and competitive. This research demonstrated

Participants’ responses illustrated that Zimbabwean PR practi -

all three boundary spanning functions. For example, practitioners re-

via strategies afforded by public relations. Findings from this study 
indicate that the Zimbabwean public relations profession has embraced 
the value of boundary spanning, and Zimbabwean PR practitioners seem 
to be serving as boundary spanners with one foot in the organization and 
one foot outside. Future research needs to investigate this assertion 
further. 
5.2. Public Relations Roles in Zimbabwe 
The analysis focused on three roles: Dozier’s (1983) technician and 
manager roles, as well as Steyn’s (1999) strategist role. Consistent with 
previous research, Zimbabwean PR practitioners perform day-to-day 
activities associated with Dozier’s manager and technician roles found 
in most contexts. In addition, Steyn’s strategist role emerged as one of 
the main roles with which Zimbabwean professionals identify. This in-
dicates that the public relations function in Zimbabwean organizations 
is being performed at the micro, meso and macro levels as all three roles 
were present, allowing the organizations to perform all three boundary 
spanning functions (input, output, throughput) necessary for open sys-
tems and essential for the organization’s survival in the competitive 
global environment. 
Importantly, factor analysis confirmed the existence of three distinct 
components as three distinct public relations roles. This finding is 
similar to Steyn (1999) but different from Van Heerden and Rensburg 
(2005). Although Van Heerden and Rensburg (2005) recognized the 
existence of three public relations roles among practitioners in Southern 
and Eastern African countries, their findings revealed a combined 
technician and manager role as responsibilities between these two 
functions overlapped. 
As an important contribution to the literature on global public re-
lations, this study further confirms and solidifies the role of the PR 
practitioner as a strategist, complementing the works by Steyn (1999, 
2009), Tindall and Holtzhausen (2011), and Van Heerden and Rensburg 
(2005). The statistical analysis demonstrated that the strategist role was 
widely accepted by Zimbabwean PR practitioners, indicating the 
growing importance of the input function in Zimbabwe’s public re-
lations practice. As Steyn (2009) contended, in addition to having PR 
practitioners enacting the traditional technician - manager roles, to be 
excellent, organizations should have practitioners perform the PR 
strategist role as well. 
The distinct role of the strategist observed among the Zimbabwean 
practitioners may be explained by the growth of social media globally. 
The strategist role associated with the input function becomes more and 
more critical due to the rise of social media. The increasing role of social 
media in public relations practice certainly offers more opportunities for 
"input" information flow (it’s easier to get data, social listening, etc.) and 
creates more volatile organizational environments that require more 
research and constant environmental scanning. These activities are associated with the strategist role because practitioners in this role are 
responsible for performing the input boundary spanning function within 
an organization (Steyn, 1999). 
However, as Tindall and Holtzhausen (2011) suggested, public re-
lations roles are not mutually exclusive and, in fact, can be performed 
cross-functionally. Specifically, because social media represents a vola-
tile media environment that requires more research and constant social 
listening, it is also associated with an output function and the technician 
role as the needs to drive engagement demand more content output. As 
underscored by the Ngondo (2019) study, while Zimbabwean PR prac-
titioners are dedicating a sizable portion of their daily public relations 
duties to digital and social media, performed mostly at the micro/ -
implementational level (output), the practitioners also believe that the 
public relations department should be responsible for monitoring and 
managing social and digital media as well, implying the input and 
throughput boundary spanning functions. Future research should 
investigate further the connection between the growing importance of 
social media in public relations and differentiated or integrated public 
relations roles. 
In general, studies of the public relations roles on the African 
continent provided mixed results with roles blurred and merged (Steyn, 
2009; Tindall & Holtzhausen, 2011; Van Heerden & Rensburg, 2005). In 
this context, Zimbabwe’s public relations practice differs from the 
practice in its neighboring nations, such as South Africa, as practitioners 
appear to perform the roles of technician, manager, and strategist quite 
distinctly. This finding answers the question that the structure and 
performance of public relations roles indeed differ across cultural and 
national borders. 
5.3. Understandings of Public Relations in Zimbabwe 
In Zimbabwe, public relations is understood very similarly to the 
Western/American view, as the majority of respondents expressed 
agreement with PRSA’s definition. This is somewhat consistent with 
Muchena’s (2017) findings, who argued that Zimbabwean practitioners 
perceive public relations as a strategic management function. This is key 
as the field had faced challenges in clearly defining itself. This finding 
provides insight into how the field is defined around the globe. 
The views on public relations held by practitioners may impact how 
public relations is practiced and, therefore, constrain professional roles. 
The small correlation between the public relations definition and public 
relations roles did not provide enough insight into whether views and 
perspectives on public relations affect the types of roles practiced by 
Zimbabwean practitioners. All practiced public relations roles were 
associated with the PRSA’s definition. Still, the roles of the manager 
(throughput function) and the strategist (input function) demonstrated 
the strongest relationship, indicating Zimbabwean practitioner’s 
commitment to practice public relations as a strategic management 
function. This finding in itself may suggest future avenues for explora -
tion of the topic. If interpreted cautiously, the higher correlation be-
tween manager and strategist roles and PRSA’s definition may 
demonstrate that commitment to professionalization is associated with 
higher-level public relations roles. 
5.4. Public Relations Professionals in Zimbabwe 
As another unique contribution to the literature, the study provided 
background on Zimbabwean PR professionals and public relations ed-
ucation. Two main takeaways offered: Zimbabwe is yet to develop 
formal public relations education programs, and the public relations 
labor force consists of professionals coming from different fields and 
with various professional and educational backgrounds. 
Overall, based on the studied sample, the study confirms the sparsely 
available depictions of the public relations labor force in Zimbabwe, 
showing that marketing education dominates among PR practitioners. 
Notably, the intertwining of marketing and public relations continues to P.S. Ngondo and A. 9be problematic as more practitioners had marketing degrees compared 
to public relations. As such, this finding confirms Oksiutycz and Nhed -
zi’s (2018) assertion and Muchena’s (2017) findings of Zimbabwean PR 
practitioners being transplants from other disciplines. The respondents 
had degrees from various backgrounds, namely marketing, mass 
communication, business administration, and journalism. Practitioners 
with public relations degrees were a mere 14% of the sample. 
Despite the challenges of formal training outlined by Muchena 
(2017, 2018), the majority of Zimbabwean PR practitioners seem to 
understand what public relations is. As indicated by the overwhelming 
support for the PRSA’s definition, practitioners treat and understand 
public relations as strategic management of communication that builds 
mutually beneficial relationships. 
As the literature outlined, understanding how marketing, journalism, 
and related fields complement public relations is important, especially 
for integrated marketing communications and strategic communication. 
Akin to Azionya et al. (2019) who developed and emphasized a Value 
Based Education approach to public relations education in South Africa, 
Zimbabwean practitioners and academics should continue to develop 
the public relations curriculum until it is offered as its own degree at 
universities across the country. Moreover, practitioners should be 
encouraged to be members of public relations professional organizations 
as this not only keeps them connected to the field; it also provides op-
portunities for training through certifications. 
5.5. Limitations and Future Research 
Despite several limitations of this research, the study provides a se-
ries of valuable insights into how public relations is understood and 
practiced in Zimbabwe. However, it is important to stress that the re-
searchers relied on snowball sampling to recruit participants; therefore, 
these results cannot be generalized to all PR professionals in Zimbabwe. 
In addition, the data is based on respondents’ self-reporting, with some 
possibly misrepresenting their job functions. 
Now that Zimbabwean practitioners’ public relations roles have been 
defined, future research should explore which public relations models 
are practiced in Zimbabwe and how they compare across the globe via a 
cross-cultural comparison. Likewise, investigating the impact culture 
has on how public relations is practiced may provide a further under -
standing of the differences and similarities observed in public relations 
roles (van Heerden & Rensberg, 2005), in Zimbabwe and across the 
globe. For example, Tindall and Holtzhausen’s study (2011) found that 
political and cultural environments shape the context and content of 
public relations roles in South Africa. 
Future research should also investigate Zimbabwe’s public relations 
from the Global Capability Framework (Gregory & Fawkes, 2019), 
which captures professional public relations competencies. Under -
standing commonly shared public relations roles and duties will help 
develop professional public relations education in Zimbabwe by incor -
porating the needed skills and expertise into the curriculum. 
It is also essential to better understand the three factors that may 
influence public relations roles: professionalization (moving from one- 
way to two-way), the public relations curriculum, and organizational 
settings and environment. As van Heerden and Rensberg (2005) 
explained, determining what is being taught in African institutions and 
organizations will impact the roles and perceptions of African PR 
practitioners. 
6.Conclusion 
The recognition of cultural diversity in the practice and theory of 
public relations propels the development of the discipline, opening 
doors to new ideas, and shaping new approaches. This study responds to 
the call for more diversified research from Africa and contributes to the 
promotion of cultural inclusivity in public relations scholarship (Cree -
don & Al-Khaja, 2019). Public relations roles are one of the well-researched areas of public relations, yet most research is conducted 
within the U.S. In Africa, Zimbabwe is now among the few countries 
where public relations roles are examined. While examining public re-
lations roles generally emphasizes skills and effective communication, 
studying the diverse practices opens up spaces for acknowledging cul-
tural differences (Dutta & Elers, 2020). Azionya et al. (2019) reiterated 
that scholarship from emerging markets provides “perspectives that 
inform the cultural scope of communication research and practice, and 
issues that are not apparent to scholars working in different 
societies"(p.2). 
The results of this study allow scholars and practitioners to extend 
and clarify previous work on public relations roles in Africa, confirm the 
existence of technician, manager, and strategist roles, as well as offer 
unique insights into the public relations activities and the composition 
of the labor force in Zimbabwe.
