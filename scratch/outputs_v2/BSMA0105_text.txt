This cross-sectional study (conducted in April–May 2011) explored
associations between partnership functioning synergy and sustainability
of innovative programmes in community care. The study sample con-
sisted of 106 professionals (of 244 individuals contacted) participating in
21 partnerships that implemented different innovative community care
programmes in Rotterdam, The Netherlands. Partnership functioning
was evaluated by assessing leadership, resources administration and
efﬁciency. Synergy was considered the proximal outcome of partnership
functioning, which, in turn, inﬂuenced the achievement of programme
sustainability. On a 5-point scale of increasing sustainability, mean
sustainability scores ranged from 1.9 to 4.9. The results of the regression
analysis demonstrated that sustainability was positively inﬂuenced by
leadership (standardised regression coefﬁcient b= 0.32; P< 0.001) and
non-ﬁnancial resources ( b= 0.25; P= 0.008). No signiﬁcant relationship
was found between administration or efﬁciency and programme sustain-
ability. Partnership synergy acted as a mediator for partnership function-
ing and signiﬁcantly affected sustainability ( b= 0.39; P< 0.001). These
ﬁndings suggest that the sustainability of innovative programmes in
community care is achieved more readily when synergy is created
between partners. Synergy was more likely to emerge with boundary-
spanning leaders, who understood and appreciated partners’ different
perspectives, and could bridge their diverse cultures and were comfort-
able sharing ideas, resources and power. In addition, the acknowledge-
ment of and ability to use members’ resources were found to be
valuable in engaging partners’ involvement and achieving synergy in
community care partnerships.
Keywords: community care, innovation, quality, sustainability, synergy
Introduction
In recent years, considerable resources have been
devoted to the implementation and validation of innova-
tive programmes in communit y care. These programmes
have focused primarily on successful implementation,
without creating assurances of the ‘life of innovations’after extramural funding (fun ding from outside the orga-
nisation that is used to support a programme or project)
has ended (Goodman et al. 1993, Shediec-Rizkallah &
Bone 1998, Akerlund 2000, Vaughn et al. 2000). Given
this problem, there is a general consensus that sustain-
ability guidance is essential (Goodman & Steckler 1989,
Backer 2000). Sustainability is increasingly important to

Sustainability is important in community care
because the effects of innovative programmes can be
maintained over a long period (Puska et al. 1996, Manf-

For example, many communitycare programmes aim to increase health or well-being,and must usually survive for extended periods of time toachieve such changes (Steckler & Goodman 1989).Changes in health and well-being outcomes in mostcommunity health areas may not be detectable for
3–10 years after programme initiation (Roussos &
Fawcett 2000). In addition, the absence of sustainabilityin programmes perceived as beneﬁcial for the health andwell-being of targeted populations represents an invest-ment loss for the organisations and people involved (Yin1979, Steckler & Goodman 1989, Goodman et al. 1993,
Rissel et al. 1995, O’Loughlin et al. 1998, Shediec-Rizkal-

community programmes brings disillusion to partici-
pants, and therefore poses obstacles to subsequentcommunity mobilisation (Goodman et al. 1993, Shediec-
Rizkallah & Bone 1998).
A literature review focusing on the sustainability of
health-related innovations in community care showedthe importance of leadership. Leaders have often enthu-siastically advocated for a programme’s needs, particu-
larly to help secure resources for its continuation
(Scheirer 2005). More than three-fourths of studies exam-ining inﬂuences on sustainability have cited the impor-tance of a champion – a person whose strategicp l a c e m e n tw i t h i na no r g a n i s a t i o na l l o w sh i m ⁄her to
advocate effectively for the programme. Buchanan et al.
(2005) found that, in addition to leadership, sustainabilityis dependent on resources, administration, managementand efﬁciency. Lasker et al. (2001) identiﬁed leadership,
efﬁciency, administration and management andresources as the four dimensions of partnership function-ing, which can result in partnership synergy. Synergy isthe degree to which the partnership combines the com-
plementary strengths, perspectives, values and resources
of all partners in the search for better solutions (Gray1989, p. 5), and is generally regarded as the product of apartnership (Lasker & Weiss 2003). The synergy that apartnership can achieve is more than simply anexchange of resources among its partners. Theoretically,when partners effectively merge their perspectives,knowledge, and skills to create synergy, they create
something new and valuable – a whole that is greater
than the sum of its parts. A community care partnershipis able to create synergy by combining the perspectives,knowledge and skills of diverse partners in a way thatenables the partnership to do the following (i) think innew and better ways about how it can achieve its goals;(ii) plan more comprehensive, integrated programmes;and (iii) strengthen its relationship with the broader com-
munity (Lasker et al. 2001). The identiﬁcation of factors
that inﬂuence the ability of a community care partner-ship to attain synergy can help strengthen its capacity tocreate a successful collaborative process (Weiss et al.
2002), and thereby to achieve the sustainability of aninnovative programme.
The relationships between partnership functioning,
synergy and the sustainability of innovative programmes
in community care have not been elucidated to date.
Therefore, this study explored the associations betweenthese factors with the aim of enhancing knowledge aboutthe means by which partnership synergy and sustain-ability may be achieved in these programmes (Figure 1).In the study framework, synergy was considered theproximal outcome of partner ship functioning, which, in
turn, inﬂuenced programme s ustainability. We hypoth-
esised that the ability to create partnership synergy
would be an essential factor for the achievement of sus-
tainability in innovative programmes in communitycare.

Setting
This study examined community care programmes in
Rotterdam, The Netherlands . Tasks and responsibilities
have been shifted increasingly from the Dutch national
government to municipalities. Since January 2007,municipalities have been responsible for the execution ofthe ‘Wet maatschappelijke ondersteuning’ (a law man-dating social support for co mmunities), and have needed
to ﬁnd innovative ways to fulﬁl these new tasks. Themunicipality of Rotterdam has set up a fund for innova-tion to establish new initiati ves in housing, welfare and
care; this fund supported the 21 innovative projects
examined in this study. To qualify for funding, theprogrammes were required to be innovative and (i)
enhance the self-reliance of community members and ⁄or
(ii) integrate health-care, social services and ⁄or welfare.

Measures
Sustainability was assessed with a 9-item instrument
developed by Slaghuis et al. (2011). Examples of items
a r ea sf o l l o w s :‘ t h en e wp r a c t i c ei sr e g a r d e da st h es t a n -
dard way to work’ and ‘all colleagues involved in thenew work practice are knowledgeable about it’.Responses are using a ﬁve-point scale, with higher scoresindicating greater sustainability. The sustainability scorewas derived by calculating the mean of the total 9-itemscores.
The Partnership Self-Assessment Tool: Short version
(PSAT-S) was used to measure partnership synergy and
dimensions of partnership functioning (Cramm et al.
2011). This instrument contains nine items that measurepartnership synergy (e.g. ‘By working together, how wellare the partners able to identify new and creative waysto solve problems’?) and components that measure ﬁvedimensions of partnership functioning: leadership (fouritems; e.g. inspiring or motivating people and taking
responsibility), efﬁciency (three items; e.g. how well the
partnership uses the partners’ ﬁnancial resources),administration and managem ent (four items; e.g. evalu-
ating the progress and impact of the partnership andorganising partnership activ ities, including meetings and
projects), and non-ﬁnancial resources [four items e.g.data and information (statistic al data, information, skills,
expertise)]. Responses to all items are structured using a
ﬁve-point Likert scale. Butt et al. (2008) and Cramm et al.
(2011) have recently conﬁrmed the validity of the PSATfor the measurement of partnership processes. Partner-ship synergy and partnership functioning dimensionscores were derived by calculating the sum score ofresponses within each concept. The partnership synergyscore reﬂected the extent to which participants in eachpartnership were accomplishing more together than they
could individually.

ergy allows researchers to determine how collaboration
works. In the evaluation of early efforts, programmemembers need to be able to document how well the part-nership has achieved synergy. To strengthen the abilityof partnerships to realise their full potential, participantsneed to know what they must do together to achievesynergy. This research showed that partners seek,through collaboration, to a chieve synergy that extends
beyond the mere exchange of resources. By combining
partners’ individual perspectives, resources and skills, apartnership creates a new and valuable whole that isgreater than the sum of its individual parts (Shannon1998, Cramm et al. 2011). Synergy is manifested in the
thoughts and actions that result from collaborationwithin the partnership (Lasker et al. 2001).
Our study found a strong relationship between lead-
ership and the sustainability of innovative programmes
in community care. This effect was mediated by partner-ship synergy, which additiona lly indicates a strong rela-
tionship between leadership and synergy. Theseﬁndings are consistent with those of other research,which has documented the importance of leadershipthroughout all phases of partnership development(Roussos & Fawcett 2000, Weiss et al. 2002, Jones & Barry
2011). High levels of synergy are associated with leader-
ship that effectively facilitates productive interactionsa m o n gp a r t n e r sb yb r i d g i n gd i v e r s ec u l t u r e s ,s h a r i n gpower, facilitating open dialogue and revealing and chal-lenging assumptions that limit thought and action. Theidentiﬁcation of leadership capacities that are critical forsynergy helps us to better understand the types of lead-ers necessary for effective pl anning and implementation
in different situations and at various levels. Previous
studies have indicated that partnerships need leaders,who are able to understand an d appreciate partners’ dif-
ferent perspectives, empower partners and performboundary-spanning functions (Alter & Hage 1993, Wolff2001, Weiss et al. 2002); these qualities may also apply to
leaders of partnerships in community care. Most sectorsand professions do not currently produce leaders of the
type required to achieve synergy; this factor poses a key
challenge for community care partnerships. In contrast,community care partnerships need boundary-spanningleaders who understand and a ppreciate partners’ differ-
ent perspectives, can bridge their diverse cultures andare comfortable sharing ideas, resources, and power(Alter & Hage 1993, McKinney et al. 1993). Moreover,
many partnerships involve a number of people in the
provision of leadership, in both formal and informal
capacities (Chrislip & Larson 1994).
Our study found a strong association between part-
nership resources and the sustainability of innovativeprogrammes in community ca re that was mediated bypartnership synergy, which suggests a strong relation-
ship between partnership synergy and resources. Straub
et al. (2007) also found that fostering equitable involve-
ment by acknowledging and using members’ resources,in particular their knowledge and expertise was valuablein achieving partnership synergy. Beyond basicresources, such as money, space, equipment and goods,community care partnerships need a broad array of skillsand expertise to engage partners in supporting the col-
laboration process, and perform and coordinate the mul-
tiple components of their innovative programmes. Suchskills may include communication, organisation, collabo-ration, information technology and evaluation. Informa-tion, which forms the basis for joint problem-solving, isalso an essential resource for achieving synergy (Laskeret al. 2001). The types of information required by partner-
ships extend beyond statisti cal data to include the per-
spectives, values, and ideas o f different stakeholders and
community groups, as well as information about thecommunity’s assets, political environment and history.The ability of collaboration to bring together differenttypes of information from various disciplines and theperceptions of diverse community groups is a greatstrength of partnership synergy.

when interpreting the ﬁndings. Most importantly, the
