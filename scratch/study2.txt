The question of when and how \nboundary spanning employees will win supervisor support remains unanswered. To address this issue, drawing \non relevant concepts of interpersonal interaction, this study constructs a moderated mediation model to examine \nhow employee upward advice seeking influences supervisors’ cognitive and affective trust and subsequent \ninterpersonal support towards boundary spanning employees. \nHypotheses were tested through a scenario experiment (Study 1) and a multi-wave, supervisor-employee \nmatching field survey (Study 2). For the experimental study, we recruited 220 full-time employees from \ndifferent companies in China through our alumni network. We adopted a 2 (high employee boundary spanning \nvs. low employee boundary spanning) × 2 (high upward advice seeking vs. low upward advice seeking) \nbetween-subjects design to generate four scenarios, with participants randomly assigned to each of the scenarios. \nWe asked participants to imagine that they were the supervisor in the scenario and to read the materials related \nto employee boundary spanning and upward advice seeking in order. Next, participants completed measures of \ncognitive trust, affective trust, supervisor support, manipulation check, and demographics. For the field study, \nwe conducted a three-wave questionnaire survey and collected data from 406 supervisor-employee dyads in \nknowledge-intensive firms in Hubei, Zhejiang, Shanghai, and Beijing provinces. At Time 1, employees assessed \ntheir boundary spanning behaviors and upward advice seeking and provided their demographics. At Time 2 (one \nmonth after Time 1), immediate supervisors of employees who completed the Time 1 survey were invited to \nassess their cognitive and affective trust in their subordinates. At Time 3 (one month after Time 2), immediate \nsupervisors continued to be invited to report the support provided to their employees. We conducted \nconfirmatory factor analysis, regression analysis, and path analysis via SPSS 22 and Mplus 8.0 to analyze the \ndata. \nThe results of Study 1 (N = 220) indicated that employee boundary spanning and upward advice seeking \ninteracted to affect supervisor cognitive trust and affective trust, which in turn influenced supervisor support. \nWhen employee upward advice seeking was high, employee boundary spanning would have a stronger impact on \nsupervisor cognitive and affective trust, which in turn resulted in increased supervisor support. Study 2 (N = 406) \nagain revealed that upward advice seeking positively moderated the direct effect of employee boundary spanning \non supervisor affective trust as well as the indirect effect of employee boundary spanning on supervisor support \nvia affective trust. However, it did not support the moderating role of upward advice seeking on the supervisor \ncognitive trust path, suggesting that in the real workplace, cognitive trust stems from supervisors’ long-standing \nindependent rational judgments of employee competence. \nThis study makes several theoretical contributions. First, we contribute to the literature on employee \nboundary spanning behavior by adopting a supervisor-focused perspective and exploring supervisors’ positive \nattitudes toward boundary spanning employees. Second, we reveal the boundary conditions under which \nemployee boundary spanning can win the supervisor support by selecting employee upward advice seeking as a \nmoderator, which deepens the research on upward influence strategy from the perspective of supervisor- \nemployee dyadic interaction. Third, we enhance the understanding of how the interaction of employee boundary \nspanning and upward advice seeking increases supervisor support by examining the mediating role of supervisor \ncognitive and affective trust. \nKeywords  employee boundary spanning behavior, supervisor support, cognitive trust, affective trust, upward \nadvice seeking \n \n"]
