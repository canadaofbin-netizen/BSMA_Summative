Abstract
Purpose – The rapid advancement of digital transformation requires a shift in firms’ focus from past met
needs to both latent future and unmet past needs. However, how boundary-spanning search with future
orientation and past orientation affects breakthrough innovation remains unclear. This study thus aims to
investigate the relationship between boundary-spanning search and breakthrough innovation from the
perspective of search orientation.
Design/methodology/approach – In terms of search orientation, this study divides boundary-spanning
search into forward-looking search and backward-looking search. Drawing on resource-based view, this study
develops a theoretical model in which big data analytics capability moderates the effects of forward-looking
and backward-looking searches on breakthrough innovation. Empirical analyses were conducted on data from
China’s advanced manufacturing firms. Research model and hypotheses were tested through multiple
regression.
Findings – The results confirm that forward-looking search has a positive effect on breakthrough innovation,
and big data analytics capability strengthens this positive effect. Furthermore, backward-looking search has
an inverted U-shaped effect on breakthrough innovation. Interestingly, as big data analytics capability
increases, this inverted U-shaped curve flattens and becomes almost linear.
Originality/value – This study uncovers the different effects of boundary-spanning search with different
orientations on breakthrough innovation and extends the research on the relationship between boundary-
spanning search and breakthrough innovation by incorporating search orientation. Furthermore, by
demonstrating the moderating role of big data analytics capability, this study provides a crucial condition
under which boundary-spanning search can enhance breakthrough innovation.

Boundary-spanning search refers to firms’ knowledge search activities that cross
organizational or technical boundaries (Lee et al., 2024). It can access to heterogenous and
novel knowledge, perspectives and ideas to think outside the box and generate creative
business insights (Acharya et al., 2022; Menguc et al., 2014). Therefore, it has been a critical
driver of breakthrough innovation. In terms of orientations to latent future needs and unmet
past needs, boundary-spanning search can be divided into forward-looking search and
backward-looking search. Specifically, forward-looking search refers to the process by which
firms seek knowledge beyond their borders to anticipate and satisfy latent future needs
(Jissink et al., 2019). Firms that conduct forward-looking search frequently scan for external
knowledge to capture market trends and potential opportunities for new products.
Backward-looking search refers to the process by which firms seek knowledge beyond
their borders to meet needs not met in the past (Chen, 2008; Zan et al., 2022). Firms that
conduct backward-looking search constantly look for external knowledge to improve
existing products and processes.
Existing studies have explored the effect of boundary-spanning search on breakthrough
innovation from various perspectives. From the perspective of knowledge type, Jung and Lee
(2016) fund the positive relationship between original knowledge search and breakthrough
innovation. From the perspective of search scope, Wang et al. (2020) identified the inverted
U-shaped effects of search depth and search breadth on firms’ innovation. Furthermore, from
the perspective of search timing, Yang et al. (2021) demonstrated the mechanism of how
proactive search and responsive search influence firms’ innovation. However, few studies
explored the relationship between boundary-spanning search and breakthrough innovation
from the perspective of search orientation. Since a firm’s strategic orientation largely
determines how it conduct and manage its business activities (Spanjol et al., 2012), the search
orientations to latent future needs and unmet past needs may result in different search efforts
and outcomes. Therefore, it is necessary to investigate how forward-looking search and
backward-looking search affect breakthrough innovation.
Moreover, recent studies highlighted big data analytics capability as an accelerator of
firms’ innovation processes (Al-Omoush et al., 2024; Bag et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2021). Big
data analytics capability is the ability to quickly process vast quantities of data to provide
firms with timely environment information (Al-Omoush et al., 2024). The continuously
advancement of digital transformation generates unprecedented volume, variety and
velocity data, namely big data, increasing the demand for data analytics (Nisar et al., 2021).
Firms with higher big data analytics capabilities can extract valuable information from large
amounts of data sets to capture consumer needs and market trends (Hyun et al., 2023). Such
information is vital for firms to leverage the knowledge obtained through boundary-
spanning search to create novel insights, identify emerging opportunities, and make
informed decisions (Lozada et al., 2023). That is, big data analytics capability may moderate
the effects of boundary-spanning on breakthrough innovation. However, whether and how it
accelerates the transformation from boundary-spanning search to breakthrough innovation
is still unclear.
To fill the above research gaps, this study investigates how boundary-spanning search
affects breakthrough innovation from the perspective of search orientation and examine the
moderating role of big data analytics capability. In terms of firms’ search orientations to
latent future needs and unmet past needs, this study divides boundary-spanning search into
forward-looking search and backward-looking search (Chen, 2008; Jissink et al., 2019).
Drawing upon resource-based view (RBV), this study develops a theoretical model in which

2. Theoretical background and hypotheses
The literature about boundary-spanning search, big data analytics capability, and
breakthrough innovation was acquired from WOS and CNKI databases. By analyzing
relevant literature through Citespace software (Chen, 2006), we induce the definitions,
dimensions, antecedents and research trends of each variable, and identify the foundation
theory, namely RBV. According to RBV, resources, especially knowledge resources, are the
critical sources of firms’ sustainable competitive advantage. Boundary-spanning search is an
important way to access to various knowledge which in turn can foster firms’ innovation, and
subsequently promote sustainable competitive advantage (Lee et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2021).
Therefore, based upon RBV, we develop a theoretical model to investigate the mechanism
through which boundary-spanning search affects breakthrough innovation.
2.1 Boundary-spanning search and breakthrough innovation
2.1.1 Forward-looking search and breakthrough innovation. Forward-looking search refers to
the process by which firms seek knowledge beyond their borders to anticipate and satisfy
latent future needs. Firms that conduct forward-looking search frequently scan for external
knowledge to capture market trends and potential opportunities for new products. The

influence of forward-looking search on breakthrough innovation can be manifested in three
ways. First, forward-looking search promotes proactive strategy planning for breakthrough
innovation. According to organizational learning theory, forward-looking search may
acquire external knowledge which involves environment changes and forthcoming trends.
Such knowledge is the basis to formulate proactive strategy for new product development,
new market entry and new technology adoption (Bhatia, 2021). As business strategy theories
suggest, proactive strategy is a medium that figures out firms’ long-term direction and shared
goals. Under the guidance of proactive strategy, firms are able to develop plans about future
scenarios (Marinkovic et al., 2022). While scenario planning determines how to integrate
various resources, shared goals promote coordinate multiple innovation activities (Patricio
et al., 2020). In this way, firms could effectively deploy and utilize various innovation
resources, thus enhancing breakthrough innovation.
Second, forward-looking search generates novel insights to initiate breakthrough
innovation. Firms that conduct forward-looking search usually scan for the knowledge
about customer requirements, emerging technologies and competitors’ actions (Jissink et al.,
2019). While constantly scanning for customer requirements allows firms to capture the
trends in customer needs and anticipate future customer needs, focusing on emerging
technologies enables firms to utilize novel and powerful technologies to meet new customer
needs (Marinkovic et al., 2022; Schwarz et al., 2020). Additionally, by monitoring competitors’
R&D projects, patenting programs, and new product launch activities, forward-looking
search may acquire heterogeneous knowledge and create different ideas about new products
and service ways (Yang et al., 2021).
Third, forward-looking search challenges conventional routines to enhance breakthrough
innovation. By aiming at future needs, forward-looking search may bring in valuable
knowledge to deepen firms’ understanding about future and help to develop corporate
foresight (Schwarz et al., 2020). When the perceived signals of breakthrough innovation do
not fit firms’ expectations, a deep understanding about future may restructure firms’
interpretation routines (Jissink et al., 2019; Rohrbeck and Gem€unden, 2011). This encourages
firms to interpret innovation signals in alternatives ways, and enables firms to seize
innovation opportunities. On the other hand, as Marinkovic et al. (2022) suggested, firms who
have corporate foresight are more capable to create agile R&D routines. Such agile routines
are critical for firms to overcome managerial myopias, engage in under-explored domains and
capture weak signals of breakthrough innovation.
Based upon the above arguments, this study hypothesizes:
H1a. Forward-looking search has a positive effect on breakthrough innovation.
2.1.2 Backward-looking search and breakthrough innovation. Backward-looking search refers
to the process by which firms seek knowledge beyond their borders to meet needs not met in
the past. Firms that conduct backward-looking search constantly look for external
knowledge to improve existing products and processes (Chen, 2008; Zan et al., 2022).
According to behavioral theory, the discrepancy between companies’ actual performance and
aspirations affects companies’ decision-making in R&D activities. Agency theory further
suggests that, companies generally take actions to minimize the potential negative outcomes
that could harm their performance. To a certain extent, unmet needs reflect the discrepancy
between the actual effectiveness and aspirations of firms’ products and processes, and may
lead to negative performance. Given that breakthrough innovation can effectively improve
firms’ products and processes, unmet needs may be important triggers for breakthrough
innovation (Lv et al., 2019). In this logic, backward-looking search may strengthen firms’
motivation to obtain nonlocal knowledge for new product development and business process
revolution. Additionally, unmet needs provide a negative performance frame for decision
makers. Based on financial theory, firms’ attitudes toward risk will shift from risk averse to

risk seeking (Fehle and Tsyplakov, 2005). That is, firms may accept risks to avoid negative
performance. Hence, firms that conduct backward-looking search may have higher risk
tolerance and deploy the obtained knowledge into more radical R&D activities (Hu
et al., 2022).
Besides the above positive effects, backward-looking search also has some limitations.
First, by focusing on unmet past needs, backward-looking search may obtain obsolete
knowledge which cannot support breakthrough innovation. The advancement of
digitalization continuously creates new digital technologies and application scenarios,
positioning firms in a rapidly changing environment. To adapt to technological changes,
firms should quickly capture market trends and predict future needs to conduct
breakthrough innovation, so as to enhance their agility and dynamic capability (Al-
Omoush et al., 2024). However, unmet past needs may have been addressed by outdated
technologies or methodologies that are no longer relevant or effective in the current market.
Hence, the knowledge obtained through backward-looking search may be outdated to
respond to the ever-changing environment and achieve valuable breakthrough innovation.
Second, backward-looking search pays more attention to the knowledge about specific
problems of products and processes, restricting firms’ search scope (Posen et al., 2018).
Focusing on specific problems may consume significant resources in terms of time, human
resources, and finances. According to attention theory, there may be fewer resources
available for exploring alternative knowledge or new opportunities outside the scope of these
problems (Brielmaier and Friesl, 2023). Given that breakthrough innovation relies more
heavily on various and heterogenous knowledge, the limited knowledge may decrease the
likelihood of generating path-breaking novelty (Jung and Lee, 2016). Third, as Angus (2019)
argued, searched knowledge about a given problem is less applicable in a dissimilar context.
Hence, when exploring a new domain or developing new products, backward-looking search
may be unable to generate adequate innovation outcomes.
A possible explanation for these inconsistent arguments may be that extant research has
not taken the level of backward-looking search into consideration. Specifically, as the levels of
backward-looking search increase from low to moderate, firms’ risk tolerance and motivation
to breakthrough innovation increase (Hu et al., 2022). This will encourage firms to venture the
obtained knowledge into more radical R&D activities (Lv et al., 2019). Although there may
come obsolete and limited knowledge reducing the novelty of breakthrough innovation, firms
can overcome these disadvantages through continuously environment-scanning and
knowledge integration activities (Yang et al., 2021). Thus, from low to moderate levels, the
positive effect of backward-looking search on breakthrough innovation is greater that its
negative effect. However, as the levels of backward-looking search increase from moderate to
high, firms have to spend more efforts and costs to reduce the negative impact deriving from
the decreasing innovation novelty (Jung and Lee, 2016). As a result, the negative effect of
backward-looking search will gradually outweigh its positive effect and eventually be
dominant in the relationship between backward-looking search and breakthrough
innovation. Therefore, this study hypothesis:
H1b. Backward-looking search has an inverted U-shaped effect on breakthrough
innovation.

Big data analytics capability refers to the ability to quickly process vast quantities of data to
provide firms with timely environment information. The influence of big data analytics
capability on the relationship between forward-looking search and breakthrough innovation
can be manifested as follows. First, big data analytics capability enables informed strategy
plans. Due to the character of evidence- and data-based, big data analytics is with less human

complex analysis on various types of data, and extract accurate information about future

looking search to develop more informed strategy plans for breakthrough innovation (Lozada
et al., 2023). By enabling informed strategy plans, big data analytics capability may improve
the likelihood of transforming forward-looking search into breakthrough innovation.
Second, big data analytics capability increases creative idea identification. With
assistance of advanced algorithms, big data analytics can collect and analyze vast
amounts of data from various sources (Mikalef et al., 2019). Through these analyses, firms
may uncover novel business patterns that go beyond simple and traditional correlations
(Lozada et al., 2023). Furthermore, big data analytics can also recognize anomalies in the data,
which might indicate potential opportunities for innovation (Habeeb et al., 2019). These
patterns and opportunities provide more creative ideas for firms to leverage the knowledge
obtained by forward-looking search to achieve breakthrough innovation. In other words, by
identifying creative ideas, big data analytics capability may speed the transformation from
forward-looking search to breakthrough innovation.
Third, big data analytics capability facilitates cross-functional collaboration. Big data
analytics can analyze data from different departments and teams, identify the common
interest among functions, and indicate collaboration opportunities. This facilitates insights
and knowledge sharing among marketing, R&D, and design teams, and encourages
synthesis of diverse ideas and perspectives. In this way, firms with higher big data analytics
capability are able to fully interpret innovation signals, and efficiently deploy the knowledge
obtained by forward-looking search to promote breakthrough innovation (Ansari and
Ghasemaghaei, 2023; Dubey et al., 2019). Therefore, by facilitating cross-functional
collaboration, big data analytics capability may increase the efficiency in transforming
forward-looking search into breakthrough innovation.
Based upon the above arguments, this study hypothesizes:
H2a. Big data analytics capability moderates and strengthens the positive effect of
forward-looking search on breakthrough innovation.
The influence of big data analytics capability on the relationship between backward-looking
search is twofold. On the one hand, as backward-looking search increase from low to
moderate level, big data analytics capability strengthens its positive effect on breakthrough
innovation. As Lozada et al. (2023) suggested, analyzing data on external factors such as
market volatility, technology trends and customer behavior can help firms to predict
potential risks. Accordingly, firms with higher big data analytics capability can proactively
develop risk management strategies to increase risk tolerance and carry out more radical
innovation projects. The other advantage of big data analytics is that it can use various types
of data to identify emerging innovation opportunities (Kumar et al., 2021). When perceiving
opportunities, firms will be encouraged to set ambitious goals to achieve innovation (Chen
and Liu, 2020). In other words, by identifying innovation opportunities, big data analytics
capability increases firms’ motivation to breakthrough innovation. Taken together, big data
analytics capability can enhance firms’ motivation and risk tolerance in leveraging
backward-looking search to achieve breakthrough innovation.
On the other hand, as backward-looking search increase from moderate to high level, big
data analytics capability mitigates its negative effect on breakthrough innovation. When the
level of backward-looking search is high, firms may devote their most efforts to search for
knowledge about unfulfilled needs. As mentioned before, high-level of backward-looking
search may lead to obsolete and limited knowledge, decreasing innovation novelty.
As Habeeb et al. (2019) argued, big data analytics can provide a rapid feedback loop by
monitoring real-time data. This allows firms to detect environment changes, identify new

3. Research design
3.1 Sample and data collection
This study selected China’s advanced manufacturing firms as the research sample, which
include intelligent manufacturing equipment, marine engineering equipment, aerospace
equipment, new materials, new energy vehicles and so on. First, China has a huge
manufacturing system, among which advanced manufacturing industries are the most active
field of innovation (Yang et al., 2021). Firms in these industries invest more efforts to improve
innovation capabilities. Second, digital technologies are widely adopted in advanced
manufacturing industries (Yang et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022). Sampling firms in these
industries can deeply understand the influence of digital transformation. Third, the
government of the People’s Republic of China published the 2035 Long-term Goal Outline. It
emphasizes the important strategic role of advanced manufacturing in enhancing China’s
industrial capabilities, and provides many support policies.
This study collected data from advanced manufacturing firms in Shenzhen, Suzhou, Xi’an,
Zhengzhou, Weifang and Hengyang. According to the Report on China’s Top 100 Advanced
Manufacturing Cities in 2022, the development of advanced manufacturing firms can be
divided into four stages. These stages are the leapfrogging, expansion, developing, and start-
up stages. Firms in the leapfrogging stage have established solid manufacturing bases and
industrial competitiveness, and are mainly located in Shenzhen, Suzhou and other East China
regions. Firms in the expansion stage have developed multiple industrial chains, but there is
still a gap with the first level. They are mainly located in Xi’an and Zhengzhou. Firms in the
developing stage have gained strong competitive advantages in the subdivision of industrial

3.3 Bias and common method variance
This study used t-test in firm size, firm age, R&D intensity and digitalization experience to
examine non-response bias and later-response bias (Gerbing and Anderson, 1988). According
to the measurements, these variables were operationalized as continuous variables. We
respectively compared the means of these variables between respondents and non-
respondents, early respondents and later respondents by SPSS. Results indicated no
significant differences between respondents and non-respondents in firm size (p 5 0.482),
firm age (p 5 0.365), R&D intensity (p 5 0.359), digitalization experience (p 5 0.970).
Moreover, there were no significant differences in firm size (p 5 0.427), firm age (p 5 0.664),
R&D intensity (p 5 0.243), digitalization experience (p 5 0.925) between early respondents
and later respondents. Hence, neither non-response bias nor later-response bias is a serious
concern in this study.
To assess the potential common method variance (CMV) deriving single source, this study
compared two nested models through confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) by Smart-PLS
(Podsakoff et al., 2012). One model is the substantive factor model, in which items were loaded
on their own research variables. The other is the common method factor model, in which a
latent common method factor including all items were added in the substantive factor model.
As shown in Appendix, the results show that the average explained variance of the items in
the substantive factor model is 0.712, and 0.713 for the common method factor model. That is,
the common method factor averagely explains 0.001 of total variances, indicating a very
small magnitude of CMV. Furthermore, almost loadings on common method factor are
insignificant, suggesting an insignificance of CMV. Additionally, we also performed
Harman’s single-factor test which entails composite-based and factor-base analyses (Kock,
2021). In Harman’s single-factor test, a model with a single latent variable was created, and
the total variance explained is in fact the average variance extracted (AVE). An AVE value
greater than 0.5 indicates the presence of CMV. We performed composite-based PLS test and
factor-based PLS test by WarpPLS. The AVE of each test is 0.400 and 0.361, both are less
than 0.5. Therefore, CMV is not a major threat in this study.

The reliability of research variables was evaluated via construct reliability by Smart-PLS.

The validity of research variables was tested in terms of convergent validity and

5. Discussion and implications
5.1 Discussion
The purpose of this study is to investigate how boundary-spanning search affects
breakthrough innovation from the perspective of search orientation and examine the
moderating role of big data analytics capability. Based upon empirical analyses on data from
China’s advanced manufacturing firms, this study draws the following findings.

First, forward-looking search has a positive effect on breakthrough innovation. Firms that
conduct forward-looking search frequently scan for external knowledge to capture market
trends and potential opportunities for new products. Such knowledge is the basis for firms to
develop proactive strategy plans and think outside the box, thus generating novel insights
for breakthrough innovation. It also enables firms to restructure interpretation procedure and
create agile R&D routines to enhance breakthrough innovation. Hence, firms are able to think
differently than before and capture novel signals of breakthrough innovation. This finding
elucidates the influences of forward-looking search on breakthrough innovation, and
highlights forward-looking search as a key driver of breakthrough innovation. It is also
consistent with the arguments that forward-looking search plays a critical role in firms’
innovation activities (Jissink et al., 2019; Marinkovic et al., 2022).
Second, backward-looking search has an inverted U-shaped effect on breakthrough
innovation. Firms that conduct backward-looking search constantly look for external
knowledge to improve existing products and processes. When the level of backward-looking
search increases from low to moderate, breakthrough innovation increases as backward-
looking search increases firms’ motivation and risk tolerance to achieve breakthrough
innovation. However, when the level of backward-looking search increases from moderate to
high, it will constraints firms’ search scope and obtain outdated knowledge, thereby
hampering breakthrough innovation. This finding reveals that the positive effect of
backward-looking search on breakthrough innovation initially increases, but after a certain
point, it will decrease and finally disappear. Existing studies have inconsistent arguments
about the effects of backward-looking search on breakthrough innovation. Some suggested
the positive effects (Hu et al., 2022), while others claimed the negative effects (Posen et al.,
2018). By taking the levels of backward-looking search into consideration, this finding
provides a new perspective to understand the relationship between backward-looking search
and firms’ innovation.
Third, big data analytics capability respectively moderates the effects of forward-looking
search and backward-looking search on breakthrough innovation. By enabling informed
strategy plans, creative idea identification and cross-functional collaboration, big data
analytics capability strengthens the positive effect of backward-looking search on
breakthrough innovation. On the other hand, big data analytics capability makes the
inverted U-shaped curve between backward-looking search and breakthrough innovation
more linear by strengthening the positive effect and mitigating the negative effect of
backward-looking search. This finding demonstrates the moderating roles of big data
analytics capability in utilizing two types of searches to promote breakthrough innovation. It
also supports the arguments of Hyun et al. (2023) and Mikalef et al. (2019) that big data
analytics capability is an important accelerator in firms’ innovation activities.
Given China’s huge manufacturing industry, fast innovation and widely digital
technology adoption, these findings may effectively capture the relationships among
boundary-spanning search, big data analytics capability, and breakthrough innovation in the
digital era. Similarly, these findings may be applied in various contexts where firms are
striving to enhance their innovation capabilities and adopt new digital technologies, such as
technology and IT industry, healthcare industry, energy and utilities industry.
5.2 Theoretical implications
Our findings contribute to extant research by three ways. First, this study extends the
research on the relationship between boundary-spanning search and breakthrough
innovation by taking search orientation into account. The rapid advancement of digital
transformation requires a shift in firms’ focus from unmet past needs to both latent future
needs and unmet past needs. Although extant research has investigated the influences of

Second, this study uncovers the different effects of boundary-spanning search with
different orientations on breakthrough innovation. A firm’s search orientation partially
determines its search efforts and outcomes (Spanjol et al., 2012). However, there is no
adequate understanding of how boundary-spanning search with different orientations
affects breakthrough innovation. Our results confirm the positive effect of forward-looking
search on breakthrough innovation and the inverted U-shaped effect of backward-looking
search on breakthrough innovation. In this way, this study identifies the different influences
of forward-looking and backward-looking searches on breakthrough innovation, and
articulates the relationship between boundary-spanning search and breakthrough
innovation from the perspective of search orientation. Moreover, by revealing the inverted
U-shaped effect of backward-looking search on breakthrough innovation, this study also
offers reasonable explanations to the inconsistent arguments on this relationship (Hu et al.,
2022; Posen et al., 2018).
Third, by examining the moderating role of big data analytics capability, this study
clarifies the crucial condition under which firms can increase the efficacy of boundary-
spanning search in achieving breakthrough innovation. Recent studies highlighted big data
analytics capability as an accelerator of firms’ innovation processes (Hyun et al., 2023;
Kumar et al., 2021), but whether and how it accelerates the transformation from boundary-
spanning search to breakthrough innovation is still unclear. This study suggests that big
data analytics capability strengthens the positive effect of forward-looking search on
breakthrough innovation. What’s more, as big data analytics capability increases, the
inverted U-shaped curve between backward-looking search and breakthrough innovation is
flattened and becomes almost linear. Hence, this study sheds light on the underlying
mechanism by which boundary-spanning search can enhance breakthrough innovation,
and provides a contingent viewpoint to deeply understand the relationship between
boundary-spanning search and breakthrough innovation. Furthermore, this study also
extends the research on boundary-spanning search and breakthrough innovation by
including big data analytics capability as a moderator (Jung and Lee, 2016; Wang et al., 2020;
Yang et al., 2021).
5.3 Managerial implications
This study has several managerial implications. First, managers should pay attention to the
influence of digital transformation on firms’ knowledge and innovation management. Digital
technologies have revolutionized firms’ knowledge and innovation management by
enhancing external knowledge access, enabling open innovation, facilitating
communication and collaboration and so on. To improve the efficiency of boundary-
spanning search, firms can leverage various digital technologies. For example, firms can
subscribe to industry experts’ blogs or social media accounts to obtain updated insights and
suggestions from them. It can also be helpful to employ specialized technology tools and
intelligence services to track competitor, marketing trends and emerging technologies.
Additionally, firms can participate in open innovation platforms and ecosystems to connect
with other firms, innovative start-ups and research institutes.

Second, managers should be aware of the differences resulting in different search
orientations. On the one hand, firms should foster corporate foresights in boundary-spanning
search to capture future needs and trends. Managers can regularly participate in industry
seminars and research reports, and pay attention to industry trends and emerging
technologies. Reading industry news, communicating with peers and practitioners may also
help to broaden managers’ thinking to anticipate future changes. On the other hand, when
searching knowledge for unmet past needs, managers should control the search activities at
an appropriate level. Managers can set the goal of their search activities according to firms’
current demands, and develop corresponding indicators to measure the activity effectiveness.
Moreover, managers can utilize digital tools and analytics to monitor and evaluate the
effectiveness, and make timely adjustments about search strategies and search methods.
Third, managers should take several steps to improve firms’ big data analytics capabilities.
The key to improving firms’ big data analytics capabilities is to build an efficient data pipeline to
collect, clean, integrate, and reserve data from various resources. It is also important to adopt
appropriatebigdataanalyticstoolsandtechniquesaccordingtofirms’ demandsanddatasize.In

can be responsible for data processing, modeling, and interpretation. Last but not least, given
that big data analytics is an iterative process, managers should continuously monitor and
evaluate analysis results. Based on feedbacks and data-driven insights, managers can make
adjustments and optimizations to improve the effectiveness and value of data analytics.
Moreover, this study also has social contributions regarding to the United Nations
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). One of the SDGs is Industry, Innovation, and
Infrastructure: build resilient infrastructure, promote inclusive and sustainable
industrialization, and foster innovation. By investigating the mechanism underlying the
relationship between boundary-spanning search and breakthrough innovation in the digital
era, this study provides insights about how organizations can leverage boundary-spanning
search to drive innovation and technological advancements. Since boundary-spanning
search often involves collaboration and partnerships with innovation actors, this study may
also facilitate the development of global partnerships that can enhance innovation and deal
with complex challenges, thus promoting sustainable development.
6. Conclusion, limitations and future research
The rapid advancement of digital transformation requires a shift of firms’ focus from unmet
past needs to both latent future and unmet past needs. However, how boundary-spanning
search with future orientation and past orientation affects breakthrough innovation remains
unclear. From the perspective of search orientation, this study divides boundary-spanning
search into forward-looking search and backward-looking search. Based upon RBV, this
study develops a theoretical model to explore the effects of forward-looking search and
backward-looking search on breakthrough innovation, and tests the moderating role of big
data analytics capability. Data from China’s advanced manufacturing firms were used to
examine the theoretical model and research hypotheses. The empirical results confirm that
with the increase of forward-looking search, firms’ breakthrough innovation increases, and
big data analytics capability can accelerate the transformation from forward-looking search
to breakthrough innovation. Furthermore, with the increase of backward-looking search, the
likelihood that firms achieve breakthrough innovation will initially increase, but then
decrease. Interestingly, big data analytics capability can mitigate the negative effect of
backward-looking search, and increase the efficacy of the transformation from backward-
looking search to breakthrough innovation. Our findings provide some theoretical and
managerial contributions for firms’ innovation in the digital era.
