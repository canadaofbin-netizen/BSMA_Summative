Abstract
Purpose – The recent COVID-19 pandemic has (triggered) lots of interest in work from home (WFH)
practices. Many organizations in India are changing their work practices and adopting new models of getting
the work done. The purpose of the study to look at the boundary-ﬁt perspective (Ammons (2013) and two
factors, namely, individual preferences (boundary control, family identity, work identity and technology
stress) and environmental factors (job control, supervisor support and organizational policies). These
dimensions are used and considered to create various clusters for employees working from home.
Design/methodology/approach – K-mean clustering was used to do the cluster analysis. Statistical
package for social sciences 23 was used to explore different clusters based on a pattern of characteristics
unique to that cluster, but each cluster differed from other clusters. Further analysis of variance test was
conducted to see how these clusters differ across three chosen outcomes, namely, work-family conﬂict,
boundary management tactics used and positive family-to-work spillover effect. The post hoc test also
provided insights on how each cluster differs from others on these outcomes.
Findings – The results indicated four distinct clusters named boundary-ﬁt family guardians, work warriors,
boundary-ﬁt fusion lovers and dividers consistent (with previous) research. These clusters also differ across at
least two major outcomes like boundary management tactics and positive spillover. The high control cluster
proﬁles like Cluster 3 (boundary-ﬁt fusion lovers) and Cluster 4 (dividers) showed low technostress and higher
use of boundary management tactics. Cluster 3 (boundary-ﬁt fusion lovers) and Cluster 1 (boundary-ﬁt family
guardians) having high environmental inﬂuencers also showed higher positive family-to-work spillover.
Research limitations/implications – Because this study is very speciﬁc to the Indian context, a broad
generalization requires further exploration in other cultural contexts. The absence of this exploration is one of
the limitations of this study. On the culture continuum, countries may vary from being individualistic on one
extreme to being collectivistic on the other extreme. Interaction of these two cultural extremities with the
individual and the environmental dimension, as espoused in this research, can be examined further in a
different cultural setting.
Originality/value – This study has extended the work of Ammons (2013) and added external inﬂuencers
as a dimension to the individual preferences given by (Kossek 2016), and created the cluster for employees in
the Indian context. This study has demonstrated the importance of reduced technostress, and the use of
boundary management tactics (temporal and behavioral) leads to positive family-to-work spillover. It has also
emphasized the relevance of organization policies and supervisor support for better outcomes in WFH.
Keywords Work from home, Boundary-ﬁt, Work-family conﬂict, Boundary management tactics,
Positive spillover, Cluster analysis

This disruption has compelled people to work from home (WFH), and consequent to this,
many organizations have changed their human resource (HR) policies to accommodate these
unforeseen changes (Carnevale and Hatak, 2020). Work from home (WFH) has become the
new normal. Individuals are adopting technology and also learning new skills to remain
updated and communicate virtually. This has led to renewed interest in work from home
phenomenon (WFH). It is well documented that technological innovation, the rise of dual-
income families and globalization have been the drivers of the integration of work and
nonwork domains (Rothbard and Ollier-Malaterre, 2016). There is growing interest and
research in the dynamics of integration of work and nonwork settings and how it affects
employee well-being and their health, but the ﬁnding in many of the studies are inconclusive
(Allen et al., 2014; Rothbard and Ollier-Malaterre, 2016).
Pre COVID-19, WFH was in infancy in India, and the general populace was not very
warm to the concept of WFH; and also, companies were not very receptive to WFH as a
workplace arrangement option. COVID-19 has compelled Indian companies to use WFH
as a workplace arrangement in a very crude way to tide over the COVID-induced disruption.
WFH has moved from the fringes to become a mainstream workplace arrangement, and it
seems it has gained permanency. Looking at the new normal, which includes “future of work”
and “role of ofﬁce” leads to concerns about well-being and mental health (Guest et al., 2021).
There is a lot of learning for managers and organizations to create the right support during this
pandemic (Kirchner et al., 2021). The current study provides insight and a direction to the
practitioners and organizations about the importance of organizational support to employees
who are performing their tasks in the WFH kind of workplace arrangement. The research
study also gives a direction that using and generalizing, one size ﬁts all, approach in managing
the new WFH workplace arrangement would not yield optimum results. A speciﬁc strategy for
each cluster and a calibrated approach for each cluster would be more desirable.
Although work to nonwork integration can bring positive spillover in the individuals
leading to higher enrichment (Powell and Greehaus, 2010), work to nonwork integration can
also lead to negative spillover in the individuals leading to more conﬂict (Hecht and Allen,
2009; Kossek et al., 2012; Powell and Greenhaus, 2010; Wepfer et al., 2018). Teleworking can
also lead to higher work-family conﬂict (WFC) due to overlapping roles in the work and
family domain (Madsen, 2003). Apart from the contradictions in the studies concerning the
beneﬁt and the maladies of the abovementioned integration, there is a limited understanding
of how technology and its usage outside working hours are affecting work and nonwork
outcomes (Boswell and Olson-Buchanan, 2007). The traditional work and home environment
are divided by temporal, physical and psychological boundaries. Working from home has
changed (blurred) the previously set boundaries like physical, psychological and temporal
between work and family domain (Greer and Payne, 2014). WFH has blurred these
boundaries and made them porous, and in the absence of proper boundary management
tactics, we have instances of WFC. Given the scale of adoption of WFH and the ensuing WFC,
there is evidence of the sparse attention given to how work and nonwork boundaries are
managed (Bulger et al., 2007) and how they affect the major work-life outcomes (Kossek et al.,
2006).
A lot of work has been done in the work-life balance domain, and to reduce the WFC,
individuals may try to reach some level of work-family balance (Clark, 2000; Greenhaus
et al., 2003). Organizations are looking for ways to provide a workplace where employees
can achieve work-life balance, and many companies have introduced “family-friendly
practices” (FFP) to reduce the WFC (Lockwood, 2003). Often the beneﬁt obtained from WFH
is more stressed, like increased ﬂexibility leading to better manage the demand for work and
family at the same time (Gajendran and Harrison, 2007). It is considered that WFH brings

particular clusters with more positive family-to-work spillover.
This study has two important contributions to make. First, it investigates both

given by Ammons (2013) to divide individuals into various clusters, and second, it compares

2. Literature review
2.1 The theoretical perspective of boundary management
When looking at the boundary management related to work and nonwork, two important
theories with different origins are studied (Allen et al., 2014). These are boundary theory
(Ashforth et al., 2000; Allen et al., 2014; Nippert Eng, 1996), which focuses on the work-life
domain, and border theory (Clark, 2000), which focuses on the work and family domain. As
per these theories, individuals are forced to set boundaries to balance their work and family
roles. Some create a boundary to segment the roles, whereas others create weaker
boundaries to integrate the roles.
The boundary theory and border theory both investigate how individuals manage to set
boundaries between work and their families. Although boundary theory takes up the
sociological and cognitive perspective to understand individual learning about setting
boundaries and its implications on social life. It dwells into the interaction between work
and family and how people put meaning to these two aspects in their life (Nippert-Eng, 1996)
and how they can change their roles when moving from one domain to another (Ashforth
et al., 2000; Desrochers and Sargent, 2004) the border theory focusses on the transition from
one aspect to other and managing the border between them (Clark, 2000).
Most researchers have used a variable level approach by taking independent variables
(choice for integration/segmentation) and outcome variables (WFC) as per (Rothbard
et al., 2005). Others have used a continuum to describe various characteristics of
individuals using a mix of approaches or remain in extreme ends of the continuum
(Kossek and Lautsch, 2008; Bulger et al., 2007; Kossek et al., 2012). They have also
suggested the directionality to these concepts as to if the individual is integrating or
segment from work to family or family to work (Kosekk and Lautsch, 2008; Ashforth
et al., 2000; Kossek et al., 2012). Boundary scholars (Ashforth et al., 2000; Kossek et al.,
2005; Nippert-Eng, 1996; Kreiner, 2006; Kossek et al., 1999) have also differentiated
between desired and actual boundaries. “Enacted boundaries” are the actual demarcation
individual makes, whereas “boundary preferences” are the boundary they desire to have.
As per (Kossek et al., 2005; Kossek and Lautsch, 2008) the preferred boundary can be a
part of the enactment boundary.
Another aspect studied is the role of ﬂexibility and permeability affecting the
boundaries between the two domains (Asforth et al., 2000; Clark, 2000). Flexibility is the
expansion and contraction of the boundary in time and space as the demand varies for
one domain over the other (Clark, 2000). The permeability of the boundary is the extent of
other domain elements entering into the phenomenon (Clark, 2000). The boundary with a
high level of ﬂexibility and permeability leads to higher integration of roles between
work and nonwork domains. The three borders that work between work and family
domains are temporal, physical and psychological (Clark, 2000). Preference for
segmentation/integration also leads to the use of work-related technology in nonwork
time (Park et al., 2011; Piszczek, 2017).

2.2 Person-centered approach
A person-centered approach can be applied to boundary management, which can be used to
identify different proﬁles having various styles of boundary management as a part of
distinct psychological behaviors pattern. The person-centered approach can divide
individuals into distinct clusters but different clusters differing from other clusters (Sinclair
et al., 2005). This approach considers boundary management as a holistic process having
multiple aspects existing together.
Kossek and Lautsch (2008) divided the individuals into high control (fusion lovers,
quality timers and work or family ﬁsters), showing high WFC and psychological distress
than individuals with low BC (job warriors, captives and reactors). Kossek et al. (2012)
identiﬁed that the three characteristics of boundary management are cross-role
interruptions behaviors, role identity salience and BC. Cross-role interruption behaviors are
the extent to which an individual allows interruptions from one role to another. Role identity
centrality shows the relative value individual places on his or her different identities.
Perceived BC is the amount of control one perceives to have over his or her boundary
environment. Combining all these three factors Kossek et al. (2012) gave six possible styles:
work warriors, family guardians, overwhelmed reactors, dividers, fusion lovers and
nonwork eclectics.
Bulger et al. (2007) created four clusters using boundary strengths of ﬂexibility and
permeability. Although Cluster 1 had high boundary strength and preferred high
integration, Cluster 2 refereed preferred integration over-segmentation but did not have a
strong urge to do so. Cluster 3 was neutral on all the boundary strength, but a low score for
work ﬂexibility and Cluster 4 was high on work ﬂexibility and permeability but low on
family ﬂexibility and permeability
Kossek (2016) used BC, work-non work interruption behavior, career-family identity
centralities, needing time for self and technological dependence to create four major clusters
such as integrators, cyclers, separators and role ﬁsters. Further strategies for self-
management for personal BC in terms of physical, mental and social were suggested.
Strategies for leaders and organizations to manage and support boundary styles were listed.
2.3 Boundary-ﬁt perspective
Ammons (2008) came up with a unique perspective and suggested that boundary
preferences and enactment are different but interrelated, and their relation to boundary-ﬁt
leads to various outcomes like WFC and work-life balance. This approach adopts a person-
environment ﬁt perspective and investigates the alignment of individual boundary
preferences and boundaries provided by the external environment or workplace. In the
person-environment approach, when there is the alignment of environmental conditions like
workplace policies and practices with individual boundary preferences, it results in
“boundary congruence” (Kreiner, 2009), which results in reduced conﬂict among work-
family domain (Chen et al., 2009) and higher satisfaction and commitment for the job
(Rothbard et al., 2005).
Allen et al. (2014) introduced boundary management styles using integration and
segmentation continuum and distinguished between boundary preferences and enactment.
Boundary preferences refer to the desired level of integration and segmentation (Ammons,
2013; Kreiner, 2006), whereas boundary enactment refers to the individual actual degree of
integration and segmentation, which he or she achieves to fulﬁll his work and nonwork roles
(Allen et al., 2014). As per Allen et al. (2014), boundary management preferences and
enactment for integration and segmentation are related to physical, psychological and
behavioral aspects of the boundary between work and nonwork domains.

studies have focused on the boundary and above outcomes, the level of ﬁt between actual
and desired boundaries may provide a better understanding.
Ammons (2013), through a qualitative approach, studied the employees working in the
environment, which facilitated ﬂexibility in the context of the time (when) and place (where)
they work and observed four dominant styles. The ﬁrst style, called “protecting family” kept
work away from family, and the second was called “above and beyond” and showed absence
to little integration of family into the work domain. The second category had two subgroups,
“eager above beyonders” who were completely engrossed in work and enjoyed it, whereas
“reluctant above and beyonders” were not happy about integrating. The third category was
called “enhancing family” as they aligned family responsibility into work, and the fourth

Boundary management styles can be divided into various groups depending upon the
following two factors, namely, individual preferences and environmental inﬂuencers. The
individual preferences talk about the factors chosen at the individual level such as BC, work
and family identity and technostress, whereas environmental factors are outside related to
the job, supervisor and organizational policies.
2.3.1 Individual preferences
2.3.1.1 Boundary control. BC differs from identities as they focus on understanding of
managing and controlling boundaries and do not investigate any personality traits. As
per Clark (2000), ﬂexibility is the degree to which people expand or contract the level of
the boundary they have created. Karasek (1979) suggested job strain model can be used
to apply to WFH as stress is created when an individual is not able to manage the
demand of the environment when freedom to control job/job demand is not granted to
him/her. The individuals having a high level of perceived BC will have a belief that they
may manage time, frequency and direction of the crossing of boundaries to better ﬁt their
identities to the role, whereas individuals showing low control may feel that they cannot
manage the crossing of boundaries to ﬁt the identity given for each role.
2.3.1.2 Identity centrality of work and family roles. The role identity theory (Burke and
Reitzes, 1991) can be used to explain role centrality, which investigates how an individual
puts relative value to his or her identity in terms of investing time and energy in a given role.
Many researchers believe that almost all the employees perceive themselves as having high
family identity than work identity (Casper et al., 2007; Routhausen, 1999). People differ as to
how they give importance to different roles. Some people have a high family identity
wherein they are family-centric, whereas some people place greater importance on career
identity, prioritizing work-related tasks over family and are work-centric. Dual-centricity
people are those who focus on both tasks related to work and family and have an equal
identity for both job and family (Lobel and St. Clair, 1992).

work from home, providing ﬂexible work schedules (Valcour and Hunter, 2005). In their
study for IBM, Hill et al. (2003) have shown that working via ICT has provided a lot of

2.3.2 Environment inﬂuencers
2.3.2.1 Psychological job control. Kossek et al. (2006) proposed a psychological job control
scale, based on Hackman and Oldham’s (1980) job control scale, measuring job
characteristics of how individuals perceive control of their work. The study found that the
perception of an individual of having control over where, when and how work will be done
predicts conﬂict between work and family more than how these can be integrated through
organizational policies (Kossek et al., 2006).
2.3.2.2 Supervisor support. The organizations that provide support in working hours
scheduling and ﬂexibility may give authority to supervisors who decide on workload and
stress generated from work (Beehr et al., 2003). As the supervisor plays an important role in
managing the WFC, it becomes crucial for an organization to study the supervisor’s
behavior inﬂuencing these aspects of employee work (Hopkins, 2005). Supervisor support,
which is a source of social support, is related to a lower level of work-family conﬂict (Frye
and Breaugh, 2004; Lapierre and Allen, 2006; Thomas and Ganster, 1995) and a higher level
of positive spillover between work and family (Thompson and Prottas, 2006). Supervisor
support also leads to higher job satisfaction and is negatively related to turnover intentions
(Thomas and Ganster, 1995; Thompson and Prottas, 2006).
Family supportive supervisor behavior (FSSB) is deﬁned as the behavior shown by the
supervisor which supports family and includes emotional support, role modeling,
instrumental support and creative work-family management (Kossek et al., 2007). FSSB has
been deﬁned as where supervisors empathize with employees for having a work-family
balance (Thomas and Ganster, 1995).

perspective considers how the environment satisﬁes personal needs, values and preferences
(Kristof, 1996). As the workplace environment provides the desired supplies, the ﬁt is
achieved as contrary to when the preferred supplies are provided in a small amount or large
amount, leading to the mismatch. As per P-E ﬁt theory, the match will lead to a reduction in

family identity, work identity and technology adoption) and environmental factors (job

analysis technique using all the variables:

H1b. Employees can be divided into different boundary management proﬁles based on
environmental factors (job control, supervisor support and organizational

2.4 Work to family outcomes
2.4.1 Boundary management tactics. Kreiner et al. (2009) have identiﬁed four categories of
tactics used by individuals for segmentation and integration. These four tactics are
behavioral, temporal, physical and communication. Behavioral tactics use other people’s
support, technology usage, giving priority to the task and allowing permeability. Temporal
tactics involve having control of time by blocking speciﬁc times and removing oneself from
work/home tasks for some duration of time. Physical tactics use creating boundaries
physically, manipulating physical space and managing physical artifacts. Although
communication tactics use setting expectations by informing family members and
confronting boundary violators. Sturges (2012) has further suggested techniques and
activities which she called “crafting behaviors” as they are initiated by self and proactive
behaviors. Three crafting behavior identiﬁed are physical, cognitive and relational. Physical
crafting includes temporal, locational, choosing a convenient job and reducing the time for
traveling. Cognitive crafting consists of deﬁning work-life balance, deciding priorities for
work and making choices to compromise. Although relational crafting included handling
relationships in work, managing nonwork relationships. Carlson et al. (2019) found that job-
focused and supervisor focused impression management (IM) behavior affects family-to-
work-conﬂict and self-focused, coworker-focused and supervisor-focused IM behavior
affects family-to-work enrichment.

2.4.3 Family-work positive spillover. Edwards and Rothbard (2000) have categorized
positive spillover into four types and explained the process for the occurrence of this
spillover. These four types of spillover are effect, value, skills and behavior. Each type of
spillover occurs from work to family and family to work. Work-family positive spillover can
be deﬁned “as the transfer of positively valence effect, skill, behavior and values from the
originating domain to the receiving domain and thus providing beneﬁt to the receiving
domain” (Edwards and Rothbard, 2000).
Yet another deﬁnition used is work-family facilitation, which describes the beneﬁt of
taking part in both the work and family (Wayne et al., 2004, 2007). Work-family facilitation
is deﬁned as “the extent to which an individual’s engagement in one domain of life (e.g. work
or family) yields developmental, affective, capital or efﬁciency gains that result in enhanced
functioning in another life domain (e.g. family or work)” (Wayne et al., 2004). The basic
difference is that in positive spillover, there is the transfer of personal gains like skills,
behavior and values from one aspect of life to another and transferring the beneﬁt to the
other aspect, whereas work-family facilitation does not only occur through personal gains
but also capital gains like money, employment beneﬁts and social contacts.
Greenhaus and Powell (2006) came up with the concept of enrichment, which occurs
when there is the transfer of resources generated in one role like family to another role like
work and that improves the quality of life. Hence, the construct positive spillover and even
work-family facilitation can be put under the umbrella of work-family enrichment. Many
studies have shown that positive spillover in work and family leads to greater satisfaction in
job as well as family (Crouter,1984; Edwards and Rothbard,2000; Grzywacz et al., 2002).
Carlson et al. (2019) showed family-to-work enrichment is affected by supervisor-focused,
coworker-focused and self-focused IM.

3. Research methodology
3.1 Data and sample
The data were collected from people working in the COVID-19 period and working from
home in Mumbai (India). A total of 186 data was collected. Demographic details concerning
age, gender, number of dependents, number of rooms in the house and income were
captured. It was seen that there were 53% male and 47% female and average they have
worked for 30 h in a day. More than 80% lived in a house with only two bedrooms.
3.2 Measures
Individual preferences were captured by BC, which was measured through three items
adapted from Kossek (2012) scale. Family identity and work identity had two items each and
were measured by Kossek (2012). Technology adoption/stress was measured by two items
each for techno overload, invasion, complexity and privacy adapted from Nimrod’s (2018)
study.
Environmental preferences were measured by psychological Job control adapted from
the (Kossek, 2006) scale. Supervisor support (Hammer et al., 2011) with four items for
emotional support and three items for instrumental support. Organizational support
(segmentation supplies) had four items adapted from (Kreiner, 2006).
WFC items were adapted from Carlson et al. (2000) scale having three items each for time-
based and strain-based work-family interference. Boundary management tactics items
included three items each for temporal, physical, behavioral and communicative and
adapted from Carlson et al. (2015) study. Family-to-work positive spillover included four
items for affective, four items for behavioral and three items for value-based based on
Hanson et al. (2006).

3.3 Method
Scale reliability for each item was tested, and it was seen that Cronbach’s alpha for all the scale
items was more than 0.08. Cluster analysis was conducted to segregate individuals working
from home in various categories based on their responses to the questionnaire. Cluster analysis
helps in identifying and dividing respondents based on similar characteristics. This can be very
helpful in the context of WFH as it provides an idea about the unique characteristics of each
group. K-mean cluster analysis using statistical package for social sciences (SPSS) was used to
explore different clusters based on a pattern of characteristics unique to that cluster but
differing signiﬁcantly from other clusters. Previous research has shown that cluster analysis is
used in WFH studies and can provide very signiﬁcant inputs. K-mean clustering is ideal when
the number of clusters can be speciﬁed in the beginning. From the study of previous research, it
was clear that a maximum six cluster solution can be achieved i.e. Kossek et al. (2012) have
suggested six clusters for different individuals as per the boundary management approach.
The K-means cluster analysis was run using six, ﬁve and four clusters and concluded that the
four clusters solution was most ideal for explaining our theory considering two distinct aspects
like individual preferences and environment inﬂuencers. All the variables used also
signiﬁcantly contributed to the cluster formation.
Further analysis of variance (ANOVA) test was conducted to see how these clusters
differ across three chosen outcomes, namely, WFC, boundary management tactics used and
positive family work spillover felt. The post hoc test also provided which clusters differ
from others on these outcomes.
4. Results and ﬁndings
As shown in Figure 2, four distinct clusters have emerged from the K-mean cluster
analysis done in SPSS. The four clusters have clear cluster centers. They can be deﬁned
based on individual preferences (BC, work identity, family identity and technostress) and
environmental inﬂuencers (job control. supervisor support and organization support). All
the variables for individual preferences and environmental inﬂuencers are signiﬁcant in
deﬁning clusters as they have high F-values, which are all signiﬁcant, as shown in Table 2.
Individual preferences i.e. BC (47.00), work identity (11.84), family identity (43.10) and
technostress (8.29), showed p values (0.00) as signiﬁcant and environmental preferences
i.e. job control (77.98), supervisor support (16.95) and organization support (22.88)
showed p values (0.00) as signiﬁcant. This led us to accept both hypotheses H1a and H1b
and further name each cluster as per the unique characteristics they showcased
concerning chosen variables.

5. Discussions
COVID-19 situation has led many to work from home for an exceptionally long time in
India, and organizations have also understood the need to support employees working
from home in this era of new normal. In India, work from home is still not so popular,
and there is a need for an organization to understand how they must change their work
culture and mindset concerning WFH. One way of doing this is to design generalized
policies and HR practices for all the employees working from home, and the second
way would be to segregate employees based on various clusters and identify the
characteristic of each cluster. Based on this cluster group and characteristics, devise
the right policies and HR practices for each cluster to integrate them into a new work
culture. The current research is focusing on bridging the gap between the theory and
practice and emphasizing the designing of very speciﬁc strategies as per cluster
characteristics.
The advantages of the latter would be phenomenal as it will allow the organization to
focus on each group of a particular cluster and then understand their characteristics and
design strategies as per their need. It would also lead to better results as strategies are
targeted very speciﬁcally for each cluster in terms of their developmental area.
5.1 Theoretical implications
The study has three important contributions to make. First, it has applied the concept as
suggested by Ammons (2013) for a boundary-ﬁt perspective, which states that when looking
at the boundary management studies, it is imperative to consider how individuals make
efforts to align the individual preference with the external environment. The study has
investigated the employees and divided them into various clusters as suggested by Kossek
et al. (2012) and Kossek (2016) work by taking individual preferences (BC, work identity,
family identity and technostress) and environmental inﬂuencers (job control, supervisor
support and organization policies). There is no other study that has approached the
boundary management proﬁles from both internal and external factors and looking at
holistically the proﬁles.

6. Managerial implication
Clusters 3 and 4 are low on technostress, which is helping them to have a more positive
family-to-work spillover as they do not feel technology is overloading or complex to handle
and do not see that technology is invading and affecting their privacy. Organizations can
also assist employees in handling technology-related stress, as this will reduce their anxiety
and help in managing their work more efﬁciently from home. Some studies have focused on
differences in individual behaviors when feeling technostress like young workers feel more
technostress in terms of overload and invasion (Ayyagari et al., 2011; Tarafdar et al., 2007),
whereas senior workers are more impacted by inclusion, privacy and complexity (Nimod,
2017). This leads us to the fact that there is a need to integrate older workers by reducing the
complexity of the technology used and creating more inclusive practices for the use of

7. Limitation and future scope
The study is speciﬁc to India, and hence, the behavior of the variables in the study in other
cultures is questionable. The culture may play an important role in deciding whether
individual preferences or environmental inﬂuencers will play an important role. It is a well-
