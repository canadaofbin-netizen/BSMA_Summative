Abstract
Information processing theory suggests the need for different types of integration mechanisms
in R&D project management depending on levels of uncertainty and equivocality. This paper
examines the use of these mechanisms and their links to project performance in a sample of 121
R&D projects in a large research laboratory. Overall, it is found that formal leadership, planning
and process specification, and to a lesser extent information technology use are related to project
performance while the positive effects of horizontal structures are apparently balanced out by their
costs. The integration mechanisms studied act on performance partly through their effect on
horizontal communications. Modest support was found for the contingency hypotheses derived
from information processing theory. It appears that managers adjusted their use of horizontal
structures, planning and process specification, and informal leadership to project uncertainty but
not to project equivocality. The positive effects of horizontal communications on performance
were found to be greatest under high project equivocality as would be predicted by information
processing arguments. Moreover, with the exception of formal leadership, the use of integration
mechanisms did not enhance performance in contexts of low uncertainty and low equivocality.

2.2. Information processing requirements and integration mechanisms
As we noted in the introduction, successful innovation requires the collaboration of
many different specialists whose work must be integrated to create a coherent whole. A
variety of different integration mechanisms can be used to achieve this. From an
information processing perspective, these mechanisms can be seen as means for
Ž
.
reducing either uncertainty or equivocality. Daft and Lengel 1986 examine the types of
communication media and structural mechanisms that favor each or these objectives.
They argue that while formal rules and regulations, information systems and special
reporting procedures are capable of handling large quantities of factual information and,
thus, of reducing high levels of uncertainty, these mechanisms will be inadequate under
conditions of high equivocality. In such situations, ‘‘rich’’ information media such as
face-to-face meetings and personal contact are required to provide opportunities for
people to share understandings, debate issues, and develop consensus on problem
Ž
.
Ž
.
definitions Daft and Lengel, 1984, 1986 . Specifically, Daft and Lengel 1986 identify
group meetings and integrator roles as structural mechanisms that can permit the
reduction of equivocality through close interpersonal contact.2 They see formal planning
systems as intermediate mechanisms that may assist in both the reduction of uncertainty
and equivocality. For example, they argue that planning begins with face-to-face
meetings to establish objectives and a general course of action, thus reducing equivocal-
ity. Subsequently, plans become a data processing and control device more closely
associated with uncertainty reduction.

3. Research hypotheses
3.1. Horizontal structures
Horizontal structures overlay traditional ‘‘functional’’ structures. They lie on a
continuum ranging from informal mechanisms such as temporary assignments and task
forces in which functional authority continues to be dominant to matrix organizations in
which dual lines of authority are permanently embedded to organizations in which
Ž
cross-functional project teams are the dominant unit and functions play a staff role Ford
.
and Randolph, 1992 . These mechanisms have long been associated with effective
Ž
.
innovation in the organization theory literature Galbraith, 1973; Mintzberg, 1979 . They
are intended to counteract the centrifugal forces created by high levels of functional
specialization among participants in innovation projects by forcing face to face contact,
Ž
mutual adjustment and lateral communication
e.g., Hitt et al., 1993; Keller, 1994;
.
Adler, 1995 . By improving the amount and quality of information exchange at the
project level, these mechanisms also enable resources to be quickly reallocated as needs
Ž
.
change over time Larson and Gobeli, 1988 . The use of cross-functional teams has been
Ž
shown in prior research to be strongly related to project performance
Zirger and
.
Maidique, 1990; Brown and Eisenhardt, 1995 . Therefore, as a working hypothesis, we
predict that the relationship between the use of horizontal structures and performance
will be positive.
Ž
These structures are not without disadvantages, however Ford and Randolph, 1992;
.
Hitt et al., 1993 . While intended to improve communications and coordination, they can
Ž
.
be costly and time-consuming Larson and Gobeli, 1988 . Dual lines of authority can
Ž
.
lead to new forms of stress Nonaka, 1990 including ambiguity about responsibilities,
and conflict between functional and project managers on issues such as project priorities,
Ž
.
schedules and resources Barker et al., 1988; Ford and Randolph, 1992; De Laat, 1994 .
Thus, these structures may not always be beneficial. Potential communication benefits
must be traded off against the costs involved.
Ž

According to Daft and Lengel 1986 , the communication benefits of mechanisms
Ž
.

equivocality. Face-to-face contact allows immediate feed-back and adjustment and can
Ž

Ž
.

Ž
.
Ž
.
project parameters are familiar and clear i.e., under low equivocality . Adler 1995 also
argues that interactive mechanisms such as teams are optimal for highly novel projects
but too costly for more routine ones. Therefore, we expect that horizontal structures will
be more frequently used and more effective in the case of projects of high equivocality
and they will be less prevalent and less effective in contexts of low equivocality. The
following three hypotheses summarize the arguments of the last three paragraphs.

3.2. Formal leadership
Formal project leadership plays a key integrative role in innovation projects both
within the project team and between the team and outsiders. As Brown and Eisenhardt
Ž
.
1995 indicate in their extensive review of the product development research literature,
powerful project leadership is strongly associated with effectiveness. In their internal
integrator role, project leaders can help motivate team members to work together to
resolve differences and work out problems, relying on their authority, expertise and
personal credibility. In their external integrator roles, formal project leaders act as a
Ž
‘‘bridge between the project team and senior management’’ Brown and Eisenhardt,
.
1995 . The greater the power accorded to formal project managers, the greater will be
Ž
their capacity to mobilize resources and create a cohesive team Ancona and Caldwell,
.
Ž
.

1993: 378
Ž
.

of a product development team often hinges on its ability to buffer certain design
activities against outside interference.’’ Leaders with authority over all aspects of the
project will be better able to manage the interface with top management as well as
internal team dynamics.
Ž
.
Formal project leaders, as ‘‘integrators’’ in Daft and Lengel’s 1986 terminology,
contribute to both uncertainty and equivocality reduction. They reduce uncertainty by
acting as a formally recognized conduit for data transmission between team members
and between the team and top management. However, their most important role is likely
to be in equivocality reduction. They are in a privileged position to interpret top
management concerns and to organize and arbitrate face-to-face discussions and negotia-
tions among team members concerning the project. Therefore, the reliance on formal
project leadership is likely to be more prevalent and more crucial for performance for
projects involving both high uncertainty and high equivocality. The following hypothe-
ses summarize our arguments here.

3.3. Planning and process specification:
Many writers have noted that careful planning and process specification can improve
project performance. Indeed, several project management techniques have been designed
based on the idea that by breaking down projects into their component parts and
projecting timelines and milestones for each element, it is possible to better coordinate
Ž
the whole Moenaert and Souder, 1990; Von Hippel, 1990; Wheelwright and Clark;
.
1992; Cooper, 1996 . It can be argued that when everyone knows exactly what they
have to accomplish and when, the need for ongoing mutual adjustment is lessened,
improving communication and performance. Thus, planning and process specification is
a potentially powerful integration mechanism and several empirical studies have con-
Ž
firmed its role in improving project performance
Cooper and Kleinschmidt, 1987;
.
Zirger and Maidique, 1990 . On the other hand, another current of literature is less
Ž
.
enthusiastic about the benefits of planning. For example, Mintzberg 1994 suggests that
the creative process of innovation and the constraints of formal planning are to some
Ž
.
extent incompatible and Eisenhardt and Tabrizi 1995 found that planning tended to
slow down product development. This might lead us to question the role of formal
planning, especially under conditions of equivocality where radically new innovations
are being considered. Based on data concerning the design–manufacturing interface in
Ž
.
13 firms, Adler 1995 argued that planning mechanisms are optimal at moderate levels
of novelty but less appropriate for extremely novel or for routine projects.
Ž
.
Daft and Lengel 1986 suggest that ‘‘planning is a dynamic process that includes
elements of both equivocality reduction and data sharing.’’ As noted above, they view
the initial stages of the planning process as a means to reach consensus through the
exchange of rich information, with the more bureaucratic and uncertainty reducing
elements dominating later in the process. Based on this logic and encouraged by the
weight of empirical evidence, we adopt the working hypothesis suggested by Daft and
Lengel: that planning and process specification is generally beneficial, but most useful
under conditions of high uncertainty and equivocality. An alternative hypothesis consis-
Ž
.
tent with Mintzberg’s 1994 position would associate it mainly with uncertainty, while
Ž
.
Adler’s 1995 proposal would suggest a curvilinear relationship with equivocality. We
return to these issues in the conclusions.

3.4. Information technology
The use of information technology is expected to facilitate coordination during
innovation projects by increasing the speed of communication, reducing its cost,
widening the scope of the information network and allowing access to communal
Ž
information Dean and Snell, 1991; Hitt et al., 1993; Adler, 1995; Fulk and DeSanctis,
.
1995 . Useful technologies in this respect may include electronic mail, voice mail, fax,
decision support systems, CADrCAM, and electronic data management. For example,
Ž
CADrCAM technologies allow easy shared access to ongoing product designs Adler,
.
1995 , while electronic communications facilitate horizontal communications across
intra-organizational and inter-organizational boundaries, lessening the need for physical
Ž
.
proximity Fulk and DeSanctis, 1995 .
Ž
.
Daft and Lengel 1986 view information technology in general as a medium highly
appropriate for uncertainty reduction, but less useful for rich communication and,
therefore, not strongly associated with equivocality reduction. This is the form of the
hypothesis that we have adopted here. It should be noted, however, that there is
considerable controversy in the literature over the role of newer technologies such as
electronic mail and voice mail. For example, while some authors have confirmed the
Ž
.
low rating of electronic mail for equivocality reduction Schmitz and Fulk, 1991 , others
Ž
have suggested that it can be used to communicate richer information Kiesler, 1986;
.
Ž
.
Rice and Gail, 1989 . Markus 1994 argues that newer communication media are not
inherently ‘‘rich’’ or ‘‘lean’’ but that different social groups can construct them so that
they are used in richer or more structured ways. For example, she showed that while
electronic mail does not, as such, appear to be open to the flexible formulation of
thoughts and immediate feedback that can be gained in face-to-face conversation, certain
organizational groups may come to adopt them for use in this way. Thus, the potential
for equivocality reduction via the use of technology may be socially constructed and
Ž
.
context-dependent see also Zack and McKenny, 1995 . Even so, these asynchronous
technologies do not seem to be inherently favorable to rich exchanges to the same extent
Ž
.
as telephone calls or personal and group meetings Hinds and Kiesler, 1995 . Therefore,
we view them here mainly as tools for uncertainty reduction.

Hypothesis 4b. Information technologies will be used to a greater extent for projects of

Hypothesis 4c. The use of information technologies will be more strongly related to

Measures of uncertainty and equivocality were constructed to reflect the dimensions

The uncertainty dimension reflects the number of information bases and the technical
and administrative complexity of the project. It combines three indicators:
Ø The logarithm of the number of project participants. The number of participants was
obtained from the senior researcher for each project by asking him or her to list their
names. For 83 projects, this indicator was checked in an interview with the Director
Ž
.
of the research unit the correlation between the two measures was rs0.57 .
Ø Assessments of the administrative complexity and technical complexity, respectively
of the project on two 7-point scales obtained from the Director of the research unit.
Ž

This indicator of uncertainty or task variety is constructed somewhat differently
Ž
.

reflect individual-level task variety rather than task variety across an entire project

.

Ž
.
1992

multiplicity and diversity of components within the project team than in measures of
Ž
non-routineness for individual members. In fact, averaging individual perceptions of
.
personal task-variety may produce little variation between projects . Technical complex-

better proxy for project-level task variety. Multiple researchers is both a reflection of
high complexity and a factor that in itself leads to diversity in bases of information.

This research aimed to contribute to a better understanding of the role of integration
Ž
mechanisms in R&D project success. The information processing perspective Galbraith,
.

coherent theoretical framework for understanding the contingency relationships affecting
the appropriateness of different mechanisms. Table 10 synthesizes the results obtained
for the full set of hypotheses. In this section, we highlight the main conclusions, discuss
certain surprising results, note the limitations of this research, and examine the implica-
tions of our study for research and practice.
6.1. Integration mechanisms and project performance
First, we confirmed that several integration mechanisms discussed in the literature
can contribute to project success. In particular, there is evidence that empowered project
leadership is critical. This supports empirical observations by Ancona and Caldwell
Ž
.
Ž

strong formal leadership is not restricted to projects of high uncertainty or high
equivocality as we predicted based on information processing theory. This means that

only important role of these leaders. We may speculate, for example, that in the case of
smaller and simpler projects, powerful leaders may contribute to success through their
capacity to attract resources to projects that might otherwise be neglected. The only
context in which formal leadership was not significantly related to performance was the
low uncertainty, high equivocality condition. This result is more difficult to explain.

6.2. Degree of support for the information processing perspectiÕe
Over and above specific observations concerning the performance effects of various
integration mechanisms, how much overall support did we find for the information
processing perspective? Hypotheses H1b–H5b reflect the ‘‘selection’’ approach to the
theory, which suggests that integration mechanisms will be used more extensively when
they are ‘‘appropriate’’ to the context. We found rather limited support for these
hypotheses. Centerlab did not tend to modulate its use of integration mechanisms as a
Ž
function of project equivocality. Moreover, its adjustments for project uncertainty for
.
planning, horizontal structures and informal leadership were not always in the predicted
directions. These results tend to suggest that R&D laboratories may have some
difficulty in adjusting their project management approaches to a wide range of different
situations. Internal rules and traditions may tend to lead to some uniformity. When
adjustments are made, they may be more readily justified by tangible factors such as the
Ž
complexity of the project and the number of different specialties involved
factors
.
related to project uncertainty rather than by less tangible considerations such as the
Ž
.
novelty of the product or process being developed project equivocality . One hypothesis
worthy of future research is that while task uncertainty is treated as an organizational
problem, equivocality is more of an individual problem which will be handled by
individual-level rather than organizational-level strategies.

the two mechanisms that show the strongest effects here, despite the fact that their use
did not emerge as significantly affected by context in the ‘‘selection’’ approach analysis.

approaches to contingency theory. When the firm adjusts its approach to the context,
performance effects may be undetectable because unpropitious cases are eliminated
beforehand. When it does not do so but should, significant performance effects are more
likely to emerge. This argument would lead one to expect that the ‘‘selection’’ approach

6.3. Strengths and limitations of the research
Research on technological innovation has often been criticized for its largely atheoret-
Ž
.
ical nature e.g., Brown and Eisenhardt, 1995 . The information processing perspective
Ž
.
provides one approach to filling this theoretical gap Gales et al., 1992 . A number of
recent studies have adopted this perspective for the study of R&D project success. For
Ž
.
Ž
.
example, Gales et al. 1992 and Keller 1994 focused on the effects of task variability
and analyzability on the amounts of information processed while Gales and Mansour-Cole
Ž
.
1995 examined the relationship between client involvement, project uncertainty and
performance. In this study, we attempted to take a further step forward by examining the
role of integration mechanisms in relation to uncertainty and equivocality and the
Ž
.
mediating effect of horizontal communication Daft and Lengel, 1986 .
Like any other study, ours is not without limitations. Our focus on a single research
laboratory allowed the control of certain contextual variables and enabled us to gather
data on a fairly large sample of projects through personal interviews and questionnaires
from several sources. The use of multiple respondents for each project is relatively
unusual in this type of research and enhances the quality of the data base. However, a

(
)
H. Sicotte, A. LangleyrJ. Eng. Technol. Manage. 17 2000 1–37
31
single site does limit the generalizability of the research and may have restricted the
Ž
.
range of certain variables e.g., horizontal structures limiting our capacity to detect
significant relationships. Future work should also use measures of performance based on
independent client assessments. Our use of researcher assessments of performance may
raise a concern about common method bias. To mitigate this, we made an effort to
Ž
.
measure this variable using factually formulated questions
see Appendix . Another
source of bias could arise from the pooling of data which included more than one project
rating from each respondent. This concern is somewhat mitigated by the process of
aggregation which took responses from people involved in evaluating different project
pairs. Finally, as a cross-sectional field study, this research provides evidence of
association, but not causality.
6.4. Implications and directions for research and practice
This study suggests several directions for future work, several of which have been
mentioned above. First, there is a need to verify these results in other contexts using
similar and different methodologies. For example, client assessments of performance
would be useful, while longitudinal methods would help establish causal linkages. In
addition, there is a need to test richer explanatory models that incorporate networks of
variables. For example, we have seen that horizontal structures may well have counter-
vailing effects: better communication on the one hand, but higher cost on the other,
Ž
producing an ambiguous effect on overall performance Ford and Randolph, 1992; Hitt
.
et al., 1993 . These relationships could be modeled and tested explicitly in different
types of contexts using intervening variables and structural equations modeling.
Ž
.
Curvilinear models relating particular integration mechanisms
e.g., planning
to
Ž
information processing context are also theoretically defensible e.g., Boynton et al.,
.
1993; Adler, 1995 and should be tested further. Certain integration mechanisms studied
also warrant finer definition. For example, formal and informal leaders may have a
variety of different styles that could be more or less appropriate in different contexts.
Finally, the role of uncertainty and equivocality as significant moderating variables also
underlines the need for research on innovation to take these conditions into account
when selecting samples for study. The pooling of diverse sets of projects into a single
Ž
.
sample potentially contributes to instability in results Downs and Mohr, 1976 .
Our research also offers lessons for managers. First, we note that smaller and simpler
projects performed no better than larger and more complex ones despite the head-start
they had in principle. Managers should beware of neglecting simpler projects that
nevertheless make up a large proportion of the R&D budget. Our data suggest that
formal leadership appears to be as important in this context as in others, though probably
for different reasons. Here its effects are likely to be related to the capacity to mobilize
resources and attention to get the job done. For more uncertain and equivocal projects,
the integrative role will be more significant.
Secondly, we note a tendency to adjust project management practices to uncertainty
but not to equivocality. This is perhaps partly because uncertainty is more obvious and
more easily detectable. Managers need to ensure that the special needs of highly
innovative projects are recognized. Another reason for this may be that equivocality is

(
)
H. Sicotte, A. LangleyrJ. Eng. Technol. Manage. 17 2000 1–37
32
simply less manageable by formal mechanisms alone. Our results suggest that while
formal planning is not without merit in these circumstances, success may be more
dependent on individual behavior and the resulting spontaneous chemistry that develops
Ž
.
between project participants see also Dougherty and Hardy, 1996 . To handle this, ways
need to be found to stimulate effective horizontal communications beyond what can be
achieved simply with formal structures, procedures and technologies. This might involve
the selection of people to take part in projects taking into account not only their
technical prowess, but also their capacity to collaborate, their compatibility with other
project participants and their informal leadership potential. This is a task that requires
subtlety and foresight, or possibly some form of mutual self-selection. In conclusion, we
suspect that softer and more unpredictable management skills need to be combined with
formal integration mechanisms to optimize the innovative capability of R&D project
groups.
