Abstract
Purpose – The purpose of this paper is to investigate how frontline service employees’ (FSEs) perceptions of
corporate social responsibility (CSR) can enhance meaningful work perceptions as well as help alleviate FSEs’
perceptions of verbal dysfunctional customer behavior.
Design/methodology/approach – The conceptual model is empirically examined through a survey of
306 FSEs of a large insurance company in South Korea and tested via structural equation modeling.
Findings – The results indicate that FSEs’ perceptions of CSR are negatively related to their perceptions of
verbal dysfunctional customer conduct, which in turn is shown to be directly linked to emotional exhaustion.
FSEs’ CSR perceptions strengthen their view that they are performing meaningful work (i.e. perceived task
significance), which in turn strengthens their job satisfaction.
Practical implications – CSR has a preventive effect on workplace stress reduction, as FSE perceptions of
CSR may help them cope with the emotional fatigue of dealing with dysfunctional customer behavior.
CSR also provides a needs fulfillment effect, as FSEs’ perceptions of CSR foster perceived task significance
and helps reduce their emotional exhaustion from work.
Originality/value – This is the first study to examine the potential impact of CSR within the context of
FSEs’ boundary spanning emotional labor.
Keywords Corporate social responsibility, Emotional exhaustion, Dysfunctional customer behavior
Paper type Research paper
Introduction
In 1983, Hochschild first introduced the concept of emotional labor which requires frontline
service employees (FSEs) to manage their own emotions while producing organizationally
desired emotions in order to deliver quality service. FSEs often interact with demanding,
unfriendly and sometimes even abrasive customers, yet they are expected to remain
courteous and professional throughout each service encounter (Geng et al., 2018;
McColl-Kennedy et al., 2009). As boundary spanners, FSEs serve as bridges between the
service firm and its customers, which can often contribute to negative emotions
and emotional exhaustion, which in turn negatively impacts their job performance
(Dallimore et al., 2007; Singh et al., 1994). To overcome verbal dysfunctional customer
behaviors, FSEs are encouraged to employ practical coping tactics to alleviate the
stress they experience when customers behave in deviant fashions (Echeverri et al., 2012;
Reynolds and Harris, 2006). Furthermore, FSEs expect that their organizations will be fair
and provide proper managerial interventions when dealing with verbally dysfunctional
customer behavior (Gong et al., 2014).

In this study, we expand the relevant service marketing literature by investigating the
potential impact of corporate social responsibility (CSR) as a mechanism to improve
frontline customer service work and help alleviate the negative effects of verbal
dysfunctional customer behavior perceptions of FSEs. CSR has emerged as one of the most
important imperatives for contemporary business practices. A survey by Cone (2016) found
that 74 percent of workers in the USA agreed that their job is more fulfilling when they are
provided opportunities to make a positive impact on social and environmental issues. CSR is
broadly defined as a company’s activities and status relative to its societal obligations
(Brown and Dacin, 1997). Delivered in an array of formats, CSR initiatives typically focus on
corporate brands and their societal relationships with external constituencies, including
customers, not-for-profit organizations and local communities (Ellen et al., 2006). While it is
well established in the literature that CSR can effectively enhance job performance of
frontline employees (e.g. Korschun et al., 2014; Larson et al., 2008), far less is known about
the potential value of CSR in relationship to workers who routinely expend emotional labor
in their roles as FSEs. In particular, there is a lack of research on how CSR perceptions held
by employees may contribute to reducing the emotional duress associated when managing
verbal dysfunctional customer behaviors in a frontline services setting.
In an effort to fill this void, we examine how the presence of strong CSR perceptions held
by FSEs weaken the extent to which they experience emotional exhaustion suffered from
dealing from verbal dysfunctional customer conduct. Moreover, we investigate how CSR
perceptions held by FSEs may strengthen their view that they are performing meaningful
service (i.e. perceived task significance) and its consequential positive impact on
strengthening job satisfaction. We begin with a review of relevant literature to gain a deeper
understanding of how FSEs’ CSR perceptions may help shape their perceptions of verbal
dysfunctional customer behavior, emotional exhaustion and task significance. This is
followed by a description of the research methodology we used to empirically test the
hypothesized relationships of our conceptual model. Finally, we discuss the key findings
and their managerial implications, and offer future research opportunities to build on this
research study.
Conceptual framework and hypotheses development
The proposed conceptual model is depicted in Figure 1. We anticipate that FSEs’ strong CSR
perceptions of their service firm employer are negatively related to their verbal
dysfunctional customer behavior perceptions. FSEs’ perceived verbal dysfunctional
customer behavior is also expected to mediate the linkage between FSEs’ CSR perceptions
and emotional exhaustion. Furthermore, we anticipate that FSEs’ strong CSR perceptions
directly affect the positive task significance of their work, which sequentially leads to job
satisfaction. We also expect perceived task significance will have a negative relationship
with emotional exhaustion. Next, we develop the constructs of our model and present
supporting literature for their respective posited relationships.
FSEs’ perceptions of CSR and verbal dysfunctional customer behavior
Dysfunctional customer behavior is analogous to problem customers (Bitner et al., 1994),
consumer misbehavior (Fullerton and Punj, 2004), deviant customer behavior (Moschis and
Cox, 1989) and customer aggression (Ben-Zur and Yagil, 2005). A common thread among all
these terms is the notion that some customers violate generally accepted standards of
conduct and display behavior which is commonly viewed as disreputable by organizations,
employees and other customers (Harris and Reynolds, 2003). In this study, we consider the
perceptions of employees who are at the frontline, interacting with customers who violate
norms through various verbal dysfunctional behaviors, such as unwarranted complaining

and Miao, 2016; Van Jaarsveld et al., 2010).
Our focus is on FSEs’ perceptions of their company’s externally focused CSR activities
and the important role that CSR can play in reducing their perceptions of verbal
dysfunctional customer behavior. FSEs’ perceptions of verbal dysfunctional customer
behavior describe their views about various forms of voiced customer sentiments which are
regarded to violate generally accepted norms with such attitudes generally viewed to be
overly demanding and sometimes dishonest (McColl-Kennedy et al., 2009). To the best of our
knowledge, the negative relationship between FSEs’ CSR perceptions and their verbal
dysfunctional customer behavior perceptions has not been examined prior to this study.
Previous studies have focused on employees’ perceptions to understand the
psychological mechanisms that link CSR activities and desirable individual employee
characteristics, including job satisfaction, employee commitment and outstanding work
performance (e.g. De Roeck and Maon, 2016; Korschun et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2013).
Employee perceptions of CSR, depending on the type of beneficiaries (De Roeck and Maon,
2016), can be broadly categorized as evaluations of internal initiatives (e.g. policies aimed
at improving working conditions, education programs, etc.) and external initiatives
(e.g. involvement with charitable causes, funding nonprofit organizations, providing
support for the community, etc.).
From a theoretical perspective, Ashforth and Humphrey (1993) linked the concept of
emotional labor to social identity theory. Social identity theory helps explain people’s
conduct within groups (Hogg and Abrams, 1988). Social identity theory illuminates how
shared organizational activities can be used to signal group identity and express group
belonging (Hogg and Abrams, 1988; Tajfel and Turner, 1986). High social identity positively
contributes to employees’ self-perceived status and fosters their sense of self in society
(Gond et al., 2010). Employees generally identify with a company when they believe that the
company possesses admirable traits that reflect their self-concept (Kim et al., 2010).
Employees feel better about themselves when they perceive that they are part of an
organization that practices good corporate citizenship (Bhattacharya et al., 2008). Hence,
employees who identify strongly with their employer view the company’s traits and
activities as their own (Bhattacharya and Sen, 2003).

FSEs’ dysfunctional customer behavior perceptions and emotional exhaustion
Emotional labor is related to employee burnout and specifically to emotional exhaustion
(Abraham, 1998; Brotheridge and Lee, 2002). Emotional exhaustion refers to a state of
deleted energy caused by excessive emotional demands (Saxton et al., 1991). Previous
research has explored the relationship between dysfunctional customer behavior and FSEs’
boundary spanning emotional exhaustion (Karatepe et al., 2009a, b; Singh et al., 1994;
Van Jaarsveld et al., 2010). FSEs often develop their own coping strategies in response to
overly demanding and rude customers (Echeverri et al., 2012; Rupp et al., 2006; Yagil and
Schultz, 2017). Even then, FSEs may still struggle to successfully cope with dysfunctional
customer behavior. Van Jaarsveld et al. (2010) found that customer incivility toward
employees is linked to emotional exhaustion resulting from their job demands. Furthermore,
dealing with verbally dysfunctional customers leads to a heightened sense of loss of control
by employees when confronted by customers’ incivility, voiced aggression and other
disruptive behaviors (Hockey, 1993).
FSEs’ emotional exhaustion can be directly affected by their perceptions of verbal
dysfunctional customer behavior (Singh et al., 1994). Huang and Miao (2016) documented the
emotional plight of hotel and restaurant workers when they were expected to meet
unreasonable demands from deviant customers, contributing to role conflict. FSEs are more
susceptible to work overload because they spending additional time working to resolve
dysfunctional customer demands with their supervisors and other coworkers (Singh et al., 1994).
Indeed, in a hotel setting, Karatepe et al. (2009b) found that FSEs’ perceptions of customer’s
aggression were directly linked to emotional exhaustion. In a mobile phone service provider
study, Baranik et al. (2017) demonstrated that FSEs’ perceptions of customer mistreatment were
related to emotional exhaustion. Hence the following hypothesis is proposed:
H2. FSE’s perceived verbal dysfunctional customer behavior is positively related to their
emotional exhaustion.

Researchers have demonstrated how CSR can be instrumental in fulfilling psychological needs
of employees (e.g. Bauman and Skitka, 2012; Bhattacharya et al., 2008; Gong et al., 2014;

Rosso et al., 2010; Rupp et al., 2006). Employees may be able to feel a greater sense of meaningful
life existence at work when they believe that their employer contributes to improvements in
other people’s lives through CSR activities (Bauman and Skitka, 2012). Raub and Blunschi
(2014) demonstrate how perceived task significance plays a mediating role between CSR and job
satisfaction among FSEs in a hotel chain setting. Task significance refers to the judgments that
one’s job influences the lives or work of others (Morgeson and Humphrey, 2006). Studies on job
design show that employees judge their jobs highly in task significance when they experience
their work as more meaningful (Morgeson and Humphrey, 2006; Raub and Blunschi, 2014).
Based on the highlighted literature, we anticipate that FSEs’ perceived CSR of their employer
will have a direct and positive effect on their perceptions of task significance:
H3. FSEs’ CSR perceptions are positively related to their perceived task significance.
Perceived task significance and emotional exhaustion
Scholars have argued that to the extent that employees are able to satisfy their
psychological needs (e.g. meaningful work) through their company’s CSR activities,
employees may experience reduced emotional exhaustion on the job (Bauman and Skitka,
2012; Raub and Blunschi, 2014). Thus, we anticipate that CSR has an impact on FSEs’
emotional exhaustion through perceived task significance, as a need fulfillment effect.
Intrinsically motivated employees are able to cope psychologically with stressful stimuli
(Low et al., 2001). Intrinsic motivation is closely related with basic need satisfaction,
which is the motivational process capturing employees’ psychological energy (Van den
Broeck et al., 2008).
Previous studies demonstrate that work motivation allows employees to cope
with emotional exhaustion by biasing perceptions of stressful stimuli (Karatepe and
Tekinkus, 2006; Low et al., 2001). Similarly, motivated employees expend additional time
and energy to complete their assigned tasks (Zhang and Bartol, 2010). FSEs who perceive
their work as meaningful are better positioned to deal with demanding customers,
and therefore less likely to suffer from emotional exhaustion (Raub and Blunschi, 2014;
Van Jaarsveld et al., 2010). Therefore, the following hypothesis is proposed:
H4. FSEs’ perceived task significance has a negative relationship with their emotional
exhaustion.
Perceived task significance and job satisfaction
The creation of customer oriented boundary-spanning behaviors (e.g. external
representation, internal influence, service delivery) depends on the level of job
satisfaction (Bettencourt and Brown, 2003). Job satisfaction refers to “the emotional
affective response to a job or specific aspects of a job” (Spector, 1985, p. 695). Job
characteristics have long been understood as antecedents of positive work outcomes
(e.g. Hackman and Oldham, 1975). Employees who perceive their work as significant and
valuable report higher levels of job satisfaction (Glavas and Kelley, 2014; Morgeson and
Humphrey, 2006; Raub and Blunschi, 2014). Furthermore, Seligman (2002) suggested that
living a meaningful life provides the most lasting form of happiness. In this sense,
employees can live a meaningful life through meaningful work and job satisfaction.
In other words, prosocial elements of work are meaningful because they bring individuals
into harmony with others (Rosso et al., 2010). In line with the examined management
literature, we anticipate that FSEs’ perceived task significance of their work will be
positively related to their job satisfaction:
H5. FSEs’ perceived task significance has a positive relationship with their job satisfaction.

Emotional exhaustion and job satisfaction
High levels of employee emotional exhaustion lead to low job performance (Hur et al., 2015).
Specifically, emotional exhaustion is known to result in numerous negative effects on
employee performance, such as job dissatisfaction and higher employee turnover (Babakus
et al., 2009; Bakker et al., 2004; Lu and Gursoy, 2016). Given that job satisfaction is an
affective-based overall evaluation of employees’ job situation and experience (Bettencourt
and Brown, 2003), emotional exhaustion is anticipated to be negatively related to job
satisfaction (Singh et al., 1994). Employees suffering from emotional exhaustion are in a
state of depleted emotional resources (Singh et al., 1994) or psychological withdrawal in the
form of reduced job satisfaction (Bettencourt and Brown, 2003). Karatepe et al. (2009b)
examined the influence of customers’ verbal aggression on hotel employees’ emotional
exhaustion, which in turn negatively impacted their job satisfaction. Hence, in the light of
extant research, it is logical to anticipate a negative relationship between emotional
exhaustion of FSEs and their job satisfaction:
H6. Emotional exhaustion of FSEs has a negative relationship on their job satisfaction.
Moderating variables
Given the scarcity of research that investigate the linkages between CSR and boundary
spanning emotional labor, this study also examines the potential impact of individual
variables on the posited relationships of this study. More specifically, previous research
suggests that gender, age, job position and years of work experience with current employer
may affect our model’s results. Valentine and Fleischman (2008) found that CSR, gender and
age were positively related to job satisfaction. In another study, members of the Millennial
generation were shown to hold stronger normative beliefs regarding ethical consumerism
compared with older adults, with women displaying more awareness and concern for
supporting CSR initiatives than men (Bucic et al., 2012). Job characteristics also may impact
the posited model. For example, higher demanding jobs often lead to emotional exhaustion
(Alarcon, 2011). Thus, managers would be expected to be associated with higher levels of
perceived task significance, emotional exhaustion and job satisfaction than their non-
manager counterparts.
Research method
To test the posited relationships of our conceptual model, we conducted a survey of FSEs
with the support of one of the largest property and casualty insurance companies in
South Korea. The survey data were provided by customer service representatives and
their supervisors who work in the participating insurance company’s claims department.
Serving individual customers with property and casualty insurance coverage, the primary
duty of the sampled customer service representatives and their supervisors was to assist
customers when filing a claim, such as serving those customers who experienced a car
accident. In many instances, these FSEs were interacting with customers under very
stressful circumstances. In our preliminary discussions with employees of the insurance
company, we learned that it was common for customers to insist on receiving greater
compensation than allowed under the terms and conditions of their insurance policies.
The data collection was done via web-based questionnaires. Included with the link to the
online survey was an email of instructions from a human resource manager of the insurance
company. The survey was sent to all eligible customer service representatives and their
supervisors and direct managers who worked in the auto insurance division of the
insurance company. The link to the survey was left open for two weeks in July 2015.
Out of 348 subjects approached, a total of 328 responses were collected. Elimination of
22 incomplete answers resulted in 306 valid responses (a valid response rate of 87.9 percent).

Measurement and scale items
Understanding the conceptual model’s posited relationships required measurement of FSEs’
perceptions of their employer and customers. Existing scales from the literature were used
to measure the constructs of interest with slight modifications to fit the study context.
All five constructs were operationalized using multi-item measures and used five-point
Likert type scales, anchored by 1 ¼ strongly disagree and 5 ¼ strongly agree.
A seven-item scale measuring FSEs’ CSR perceptions was adapted from Berens et al.
(2005), Lichtenstein et al. (2004) and Wagner et al. (2009). Each of the selected CSR items
gauges the respondent’s perceptions about how their company serves nonprofit
organizations, their community, as well as societal and environmental issues. Five items
of FSEs’ perceptions of verbal dysfunctional customer behavior were adapted from
Wang et al. (2011), McColl-Kennedy et al. (2009) and Karatepe et al. (2009a) and modified to
match the context of the study and its conceptualization of various forms of customer
misconduct because they violate generally accepted norms. The five-item perceived task
significance scale was adapted from Grant (2008) and Morgeson and Humphrey (2006) to
focus on whether FSEs view their work as having a significant impact on the lives of others.
Three items were adapted from Maslach and Jackson’s (1981) burnout scale to assess FSEs’
perceptions of emotional exhaustion. Finally, in order to capture job satisfaction, three
measurement items from Homburg and Stock (2004) were adopted to evaluate overall job
satisfaction rather than specific dimensions of job satisfaction. The scale items are listed
in the Appendix.
Data analysis, reliability and validity
The hypothesized relationships were tested using the two-step structural equation modeling
(SEM) procedure advocated by Anderson and Gerbing (1988). This method of measurement
and testing relationships allowed for rigorous testing of measurement reliability and
validity of the data before subjecting the structural model to tests of fit. A covariance matrix
was created and subjected to confirmatory factor analysis. Despite the po0.01 significance
of the χ2 ( χ 2

(NFI) ¼ 0.94, comparative fit index (CFI) ¼ 0.97, goodness of fit index (GFI) ¼ 0.89 and root
mean square error of approximation (RMSEA) ¼ 0.056. The factor-loading scores confirm
that all measures exhibit convergent validity (see Table I). Moreover, the Pearson
correlations among the measures confirm that all measures were distinct from each other,
thus resulting in sufficient evidence of divergent validity (see Table II).
The construct measures yielded sound reliability and validity properties (Table I).
All measures exhibit a Cronbach’s α of 0.89 or higher and a composite reliability exceeding
0.90, suggesting a satisfactory level of reliability (Bagozzi and Yi, 1988). Evidence of
discriminant validity was displayed via average variance extracted ranging from 0.64 to 0.82,
with each measure exceeding the 0.50 benchmark (Bagozzi and Yi, 1988). To examine for
common method bias, a confirmatory factor analysis was estimated in which all indicators
included in the model were restricted to load on a single factor (Podsakoff et al., 2003). The fit
indices obtained from this test showed a poor fit (e.g. NFI ¼ 0.61; GFI ¼ 0.48; RMSEA ¼ 0.192),
indicating that common method bias did not pose a problem (Podsakoff et al., 2003).

Following the procedures prescribed by Joreskog and Sorbom (2006), the SEM multi-
group comparison reveals that gender positively moderated H2’s relationship between
FSEs’ perceived verbal dysfunctional customer behavior and their emotional exhaustion
(p ¼ 0.02). More specifically, women were significantly more likely to experience emotional
exhaustion than men as a result of their perceptions of verbal dysfunctional customer
behavior. None of the other five hypotheses of the model were impacted by gender.
Moreover, age was not shown to have a significant moderating impact on any of the six
hypothesized paths of the conceptual model.
The SEM multi-group analysis also reveals marginal support for the respective
moderation effects of respondent’s job position (staff vs their supervisors/direct
managers; p ¼ 0.057) and years of work experience with their employer (o3 years vs
3+ years; p ¼ 0.052) toward intensifying the negative relationship between FSEs’
perceived task significance and their emotional exhaustion (H4). To be more precise,
supervisors and direct managers were more likely to experience emotional exhaustion
than their staff members as a result of perceived task significance. Similarly, FSEs with
three or more years of work tenure were more apt to experience emotional exhaustion
(than those FSEs reporting less than three years of experience) as a consequence of
perceived task significance. Neither years of work experience nor job position held by the
respondents was shown to have a significant moderating impact on any of the remaining
five paths of our model.
In summary, the SEM multi-group results show weak empirical evidence of moderation
effects among the examined demographic and employee characteristics. Only 3 of the

moderation effect at the po0.05 level.

The implications of lowering verbal dysfunctional behavior perceptions of FSEs are

by their perceptions of dysfunctional customer demands tend to become emotionally
exhausted, which in turn negatively influences their job satisfaction. On the other hand,
the results show that CSR perceptions held by FSEs may strengthen their view that they
are performing meaningful work (i.e. perceived task significance). The consequences of
perceived task significance are seen in other linkages of our model; specifically, FSEs’ task
significance works to mitigate FSEs’ emotional exhaustion, which in turn helps improve
their job satisfaction.
The results offer new insights into how employee CSR perceptions can help frontline
workers who are struggling with verbal dysfunctional customer behavior. The findings
suggest that working for a service firm that is perceived to be socially responsible can
help FSEs cope with emotional fatigue that results from the job stress of regularly dealing
with deviant customers. As the first study to explore this previously unexplored
relationship, CSR can indeed play a pivotal preventive mechanism on weakening
emotional exhaustion effects by helping FSEs better manage their perceptions of vocal
dysfunctional customer conduct.
Extending the work of Bauman and Skitka (2012), our results also suggest that CSR can
help fill an individual’s need for meaningful work in a frontline service environment. Hence,
FSEs tend to experience less emotional exhaustion when they perceive their work tasks as
meaningful via the notion that their work is positively contributing to other people’s lives.
The results also shed new light on the role of FSEs’ CSR perceptions on the relationship
between perceived task significance and emotional exhaustion of FSEs.
With job satisfaction serving as the outcome of our model, we have successfully
replicated and extended the work previously published by psychology and sociology
scholars by demonstrating that both perceived task significance and emotional
exhaustion are directly associated with job satisfaction. Hochschild’s (1983) theoretical
work on emotional labor supports the negative relationship we found between
emotional exhaustion and job satisfaction. In dealing with FSEs’ perceptions of
dysfunctional customer demands, CSR can play an effective role in reducing the strain
that undermines job satisfaction. By showing how CSR can directly influence the
meaningfulness of a worker in a frontline service setting, scholars are better positioned to
enhance the positive link between task significance perceptions and job satisfaction
(Morgeson and Humphrey, 2006).
Managerial implications
This study extends extant research about FSE boundary spanning emotional labor,
especially regarding how CSR can serve as a mechanism for supporting FSEs as a
safeguard to voiced dysfunctional customer behaviors. While a few scholars (e.g. Raub and
Blunschi, 2014) have begun to recognize that employees’ awareness of CSR heightens task

significance of their work, this marks the first study to examine the impact of CSR on FSEs’
perceptions of verbal dysfunctional customer behavior. Our results are particularly
important to service managers who are charged with helping FSEs cope with verbal
dysfunctional customer behavior. The major managerial implications of our findings
suggest that CSR has two potential effects on emotional exhaustion. First, CSR has a
preventive effect on workplace stress reduction, as FSEs’ perceptions of CSR may help them
to be less affected by the emotional labor from serving overly demanding customers.
Second, CSR provides a needs fulfillment effect, as FSEs’ perceptions of CSR foster
perceived task significance, which in turn reduces emotional exhaustion.
This study also has managerial implications regarding the value of service firms for
ensuring their employees’ involvement with CSR initiatives and regularly communicating
their CSR initiatives to their FSEs. Not only does CSR improve the public image of service
firms but it helps with the level of FSE engagement by giving them a sense of they are part
of an organization that actively contributes to “the greater good.” The stronger the
alignment between an organization’s FSEs and the firm’s particular CSR activities, the more
likely that FSEs are to share their identification with the organization. CSR gives FSEs
something to believe in and identify with on a deeper level instead of just as a place of
employment. CSR can help FSEs feel less stressed when they find themselves having to
defend their company’s reputation to hostile customers.
Given the broad range of CSR initiatives companies may focus on, it is paramount to
identify the social and environmental issues that are most important to their employees. In
reality, most employees are not close to their employer’s CSR efforts and therefore may only
be vaguely aware about the specific CSR activities the company engages in (Sen et al., 2006).
At a minimum, companies need to inform their FSEs more fully about their good works.
Preferably, companies should directly seek their participation in CSR activities. As an
illustration of this point, a recent survey by Cone (2016) found that 78 percent of workers in
the USA agreed that their job is more fulfilling when they are provided opportunities to
make a positive impact on social and environmental issues. The results of our study further
reveal that meaningful employee engagement around CSR can serve as a key factor toward
enhancing the meaning of their work.
Limitations and directions for future research
The present study provides scholars and marketing managers with a better understanding
of the process by which FSEs’ perceptions of CSR impact emotional exhaustion and job
satisfaction. However, this work should be evaluated in light of its limitations, which lead to
future research opportunities. The hypothesized model was tested using field sample survey
data drawn from a large South Korean insurance company. Future research should
investigate the examined relationships of our model with subjects drawn from other service
industry contexts to improve the generalizability of our results.
The field work nature of our study afforded less researcher control. Because the model’s
constructs were measured by using field survey self-report measures of respondents’
perceptions, strong evidence of causal effects cannot be concluded unequivocally.
We utilized a correlational design to examine the relationships among the five constructs of
our model, which inherently leaves an unclear causality among the constructs. For example,
with respect to service delivery, dissatisfied FSEs experiencing vocally dysfunctional
customer behavior can become engaged in deviant behavior toward their customers
(Van Jaarsveld et al., 2010). Future research in manipulating the constructs in an
experimental design setting and an analysis of the effects of manipulation would clarify
these causality issues in our conceptual model.
This study investigates how FSEs’ perceptions of CSR can enhance meaningful work
perceptions as well as help alleviate their views of verbal dysfunctional customer behavior.
