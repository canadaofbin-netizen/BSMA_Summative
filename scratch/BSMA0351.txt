ABSTRACT
From a knowledge-based perspective, this paper highlights the need for a
framework linking perceived competition of the industry and ﬁrms’
strategic location choices of external knowledge search and examineswhether the perceived competition increases the propensity of clusterﬁrms to search more widely from extra-cluster knowledge sources than
intra-cluster knowledge sources. Furthermore, we emphasise that clusterﬁrms with varying degrees of tacitness in their knowledge base might
respond to such competition di ﬀerently when conducting external
knowledge search activities. Using a sample of 310 cluster ﬁrms in the
Zhejiang Province of China, we ﬁnd that the cluster ﬁrm would increase
the propensity for more geographic boundary-spanning search relativeto local search while under the pressure of competition. Moreover, the
positive relationship between perceived competition and the propensity
towards geographic knowledge search is weaker when the tacitness ofthe cluster ﬁrm’s knowledge base is higher. The ﬁndings contribute to
the understanding of the relationship between competition and thechoice of location strategies in external knowledge search.ARTICLE HISTORY

1. Introduction
As the knowledge-based view (KBV) argues, the heterogeneous knowledge base among ﬁrms is the
main determinant of the creative capacity and above-average performance because it inﬂ uences a
ﬁrm’s opportunities to reduce costs or increase di ﬀerentiation from competitors (Grant 1996 ;
Maskell and Malmberg 1999 ; del-Corte-Lora, Molina-Morales, and Vallet-Bellmunt 2016 ; Chen,
Zhou, and Probert 2017 ). When a ﬁrm confronts a severe competition, it may be triggered to
search for new ideas and knowledge which may help to recon ﬁgure and update its knowledge port-
folios to defend the existing competitive advantage (Chen 2008 ; Theeke 2016 ). The innovation search
pieces of literatures suggest that it is important to engage with a wide range of external sources and
actors, such as competitors, suppliers, clients, and universities, for acquiring new ideas, insight and
knowledge to stay abreast of the competition (Laursen and Salter 2006 ; del-Corte-Lora, Molina-
Morales, and Vallet-Bellmunt 2016 ). However, the nature of the relationship between competition
and external knowledge search is not clear-cut. On the one hand, scholars state that a ﬁrm may
be expected to pursue more external knowledge search if it will be able to obtain new and

competition (Menon and Pfe ﬀer2003 ). On the other hand, competition pushes the ﬁrm to be more
eﬃcient and inhibits the pursuing of risky activities (Melville, Gurbaxani, and Kraemer 2007 ). For
example, Morandi Stagni, Fosfuri, and Santalo ( 2017 )ﬁnd that tougher product-market competition
causes an increase in technological exploitation and a decrease in technological exploration. Thus,
competition may promote more local, less risky and costly search (Chen and Miller 2007 ; García-
Granero, Vega-Jurado, and Alegre 2014 ). One of the possible explanation for the inconsistency is
that these studies fail to distinguish the type of external knowledge search with di ﬀerent geographic
location choices.
The literature in innovation search and economic geography has a debate over the di ﬀerences of
local search and geographic boundary-spanning search (Bathelt, Malmberg, and Maskell 2004 ;
Alcácer and Chung 2007 ). It emphasises the relative advantage of local search in the eﬃciency of
knowledge search process due to the geographic and social proximity of spillover (Letaifa andRabeau 2013 ; Roper, Love, and Bonner 2017 ). Conversely, the relative advantages of geographic
boundary-spanning search against local search are from increasing the probability of acquiring het-
erogeneous knowledge and reducing the likelihood of leakage of its own knowledge (Wang 2015 ).
Given these di ﬀerences, a ﬁrm would strategically choose the locations of search to trade-o ﬀ
between attaining the e ﬃciency of the knowledge search process, and acquiring heterogeneous
knowledge and reducing knowledge leak-out. Furthermore, under ﬁerce competition, the managerial
decisions of a ﬁrm are not only to increase or decrease the magnitude of external search but more
importantly to make choices about how much to invest in local search and geographic boundary-spanning search while considering the scarcity of the resource. However, it is largely unexplored
about how the perceived competitive pressures in the industry may shape a ﬁrm’s strategic location
choices between local search and geographic boundary-spanning search. Furthermore, the paper
also suggests that the relationships between the perceived competition and location choices of
external knowledge search are contingent upon the level of tacitness in a ﬁrm’s knowledge base.
Prior research has demonstrated that tacit knowledge is di ﬃcult to be imitated by the competitors
and transferred smoothly among actors (Kogut and Zander 1992 ; Kikoski and Kikoski 2004 ). There-
fore, we argue that the tacitness of knowledge base may inﬂ uence the extent to which a ﬁrm will
be threatened by the competition, then increase or hinder the motivation to acquire heterogeneous
knowledge and control its own valuable knowledge away from leak-out (Visser and Boschma 2004 ).
To test our hypotheses, our study focuses on the empirical setting on the industrial clusters in the
Zhejiang province of China. Recent research focuses on the industrial cluster ’s role as a geographi-
cally agglomerated knowledge system (Guo, Jin, and Li 2019 ),
 and how the cluster ﬁrms interact
with both intra-cluster and extra-cluster knowledge sources in the innovation process (Wang
2015 ). Based on a random sample of 310 cluster ﬁrms, the results show that the perceived compe-
tition has a signi ﬁcant and positive impact on the propensity towards geographic boundary-spanning
search (referred to as ‘PTGBS ’). Moreover, we also ﬁnd that knowledge base tacitness undermines the
positive relationship between perceived competition and PTGBS. Thus, it shows that a ﬁrm’s strategic
location choices of external knowledge search not only depend on its competition pressure in theexternal environment but also on the characteristic of the knowledge base. Our study aims tomake several contributions to the extant literature on industry competition and innovation search.
We further advance the relationship between industry competition and innovation search literature
not only by highlighting the importance of competition as a determinant of external knowledgesearch (Drechsler and Natter 2012 ) but also by pointing to the di ﬀerent e ﬀects that exerts on local
search and geographic boundary-spanning search in terms of the propensity of search within orbeyond geographical boundary of the cluster. Such ﬁndings have an important implication that com-
petition not only inﬂ uences the magnitude of external knowledge search but also the trade-o ﬀ
between two types of geographical knowledge search. Hence, this study provides a morenuanced view of how the interplay of industry competition and knowledge base tacitness mayshape the location choices of external knowledge search.712 Q. LI AND J. GUO2. Theory and hypotheses
2.1. The trade-o ﬀbetween knowledge search within and beyond geographical boundaries
of the cluster
To sustain a competitive advantage, a ﬁrm need to constantly engage external knowledge search in
response to the challenge of the dynamic market and intense rivalry (Laursen and Salter 2006 ). Scho-
lars in the industrial cluster ﬁeld have identi ﬁed two strategic location choices for external search:
local search and geographic boundary-spanning search (Caragliu and Nijkamp 2015 ). A local
search involves searching for new knowledge or solutions from intra-cluster knowledge sources
within the cluster ’s geographic boundary (Caragliu and Nijkamp 2015 ). The industrial cluster and
economic geography literatures emphasise the superiority and dominant tendency to conduct
local search which is also recognised as the most e ﬃcient mode of external knowledge search
(March 1991 ). This is due, largely, to the greater ease and relatively lower cost of searching locally
because of proximity arguments (Maskell and Malmberg 1999 ; Letaifa and Rabeau 2013 ). However,
the mere reliance on intra-cluster knowledge can result in ‘knowledge homogenisation ’of the
cluster that remains locked into an increasingly obsolete technological trajectory (Levinthal andMarch 1993 ). Furthermore, the intra-cluster knowledge sources are not only providers of new knowl-
edge but also channels by which valuable knowledge will be spilt to rivals (Alcácer and Chung 2007 ).
The risk is even higher when the cluster ﬁrms are searching within the industrial cluster where the
local actors have more advantages in assimilating and transfer the spilt knowledge by face-to-faceinteractions. Therefore, it implies that the proximity of open innovators has the downside of knowl-
edge homogenisation and negative externalities caused by overly local search.
Geographic boundary-spanning search involves searching for the new knowledge or solutions
from extra-cluster knowledge sources beyond the geographic boundary of the cluster (Wang2015 ). Recent research has suggested that the primary motivation for the geographic boundary-span-
ning search is the need to secure heterogeneous knowledge necessary to support the innovation
activities of the ﬁrm (Visser and Boschma 2004 ). It may allow cluster ﬁrms to access more technologi-
cal opportunities and acquire new knowledge that are not available through intra-cluster knowledgesearch (Rosenkopf and Nerkar 2001 ). Moreover, geographic boundary-spanning search can reduce
the risk of unintentional knowledge spillover to local actors who are the major rivals competing
for the scarce resources in the same region (Wang 2015 ). Both of geographic distance and unfamiliar
contexts inhibit the unintentional spillover of knowledge to the actors outside of the industrial
cluster. However, establishing and maintaining the relations with distant ﬁrms requires a large
amount of time, e ﬀorts and speci ﬁc investment (Bathelt, Malmberg, and Maskell 2004 ). It involves
intense e ﬀorts and investments to develop a shared interpretative and institutional context for
joint problem-solving and learning.
As the ﬁrm may have limited time and resources, there are limits to the number of external knowl-
edge sources that a ﬁrm can manage simultaneously (Laursen and Salter 2006 ). The question thus
becomes whether intra-cluster knowledge sources or extra-cluster knowledge sources should be
prioritised. The inherent trade-o ﬀthat exists between the search within and outside of industrial
cluster results from the con ﬂicting e ﬀects of local or non-local knowledge sources on the innovation.
Local search helps to keep the costs of coordination and di ﬃcult of knowledge transfer at a minimum
(Alcácer and Chung 2007 ).
 While critical, such local search can not provide a cluster ﬁrm of diverse
and non-redundant knowledge, and also is more likely to leak out its valuable knowledge to external
actors (Roper, Love, and Bonner 2017 ; Nan, Liu, and Ma 2018 ). On the other hand, geographic bound-
ary-spanning search may enable to access the diverse knowledge distributed widely across large geo-graphic areas (Visser and Boschma 2004 ). The trade-o ﬀis that it will not only spend much more
resources and managerial attention, but also it will be more risky and uncertain in the ﬁrm’s knowl-
edge search process (Phene, Lindquist, and Marsh 2006 ). Thus, the cluster ﬁrms have to prioritise the
choice between attaining proximate-based e ﬃciency of the knowledge search process through localTECHNOLOGY ANALYSIS & STRATEGIC MANAGEMENT 713search and heterogeneous knowledge through geographic boundary-spanning search. When the pri-
ority for diverse knowledge is greater than the e ﬃciency of the knowledge search process, a cluster
ﬁrm will choose to search external knowledge more widely from extra-cluster knowledge sources
than from intra-cluster knowledge sources, and vice versa.
2.2. Competition and the propensity towards geographic knowledge search
Competition is regarded as a common threat related to the ﬁrm survival and may have impact on
the motivation of search and strategic change (March 1991). Within an industrial cluster, compe-
tition is intense because the cluster ﬁrms usually sell seriously homogeneous products to the
same market and access to the similar technologies or knowledge (Guo, Jin, and Li 2019; Ferras-Her-
nandez and Nylund 2019). We argue that ﬁerce competition may trigger cluster ﬁrms to ﬁnd novel
ways or heterogeneous knowledge to maintain their competitive advantage (Tsai and Hsu 2014).
Prior studies have suggested intensi ﬁed competition may encourag e competitive imitation
within the cluster (Porter 2000). When it occurs, due to the fast di ﬀusion of knowledge aﬀ orded
by geographic proximity, ﬁerce competition may result in the p roblem of seriously homogeneous
products and then diminish a cluster ﬁrm’s competitive advantage (Grant 1996;P o r t e r2000). To
sustain the competitive advantage, the cluster ﬁrms tend to search externally to look for the
superior alternatives to their current meth ods in both product and process, explore new
markets, and ﬁnd new and better ways to compete (Chen 2008;T h e e k e 2016). Although the
intra-cluster knowledge has an economic e ﬀect for a cluster ﬁrm, it fails to provide competitive
advantages due to the homogenisation or redun dancy of the knowledge (Visser and Boschma
2004; Bathelt, Malmberg, and Maskell 2004). However, extra-cluster knowledge sources across
locations will have a greater amount of heterogeneous kno wledge due to the di ﬀerences in
initial endowments, the actions of actors engage d in R&D, and educational systems and regulation
(Alcácer and Chung 2007 ). As a result, while competition is intensi ﬁed, a cluster ﬁrm will be more
likely to search beyond the geographic bounda ry of the industrial cluster to establish a di ﬀeren-
tiation advantage.
Moreover, competitive threats may raise the motivations of cluster ﬁrms for protecting their
knowledge when they engage in external collabo ration and knowledge sourcing (Guo, Jin, and
Li2019 ). Research has shown that there are substant ial risks of being imitated or copied while a
ﬁrm is involved in accessing external sour ces of knowledge (Drechsler and Natter 2012 ; Giarratana

easily imitate a ﬁrm’s products. The fact is that to obtain external knowledge, a ﬁrm has to
reveal some parts of its knowledge to t he external partner (Alcácer and Chung 2007). While a
cluster ﬁrm spills valuable knowledge to its competitors, the competitive advantage will be

The distinction between codi ﬁed and tacit knowledge is the key to understanding organisational
knowledge (Hsiao, Chen, and Choi 2017 ). Codi ﬁed knowledge is always embodied in the written
language, code or formula and as a consequence it can be verbalised and communicated, processed,
transmitted and stored relatively easily between individuals and organisations (Zander and Kogut
1995 ). Tacit knowledge is deeply rooted in experience, action, procedures, skills and know-how,
etc, and yet is di ﬃcult to articulate in a way that is meaningful and complete (Kogut and Zander
1992 ). Some scholars have identi ﬁed that the knowledge can be classi ﬁed on a continuum
between fully tacit and fully codi ﬁed (Pérez-Luño, Alegre and Valle-Cabrera 2019 ), so we will refer
to knowledge tacitness instead of tacit or codi ﬁed knowledge.
We argue that competition is less likely to increase PTGBS of those cluster ﬁrms that have higher
levels of knowledge base tacitness. As noted by Kikoski and Kikoski ( 2004 ), tacit knowledge may
create a learning curve for other ﬁrms di ﬃcult and time-consuming to follow. Hence, cluster ﬁrms
with higher knowledge base tacitness are harder or more costly for competitors to imitate and expro-priate (Kogut and Zander 1992 ; Kikoski and Kikoski 2004 ). As the distinctiveness and value of tacit
knowledge assets are eroded relatively slowly, they are more likely to sustain unique capabilities
and di ﬀerential in products. It may be less threatened by the ﬁerce competition and as a result
will be less triggered to select the risky and costly geographic boundary-spanning search for newknowledge. In contrast, a ﬁrm with lower levels of knowledge base tacitness is more vulnerable to
competition within the cluster. As codi ﬁed knowledge can be easily di ﬀused and known by other
cluster ﬁrms, it is harder for a cluster ﬁrm to create unique capabilities depending on the production
and use of codi ﬁed knowledge (Maskell and Malmberg 1999 ; Hartono and Sheng 2016 ). As a
response, while these cluster ﬁrms face intensi ﬁed competition, they are more prone to emphasise
on the priority for searching heterogeneous knowledge outside of the cluster to sustain the competi-tive position.
As previously noted, competition threat may raise more concerns on knowledge leak-out issues
than search e ﬃciency when engaging in external knowledge sourcing (Guo, Jin, and Li 2019 ).
However, to what extent that a cluster ﬁrm responds to such a threat depends on how easily its col-
laborators can expropriate the focal ﬁrm’s valuable knowledge base in the knowledge exchange
process. Tacit knowledge is highly context-speci ﬁc and hard to be transferred easily (Kogut and
Zander 1992 ). So a tacit knowledge base can serve as an isolating mechanism that increases the
diﬃculty
of replication by outsiders (Szulanski and Jensen 2006 ). Because of these di ﬃculties,
while such cluster ﬁrms interact with external knowledge sources, the unintentional knowledge
leak-out is less likely. When a cluster ﬁrm is more optimistic about the knowledge leak-out issues,
it is more likely to draw attention on the e ﬃciency of the knowledge search process than safeguard
of a knowledge base as a response to competition. In sum, for a cluster ﬁrm with a high tacit knowl-
edge base, the desire to seek heterogeneous knowledge and reduce knowledge leak-out triggeredby competition will be lower. Thus, we suggest the following hypotheses:
H2. Knowledge base tacitness will negatively moderate the main positive e ﬀect of competition intensity on a
cluster ﬁrm’s PTGBS: the e ﬀect will be weaker when tacitness is high and stronger when tacitness is low.
3 Research methodology
3.1. Data collection and sample
We identi ﬁed a random sample from a number of industrial clusters located in the Zhejiang Province
of China. For budgetary reasons, we randomly selected 600 cluster ﬁrms from six cities in the Zhejiang
Province. Using a systematic random sampling design, in each of six cities we sampled 100 ﬁrms from
the list of cluster ﬁrms provided by the local Economic and Trade Commissions and Statistical
Bureaus. We designed our questionnaire using existing scales and used its Chinese version after aTECHNOLOGY ANALYSIS & STRATEGIC MANAGEMENT 715back-translation process to avoid cultural bias. The questionnaire items were measured on a ﬁve-
point Likert scale, from strongly disagree to strongly agree. We adopted a double-respondent ques-
tionnaire design method by dividing the questionnaire into two parts, which were required to be
ﬁnished by two top managers of the same ﬁrm independently and anonymously. We identi ﬁed
senior managers, mid-level managers who were knowledgeable about the ﬁrm’s product innovation
as the respondents. The respondents were assured that the data would be kept con ﬁdential to urge
them to express their true opinions. After several rounds of follow-ups, we obtained data from 338
ﬁrms, thus achieving a participation rate of 56.3%. The industry distribution of our sample revealed
that most of the cluster ﬁrms were from telecommunication and electronics (11.5%), textile and cloth-
ing (24.9%), general or special equipment (21.3%), machinery and metal (18.6%), pharmaceuticalsand chemicals (11.8%), and furniture (9.2%). About 72.78% were of small size with less than 300employees, 16.86% were of medium size with between 300 and 1000 employees and 7.40% were
of large size with more than 1000 employees. About 21.60% had been in business between 5 and
10 years, with another 52.37% aged between 10 and 20 years, 10.95% aged between 20 and 30,2.66% older, 8.28% aged less than 5 years, and 4.14% of the sample (14 ﬁrms) had the missing
value. We then eliminated 28 copies of questionnaires with excessive missing data and with asingle respondent. Our ﬁnal sample included 310 valid questionnaires, representing an eﬀ ective par-
ticipation rate of 51%.
3.2. Variable measures
3.2.1. Dependent variable
We included eight external knowledge sources used in innovation: customers, competitors, suppliers
of equipment, suppliers of material or components, consultants, commercial laboratories, universities
or other higher education institutes, public research institutes (Laursen and Salter 2006 ). First, we fol-
lowed Wu, Wang and Li ( 2015 )’s study in deﬁ ning intra-cluster and extra-cluster sources of knowl-
edge. Intra-cluster refers to the municipal district (namely, the city) where the focal cluster ﬁrm is
located in, and extra-cluster refers to the space outside the municipal district.
Furthermore, we asked the respondents to indicate whether their ﬁrms had used eight types of
both intra-cluster and extra-cluster sources of knowledge over the last three years using a binary vari-able coded 1 if the source was used and 0 otherwise. Third, we summed up these codings to obtain a
composite measure of intra-cluster knowledge search and extra-cluster knowledge search. Finally, wecalculated the ratio of geographic boundary-spanning search to the sum of both local and geo-
graphic boundary-spanning search to measure PTGBS. Firms with a higher ratio were considered
as placing a heavy emphasis on geographic boundary-spanning search, reﬂ ecting the ﬁrm’s propen-
sity to search more widely from extra-cluster knowledge sources rather than intra-cluster knowledge
sources.
3.2.2. Independent and moderating variables
Following Jaworski and Kohli ( 1993 ), we used the perception of the competitive environment to
measure competition intensity which includes three items. Those items reﬂ ect the managers ’percep-
tion of competition that eventually counts for the decision-making in whether a ﬁrm will invest in
diﬀerentiating and retaining the competitive advantages.
When the ﬁrm’s knowledge base is di ﬃcult to articulate, communicate and understand, that
knowledge base is considered more tacit and less codi ﬁed. Based on these concepts, we adopted
three ﬁne-grained items to measure the degree of knowledge base tacitness (Zander and Kogut
1995 ).
3.2.3. Control variables
To control for internal R&D, we included a dummy variable taking the value of one if the cluster ﬁrm
had R&D centre or department and of zero otherwise. Firm age was measured as the number of years716 Q. LI AND J. GUOsince the ﬁrst operation in its logged format. Similarly, the ﬁrm size was measured by the natural log-
arithm of the number of full-time employees. Moreover, because foreign ownership and export may
help the cluster ﬁrm identify useful knowledge in other regions or countries (Lyles and Salk 1996 ),
foreign ownership was included and measured by a dummy variable that equals to 1 if the clusterﬁrm was classi ﬁed as a foreign-invested company or JVs, and 0 to indicate otherwise. We included
a dummy variable to control whether the cluster ﬁrm had export activities or not. To control for di ﬀer-
ences in the ﬁnancial resources, we used the adapted scale developed by Tan and Peng ( 2003 )t o
measure ﬁnancial slack. We introduced the construct of technological turbulence to account for
the speed of change and instability in a technological environment (Jaworski and Kohli 1993 ).
Finally, we included industry dummies to account for potential industrial di ﬀerences.

4. Results
Our dependent variable is the fraction of geographic boundary-spanning search from the sum of
both local and geographic boundary-spanning search, which assumes values between 0 and 1. It
is‘censored ’or has a corner solution of zero, so we perform multiple Tobit regressions analysis
with Stata 12. Table 2 shows the descriptive statistics and the correlation matrix between the vari-
ables included in the empirical analysis. Table 3 reports the results for the main and moderating
eﬀects. Model 1 includes the control variables only. Model 2 introduces the main eﬀ ect of compe-
tition intensity, and models 3 and 4 add moderating variables and the interactive eﬀ ect of compe-
tition intensity and knowledge base tacitness. Finally, Model 5 is the full model, which is used totest the hypotheses.
In model 2, as hypothesised, we ﬁnd a positive and signiﬁ cant relationship between competition
intensity and PTGBS ( β= 0.080, p< 0.05). Model 4 provides further support for Hypothesis 1, in that
the linear term of competition intensity is positive and signi ﬁcant ( β= 0.092, p< 0.05). Hence, Hypoth-
esis 1 is con ﬁrmed. The result indicates that perceived competition may inﬂ uence the location choice
of external knowledge search by triggering the cluster ﬁrm to rely relatively more on geographic
boundary-spanning search rather than local search. In Model 4, we introduce the interaction term
of competition intensity and knowledge base tacitness to test whether there is a moderating
eﬀect of knowledge base tacitness on PTGBS. In this model, the coe ﬃcient of the interaction term
is negative and signiﬁ cant ( β=−0.127, p< 0.05), resulting in strong support for Hypothesis 2. It
means that knowledge base tacitness will negatively moderate the main positive eﬀ ect of compe-
tition intensity on a cluster ﬁrm’s PTGBS.

Ourﬁndings yield important theoretical contributions to the literature on innovation search and
industry competition. First, they provide an interesting contribution to open innovation literature onthe choice of search mode decisions. To the best of our knowledge, the majority of research studies
investigate the choice of governance modes such as internal and external search (Berchicci 2013 )o r
JVs and acquisition for external knowledge sourcing (Riccobono, Bruccoleri, and Perrone 2015 ). In
response to the call for future studies into how ﬁrms ’structure R&D activities (Berchicci 2013 ), we par-
ticularly investigate the choice between searching external knowledge beyond and within the geo-graphical boundary of the cluster. Previous studies have reached a consensus on the need for
searching knowledge simultaneously within and beyond the geographic boundary of the industrial
cluster (Wu and Wu 2014 ;Wu, Wang, and Li 2015 ). However, the common emphasis of those studies
has to be given to treat local search and geographic boundary-spanning search as a dichotomy or
two quite di ﬀerent alternatives (Wu, Wang, and Li 2015 ) or examine the interactive e ﬀect of two
sources of knowledge search (Wu and Wu 2014 ). The results of Wu and Wu ( 2014 ) show that extensive
local search and international search complement each other and produce a more positive impact onproduct innovation. Our operationalisation of the construct contributes to a more ﬁne-grained concep-
tual understanding of the trade-o ﬀbetween two diﬀ erent geographical scales of knowledge sources as
an organisational adaptation to the competitive environment. The managerial implication is the needfor senior managers to prioritise the allocation of resources between local search and geographic
boundary-spanning search and makes a trade-o ﬀin a resource-constrained context.
Second, our ﬁndings contribute in understanding the relationship between industry competition
and external knowledge search. Industry competition has a critical role in determining a ﬁrm’s strat-
egy of resource allocation and acquisition (Melville, Gurbaxani, and Kraemer 2007 ) and search behav-
iour pattern (Chen and Miller 2007 ). So far, only limited empirical studies pay attention to the direct or
contingent roles of industry competition in shaping a ﬁrm’s external knowledge search behaviour.
For example, García-Granero et al. ( 2014 )ﬁnd that increased competition will decrease the probability
of pursuing an exploratory oriented external search strategy. Wang and Guo ( 2019 ) indicate that
industrial competition will exert a negative moderating eﬀ ect on the relationship between absorptive
capacity and external knowledge search. Our study extends the existing studies by proposing thatindustry competition may shape strategic location choices of external knowledge search in the par-
ticular context of the industrial cluster. Furthermore, our results inform the ongoing debate on the
choice of local search and geographic boundary-spanning search by ﬁrms under the ﬁerce competi-
tive environment. On the one hand, the competition will increase the incentive of cluster ﬁrms for
acquiring heterogeneous knowledge from external knowledge sources. The long-term industrial
competitiveness is related to the ability of ﬁrms
 to upgrade their knowledge base continuously
(Maskell and Malmberg 1999 ). On the other hand, competition may raise the ﬁrm’s concerns for
leak-out of valuable knowledge to external actors (Alcácer and Chung 2007 ; Guo, Jin, and Li 2019 ),
thereby reducing the use of external sources (Giarratana and Mariani 2014 ). Therefore, our
ﬁndings inform this on-going debate by theoretically proposing and empirically showing that com-
petition not only inﬂ uences the magnitude of external knowledge search but also the trade-o ﬀ
between two di ﬀerent geographical orientation of the knowledge search. The managerial implication
of the results may present a partial solution to such a dilemma by suggesting that the cluster ﬁrm
may choose and trade-o ﬀbetween two di ﬀerent geographical scales of knowledge search according
to their relative advantages.
Furthermore, our ﬁndings shed light on the role of knowledge tacitness in knowledge transfer and
external search process. Scholars have reached a consensus that proximity matters in transferringtacit knowledge because such a type of knowledge exchange continues to require regular face-to-face contact and a high degree of mutual trust and understanding (Malmberg and Maskell 2002 ;720 Q. LI AND J. GUONan, Liu, and Ma 2018 ). However, most of those studies focus their attention on the tacitness of trans-
ferred knowledge, instead of the knowledge base of the ﬁrm conducting external knowledge search
(Hsiao, Chen, and Choi 2017 ). This study, to the best of our knowledge, is one of the ﬁrst to system-
atically investigate how the extent to which tacit knowledge constitutes the knowledge base of the
cluster ﬁrm will in ﬂuence the geographic location choices of the external search as a response to the
pressure of competition. Our study indicates that knowledge base tacitness weakens the positive
eﬀect of competition intensity on PTGBS by reducing the threaten by the ﬁerce competition, while
lessening the concerns for controlling knowledge leak-out issues. It then complements arguments
on how knowledge attribute inﬂ uences the transfer, exploitation and control of innovation-related
knowledge during the innovation search process.
Our study has a number of limitations that point to directions for future research. First, given that
perceptual measures were used for both the independent and moderating variables, it might be the
possibility of common method bias. Although we employed both procedural and statistical precau-
tions, the potential of this bias could not be completely ruled out. Second, the survey data were gath-ered at one point in time, which prevented us from studying causal relationships between
competition and PTGBS. A longitudinal study may provide further insight into the dynamics of how
the cluster ﬁrms trade-o ﬀbetween types of knowledge search to adapt competition in the market.
Third, our sample was selected from the industrial clusters in China. This might raise the concern of
the generalisability issue of our ﬁndings. Additional empirical research, using samples of ﬁrms
coming from other countries, is warranted. Furthermore, future studies may consider the coexistence
of competition and cooperation between the cluster ﬁrm. Although the cluster ﬁrms compete ﬁercely,
it is worthwhile to examine how competition and cooperation interactively in ﬂuence the location
choices of external knowledge search across the geographic boundary of the industrial cluster.Finally, this paper examined the moderating e ﬀect of knowledge base tacitness, but it is reasonable
to expect that other internal contingencies, such as organisational slack and organisational inertia,may also in ﬂuence the relationship. Perceived competition may provide incentives for a cluster ﬁrm
to search comparably more from extra-cluster knowledge sources, but it is also critical for a cluster
ﬁrm to have su ﬃcient resource and capacity to adjust their external search strategy.
