Entrepreneurial teams often struggle with simultaneous task and team chal-
lenges at an early stage of new venture creation. The way in which teams shape
their teamwork is key in leveraging performance in the pre-founding phase.
Learning should help the team in establishing good teamwork and in expand-ing its members/C29 entrepreneurial capabilities. Leadership is needed to facilitate
and guide this learning. Accordingly, we investigated learning and leadership
as facilitators of performance in the pre-founding phase. Specifically, we exam-ined team reflexivity as a collective internal learning process and boundaryspanning behaviour as an externally directed individual activity, operating at
different levels in fostering team and individual performance. Charismatic
team leadership was examined as a catalyst of learning, shaping team and indi-vidual performance ultimately. The multilevel mediation model was tested
based on data from 196 members of 58 teams of a venture creation pro-
gramme. Team reflexivity predicted team and individual performance. Bound-ary spanning behaviour was not related to performance. As hypothesised,
charismatic team leadership predicted team and individual performance, both
mediated by team reflexivity. This research highlights the relevance of teamlearning in pre-founding teams and emphasises leadership in shaping learning
and moving new ventures forward.

The majority of new ventures are founded by teams, putting the team at the heart
of new venture creation (Schjoedt & Kraus, 2009a). The pr e-founding phase—a
phase that is qualitatively distinct from later phases and crucial for venture suc-cess—puts particularly high demands on the team (Fern, Cardinal, & O/C29Neill,
2012; Foo, Sin, & Yiong, 2006; McMu llen & Shepherd, 2006). Pre -founding
teams often struggle with simultaneous task and team challenges (DeMol, Kha-pova, & Elfring, 2015). A pre- founding team needs to select the most promising
idea and refine the business model while changes in all facets of the nascent ven-
ture are commonly occurring; at the same time, it needs to establish team func-tioning and expand its members/C29 entrepreneurial capabilities (Ardichvili,
Cardozo, & Ray, 2003). This raises the important question how teams manage to
perform in the light of the challenges associated with the pre-founding phase. Theliterature has seen a surge in studies exploring the success factors of entrepreneur-
ial teams (Frese, Chell, & Klandt, 2000; Ireland & Webb, 2007; Klotz, Hmieleski,
Bradley, & Busenitz, 2014). The dominant view highlights the significance ofinput factors such as team composition and prior experience (Jin, Madison,
Kraiczy, Kellermanns, C rook, & Xi, 2017). However, the way in which a team
shapes its teamwork seems crucial in leveraging performance in the pre-foundingphase (Bird, Schjoedt, & Baum, 2012; Cohen & Bailey, 1997). We put forward
the assumption that learning of the whole team and of its members will foster per-
formance and that leadership can facilitate and guide this learning.
Learning should facilitate the development of internal team functioning in
the often chaotic course of action and the enhancement of the team members/C29
entrepreneurial capabilities in seeking external support and presenting theendeavour to stakeholders (Keith, Unger, Rauch, & Frese, 2016; Marrone,
Tesluk, & Carson, 2007). Hence, we explore the contribution of internal and
external learning processes, operating at different levels in fostering team andindividual performance (Blackburn & Kovalainen, 2009; Davidsson &
Wiklund, 2001): (a) team reflexivity as a collective, internal learning process
that is characterised by continuous joint evaluation and discussion of teamgoals and strategies (Schippers, Den Hartog, & Koopman, 2007), and (b) indi-
vidual boundary spanning behaviour as an externally directed activity that is
performed by each team member to gather expertise and support and to repre-sent the business to stakeholders (Ancona & Caldwell, 1992). Given the rele-
vance of learning, it is important to elucidate the leadership behaviours that
can initiate, facilitate, and guide learning in the light of the challenges anduncertainties associated with the pre-founding phase (Gorgievski & Stephan,
2016). As an entrepreneurial undertaking is a weak situation involving high
ambiguity, uncertainty, and the need for orientation, we see the need for a leadentrepreneur who communicates a clear vision for the joint endeavour and
demonstrates confidence in the team members/C29 capability to achieve this vision
(Ensley, Pearce, & Hmielski, 2006). Charismatic team leadership projects thispicture (Jacobsen & House, 2001; Shamir & Howell, 1999). We thus investigate402

Our research advances our understanding of the factors that foster perform-

THEORY AND HYPOTHESES
The pre-founding phase puts heightened demands on the team as a whole and
on each individual team member (McMullen & Shepherd, 2006; Ravasi &
Turati, 2005). We think of a pre-founding team as two or more persons who are
committed to develop their business model, whose actions reflect the interde-pendent and interactive nature of their teamwork, and who share the responsi-
bility and accountability in the creation of the future venture (Schjoedt &
Kraus, 2009b). These teams face a high level of uncertainty, a lack of standardprocedures and work routines, and little structure in their teamwork. This is
particularly challenging as they operate in a dynamic context that is character-
ised by rapidly changing circumstances, high competition, and a need for out-of-the-box-thinking (McMullen & Shepherd, 2006). As a consequence, entre-
preneurial teams are required to quickly adapt their goals and strategies when-
ever necessary and to develop their members/C29 entrepreneurial capabilities inthe course of action (Cope, 2003). In other words, the challenges of the pre-
founding phase require constant learning, both as a team and as a member of
the team (Ravasi & Turati, 2005). Accordingly, learning of the team and itsindividual members should leverage the performance of pre-founding teams to
a large extent. Moreover, lead entrepreneurs will shape team processes and
team outcomes in the pre-founding phase to a large extent because of theirgreat autonomy and high discretion in structuring teamwork (Cogliser &
Brigham, 2004). Yet, not all entrepreneurs lead the same way, and this variance
in leadership is expected to explain variance in processes and outcomes of pre-founding teams. Accordingly, we examined the contribution of learning and
leadership in enhancing the performance of pre-founding teams.

Performance

venture on a regular basis and constantly adapt their strategies to achieve thisDRIVERS OF SUCCESS IN PRE-FOUNDING TEAMS 403

vision in order to function as a team (West, 2000). Therefore, we propose team
reflexivity, “the extent to which team members collectively reflect upon the
team/C29s objectives, strategies, and processes ( ...) and adapt them accordingly”
(West, 2000, p. 3), to be a driver of entrepreneurial performance operating on
the team level. Although reflexivity can refer to individual cognition, and indi-
vidual reflexivity may contribute to team reflexivity (Schippers et al., 2007), itdoes not represent a team property unless it manifests in shared, social cogni-
tion (West, 2000). Therefore, in line with earlier notions, our conception of
team reflexivity refers to a group process and includes collective behaviours
such as group discussion (Otte, Konradt, Garbers, & Schippers, 2017).

A growing number of studies link team reflexivity to team performance in
contexts where innovation is required and the environment changes frequently
(DeDreu, 2002; Hoegl & Parboteeah, 2006). Team reflexivity is particularly
important for teams who are still forming because it includes evaluation anddiscussion processes that foster team development (Schippers et al., 2007). It
ensures that goals and means to achieve these goals are monitored, regularly
reviewed, and adapted to changes in the environment; it further allows foreffective internal communication and coordination (Shin, Kim, & Lee, 2016).
We expect team reflexivity to help entrepreneurial teams in creating working
conditions, identifying and initiating tasks at hand, establishing shared rou-
tines and team structures, and aligning individual and team goals in pursuing
their business idea. It is thus assumed to enhance a team/C29s performance.
Hypothesis 1 : Team reflexivity is positively related to entrepreneurial team
performance.
Boundary Spanning Behaviour as Individual-Level Driver
of Entrepreneurial Performance

team members/C29 boundary spanning behaviour describes individual externally

unclear and fluctuating in the pre-founding phase (Ensley et al., 2006), each
individual team member/C29s boundary spanning efforts cannot be expected toconverge. Instead, the choice to interact with external actors is individual in
nature and the primary source of variation is on the individual level (Marrone
et al., 2007). In our research and in line with previous notions, the agent of

Cross-Level Effects of Team Reﬂexivity on Individual
Performance
Individual outcomes may vary due to team-level properties (Braun, Peus,
Weisweiler, & Frey, 2013; Mathieu & Taylor, 2007). Reflexive teams have amore profound understanding of their goals, thereby enabling their members to
proactively pursue the team goals (West, 2000). Team reflexivity is considered
to create a “climate of task excellence” that encourages team members to excelin task fulfilment (West, 1996, p. 570). Accordingly, we assume that team reflex-ivity not only influences team performance but also individual performance.
Hypothesis 3 : Team reflexivity is positively related to the performance of individ-
ual members of the entrepreneurial team.
The Signiﬁcance of Charismatic Team Leadership for
Team and Individual Processes
Leadership can be defined as the “intentional influence by one person over
others in order to guide, structure and facilitate ...activities and relationships”
(Yukl, 2002, p. 7); it has been recognised as a key factor for entrepreneurial per-
formance (Ensley, Carland, & Carland, 2000; Hmieleski & Ensley, 2007).
Leadership may facilitate team processes and outcomes through influentialacts in terms of communicating a strong vision for the joint venture andempowering every team member to follow this vision (Baum, Locke, & Kirk-patrick, 1998; Cardon, Zietsma, Saparito, Matherne, & Davis, 2005). Charis-matic leadership describes these behaviours (Jacobsen & House, 2001). It wasDRIVERS OF SUCCESS IN PRE-FOUNDING TEAMS 405

conceptualised around three main components: (a) sensitivity to opportunities
and constraints in the environment; (b) shaping respectful and positive social
relationships within the team through consideration of followers/C29 needs; and(c) empowering them to implement the shared vision through exemplary acts
at high personal cost (Conger, Kanungo, & Menon, 2000). In our study, we
adopt previous team-level conceptualisations of charismatic leadership (e.g.Agle, Nagarajan, Sonnenfeld, & Srinivasan, 2006; Nohe, Michaelis, Menges,
Zhang, & Sonntag, 2013). Entrepreneurial teams are characterised by interde-
pendence in task completion, interactivity in decision-making, and high visibil-ity of the leader. Therefore, although leadership is generic with regard to its
application to individuals, teams, or organisations (Kozloswki & Klein, 2000;
Kozlowski, Mak, & Chao, 2016; Wang & Howell, 2010), we think of charis-matic leadership as a team emergent state, where perceptions converge over
time (Fulmer & Ostroff, 2016; Klein & House, 1995).
Charismatic leadership should motivate teams to constantly challenge
the status quo (Schippers, Den Hartog, Koopman, & van Knippenberg,
2008) while creating feelings of control and reducing perceptions of threat
(Conger et al., 2000). This likely increases trust in the team and the leader(Braun et al., 2013). As a consequence, team members feel safe to speak
up and experiment with new approaches and feel comfortable in critically
discussing the team/C29s goals and strategies (Paulsen, Maldonado, Callan, &Ayoko, 2009). Charismatic team leadership is thus assumed to foster team
reflexivity.
Hypothesis 4 : Charismatic team leadership is positively related to team reflexivity.
Charismatic team leadership is expected to enhance team efficacy; this feel-
ing of team effectiveness improves a team/C29s capacity to perform eventually (e.g.
Somech, 2006). Based on our premise that team reflexivity supports team per-
formance, we hypothesise an indirect effect of charismatic team leadership onteam performance. In addition, charismatic team leaders provide clear direc-
tion and encourage followers/C29 initiative (Tesluk & Mathieu, 1999). Feelings of
empowerment put forward by charismatic team leadership enable team mem-bers to achieve the high performance expectations set by their team leader
(Shamir, House, & Arthur, 1993). Based on the above reasoning of the positive
effects of charismatic leadership on team reflexivity and the assumed relation-ship of team reflexivity with individual performance, we propose a cross-level
effect of charismatic team leadership on individual performance through team
reflexivity.
Hypothesis 5 : Charismatic team leadership is positively related to entrepreneurial
team performance and this relationship is mediated by team reflexivity.406 KNIPFER ET AL.
VC2017 International Association of Applied Psychology.
Hypothesis 6 : Charismatic team leadership is positively related to the performance
of individual members of the entrepreneurial team and this relationship is medi-ated by team reflexivity.
By putting emphasis on each follower/C29s contribution to the team endeavour,
charismatic team leadership creates a “feeling of making a difference” (Conger
et al., 2000, p. 752), heightening followers/C29 commitment to act in pursuit of theteam goals. Moreover, confidence in their followers/C29 capacity to cross teamboundaries is raised (Shea & Howell, 1999). We thus propose that charismaticteam leadership fosters boundary spanning behaviour. Based on our assump-tion that boundary spanning behaviour enhances individual performance, wehypothesise an indirect effect of charismatic team leadership on individual per-formance that is mediated by boundary spanning behaviour.
Hypothesis 7 : Charismatic team leadership is positively related to boundary span-
ning behaviour of members of the team.
Hypothesis 8 : Charismatic team leadership is positively related to the performance
of individual members of the entrepreneurial team and this relationship is medi-
ated by boundary spanning behaviour.

individual boundary spanning behaviour facilitate the performance of thewhole team and of its members (see Figure 1). Charismatic team leadership isexpected to foster these learning processes at multiple levels, influencing entre-preneurial teams to perform better.

METHOD
Sample and Procedure
We gathered data in collaboration with an innovation and start-up centre
in Germany. We studied teams who participated in a venture creation pro-
gramme that combines education and incubation (Lack /C19eus & Williams
Middleton, 2015), which often results in nascent behaviour (Bae, Qian,Miao, & Fiet, 2014; Martin, McNally, & Kay, 2013). The programme isoffered on a regular basis and advertised in the local community andbeyond. Participants signed up individually. At the beginning, they met attheidea fair , formed teams, and nominated a team leader. The team leader
had specific responsibilities such as coordinating the team/C29s activities andreporting its progress to the programme instructors. After four months ofweekly training and teamwork, the teams submitted a business plan andpresented their business model following the structure of an elevator pitch.Before the evaluation of the business plan and the business pitch were
made available, we administered a survey to 252 participants. The partici-
pants were asked to sign a consent form in which they granted access totheir performance evaluations.
For data analysis, we excluded 17 teams because not all members of
the team granted access to their performance evaluations or fewer thantwo team members provided leadership ratings. The final sample included196 participants in 58 teams of three to five members (41.4% with threeand 53.4% with four members). Participants (23.5% female) were on aver-age 23.8 years old ( SD53.2). Their background was mainly in computer
science (25.5%), engineering (19.7%), and business administration (19.4%).Twenty-six per cent of the teams pursued their business idea after finish-ing the programme by taking part in advanced training and mentoring,participating in national business plan competitions, or nascent behaviourssuch as applying for funding.

DISCUSSION
The pre-founding phase is highly demanding for entrepreneurial teams and for
each member of the team, and a team/C29s performance at this stage will have along-term impact on new venture success (Fern et al., 2012). Pre-foundingteams often struggle with multiple simultaneous challenges concerning boththe task (i.e. recognising business opportunities, selecting the most promisingidea, and constantly refining the business model while changes in all facets ofthe nascent venture occur) and the team (i.e. establishing team functioning andexpanding team members/C29 entrepreneurial capabilities while the team is stillforming). The way in which teams shape their teamwork is key in leveragingperformance at this stage (Bird et al., 2012; Cohen & Bailey, 1997). The research reported in this paper provided evidence for the viability of a
multilevel model of learning and leadership in predicting entrepreneurial per-
formance in pre-founding teams. Specifically, our study is the first to investigate
team reflexivity and individual boundary spanning behaviour as drivers of entre-
preneurial performance in pre-founding teams. We hypothesised and found that
team reflexivity enables teams to adapt their teamwork to the rapidly changing
and highly volatile pre-founding phase, thereby shaping the performance of the
whole team and of each individual team member. Contrary to our hypothesis,
boundary spanning behaviour was not related to each team member/C29s individual
entrepreneurial performance. Charismatic team leadership was found to facili-
tate team reflexivity and individual boundary spanning behaviour, providing
new insights into how leadership shapes processes and outcomes of entrepre-
neurial teams.
Theoretical Implications
Our findings make important contributions in advancing our understanding
of the facilitators that leverage performance at a very early stage of the entre-
preneurial endeavour—the pre-founding phase. They add to the literature on
entrepreneurial teams in two ways. First, we expand the dominant models of
entrepreneurial success that focused on input factors, largely neglecting process
factors (for a review see Jin et al., 2017). Although we acknowledge the signifi-
cance of input factors such as team composition characteristics, our research
points to the importance of team processes in shaping entrepreneurial team
performance (see also Kamm, Shuman, Seeger, & Nurick, 1990). Specifically,we argued that learning processes seem to be of particular relevance in the pre-
founding phase (Ravasi & Turati, 2005; Wang & Chugh, 2014). We contribute
to the advancement of research on entrepreneurial learning by highlighting the
role of team reflexivity, that is, the continuous evaluation and discussion of
team goals and means to achieve these goals. In doing so, we respond to earlier
calls to examine the team as the agent of learning (Wang & Chugh, 2014). Our
findings suggest that simply putting a team together does not imply that this
team is able to act effectively in the highly volatile and unpredictable environ-
ment of the pre-founding phase; instead, only reflexive teams seem to be418

readily prepared to face the task and team challenges associated with the early
stages of the entrepreneurial endeavour.
As a previously neglected individual learning activity, we analysed each
individual team member/C29s boundary spanning behaviour. Our findings imply
that charismatic leadership encourages boundary spanning behaviour and
augment previous theorising on the relevance of leadership for crossing teamboundaries (DeChurch & Marks, 2006; Joshi, Pandey, & Han, 2009). How-
ever, boundary spanning behaviour was not related to individual perform-
ance in our study. As Marrone et al. (2007) found that team members maysuffer from role overload when challenged to balance internal and external
activities, a plausible reason for this finding relates to the small team size in
our research; acting in a small team has likely put heightened demands oneach member of the team, resulting in detrimental effects on individual per-
formance in some cases. This finding challenges the positivist view on bound-
ary spanning behaviour, pointing to the significance of moderators (Faraj &Yan, 2009; Marrone, 2010).
Second, we contribute to the literature by investigating how leadership
impacts learning processes in the pre-founding phase, responding to calls toexplore the role of the team leader in entrepreneurial teams (Antonakis &
Autio, 2007). This perspective is needed as “all entrepreneurs are by defini-
tion leaders” (Ensley et al., 2006, p. 246). Leadership is one of the most criti-cal factors in the success of any entrepreneurial team in facilitating and
sustaining collective and individual performance in entrepreneurship (Hmiel-
ski & Ensley, 2007), “making new ventures a particularly important contextin which to examine the effects of leadership behavior” (Ensley et al., 2006, p.
219). In identifying opportunities in the environment, communicating a clear
vision, and empowering all members of the team to achieve this vision, char-ismatic team leadership of the lead entrepreneur was expected to foster both
team performance and individual performance. Our findings imply that char-
ismatic team leadership is effective in shaping both team reflexivity andboundary spanning behaviour, addressing calls to investigate the role of lead-
ers in influencing entrepreneurial teams to perform better (Gorgievski & Ste-
phan, 2016). Moreover, to our knowledge, this study is the first to examineteam reflexivity and boundary spanning behaviour as multilevel pathways of
the effect of charismatic leadership. Although warranting replication, our
pattern of results highlights cross-level effects of leadership flowing from theteam to the individual level, which have largely been ignored in favour of
within-level effects (DeChurch, Hiller, Murase, Doty, & Salas, 2010; Lord &
Dinh, 2012).
Beyond work on entrepreneurial teams, this study also extends research on
the significance of leadership in initiating and facilitating team reflexivity,
which was called for by Schippers et al. (2008). This perspective is particularlyrelevant as teams in general are not very reflexive (West, 2000). We theoriseDRIVERS OF SUCCESS IN PRE-FOUNDING TEAMS 419

and find that charismatic team leadership fosters team reflexivity, enabling
teams and individual members of the team to perform better. This findingchallenges the assumption that charismatic leadership increases group-thinkphenomena (Klein & House, 1995). In contrast, the joint vision put forward bya charismatic leader seems to function as a shared reference to critically evalu-ate progress towards the team goals (Schippers et al., 2008).
Limitations and Future Research
This study broadly contributes to the literature; however, it is not without limi-tations. First, our research model was restricted to the examination of charis-matic team leadership on the team level. Individuals may vary with regard tothe quality of their relationship with the team leader (Kozlowski et al., 2016).For example, leader-member-exchange (LMX) theory assumes that leadersdevelop individual relationships with their followers (Schriesheim, Castro, &Cogliser, 1999). Similarly, team reflexivity was examined as a collective teamphenomenon in our study. Still, each team member/C29s individual reflexivity on
their own may well contribute to team reflexivity in terms of a bottom-up pro-
cess (e.g. Gong, Kim, Lee, & Zhu, 2013). We suggest future research to includeLMX and individual reflexivity as predictors of performance.
A clear strength of our study is the field setting; still, we cannot rule out
reverse causality. Schippers et al. (2008) found that prior team performanceaffects team reflexivity. Moreover, charismatic team leadership ratings maydepend on perceived team performance and causal ascription of performanceto the team leader (Lord, Brown, Harvey, & Hall, 2001; Weber, Camerer, Rot-tenstreich, & Knez, 2001). Although the performance evaluations were onlyavailable to the participants after we assessed the variables of our research andthe outcome measures were based on expert assessments, our findings warrantreplication using longitudinal designs. Due to the field setting, we used shortmeasures in order to maximise response rates. In a comparable sample, themeasures used in this research correlated very highly with the original scales,which indicates that the core variance of the focal constructs under investiga-tion was captured. Still, we encourage researchers to replicate our findings
with the full scales provided by Conger et al. (2000), Schippers et al. (2007),
and Ancona and Caldwell (1992).
Finally, we recruited a sample of rather homogeneous teams from a venture
creation programme. This allowed us to investigate how entrepreneurial teamsshape their teamwork in unique ways under very comparable conditions. Weare confident that our findings—with some caution—can be generalised toother populations of teams as long as members of the team work together on ahighly motivated and cooperative basis in a dynamic and complex environmentcharacterised by the elevated importance of innovation.420

APPENDIX: Survey measures used in this research
Charismatic team leadership (based on Conger et al., 2000)
The team leader engaged in unconventional behaviour in order to achieve the goals of the
team.
The team leader influenced others by developing mutual liking and respect.
The team leader often incurred high personal cost for the good of the team.The team leader showed sensitivity for the needs and feelings of the other members of the
team.
The team leader often exhibited very unique behaviour that surprised other members of the
team.
Team reflexivity (based on Schippers et al., 2007)
8
We worked out what we can learn from past experience.
During the task execution, we stopped to assess whether the team was on the right track.
We regularly checked whether our activities had led to the expected results.If things didn/C29t work out as planned, we discussed the possible causes for that.
We regularly discussed our methods for getting the job done.
We often discussed what we could learn from our failures and mistakes.
We regularly discussed whether the team was working effectively.
We evaluated our project progress on a regular basis.
Boundary spanning behaviour (based on Ancona & Caldwell, 1992)
Talk up the team to outsiders.
Report the progress of the team to others.
Keep others informed of your team/C29s activities.
Find out what competing groups are doing on similar projects.Scan the environment for marketing ideas/expertise.
Scan the environment for technical ideas/expertise.
Protect the team from outside interference
