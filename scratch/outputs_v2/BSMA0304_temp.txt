Abstract
Purpose – The purpose of this research is to examine the positive relationship between leader’s boundary-
spanning behavior and employee creative behavior. Moreover, the research investigates a three-way effect by
exploring leader’s boundary-spanning behavior, need for status and creative self-efficacy on employee creative
behavior.
Design/methodology/approach – The authors conducted a cross-sectional survey involving 260
supervisor-subordinate dyads from various companies in South Korea. The research tests the hypotheses
through a hierarchical regression analysis.
Findings – The authors provided empirical evidence that leader’s boundary-spanning behavior positively
related to employee creative behavior. Moreover, the result demonstrated that the effect of leader’s boundary-
spanning behavior on creative behavior was the strongest in the context of high need for status and high
creative self-efficacy.
Research limitations/implications – The research contributes to the three streams of literature on
boundary spanning, creativity, and leadership by exploring leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior, employees’
need for status, and employees’ creative self-efficacy.
Practical implications – The findings suggest that organizations should promote leaders’ boundary-
spanning behavior, thereby enhancing employees’ creative behavior. Also, the study highlights the critical role
of individual factors, such as the need for status and creative self-efficacy, and situational factors that
determine the level of creative behavior.
Originality/value – Integrating the componential model of creativity and the interactionist perspective of
creativity, this research examines the positive influence of leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior on creative
behavior. Furthermore, the research exhibits how leader’s boundary-spanning behavior, need for status and
creative self-efficacy could develop creative conditions to promote employee creative behavior.

Interactionist perspective

the need for status and creative self-efficacy as critical individual factors. It examines how
creative conditions jointly interact to influence the relationship between leaders’ boundary-

Theoretical background and hypothesis development
Leader boundary-spanning behavior and employee creative behavior
Boundary spanning is defined as an individual or team behavior that builds and manages
external linkages with parties within an organization or across organizational boundaries
(Kou, 2021; Marrone, 2010). It is generally classified into three broad categories:
representation, task performance coordination, and general information search (Marrone
et al., 2021). Scholars have noted the positive effects of individual boundary spanning on
individual job satisfaction, performance, and role overload (Liu et al., 2018; Zhang and Li,
2021). Given the leaders’ vital power and position in the workplace, they are expected to be
key actors in connecting with external parties and obtaining valuable resources from in- and
out-group members, thereby exhibiting boundary-spanning behavior (Salem et al., 2018;
Takanashi and Lee, 2019).
Leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior comprises a broad set of externally-oriented
activities, including persuading outsiders to provide support, acquiring critical information
or resources from external stakeholders, and negotiating project contents directed toward
people outside and within their organizations (Salem et al., 2018). Despite its essential role,
previous studies have largely overlooked its complex and subtle dynamics and focused solely
on team boundary spanning (e.g. Zhang and Li, 2021). Building on the componential model of
creativity (Amabile, 1983), this study concludes that leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior
promotes employees’ creative behavior by increasing domain-relevant skills.
These leaders can increase domain-relevant skills, defined as “one’s expertise or factual
knowledge about the domain, technical skills for doing work and advancing one’s knowledge
in the domain, and special domain-relevant talents” (Amabile and Pratt, 2016, p. 160).
According to the componential model of creativity (Amabile, 1983), domain-relevant skills are
vital components for engaging in creative behavior. Boundary-spanning leaders who
maintain close ties with external parties outside their teams or organizations can provide
employees novel and insightful knowledge from external sources, facilitating knowledge
circulation and enabling subordinates to navigate larger pools of resources and expertise

Three-way interaction effects on creative behavior
We apply the interactionist perspective of organizational creativity (Woodman et al., 1993) to
better understand the drivers of employees’ creative behavior, and examine how individual
factors (i.e. the need for status and creative self-efficacy) jointly interact with situational
factors (i.e. leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior) to influence creative behavior. According
to the componential model of creativity (Amabile, 1983), this study explores employees’ need
for status and creative self-efficacy as motivational and creativity-relevant components,
respectively. We investigate a three-way effect between leaders’ boundary-spanning
behavior, the need for status, and creative self-efficacy.
Employees can acquire valuable resources (i.e. domain-relevant skills) from their
boundary-spanning leaders, who have external information and expertise. However, they
may not exhibit a high level of creative behavior without the necessary motivation and
capacity to engage in such behavior. As task motivation is essential in facilitating creative
behavior, our study highlights employees’ need for status, which is one of the fundamental
motivations for social relationships in the workplace (Weiss and Morrison, 2019).
Status, which is defined as respect, admiration, and voluntary deference, has been widely
studied in various fields, including psychology, sociology, anthropology, economics, and
organizational behavior (Anderson et al., 2015). Scholars have stated that the desire to acquire
status is a fundamental human motive (Kennedy et al., 2013). Individuals with higher status
tend to have greater subjective well-being, self-esteem, job satisfaction, and fewer intentions
to quit. Hence, they may strive for a higher standing in the workplace (Weiss and Morrison,
2019). As a significant individual factor, the need for status is defined as a person’s “motive to
attain respect or admiration by others” (Dubois et al., 2012, p. 1048). As creative behavior is
promoted in the workplace, this study argues that the need for status could be a critical
motivational factor that influences creative behavior. Thus, employees who possess a higher
need for status may engage in creative behavior to enhance their status. Previous research
has investigated the factors that increase individuals’ status, such as personal characteristics,
task expertise, and proactive behaviors (Weiss and Morrison, 2019). Weiss and Morrison
(2019) have shown how voice behavior can promote the status of employees because it is
healthy for a group’s success. Similarly, we assume that creative behavior can be used as a
tool to promote employees’ status.
Although employees obtain domain-relevant skills from their boundary-spanning leaders
and possess task motivation (i.e. a need for status), they may fail to exhibit high levels of
creative behavior because of a lack of confidence in developing new ideas or building new
ways of performing tasks, referred to as creative self-efficacy. Amabile and Pratt (2016) noted
that creativity-relevant processes are individual-level factors, including cognitive style and
skills that integrate raw materials into new ideas or products. Creative self-efficacy
represents creativity-relevant processes that determine the ability to apply diverse
information and attempt new perspectives required to demonstrate creative behaviors
(Christensen-Salem et al., 2021; Gong et al., 2020). Tierney and Farmer (2002) defined creative
self-efficacy as the belief that people can attain creative outcomes. Furthermore, they can
quickly recover when encountering failures and attribute them to inadequate endeavors or
scarce resources (Amabile and Pratt, 2016). Creative self-efficacy is necessary for creative

Methods
Sample and procedure
We distributed pairs of survey packages to students enrolled in an executive MBA program at
a university in South Korea. We collected data from various companies mainly manufacturing,
finance and IT services. Each survey package included a supervisor and subordinate surveys
together with a reply envelope, which participants delivered directly to the researchers after
completing the surveys. All participants were assured that their responses would remain
confidential.
We initially had distributed questionnaires to 339 independent dyads and collected 267
dyads, giving a response rate of 79%. After matching the subordinate surveys with the
corresponding supervisor surveys, a total of 260 pairs were used for final analyses
representing a subordinate-supervisor ratio of 1.37:1. Among the responding supervisors,
87% were male with an average age 43.52 years old (SD 5 5.32). Out of responding
subordinates, 69% were male with an average age was 35.66 years old (SD 5 5.71). The most
frequently reported level of education for subordinates was a bachelor degree (77%).
Measures
All of the variables were used established measures and translated from English to Korean
by using the conventional method of back translation (Brislin, 1980). In order to minimize
potential common method bias effect (Podsakoff et al., 2003), employee creative behavior was
rated by the employees’ direct supervisor while other variables (i.e. leader’s boundary-
spanning behavior, need for status, creative self-efficacy and control variables) were rated by
the employees. We measured all the items on a seven-point Likert-type scale (Ranging from
1 5 strongly disagree to 7 5 strongly agree).
Leader boundary-spanning behavior. We assessed leader’s boundary-spanning behavior
using 6 items of Marrone et al.’s (2007). A sample item is, “My team leader reaches out to
individuals outside of our team that can provide project-related expertise or ideas”.
Need for status. Need for status was rated by the employees using 8 items taken from
Flynn et al. (2006). A sample item is, “Being a highly valued member of my social group is
important to me”.
Creative self-efficacy. We measured employees’ creative self-efficacy using 3 items of
Tierney and Farmer (2002) measure. A sample item is, “I have confidence in my ability to
solve problems creatively”.
Creative behavior. Employees’ creative behavior was appraised by his/her direct
supervisor using 13 items of Zhou and George (2001) measure. A sample item is, “This
subordinate comes up with new and practical ideas to improve performance”.
Control variables. Demographic variables including age, gender were measured from the
employees to reduce the possibilities of unexpected influencing to this study results. Age was
measured in years, gender was dummy coded (1 5 male, 2 5 female).

Discussion
Most previous studies on leadership have focused on leaders’ internal roles (Hughes et al.,
2018). Therefore, we highlight the critical role of leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior that
facilitates collaboration between actors inside and outside the organization to investigate this
gap in the leadership literature (Marrone et al., 2021; Salem et al., 2018). Specifically, we reveal
the positive effect of this behavior on employees’ creative behavior. Furthermore, applying
the componential model of creativity and an interactional perspective, we show a three-way
effect on creative behavior by exploring leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior, employees’
need for status, and employees’ creative self-efficacy.

innovation, and team viability (e.g. Kou, 2021), with only a few empirical studies examining the
significant impact of individual boundary spanning on outcomes (Marrone et al., 2021). There is
a scarcity of research examining the effect of leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior on

and how it positively affects employees’ creative behavior at the individual level. Future
research should investigate the effects of boundary-spanning behavior from specific actors in
the workplace. For example, it would be worthwhile to examining the role of coworker or
employee boundary spanning to enhance proactive behaviors such as voice behavior.
Boundary spanning employees are likely to speak up to improve the situations since they have

Second, our study contributes to the creative literature by exploring leaders’ boundary-
spanning behavior and contextual factors based on the componential model and an
interactional perspective of creativity. Our findings show that a boundary-spanning leader
may provide an opportunity for their employees to obtain domain-relevant skills, which helps
promote employees’ creative behavior. Moreover, we empirically test how leaders’ boundary-
spanning behavior, need for status, and creative self-efficacy, representing the three
components of domain-relevant skills, creativity-relevant processes, and task motivation,
respectively, influence employees’ creative behavior. Previous research has applied the
componential model of creativity and demonstrated how the components are critical in
influencing employees’ creative behavior (Kim et al., 2021). For instance, scholars have
identified high-commitment work systems as creative situational factors that enhance
domain-relevant skills, creativity-relevant processes, and task motivation (Chang et al., 2014).
Kwan et al. (2018) examined task resources and creative process engagement as creativity-
relevant processes that foster employees’ creative behavior. Hirst et al. (2009) noted that
learning-goal orientation could promote employees’ creative behavior by increasing domain-
relevant skills and task motivation. In this study, we explore all three components and
demonstrate the simultaneous interactions among these variables.
In addition, this study draws on theories of person–situation interactions and uses three-
way interactions to advance an understanding of employees’ creative behavior by examining
individual and situational factors. Cai et al. (2019) noted that creativity is predicted to be
highest when psychological capital, supervisor support for creativity, and job characteristics
are high. Shao et al. (2019) demonstrated how creative self-efficacy mediates the three-way
interactions between paradoxical leader behavior, workload pressure, and integrative
complexity on creativity. Zhang et al. (2017) demonstrated the three-way interactions
between job autonomy, psychological performance pressure, and learning-goal orientation in
predicting creativity. Based on prior studies, we have examined the need for status and
creative self-efficacy as individual factors, whereas leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior has
been explored as a situational factor. Future research should continue to apply the
componential model and an interactional perspective and investigate how various contextual
factors influence employees’ creative behavior.
Finally, our study contributes to the leadership literature by exploring leader-specific
behavior, such as boundary-spanning behavior. Most studies have focused on leaders’ internal
activities and their effects on various outcomes, including creative behavior (Mehmood et al.,
2021). For instance, Hughes et al. (2018) investigated transformational leadership, empowering
leadership, benevolent leadership, servant leadership, authentic leadership, and supervisor
support as thedeterminants of creative behavior. Although leaders’ external activities may have
positive impacts, there is a lack of research examining the role of leaders’ boundary-spanning
behavior in the workplace. Thus, our studyis meaningful as it demonstrates theeffect of leaders’
boundary-spanning behavior on creative behavior. Future research should investigate the roles
of leaders’ boundary-spanning behaviors in the workplace. It is possible that leaders’ boundary
spanning is positively related to various employee outcomes, including task performance,
organizational citizenship behavior, and knowledge-sharing behavior. Predictably, individuals

Practical implications
This research has important practical implications. First, our study shows that leaders’
boundary-spanning behavior is crucial in evoking employees’ creative behavior. Our findings
suggest that organizations should promote leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior, thereby
enhancing employees’ creative behavior. Based on our findings of the research, we recommend
that leaders should interact with individuals outside of their team to obtain various information
and ideas that could be transferred to their employees. Organizations should find specific ways
to promote leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior. For example, given its resource-intensive
nature, organizations should design structures and systems that could encourage boundary
spanning. Organization should provide instrumental and emotional support including incentives
or rewards for boundary spanning leaders.
In addition, organizations can help leaders develop their networks, a key antecedent of
boundary-spanning behavior (Brion et al., 2012), by providing opportunities to connect with
other actors to exchange diverse information and knowledge. They may consider holding
periodic meetings with diverse teams or units that encourage knowledge sharing practices.
Rotating jobs, teams or locations could be helpful to understand external parties and transfer
the information effectively. Also, conducting leadership training or development activities is
necessary to promote leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior. Specifically, it is critical to
develop communication and interpersonal skills that help sustain contact with other units
or teams.
It is important to build organizational culture valuing externally-oriented activities. If
organizational culture promotes flexibility, openness, and sharing knowledge, leaders may
feel easier to perform boundary-spanning behavior. Moreover, organizations should provide
autonomy for leaders to demonstrate boundary-spanning behaviors (e.g. Marrone et al., 2021).
When leaders do not have sufficient discretion to interact with external parties, it is difficult to
exhibit such behaviors.
Second, our study highlights the critical role of individual factors, such as the need for
status and creative self-efficacy, and situational factors that determine the level of creative
behavior. Gong et al. (2020) demonstrated how the diversity of social ties increases creative
self-efficacy and thereby, creativity. Their study highlights the importance of diversity in
social ties and the strength of such ties as predictors of creative self-efficacy. Thus, it may
be helpful for organizations to increase functional diversity to promote employees’ creative
self-efficacy, as providing resources is critical for boosting creative self-efficacy. Moreover,
prior research has shown the critical role of leadership styles, such as empowering and
transformational leadership, in enhancing creative self-efficacy (e.g. Puente-Dıaz, 2016).
Thus, organizations should train their leaders to build appropriate leadership styles to
promote creative self-efficacy among employees. This study’s findings suggest the vital
role of individual factors; it would be fruitful for organizations to consider individual
attributes, such as a high need for status or high creative self-efficacy, when selecting
employees and assigning jobs that require creative tasks.

Limitations and future research
Despite above contributions, this study has a few limitations. First, cross-sectional data of
this study precludes from inferring causality. It would be beneficial to conduct future
research implementing longitudinal or field-experimental research designs to ensure the
causality of the relationships examined in this study. Second, we cannot rule out a potential
risk of common method bias although we collected data from different sources. Third, our
research is designed as a focused study which included limited variables. Future research
should consider the effect of mediating variables and determine the mechanism between
leaders’ boundary-spanning and employees’ creative behaviors. For example, employees
working with boundary-spanning leaders may perceive diverse resources to promote
creative behavior.
Despite these limitations, this study sheds light on the understanding of boundary-
spanning behavior, particularly leaders’ boundary-spanning behavior, and its effects on
employees’ creative behavior. Applying the componential model and interactional
perspective of creativity, our study demonstrates the importance of developing creative
conditions by simultaneously providing domain-relevant skills, creativity-relevant
processes, and task motivation to promote employees’ creative behavior.

Business Administration, Incheon National University, Republic of Korea. Her current research interests
include leadership, proactivity, coworker influence and knowledge sharing. Her recent work appeared in
the Journal of Applied Psychology, Leadership Quarterly, Human Resource Management, Journal of
Business and Psychology, and other journals.
Donghwan Lee (MS, Seoul National University) is an assistant professor at the department of
business administration, Korea Military Academy, Republic of Korea. His current research interests
include empowering leadership, boundary spanning behavior, psychological safety, and other-
orientation. Donghwan Lee is the corresponding author and can be contacted at: hwan8739@snu.ac.kr
Seokhwa Yun (PhD, University of Maryland) is a professor at the College of Business
Administration, Seoul National University, Republic of Korea. His special areas of research include
leadership, employee’s extra-role behaviors, expatriation issues, and knowledge management. His
research has appeared in journals such as Journal of Applied Psychology, Academy of Management
Journal, Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, and Journal of International Business
Studies, to name a few.
