1. Introduction
Currently there is confusion in the marketing literature regarding
the viability of customer delight, which is de ﬁned as a “profoundly pos-
itive emotional state generally resulting from having one's expectations
exceeded ”(Oliver, Rust, & Varki, 1997, p. 329 ). Essentially the debate
has been evaluated from both a cost-oriented and customer-oriented
perspective. From the former, there is concern over rising customer
expectations and declining marginal returns that may exist with a
delight initiative (i.e., Rust & Oliver, 2000 ). From a customer-oriented
perspective, where customer satisfaction is considered the paramount
goal of the ﬁrm, delighting the customer is a logical and necessary
goal (i.e., Keiningham & Vavra, 2001 ).
A third perspective that may contribute to the debate is evaluating
customer delight from the viewpoint of frontline employees —those
service providers who have direct contact with customers. This is an
important perspective for several reasons: (1) employees are a key
component of Kotler's (1994) services marketing triangle model
which links the ﬁrm with customers; (2) there is evidence that the in-
teractional nature of the service encounter impacts employees as well
as customers (i.e., Barnes, Collier, Ponder, & Williams, 2013; Homburg
& Stock, 2005 ); and (3) employee attitudes and behaviors have beenlinked to ﬁrm success within the service –proﬁt chain framework
(Heskett, Jones, Loveman, Sasser, & Schlesinger, 1994 ).
Unfortunately, this important third perspective has not been consid-
ered in previous research on customer delight. As such, the exact nature
and power of the effects of delight that may reverberate through the
service –proﬁt chain remain unclear. In response to this gap in the
literature, this research has two main goals: ﬁrst, to provide theoretical
reasoning for why employee perceptions of customer delight can im-
pact the employee; and second, to formulate a structural model to em-
pirically demonstrate the impact of customer delight on the employee.
2. Literature review2.1. Why employee satisfaction matters
An important framework that helps to elucidate the importance of
frontline employees is the service –proﬁt chain, which establishes causal
relationships in a chain formation between internal service quality, the
creation of value, and external service quality ( Heskett et al., 1994 ). In
other words, the service –proﬁtc h a i n “asserts that satis ﬁed and motivat-
ed employees produce satis ﬁed customers and satis ﬁed customers tend
to purchase more ”(Gelade & Young, 2005, p. 2 ). Support for this frame-
work has been found across different contexts utilizing multiple
methods (i.e., Homburg, Wieseke, & Hoyer, 2009; Kamakura, Mittal,
De Rosa, & Mazzon, 2002; Maxham, Netemeyer, & Lichtenstein, 2008
).

vice–proﬁt chain, stating that the relationship of employee satisfaction
to customer satisfaction is “remarkable ”(Brown & Lam, 2008 ).Journal of Business Research 68 (2015) 433 –441

Another aspect of the service –proﬁt chain that is of paramount
importance is the link between the framework and ﬁrm pro ﬁtability.
For example, research by Homburg et al. (2009) utilizing a large-scale
triadic data set provides support for an “extended ”service –proﬁt
chain which links service quality to important customer (loyalty and
willingness to pay) and ﬁrm ( ﬁnancial performance) outcomes.
Utilizing perceived ﬁnancial performance, Yee, Yeung, and Cheng
(2011) provide evidence for a positive link between the service –proﬁt
chain and Return On Assets, Return On Sales, and Return On Investment.
One avenue of research that has not been evaluated within the
service –proﬁt chain literature is situations in which employees experi-
ence elevated levels of positive affect. This is especially important
because employee positive affect has been linked to important out-
comes such as job satisfaction, affective commitment and customer-
oriented behaviors. If frontline employees experience higher levels of
positive affect because of perceived customer delight, they would act
as springboards for value creation within the service –proﬁtc h a i n .
2.2. Does customer delight impact the employee?
Because of the interactional nature of a service encounter, employee
perceptions of customer delight should impact the employee. Two
theories, emotional contagion ( Hatﬁeld, Cacioppo, & Rapson, 1994 )
and broaden-and-build ( Fredrickson, 1998, 2001 ), help to explain this
connection.
Emotional contagion refers to situations where “exposure to an
individual expressing positive or negative emotions can produce a cor-
responding change in the emotional state of the observer ”(Pugh, 2001 ,
p. 1020). Even in situations characterized by minimal contact, “emo-
tions and attitudinal states can pass between Person A (the initiator)
and Person B (the recipient) and leave a permanent trace ”(Stock and
Hoyer, 2005 , p. 540). This phenomenon has appeared in customer set-
tings, where customers who are exposed to the emotional displays of
employees experience corresponding changes in their own affective
states ( Gountas, Ewing, & Gountas, 2007; Hennig-Thurau, Groth, Paul,
& Gremler, 2006; Pugh, 2001 ). For example, customers in a bank setting
exhibited higher levels of positive affect and gave higher ratings of
service quality when confronted with positive displays of emotion
from the employee ( Pugh, 2001 ). Furthermore, and in direct relation-
ship to the present research, the contagion phenomenon that exists
from employee to customer also exists from customer to employee in
satisfactory encounters ( Homburg & Stock, 2004 ). Thus, the transfer of
emotions in satisfactory encounters can ﬂow in both directions.
To help understand the ﬂow of contagious positive emotions from
the customer to the employee, we utilize a developing theory called
the broaden-and-build theory ( Fredrickson, 1998, 2001 ). This theory ex-
plains how positive emotions can broaden people's momentary
thought –action repertoires and build their enduring resources, which
range from physical and intellectual resources to social and psychological
resources ( Fredrickson, 2001, 2003 ). Although discrete emotions (i.e.,
delight) can be temporary, their effects can be long term ( Fredrickson,2001 ). Support for the broaden-and-build theory is found in manage-
ment research that links positive affect to ﬂexible, creative, and ef ﬁcient
employees who are open to information and are better problem solvers
(i.e.,Isen, 2008; Isen & Baron, 1991 ).
Taken together, these two theories provide an enticing prediction for
why perceived customer delight will impact the employee (see Fig. 1 ).
In the ﬁrst step, employees who are exposed to perceived customer de-
light mirror these positive emotions (i.e., increases in employee positive
affect vis-à-vis emotional contagion). In the second step, through a
broadening and building of the employee's resources, these positive
emotions are changed into resources (i.e., job satisfaction, commitment,
and customer orientation) which the employee can access.
3. Model and hypotheses
Based on the stated goals of our research, we develop a model
presented in Fig. 2 that examines the direct effects of employee percep-
tions of customer delight on employee positive affect, and the indirect
effects of these perceptions on employee attitudes and behaviors. In
the following section, a brief description of each construct and its
relevance to the current study is given.
3.1. Employee perceptions of customer delight →employee positive affect
Positive affect re ﬂects the “extent to which a person feels enthusias-
tic, active, and alert ”(Watson, Clark, & Tellegen, 1988, p. 1063 ), and can
be measured as either a trait or a state. The former represents an endur-
ing personality trait that “predisposes people to experience positive
emotions and moods as well as to have a positive outlook and orienta-
tion”(George & Brief, 1992, p. 318 ). The latter refers to a more transient
affective state, and “captures how one feels at a given point in time ”
(George & Brief, 1992, p. 318 ).
There are several reasons why it is important to study the effects of
providing customer delight on the state of employee positive affect: (1)
states can be determined by situational factors such as service encoun-
ters ( George & Brief, 1992 ); (2) positive affect as a state is shown as a
signiﬁcant predictor of customer service behaviors for employees,
whereas positive affect as a trait is not ( George, 1991 ); (3) states are ex-
pected to have profound effects on thought processes and behaviors in
organizational settings ( George & Brief, 1992 ); and (4) states can have
duration and breadth to in ﬂuence outcomes long after they occur
(Pelled & Xin, 1999 ).
As previously stated, the basis for the link between employee
perceptions of customer delight and employee positive affect is found
in theories related to emotional contagion ( Hatﬁeld et al., 1994 ). Be-
cause of the importance of positive employee emotions to service
quality evaluations ( Pugh, 2001 )a n d ﬁrm success ( Hennig-Thurau
et al., 2006 ), quantifying the effect of contagion effects in a delightful en-
counter is an important step for service research. Thus,

3.2. Employee positive affect →job satisfaction
Similar to previous service research (i.e., Hartline & Ferrell, 1996 ),
Locke's (1976) conceptualization of job satisfaction “as a pleasurable
or positive emotional state resulting from a person's appraisal of his or
her job or job experiences ”is utilized (p. 1300). The link between
employee positive affect and job satisfaction has been well established
in academic literature. For example, a meta-analysis revealed a high
adjusted correlation between job satisfaction and positive affect (r =
0.34) ( Thoresen, Kaplan, Barsky, Warren, & de Chermont, 2003 ).
Weiss and Cropanzano (1996) also provide support for the power of
affect on employee attitudes such as job satisfaction. Relating directly
to this research, Niklas and Dormann (2005) found that state affect
leads to generalized job satisfaction. Thus,
H2.Employee positive affect has a positive in ﬂuence on job satisfaction.
3.3. Employee positive affect →affective commitment, and job
satisfaction →affective commitment
Organizational commitment is de ﬁned as “the strength of an
individual's identi ﬁcation with and involvement in a particular organiza-
tion”(Porter, Steers, Mowday, & Boulian, 1974, p. 604 ). Although related
to job satisfaction, the two constructs differ in that “job satisfaction is
viewed as a re ﬂection of immediate reactions to the workplace, (where-
as) organizational commitment is believed to develop more slowly as
employees learn more about their job and organization ”(Parish, Berry,
& Lam, 2008, p. 225 ). Empirical research supports this distinction by
showing a causal relationship from job satisfaction to organizational
commitment ( Parish et al., 2008 ).
Similar to previous service research (i.e., Dean, 2007; Evanschitzky,
Iyer, Plassman, Niessing, & Meffert, 2006; Paulin, Ferguson, &
Bergeron, 2006 ), the component of organizational commitment that is
of interest in this context is affective commitment. Theoretical support
for why affective commitment alone is an appropriate measure of com-
mitment has been provided in previous service research (i.e., Dean,
2007 ). Additional support for a focus on affective commitment as the
key form of commitment comes from research that shows a strong rela-
tionship between affective commitment and employee performance
(Malhotra & Mukherjee, 2003; Meyer & Allen, 1991 ); organizational cit-
izenship behaviors ( Organ & Ryan, 1995 ); and customer-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors ( Bettencourt & Brown, 2003 ).A strong relationship between affective commitment and employee
tenure has also been demonstrated ( Malhotra & Mukherjee, 2003;
Meyer & Allen, 1991 ). Furthermore, there is a correlation between
positive affect and affective commitment ( Herrbach, 2006 ). Finally,
evidence suggests that job satisfaction is an antecedent to customer ori-
entation ( Bateman & Organ, 1983 ) and organizational commitment
(i.e., Parish et al., 2008; Porter et al., 1974 ). Thus,
H3.Employee positive affect has a positive in ﬂuence on employee af-
fective commitment.
H4.Job satisfaction has a positive in ﬂuence on employee affective
commitment.
3.4. Customer-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors
Customer orientation is a corner stone of competitive advantage
and occurs when the ﬁrm is engaged in the organization-wide gener-
ation of, dissemination of, and responsiveness to market intelligence(Brady & Cronin, 2001; Narver & Slater, 1990 ). Importantly, custom-
er orientation is positively related to pro ﬁtability ( Narver & Slater,
1990 ). An essential aspect of developing a customer orientation for
the service ﬁrm is the ability of its workers to engage in boundary-
spanning roles that link the ﬁrm with the external environment
(Bettencourt & Brown, 2003; Bettencourt, Brown, & MacKenize,
2005 ). These boundary-spanning roles allow the ﬁrm to “acquire
and assimilate the information necessary to design and execute
marketing strategies that result in more favorable customer
outcomes ”(Brady & Cronin, 2001, p. 241 ).
Bettencourt and Brown (2003) andBettencourt et al. (2005) high-
light three boundary-spanning employee behaviors that contribute
to a customer orientation: (1) external representation, (2) internal
inﬂuence, and (3) service delivery. These variables appear in our struc-
tural model as dependent variables.

3.6. Internal in ﬂuence behaviors
Internal in ﬂuence behaviors occur when employees take individual
initiative in communications to the ﬁrm and coworkers to improve ser-
vice delivery by the organization, coworkers, and oneself ( Bettencourt
&B r o w n ,2 0 0 3 ). For example, an employee could recommend how to
perform a speci ﬁc service task, or make recommendations based on
their previous success. Thus, these behaviors lead to the development
of knowledge and organizational learning, which has been referred to
as the only true source of competitive advantage ( Lusch, Vargo, &
O'Brien, 2007 ).
Employees are expected to engage in higher levels of internal in ﬂu-
ence behaviors for themselves and for others after they have experi-
enced a delightful encounter. Social identity theory ( Tajfel & Turner,
1985 ) offers a social psychology perspective on employee behavior
and provides the theoretical underpinnings for why this is likely to
occur. Employees identify with the ﬁrm and develop a self-concept
from the ﬁrm's interactions ( Ashforth & Mael, 1989 ). Thus, positive
and negative ﬁrm interactions are analogous to personal successes
and failures. As such, employees who are satis ﬁed and committed are
motivated to engage in practices that are most bene ﬁcial for the service
ﬁrm, and by default themselves.
In the context of this research, our model suggests that em-
ployees will experience positive affect after they perceive delight
from the customer. Because the employee connects these states to
the delightful encounter, it is expected that the employee notes
which factors contributed to the delightful encounter and then
stockpiles these factors for future use. Positive affect also leads to
protecting the organization and helping co-workers ( George &
Brief, 1992 ), which represents internal in ﬂuence behaviors. For ex-
ample, employees in positive moods were more likely to be helpful,
regardless of whether the helpful behavior in question was part of
their job responsibilities or was above and beyond the call of duty
(George, 1991 ). Thus,
H8.Employee positive affect has a positive in ﬂuence on internal
inﬂuence behaviors.
H9.Employee affective commitment has a positive in ﬂuence on
internal in ﬂuence behaviors.
H10. Job satisfaction has a positive in ﬂuence on internal in ﬂuence
behaviors.3.7. Service delivery behaviors
Service delivery behaviors occur when employees serve customers
in a conscientious, responsive, attentive, and courteous manner
(Bettencourt & Brown, 2003 ). As an example of the power of these fac-
tors, conceptually similar concepts constitute the seminal SERVQUAL
model in previous research ( Parasuraman, Zeithaml, & Berry, 1988 ),
and have been linked to important outcomes for the ﬁrm such as rela-
tionship formation and the development of interpersonal bonds
(Gremler & Gwinner, 2000 ).
This research predicts that job satisfaction, affective commitment,
and employee positive affect all in ﬂuence service delivery behaviors.
The relationship between job satisfaction and service quality behaviors
has been well documented (i.e., Brown & Lam, 2008; Hartline & Ferrell,
1996 ). Similarly, affective commitment will lead to increases in service
delivery behaviors on the basis of social identity theory previously
discussed. Finally, this model predicts that increases in employee posi-
tive affect will be re ﬂected in increases of service delivery behaviors be-
cause of the “feel good –do good ”link established in management
literature (i.e., George & Brief, 1992 ). That is, employees in positive
moods have a greater ability to “engage others in conversation ”
(Pelled & Xin, 1999, p. 879 ), deliver conscientious service, and improve
performance ( Staw, Sutton, & Pelled, 1994 ). Thus,
H11. Employee positive affect has a positive in ﬂuence on service
delivery behaviors.
H12. Affective commitment has a positive in ﬂuence on service delivery
behaviors.
H13. Job satisfaction has a positive in ﬂuence on service delivery
behaviors.
4. Method
4.1. Research context and sample
A primary goal herein is to gain a cross-section of service employees
with the objective of reducing extraneous in ﬂuence and increasing the
generalizability of ﬁndings. To better categorize the industries repre-
sented, Bowen's (1990) taxonomy of services was utilized ( Gwinner,
Gremler, & Bitner, 1998 ). In this taxonomy, three groupings of service
ﬁrms exist: Group 1, those services directed at people and characterized
by high customer contact with individually customized service solu-
tions (e.g., ﬁnancial consulting, medical care, and hair care services);
Group 2, services directed at an individual's property, in which moder-
ate to low customer contact is the norm and the service can be custom-
ized only slightly (e.g., shoe repair, retail banking, and pest control); and
Group 3, services typically directed at people that provide standardized
service solutions and have moderate customer contact (e.g., airlines,
movie theaters, and grocery stores).
In order to obtain this cross-section of service employees, a non-
probability snowball sampling technique was orchestrated whereby
trained recruiters were required to solicit responses to the survey.
The recruiting pool consisted of 80 senior level marketing research
students at a large Southeastern university. Training included a
thorough discussion of recruiting and screening of potential subjects,
as well as an extensive discussion on the importance of developing
an authentic sample. Each recruiter received a cover sheet with in-
structions on the type of service employees they were designated
to recruit. Furthermore, detailed descriptions of each group within
the taxonomy were provided along with speci ﬁc examples. Recruiters
were encouraged to enlist employees from each of the service
categories established by Bowen (1990) . Recruiters were eligible for
1/2 percentage point per survey on a graded assignment with a
maximum of 5% (n = 10 surveys) bonus.436 D.C. Barnes et al. / Journal of Business Research 68 (2015) 433 –441
Recruiters were asked to direct respondents to an online survey
which was opened for a period of 14 days. As is the case with previous
research utilizing this method, steps were taken to encourage authentic
responses (see Gremler and Gwinner, 2008 , p. 311). For the purpose of
veriﬁcation, the survey asked respondents to provide their ﬁrst name
and a telephone number. Respondents were further alerted to the fact
that they may be contacted in order to assure authenticity. All of theindividuals contacted veri ﬁed their identity and responses. The ﬁnal
sample size was 431, including 183 respondents from Group 1, 138
from Group 2, and 110 from Group 3 of Bowen's taxonomy. Of the
total, 54% were female and the average age was 29 years.
As shown in Fig. 2 , the proposed model contains seven latent
variables, each consisting of multi-item measures. As in previous service
employee research, self-rating scales were used for all measures. Further,
all of the measures were adapted from scales that had proved reliable in
previous studies (see Table 1 ). A draft survey was pilot-tested on cus-
tomer contact employees as well as a convenience sample of academics
to establish evidence of face validity. No problems were identi ﬁed.
4.2. Measures
Table 1 contains a list of items utilized. Employee perceptions of
customer delight were measured using three re ﬂective indicators of
delight: gleeful, elated, and delighted. Similar measures of customer de-
light have been accepted in recent literature (i.e., Wang, 2011 ). For em-
ployee perceptions of customer delight, respondents were asked to give
their perception of the extent to which these emotions existed in the
customer after a speci ﬁc employee –customer encounter. Because this
research is based on the employee perception of customer emotions,
measuring customer delight in this manner is appropriate.Job satisfaction was measured using a four-item measure adapted
from previous service research ( Johlke, Duhan, Howell, & Wilkes,
2000 ). The scale items re ﬂect overall work satisfaction rather than any
speciﬁc dimensions of work satisfaction. Measuring satisfaction at this
level, as opposed to the speci ﬁc facets of the job, is recommended
when investigating the transference of emotions between customers
and employees (i.e., Brown & Lam, 2008 ).
Employee positive affect was measured using three items re ﬂective
of how employees may feel after a delightful service encounter: enthu-
siastic, delighted, and excited. These three items were selected because
they speci ﬁcally reference emotions related to the employee's work.
Affective commitment was measured using ﬁve items from the
Organizational Commitment Questionnaire of Mowday, Steers, and
Porter (1979) . Similar scale items have been used in previous service re-
search (i.e., Dean, 2007 ). The scale “captures identi ﬁcation with the
company (pride in belonging), psychological attachment (sense of
belonging), concern about the long term success of the company, and
feelings of loyalty ”(Dean, 2007, p. 166 ).
The customer-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors were mea-
sured using three components previously employed in service research
(
Bettencourt & Brown, 2003 ). External representation behaviors were
measured using a three-item scale designed to capture the extent to
which an employee is a vocal advocate to outsiders of the organization's
image, goods, and services. Internal in ﬂuence behaviors were measured
with a three-item scale intended to measure the extent to which a per-
son takes individual initiative in communicating to the ﬁrm and co-
workers about ways to improve service delivery by the organization,
co-workers, and oneself. Service delivery behaviors were measured
with a three-item scale that re ﬂects the extent to which the employee
serves customers in a conscientious, responsive, ﬂexible, and courteous
manner.

4.3. Measure validation procedures
Anderson and Gerbing's (1988) recommended two-step procedure
for testing the hypothesized model was utilized here. First, a seven-
factor correlated measurement model was estimated using LISREL
8.72. Results from the measurement model revealed one problem. A
high modi ﬁcation index indicated that the error terms for two satisfac-
tion items were related ( “My job was valuable ”and“Iw a sd o i n gs o m e -

6. Discussion
There has been much discussion within the marketing literature on
the feasibility of aiming for customer delight. While previous research
showed mixed results, the employee's perspective of a delight initiative
had not yet been evaluated. As such, the main goal of this research was
to empirically investigate the impact of employee perceived customerdelight. Importantly, our discussion and implications rest on the notion
that employee perceptions are germane to what the employee feels and
ultimately how the employee behaves on the job. Because previous re-
search notes that employees are an excellent source of data for ﬁrms
(Schneider, Macey, Lee, & Young, 2009 ), and that their perceptions of
customer satisfaction correlate with customers ( Schneider & Bowen,
1985 ), we believe that evaluating employee perceptions offers impor-
tant insights to both practitioners and researchers (please see Table 4

A majority of previous research investigating emotional contagion
has evaluated the effects of employee emotions on customers (for an
exception see Homburg & Stock, 2004 ). The present research provides
evidence that this effect also exists in the opposite direction. By showing
this link we answer previous calls to research to investigate if and how
perceived customer emotions can impact the employee (i.e., Barsade &
Gibson, 2007; Fredrickson, 2001 ). Furthermore, to the authors' knowl-
edge, no research has empirically evaluated the transfer of emotions
from customers to employees in situations characterized as delightful .
Once again, this research ﬁnds support for the transfer of perceived
elevated positive emotions between groups. In other words, contagion
effects can rise above satisfaction in either direction. This is an impor-
tantﬁnding as previous research has shown that positive emotions
like pleasure (i.e. delight) are very in ﬂuential on employee attitudes
(Mignonac & Herrbach, 2004 ).
The transfer of emotions from the customer to the employee is espe-
cially important when utilizing broaden-and-build theory as a frame-
work. The key idea espoused by broaden-and-build theory is that
positive emotions are essential to human ﬂourishing, and that positive
emotions act as “vehicles for individual growth and social connection …”
(Fredrickson, 2001, p. 224 ). Positive emotions help individuals to
ﬂourish, even when dealing in dif ﬁcult situations (i.e., Tugade &
Fredrickson, 2004 ). With the dynamic and challenging environment in
which frontline employees often ﬁnd themselves, positive emotions
are an important area to study for service researchers. In the current
research, we provide support for the main tenets of this theory by
showing that employee positive affect leads to the building and broad-
ening of frontline employee attitudes and skills.
Fig. 2 also contributes to the ongoing debate in the management lit-
erature on what causes job performance. The results support the “feel
good –do good ”link previously discussed by George and Brief (1992)
among others. That is, what really causes performance is how the em-
ployee feels, which may or may not be directly related to job satisfac-
tion. As Weiss and Cropanzano (1996) state: “affective states in ﬂuence
performance and job satisfaction but their performance implications
are, by and large, independent of their relationships with satisfaction ”
(p. 65).Another important ﬁnding of this research is that the antecedent
variables that affect different customer-oriented boundary-spanning
behaviors vary. For example, employees with higher levels of job satis-
faction are more likely to engage in service delivery behaviors, whereas
to engage in internal in ﬂuence or external representation, employees
must ﬁrst be affectively committed to the ﬁrm. Although this relation-
ship was not predicted, there is support in the literature for this unex-pected ﬁnding. Employees see certain types of customer-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors as extra-role behaviors, while other
types of customer-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors as role-
prescribed behaviors. The former represent actions on which employees
are not evaluated, nor are they behaviors that represent a part of their
formal job descriptions ( Brief & Motowidlo, 1986 ). On the other hand,
role-prescribed behaviors represent expected employee actions and
behaviors in serving the ﬁrm's customers ( Brief & Motowidlo, 1986 ).
Such behaviors include “exhibiting common courtesy, demonstrating
accurate knowledge of policies and products, addressing customers by
name, greeting and saying ‘thank you ’to customers ”(Bettencourt &
Brown, 1997, p. 42 ). These types of behaviors are “derived from implicit
norms in the workplace or from explicit obligations as speci ﬁed in orga-
nizational documents ”(Bettencourt & Brown, 1997, p. 42 ).
The present research also contributes to the customer delight litera-
ture. A main ﬁnding was that an unintended effect of aiming for custom-
er delight may be employee delight. The effects from this latter delight
should then echo through the service –proﬁt chain, which has been
shown to be an accurate framework linking employee satisfaction to
customer satisfaction and ﬁrm pro ﬁt (i.e., Brown & Lam, 2008;
Homburg et al., 2009 ). As such, one of the biggest outcomes of pursuing
customer delight has not yet been considered in the debate on the value
of it.
Finally, the value of human resource management for a service ﬁrm
cannot be overstated. In service encounters where employee –customer
interactions are important quality indicators, it is imperative to hire and
train effectively. Identifying those employees who have a greater ability
to experience contagion effects from the customer, as well as placing
those employees in situations where they can delight the customer rep-
resent important challenges to the service ﬁrm. This should include

6.1. Limitations and future research
Although the current research contributes to the understanding of
how customer emotions affect employee behaviors and attitudes,
there are limitations that should be acknowledged. The most signi ﬁcant
potential limitations deal with the operationalization and measurement
of the exogenous construct of employee perceptions of customer de-
light: ﬁrst, this construct represents a very narrow representation of
positive customer emotions. In future research, a broader spectrum of
emotions should be included in evaluating the model in Fig. 2 .S e c o n d ,
the factor loading of 0.63 for the “delight ”item falls below the ideal
0.70 threshold. Third, our conceptualization excluded non-delighted
customers from analysis. This may represent a bias as delighted cus-
tomers represent a smaller proportion of the customer base in compar-
ison with satis ﬁed customers. Fourth, we utilized three measures of
customer delight to represent the sole exogenous construct in our
model, and any excluded exogenous constructs that are correlated
with customer delight could bias all of the parameter estimates. Thus,
model underspeci ﬁcation is a possibility. Fifth, this study assessed em-
ployee perceptions of customer delight without verifying that delight
had occurred from the customer's point-of-view. Because our focus
was onlyon employee perceptions of customer delight, the actual occur-
rence of delight from the customer's viewpoint was not germane to this
research. However, future studies could evaluate the correlation be-
tween employee and customer perceptions of delight with dyadic
data. Aside from potential issues with the exogenous variable, data
were collected utilizing multiple recruiters as well as with a snowballing
procedure, both of which have weaknesses. For example, a selectionbias may exist in the data set. Finally, with regard to causality, data
were collected in a cross-sectional manner; a stronger case for causality
would be made with longitudinal or experimental data.
Aside from these limitations, there are many avenues of future re-
search that can develop from the ﬁndings presented. For example,
there are several topics relating to emotional contagion that warrant
further research, including developing an emotional contagion scale
for the service environment; illustrating how long the effects of conta-
gion last; whether contagion differs across employee populations.
Furthermore, this research considered only positive emotions; it
would be interesting to evaluate how negative emotions relate to the
relationships shown in our model.
Another interesting area of future research would be to evaluate dif-
ferent outcome variables in comparison with customer-oriented
boundary-spanning behaviors. For example, adaptability is an impor-
tant component in service quality evaluations ( Gwinner, Bitner,
Brown, & Kumar, 2005 ), and a positive relationship likely exists be-
tween employees in an elevated mood state and ability and desire to en-
gage in adapting behaviors for the bene ﬁto ft h e ﬁrm/customer. Future
research could investigate the impact of emotional contagion on non-
frontline employees, such as repairmen or auto workers. Is the ﬂow of
positive emotions from customer to employee as strong if there is no di-
rectcontact between the two? Finally, future research should incorpo-
rate a quantitative measure of pro ﬁt in relation to the impact of
perceived customer delight. As relevant research has shown a link be-
tween customer delight and variables such as willingness to pay (i.e.,Barnes, Beauchamp, & Webster, 2010 ), it is likely that a positive rela-
tionship exists between customer delight and ﬁrm pro ﬁ
t.
