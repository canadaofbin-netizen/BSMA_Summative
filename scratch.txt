Huiying Zhang, Man Yang and Baofeng Huo
College of Management and Economics, Tianjin University, Tianjin, China
Abstract
Purpose –In the field of innovation, there is growing interest in exploring the factors that determine the extent
to which firms can learn from external sources. However, most previous studies neglect the role of human
factors. Little is known about which employee behaviors are desirable for boundary-spanning learning
activities and which human resource management (HRM) practices are appropriate to respond to externalknowledge transfer across boundaries. To fill this gap, the authors investigate the role of empowerment-
focused HRM in interfirm learning and explore the integration of external inputs from the perspective of
employees.
Design/methodology/approach –Based on empirical survey data collected from different countries, the
authors test the proposed model with structural equation modeling.
Findings –The authors ’findings indicate that empowerment-focused HRM practices, including job
enrichment, job autonomy, teamwork and cross-functional communication, are positively associated with
relationship learning.
Originality/value –In this study, the authors present a theoretical explanation for how empowerment-
focused HRM may influence firm ’s innovation through relationship learning process and provide empirical
evidence regarding the specific HRM practices that can have different effects on the different phases of
relationship learning.
Keywords Empowerment-focused HRM, Relationship learning, Innovation
Paper type Research paper
1. Introduction
In recent years, leveraging external knowledge for innovation has attracted significant
attention in both research and practice ( West and Bogers, 2014 ). By learning from external
partners, firms can obtain new knowledge and resources to accelerate innovation in rapidly
changing environments ( Fang et al., 2011 ). For example, many firms from the Asia –Pacific
region, such as HTC, Acer, Haier and other original design manufacturers and original
equipment manufacturers, have developed their own innovative capabilities by leveraging
relationship learning mechanisms with their partners ( Bryan and Sinkovics, 2010 ).
Relationship learning, which involves a series of boundary-spanning learning activities, is
viewed as an effective mechanism that allows firms to learn from partners to increase their
knowledge stock ( Fang et al., 2011 ). Although the importance of sourcing external knowledge
from interfirm learning is widely discussed and recognized, the factors that explain why some
firms outperform others when learning from external partners remain largely unexplored.
Prior research often discusses interorganizational mechanisms of external learning, such asstrategic alliances, industrial clusters and social embeddedness. Little is known about the role
of firms ’internal organization mechanisms in the whole external learning process ( Ooms
et al., 2015 ).Foss et al.(2011) argued that firms ’internal practices should be taken into accountEmpowerment-

Research on relationship learning has shown knowledge inflows and integration at the
level of organizational boundary. However, these studies did not examine the role of
employees in knowledge flows across the boundary of firms. Cohen and Levinthal (1990)
noted that obtaining and integrating external knowledge is dependent on the individuals
working. External and internal sources of innovation only connect when employees engage in
boundary-spanning activities. Bogers et al. (2018) considered that the role of employees is a
research gap in exploring how firms rely on external sources to accelerate innovation
processes. de Zubielqui et al. (2019) proposed that firms that allow employees to interact with
external holders of knowledge are better at disseminating externally sourced knowledge
within the organization. Thus, employees ’behavior, attitude and knowledge base may
influence external learning. Drawing upon the behavioral perspective, human resource
management (HRM) practices can influence and align employee behaviors, and different
behaviors are required for different objectives that firms expect to achieve ( Jiang et al., 2013 ).
However, there is a lack of systematic research on what employee behaviors are desirable in
boundary-spanning activities and which HRM practices are appropriate to support
relationship learning.
Current studies find that access to external knowledge is no longer dependent on
centralized “gatekeeping ”roles, while boundary-spanning roles that can obtain external
knowledge are becoming more distributed across organizations ( Bogers et al., 2018 ). Given
the increasingly decentralized information inflow, some studies assert that control-oriented
or“centralized ”structures may hamper informal interconnections and lead to information
transmission leaks and delays, and slow decision-making is punished under dynamic
environmental conditions ( Foss et al., 2011 ).Murphy and Salomone (2013) also concluded that
organizations must give up some managerial control to enable a more diverse and larger
group of people to connect with external holders of knowledge. To foster the flow of
information and knowledge across boundaries and hierarchies, firms are expected to develop
empowerment-focused HRM, which refers to “the delegation of responsibility from
management to employees, nonhierarchical forms of work organization and sharing of
information between, and within, different levels of organization ”(Harley, 1999 ;Kazlauskaite
et al., 2012 ). Empowerment-focused HRM involves a bundle of human resource practices that
empower employees to use their knowledge and skills to achieve organizational objectives(Subramony, 2009 ;Jiang et al., 2013 ).
Therefore, the purpose of this study is to explore the role of empowerment-focused HRM in
improving relationship learning and subsequent innovation performance. Our study makes
three contributions to the literature. First, in response to the call made by Foss et al. (2011) for
further research on the appropriate intraorganizational response to a much wider range of
external knowledge inputs, we introduce HRM into the research on cross-organizational
learning. Second, we enrich the understanding of empowerment-focused HRM by identifying
empowering practices at the individual level and the organizational level. Third, most
existing research regards three phases of interfirm relationship learning as independent or
parallel, and little is known about differences and relationships between the processes of
acquisition, interaction and integration of external knowledge. Our study shows that the
improvement of different phases of relationship learning requires different forms of human
resource support.

2.1 Empowerment-focused HRM
In a stable environment, bureaucratic HRM or control-oriented HRM systems can maintain
employee alignment with the institutional context by

However, due to rapid changes in technology and increasing global competition, openness to
external sources is becoming an essential part of competitiveness ( Flor et al., 2018 ). In this
context, Shipton et al. (2017) pointed out that the HRM configuration based on open networks
and structures that emphasize job design and empowerment is the opposite of control-

decision-making down to where the information exists can increase the capacity to process
information and reduce the cost of transmitting and receiving information ( Galbraith, 1974 ).
Thus, the empowerment of employees who are close to external sources can help them rapidly
interact with outside parties and process external information effectively. In addition,
providing employees with more opportunities to participate in decision-making can increase
the number of a firm ’s interfaces with its external environment and increase employees ’
willingness to absorb and share external knowledge. In this sense, empowerment-focused
HRM, which refers to granting authority to employees, is likely to be beneficial for firms that
attempt to leverage external knowledge efficiently.
Empowerment practices are designed to share power with subordinates. It can influence
employees ’perceptions and behaviors by creating an environment where employees perceive
to be psychologically empowered ( Jiang et al. , 2013 ). In this study, we recognize these
empowerment-focused HRM practices from a multilevel perspective. The literature suggests
that the influence of empowerment practices is fundamentally a multilevel phenomenon
(Maynard et al., 2012 ). The word “power ”at the individual level has two underlying meanings –
“authority to do something ”and “ability to make something happen ”(Kazlauskaite et al., 2012 ).
The former relates to the practice of job autonomy, which describes the degree of independence
and freedom that employees experience in how they perform their tasks and roles ( Bos-Nehles
et al.,2 0 1 7 ). The latter refers to job enrichment, which can help employees develop a multitude
of skills and gain a variety of experiences by providing a wide range of work content
(Subramony, 2009 ). At the individual level, empowerment HRM practices focus on granting
employees authority and rich skills to help them perform well in their individual work. In
contrast, the organizational level of empowerment HRM practices focuses on coordination and
collaboration, such as “decentralized decision making through teamwork, ”“problem-solving
groups to encourage feedback ”and “increasing communication between different levels of
functions ”(Jiang et al.,2 0 1 3 ).Harley (1999) argued that empowerment is not only the degree of
decision-making power at the individual level but also the “empowering ”forms of work
organization, which include team-based work and various communication mechanisms.
2.2 Relationship learning
Relationship learning has been described as an interfirm learning activity by which firms can
learn from their partners to increase their knowledge base and develop innovative
capabilities ( Selnes and Sallis, 2003 ;Chen et al., 2009 ). Although firms increasingly rely on
external knowledge and resources to improve internal innovation, it does not imply that
external knowledge can be transferred easily across organizational boundaries. Implicitly,
the connection to external sources requires boundary-spanning activities based on a certain
degree of relationship. A close relationship is the foundation of substantial knowledge
exchange between exchanging parties ( Fang et al., 2011 ). Thus, relationship learning as joint
activities between supply chain partners provides a viable and effective mechanism to bring
external knowledge into firms.

2.3 Empowerment-focused HRM and relationship learning
2.3.1 Empowerment-focused HRM and information sharing. Information sharing, as a
starting point for relationship learning, enables companies to obtain crucial information and
knowledge from their partners for innovation ( Selnes and Sallis, 2003 ;Chen et al., 2009 ). Only
when information and knowledge are sufficiently and efficiently transferred across
organizational boundaries can firms utilize and integrate this information and knowledge
to support innovation ( Fang et al., 2011 ). However, the input flow of external information and
knowledge into firms is not an automatic or easy process ( Flor et al. , 2018 ).Cohen and
Levinthal (1990) noted that “a fairly broad range of prospective ‘receptors ’to the
environment ”is important when firms contact external sources of knowledge. Subsequent
studies suggested that exposure to external sources through a variety of receptors can
contribute to contact with diverse external knowledge ( Jansen et al., 2005 ). The receptors have
two meanings: one is the number of employees exposed to external sources, and the other is
the knowledge diversity possessed by these employees ( Cohen and Levinthal, 1990 ;Lane
et al., 2006 ).Foss et al. (2011) proposed that employees who work directly with external actors
are the best people to judge, absorb and pass on inputs from external environment. These
employees need to be able to make decisions and implement them rapidly in activities
involving external learning.
Job enrichment can develop multiskilling and enrich knowledge structures of employees
(Patterson et al., 2004 ). By performing multiple tasks and receiving a variety of training,
employees obtain a considerable breadth of knowledge and skills that help them to perform
complex jobs. Based on the theory of human capital, Bogers et al. (2018) theorize that
“employees ’knowledge bases can be important determinants of firms ’ability to access
external sources of knowledge for innovation. ”As mentioned earlier, increasing the number
of interfaces and the knowledge diversity of employees can improve the firm ’s receptivity,
which may facilitate identification and acquisition of external information and knowledge.
Thus, we propose ( Figure 1 ) the following:
H1a. Job enrichment is positively related to information sharing.
In addition, de Zubielqui et al. (2019) suggested that employees who are independent and
free to determine how to do their work interact more with external resources. It seems that a
high degree of autonomy may increase the number of interfaces a firm has with its external
environment. Job autonomy, as an empowerment HRM practice at the individual level,
determines the extent to which employees pa rticipate in decision-making as well as the
automatic right to intera ct with outside parties ( Bos-Nehles et al., 2017 ). Teamwork, as a
practice at the organizational level, also provides employees with more opportunities to
take part in the decision-making process and express their views and ideas freely
(Wilkinson, 1998 ;Subramony, 2009 ). The logic of teamwork is similar to that of jobIMDS

Employees who possess diverse and different knowledge structures can promote joint

Joint sensemaking includes a series of interactive activities that rely on cross-organizational
collaboration ( Cheung et al., 2011 ). These interorganizational interactions require firms to
respond quickly to external feedback and fully coordinate cross-functional and cross-
boundary personnel and resources. Maet al. (2019) noted that the centralization of authority
can facilitate the centralized management and coordination of employees ’actions, enabling
them to quickly respond to complex situations in joint activities. However, Annosi et al.(2020)
argued that the decentralization of decision-making and the low formalization of tasks
facilitate complex interactions between individuals and external environment. It seems that
the more employees are independent and free to determine their own work composition and
working methods, the more they are willing to take on additional roles and tasks ( Foss et al.,
2011;Bos-Nehles et al., 2017 ). These additional tasks include striving to build long-term close
relationships with the firm ’s external partners ( Huoet al., 2016 ). Close personal relationships
can promote effective communication and interaction between organizations, thereby
shortening cognitive distance between partners. Therefore, we propose the following:
H2b. Job autonomy is positively related to joint sensemaking.
At the organizational level, teamwork and cross-functional communication, as interactive
mechanisms inside the organization, also have impacts on interaction activities outside the
organization. Teamwork and cross-functional communication within the organization
increase firms ’interactional knowledge and experience related to interaction, feedback,
conflict management and the implementation of cooperative programs ( Johnson et al., 2004 ).
When organizations possess more interactional knowledge, they can better understand how
to interact with external partners and engage in joint activities ( Fang et al., 2011 ). Thus, we
propose the following:
H2c. Teamwork is positively related to joint sensemaking.
H2d. Cross-functional communication is positively related to joint sensemaking.

external sources, external information and knowledge from partners must be fully integrated
into organizational memory. Knowledge integration can be viewed as knowledge
institutionalization that t ransfers knowledge from employees to an organization ’s
property. Based on Cohen and Levinthal (1990) , building an effective internal
communication mechanism by sharing the same specialized language and code can
increase knowledge sharing between employees and promote knowledge transformationIMDS

base of understanding through which individuals with disparate experience and knowledge
can transfer and integrate knowledge ( Collins and Smith, 2006 ). Thus, knowledge integration

Ooms et al. (2015) stated that connectedness encourages communication and cooperation and
facilitates knowledge transformation and integration. Empowerment practices, which
emphasize interactions among individuals and information sharing between different levels

Job enrichment not only broadens the knowledge structures of employees, but also
develops organizational contacts through cross-training and rotation. On the one hand,
previous studies have shown that diverse knowledge structures of employees may lead to
redundancy and thus hinder new knowledge embedding systems and structures ( Cohen and
Levinthal, 1990 ). On the other hand, developing organizational contacts can effectively build
the internal linkages needed for knowledge integration. Therefore, we propose the following:
H3a. Job enrichment is positively related to knowledge integration.

promote interplay between various perspectives. Decision-making authority can broaden
internal communication for knowledge sharing ( de Zubielqui et al. , 2019 ). Employee
autonomy and team autonomy enable a more diverse and larger group of people to connect to
form a rich internal network of diverse knowledge. Through teamwork, firms can create a
climate of trust and cooperation to increase employees ’willingness to share the information,
experience and knowledge that they acquire from the external environment ( Haneda and Ito,
2018). Therefore, we propose the following:
H3b. Job autonomy is positively related to knowledge integration.
H3c. Teamwork is positively related to knowledge integration.
In addition, a high level of interaction among team members increases internal links to
facilitate and support shared codes and language ( Collins and Smith, 2006 ). Cross-functional
communication refers to information sharing and coordination between disciplines and
functions ( Wilkinson, 1998 ;Harley, 1999 ). Cross-functional communication leads to a
horizontal communication channel that deepens knowledge flows across functional
boundaries. Jansen et al. (2005) found that the interaction between different functions can
contribute to a unit ’s ability to overcome differences, interpret issues and build a common
understanding of new knowledge. Moreover, cross-functional communication provides an
effective way to develop linkages among employees to encourage knowledge exchange and
transfer ( Kazlauskaite et al., 2012 ;Ooms et al., 2015 ). Therefore, we propose the following:
H3d. Cross-functional communication is positively related to knowledge integration.
2.4 Relationship learning and innovation performance
Prior studies have examined the relationship between relationship learning and innovation
performance ( Chen et al., 2009 ). However, most of these studies regarded the three phases of
relationship learning as a whole or parallel in analysis. They did not distinguish between
information sharing, joint sensemaking and knowledge integration, which may have
different impacts on innovation. Fang et al. (2011) noted that relationship learning involves
knowledge inflows and knowledge store. Firms first access and receive incoming knowledge
through information sharing and then integrate and store the external knowledge into
organizational memory. Zhou et al. (2020) showed that knowledge inflow and integration, asEmpowerment-

Therefore, it is necessary to distinguish the different effects of the three phases of relationship
learning on firms ’innovation activities. In addition, we suggest that the relationships
between the three phases are not independent. Joint sensemaking acts as an interaction
mechanism that links the phases of information sharing and knowledge integration. On the
one hand, when firms acquire too much information and knowledge from external sources,
information overload becomes a concern, and joint sensemaking can filter external
information and knowledge to improve the quality and efficiency of information sharing
(Bryan and Sinkovics, 2010 ). On the other hand, joint sensemaking can help interpreting and
decoding the received information by facilitating the accumulation of shared knowledge and
then integrate this knowledge into firms ( Fang et al., 2011 ). Thus, we propose the following:
H4a. Joint sensemaking is positively related to information sharing.
H4b. Joint sensemaking is positively related to knowledge integration.
Based on the argument about the use of external knowledge, both acquisition and integration
processes contribute to improving innovation outcomes ( West and Bogers, 2014 ). Through
case studies, Eslami et al. (2018) found that the establishment of interactive platforms and
mechanisms with partners does not guarantee the success of cooperative innovation, but can
promote the transformation of external knowledge. Joint activities alone are not enough;
whether interfirm learning can influence firm innovation depends on whether external
knowledge flows in sufficiently and is stored effectively. Sukoco et al. (2018) also indicated
that joint sensemaking does not directly affect firm performance, its role is to facilitate the
exchange of knowledge between the two parties, thereby improving firm performance.
Therefore, we consider that joint sensemaking, as an interactive process, may not have a
direct impact on innovation performance. In this study, we focus on exploring the effect of
information sharing and knowledge integration on firm innovation.
Research in the field of product development shows that information and knowledge
exchange with external partners can enhance the performance of new products. Information
sharing involves exchanging important data with upstream and downstream and gathering
relevant information about the market ( Myers and Cheung, 2008 ). Firms can clearly identify
customer product needs and effectively predict market trends through information sharing.
In addition, information and knowledge from different partners can help firms solve practical
problems in the product development process ( Eslami et al., 2018 ). In this field, most empirical
studies focus on product innovation and ignore process innovation ( Un and Asakawa, 2015 ).
Product and process innovation are two different types of innovation; the former refers to the
novelty of products, and the latter relates to the efficiency in production ( Haneda and Ito,
2018). In contrast to product innovation, process innovation is more dependent on the
institutionalization of the organization to assure efficiency. Through information sharing,
firms can gain insights into efficiency targets of upstream and downstream partners and how
managers evaluate these targets internally. For instance, in the thin-film transistor –liquid-
crystal display (TFT-LCD) industry, data from supplier partners helps firms improve their
processes ( Un and Asakawa, 2015 ). Thus, we propose:
H5a. Information sharing is positively related to product innovation.
H5b. Information sharing is positively related to process innovation.
In the process of knowledge integration, firms frequently update and evaluate markets,
policies, databases and techniques across partners to integrate the knowledge “not invented
here ”and create new knowledge ( Huikkola et al., 2013 ). Knowledge integration involves not
only the storage and transformation of external knowledge but also the creation of new
knowledge. New knowledge created through the combination of external knowledge will beIMDS

3. Research methodology
3.1 Data
The data used in this study were taken from the high-performance manufacturing research
project conducted by a group of international researchers during 2012 –2015. The data were
collected from 288 manufacturing plants located in 13 different countries and three different
industrial sectors (electronics, machinery and transportation equipment). These industries
were chosen because of three reasons. First, they represent major sectors of industrialized
production in the world ( Naor et al., 2010 ). Second, they are in transition and experiencing
fierce competition. Tsai (2009) pointed out that compared with other sectors, electronics,
machinery and transportation equipment firms have to cope with a more complex external
environment and a higher degree of technological uncertainty. Third, since these industries
have relationship processes with their supply chain partners, establishing cooperative
relationships with other firms is common ( Garrido-Vega et al., 2015 ). The database ’s stratified
sample includes both traditional and world-class manufacturing plants. These plants were
randomly selected from a list of manufacturing plants in each country. Each plant in the data
set has at least 100 employees. The sample distribution by country and industry is shown in
Table 1 .
The questionnaires were prepared in English and translated into the local languages for
each country by a local member of the research team. To avoid potential biases, the
questionnaires were then back-translated into English by another local member to check
consistency with the original version. The managers of the selected plants were contacted via
phone calls to determine their willingness to participate in the project. Each interested plant
manager appointed a survey coordinator to take charge of distributing and collecting the

First, the positive association between job enrichment and information sharing supports the
argument that employees with multiskills and diverse work experience can encourage firms
to gain knowledge from external partners. In addition, the findings show that job enrichment
is positively related to joint sensemaking. These results are similar to those of Cohen and
Levinthal (1990) andBogers et al. (2018) , which suggest that the diverse knowledge of
employees facilitates knowledge transfer across organizational boundaries. Job enrichment
not only broadens individual employees ’knowledge and expertise portfolios but also allows
employees to work with new people and take on different roles and tasks. Joint sensemaking
involves a two-way interaction and consensus building between firms and their partners.
Nonaka and Takeuchi (2007) pointed out that “people do not just passively receive new
knowledge, they actively interpret it to fit their own situations and perspectives. ”Employees
with broad knowledge and rich interactive experience are more likely to build common
cognitive ground with external partners and promote joint activities. Unlike knowledge
inflows, knowledge storage requires a more stable and professional knowledge structure
(Huikkola et al., 2013 ). This is consistent with our results that job enrichment only facilitates
the effective inflow of external knowledge (information sharing and joint sensemaking); it is
not related to knowledge integration.
Second, our results show that both job autonomy and teamwork are positively associated
with information sharing and knowledge integration. Job autonomy and teamwork improve
employees ’opportunities to take part in decision-making at the individual level and the team
level, respectively. From an information processing perspective, the dispersion of decision
rights to lower levels reduces the costs of transmitting, receiving and processing information
(Foss et al. , 2011 ). As Vlachos (2008) emphasizes, granting authority to employees and
teamwork can promote employees ’commitment to participation. The high degree of
participation allows employees to interact with each other, engage in constant dialogue and
discussion and integrate their individual perspectives into a new collective perspective.Job enrichment

Participation can not only increase the range of prospective receptors to the external
environment but also promote the connection between employees. Thus, empowering
practices of job autonomy and teamwork facilitate both the acquisition and integration of
external knowledge. However, job autonomy and teamwork do not enhance joint
sensemaking. Sensemaking is the social process of searching for a common understanding
in a relational context ( Huikkola et al. , 2013 ). According to the earlier discussion, job
autonomy and teamwork can lead to collective cognition, which may deprive employees of a
broader perspective.
Third, we find that cross-functional communication is positively related to knowledge
integration. Because of specialization and departmentalization, organizations often do not
know what they know. Different departments have different norms, language codes and
knowledge bases. However, the knowledge integration process is rarely performed by a
single department. Knowledge and information differentiation among members from
different departments hinders the interpretation and exploration of new knowledge ( Nonaka
and Takeuchi, 2007 ).Chang et al. (2013) argued that the process of transferring and decoding
new knowledge within the organization requires intensive collaboration among departments.
Thus, cross-functional communication, which relates to increases in the linkage between
employees and departments, can create common codes and broad understanding for
knowledge integration and can reduce the likelihood of conflict regarding cross-sectoral
interactions. Our results show that cross-functional communication has no direct impact on
information sharing and joint sensemaking. The reason may be that the density of linkages
may increase the redundancy of information and diminish access to divergent perspectives.
Excessive internal interaction may constrain unit members to alternative ways of doing
things and result in collective blindness within the organization ( Jansen et al., 2005 ). Collective
blindness will prevent firms from building consensus with their partners, which may result in
poor information sharing and joint activities between partners.
Fourth, our findings demonstrate that joint sensemaking plays a central role in
relationship learning. Joint sensemaking enhances both information sharing and knowledge
integration. Moreover, we find that information sharing can promote product and process
innovation at the same time, while knowledge integration only has a positive impact on
process innovation. Knowledge integration in relationship learning places more emphasis on
knowledge institutionalization, which means the storage of knowledge through working
procedures and practices.
6. Theoretical and managerial implications
Sourcing knowledge from external partners has become increasingly important in recent
decades. Most previous studies focused on how to obtain knowledge from external
environments ( Flor et al., 2018 ). However, the appropriate intraorganizational response to
broader external knowledge inputs remains a “black box. ”Existing research does not
address the role of HRM in innovation activities across organizational boundaries. Thus, this
study contributes to our understanding of the role of HRM practices in interfirm learning
activities. First, HRM can affect employees ’behaviors and attitudes to act in ways consistent
with company goals. Open innovation requires a form of HRM that can improve employee
participation and knowledge sharing. We propose that empowering employees allows firms
to better respond to knowledge transfer across organizations. Our results support the
argument that empowerment-focused HRM positively influences interfirm learning. Second,
we delineate empowerment-focused HRM practices in terms of the individual level and the
organizational level. At the individual level, empowerment-focused HRM is associated with
granting employees decision-making authority and improving their abilities and skills in
their individual work. At the organizational level, empowerment-focused HRM refers toEmpowerment-

nonhierarchical forms of organization that emphasize interactions and collaboration among

reflected in our research framework. We find that empowerment-focused HRM practices
demonstrate different effects on the different phases of relationship learning.
The findings of our study also offer significant guidance for managerial practices. First,
firms that attempt to use external knowledge for innovation should design appropriate HRM
activities to support it. Implementing certain intraorganizational practices can create an
organizational context that favors externa l knowledge acquisition, interaction and
integration. Managers cannot directly intervene in organizational learning capabilities, but
they can influence interfirm learning by implementing specific HRM practices that improve
employees ’learning abilities. Thus, managers who engage in interfirm learning must better
manage and implement HRM practices. Second, our study strengthens managers ’
understanding of the role of empowerment-focused HRM practices. Empowerment-focused
HRM includes the degree of decision-making power and the ability required for decision-
making at the individual level as well as teamwork and cross-functional communication,
which enhance internal coordination and information flows at the organizational level. A high
degree of delegation of authority provides firms with more opportunities to leverage external
knowledge. Leaders from Netflix revealed that the secret of their success is to give employees
room to do their best to communicate openly and make smart decisions ( Gulati, 2018 ).
7. Limitations and future research
Although our research contributes to both theories and practices, it has several limitations
that should be addressed in future research. First, this study investigates the relationship
between HRM practices and relationship learning. However, some scholars have suggested
that using the configuration approach to measure HRM systems is more meaningful for
managerial practices. The configuration approach involves the combination of HRM
practices into a coherent package. Future research can examine the role of HRM practices in
external learning from a configurational perspective. Second, we select data from electronics,
machinery and transportation equipment industries to test our hypotheses, so the
generalizability of our findings is a concern. These sectors operate in an environment of
intense global competition. Our results indicate that empowerment-focused HRM canpositively influence relationship learning in these industries. However, it may occur that in a
relatively stable environment there is not enough motivation to implement empowering
practices. Therefore, additional research could apply our framework to other industries to
improve the generalizability of current research results.
