4,5 The Procedure
To eliminate selection bias the companies were
asked to randomly select thirty individuals involved in
the analysis, design, programming, and management of
internal information system projects. Only one company
was unwilling to provide a random sample and in one
case thirty ISDP represented the entire development
staff.
Once individuals were selected by a company for
inclusion in the study they were sent a letter from
either the vice president of information systems or the
director of personnel requesting their involvement. It
was explained they would be participating in an
attitude survey conducted by New York University
regarding how they felt about their jobs and companies.
The letter also informed the subjects that the survey
was completely confidential and would require
approximately 45 minutes to complete.
To gather the data from each company one hour
sessions were scheduled for on site administration of
the survey. Approximately ten participants were
scheduled for each hour session. Due to illnesses,
vacations, and schedule conflicts approximately twenty
five subjects per company completed the survey.

Before beginning the survey each individual was
given a cover letter signed by the researcher which
explained the study, emphasized that their individual
responses were completely confidential, and requested
they include their name on the front page to allow the
collection of followup data. A copy of the cover
letter may be found in Appendix C. Once all the
subjects had arrived at a session the researcher
introduced himself, reiterated the information
contained in the cover letter and answered questions
from the subjects.
The survey was completed in the presence of the
researcher. Four questionnaires from the on site
administrations were discarded by the researcher: one
for excessive missing data, one at the request of the
participant, and two because the subjects had been
incorrectly identified as ISDP.
One company would not permit the researcher to
conduct an on-site administration of the questionnaire.
Rather, company staff distributed and collected the
questionnaires at training sessions. These
participants received the same cover letter as the
other subjects but were requested to place the
questionnaire in a sealed envelope addressed to New
York University. The sealed envelope was returned to
the individual in charge of the session who then
forwarded the survey to NYU. A copy of this letter can

The questionnaires revealed no abnormal patterns of

Summary
This chapter describes the process of building the
questionnaire, selecting subjects for inclusion in the
study, and the administration of the questionnaire.
The careful manner by which the questionnaire was built
and administered should help assure reliability of
measurement and the validity of the items. The diverse
industries and random selection of individuals should
assure the generalizabi1ity of the findings and guard
against selection bias. The following chapter
describes the findings of the data collection process.

5.2 Assumptions of Path Analysis
Path analysis is a regression based data analytic
technique that permits the testing of causal models
using cross-sectional data. Path analysis assumes the
data conform to five specific characteristics. These
five assumptions are outlined in Billings and Wroten
[1978] and are presented below.
1) The relationships between the variables are
linear.
2) The relationships among the variables (i.e.
the regression equation) are additive.
3) The measures have interval scale properties.
4) The model is recursive, no variable feeds back
onto itself.
5) The model is fully defined, that is, no
variable has been omitted that could account for the
observed relationship between two variables.
The data were tested and found to conform to the
above five characteristics. The procedures used to
test each assumption follows.
To test assumption one scatterplots were generated
between each pair of variables. The scatterplots were
visually examined and no non-linear relationships were
detected.

--- Page 119 ---
Page 105
As the review of the literature revealed no
evidence of multiplicative or quadratic relationships
assumption two was assumed to have been met.
As described in chapter 4, this study employed
carefully constructed measures which used a reasonable
number of values and contained multiple items. Such
measures yield data with acceptable interval scale
properties [Billings and Wroten, 1978]. Assumption 3
was therefore met.
Recursiveness requires the model causality to be
in one direction only. The review of the literature in
chapter two revealed no evidence of feedback loops
which would violate the assumption of recursiveness.
Based on this literature the model built for testing is
recursive.
Demonstrating that assumption five has been
satisfied is difficult. There are a host of variables
that could potentially effect any model. Based on the
review of the literature however, the important
variables have been included. Additionally statistical
evidence was generated to determine if important third
variables were ignored which would make the observed
relationships in the model spurious. Testing for third
variables was done once the path analysis was concluded
by residualizing the variables and then computing the
correlations among the residuals. The results of the

provide evidence that no important third varaibles were
omitted.
5.3 Testing the Proposed Model and Building a Trimmed
Model
To test the relationships hypothsized by the model
path coefficients were calculated and tested for
statistical significance at the .05 (two-tailed) level.
The coefficients are presented in table 5.2.1.
Of the fourteen hypothesized paths seven were not
significant at the .05 level. Employing theory
trimming [Heise, 1969], these paths were deleted from
the model resulting in a new trimmed model. The
trimmed model is presented in figure 5.0.
The path coefficients for the trimmed model were
computed and are presented in table 5.2.2.
The trimmed model beta coefficients were all
tested for significance at the .05 level. All of the
betas were significant at the .05 level, therefore
further theory trimming was not performed. The final
trimmed path model with the respective beta weights for
each path is presented in figure 5.1.
