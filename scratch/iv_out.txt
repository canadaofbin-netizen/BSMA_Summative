To account for the nested structure and the cross-level nature of the hypotheses, I 
used random coefficient modeling (RCM)23 in the analyses. RCM allows the researcher 
to test the relationships between group level independent variables and individual level 
dependent variables, while continuing to control for and model individual level factors.

difference in outcome variables such as information access or organizational commitment 
to have a group or manager level component captured as a difference in intercepts at the 
individual level. This capability is critical because traditional individual-level analyses 
do not control for the nested structure of data and may misrepresent the between-group 
effects (Klein, Dansereau, & Hall, 1994). I grand mean centered the predictor variables,

V.La Issues of Multi-collinearity 
Table 8 reveals several instances of strong correlations among the intervening 
variables. For example the correlation between resource access and information access is 
.59 and the correlation between manager availability and ascribed identity is .66. This 
suggests that multicollinearity exists among these variables. Multicollinearity can result 
in inflated standard errors (Cohen et al., 2003), thus creating problems in parameter 
estimation and interpretation. Similarly, Table 9 reveals issues of multicollinearity 
among the level 2 independent variables as well. For example, friendship in-degree and 
advice in-degree are correlated at r=.52 and LLX-affect and LLX-contribution are 
correlated at r=.64. These correlations may present either challenges or opportunities in 
analytical interpretation. On the one hand, multi-collinearity, while not affecting the 
goodness of fit of a model, can lead to inflated standard errors and instability in 
parameter estimates (Cohen et al., 2003). As I am interested in the effects of specific 
variables in my models, multicollinearity may present a problem. At the same time, 
correlations among independent variables may suggest that the independent variables act 
as suppressors of each other (Mackinnon et al., 2000). A suppression effect occurs in a 
statistical analysis when the inclusion of a third variable, z, in an analysis of the 
predictive effect of x on y accentuates the predictive relationship of x on y (Tzeglov & 
Henik, 1991). The suppression effect may occur because the correlation between z and x, 
i.e. two independent variables, may mask the effect of x on y or because the inclusion of 
z "cleans out" variance in x unrelated to y (Tzeglov & Henik, 1991).

IV.5 An indirect effects model 
The limited number of direct empirical effects linking managers' external 
relationships to LMX and subordinate commitment and performance suggest that the 
model articulated here is an indirect effects model. An indirect effect is one in which the 
independent variable does not directly predict the dependent variable, but, rather, 
influences the dependent variable through its effect on a linking mechanism or 
intervening variable (Mathieu & Taylor, 2006). For example, in this study, a manager's 
centrality in the peer friendship network has an indirect effect on subordinate's 
organizational commitment through the linking mechanism of perceived manager status. 
When managers are central in the friendship network of their peers, the subordinates 
perceive them as high status within the organization. And, when subordinates perceive 
their managers as high status within the organization, they (the subordinates) experience 
127 
higher levels of LMX. Thus, the manager's centrality has an indirect effect on 
subordinates' LMX. LLX-contribution similarly indirectly predicts LMX. 
What then does this tell us? The critical insight here is a subtle shift in theoretical 
and empirical emphasis from manager's external relationships to managers' boundary 
spanning roles. Rather than seeing the network costs and benefits as mediators or 
intervening variables that explain the link between managers' networks and subordinate 
outcomes, this study has implicitly explored the antecedents and outcomes of managers' 
boundary spanning roles. Managers function as boundary spanners, straddling the line 
between their subordinate workgroup and the environment external to workgroup. As 
boundary spanners, managers have decidedly external roles, e.g. acting as conduits of 
information and resources and developing reputations for status throughout their 
organizations. At the same time, by virtue of being on the boundary, the manager must 
also continue to manage his or her ability to be effective within the group. In drawing
