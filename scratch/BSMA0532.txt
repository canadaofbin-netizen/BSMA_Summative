Abstract
Information processing theory suggests the need for different types of integration mechanisms
in R&D project management depending on levels of uncertainty and equivocality. This paperexamines the use of these mechanisms and their links to project performance in a sample of 121R&D projects in a large research laboratory. Overall, it is found that formal leadership, planningand process specification, and to a lesser extent information technology use are related to projectperformance while the positive effects of horizontal structures are apparently balanced out by theircosts. The integration mechanisms studied act on performance partly through their effect onhorizontal communications. Modest support was found for the contingency hypotheses derivedfrom information processing theory. It appears that managers adjusted their use of horizontalstructures, planning and process specification, and informal leadership to project uncertainty butnot to project equivocality. The positive effects of horizontal communications on performancewere found to be greatest under high project equivocality as would be predicted by informationprocessing arguments. Moreover, with the exception of formal leadership, the use of integrationmechanisms did not enhance performance in contexts of low uncertainty and low equivocality.q2000 Elsevier Science B.V. 1. Introduction
In an innovative process, distinct sets of specialized skills rarely found within one
individual are often necessary to accomplish the complex tasks required for the
 development, manufacturing and marketing of a product Allen, 1986; Roberts, 1988;
. Von Hippel, 1990 . At the same time, communication, collaboration and integration
between specialists and functions are required to maximize the synergy between the
 various interdependent parts of a project Brownlie, 1987; Moenaert and Souder, 1990;
. . Ettlie and Reza, 1992; Hitt et al., 1993 . As Dougherty 1992 shows, integration tends
to be hampered by the development of distinct ``thought worlds'' associated withindividual specialization. This tension between the need for differentiation and forintegration lies at the heart of the study of organizations and seems particularly salient to
 the problem of innovation Thompson, 1967; Lawrence and Lorsch, 1969; Daft and
. Lengel, 1986 .
To solve this problem, a number of different approaches to integration and coordina-
tion among the diverse specialists involved in R&D project development have beenproposed. These mechanisms can include formal or informal project leadership, formalplanning procedures, horizontal structural or cultural mechanisms, and the use of
 communication technologies Moenaert and Souder, 1990; Hitt et al., 1993; Adler,
.1995 .
 Integration mechanisms are not costless however, Hitt et al., 1993; Emmanuelides,
.1993 . For example, regular project meetings and status reports absorb time and energy
that might be better spent in execution. Moreover, the effectiveness of the variousmechanisms is likely to vary depending on specific project needs. In particular, largerand more complex projects seem likely to warrant more elaborate mechanisms. Simi-larly, radical innovations may require different types of organizational arrangementsfrom more incremental projects. In this paper, we use information processing theory
.Daft and Lengel, 1986 to derive hypotheses concerning the prevalence and efficacy of
five types of integration mechanisms under different project conditions. These are tested
. on a sample of projects from a large R&D laboratory labeled Centerlab .
2. Theoretical base: information processing requirements and integration mecha -
nisms
2.1. Uncertainty, equi ˝ocality and information processing needs
. Daft and Lengel 1986 propose that two distinct task dimensions determine organiza-
tional information processing needs in any given situation: uncertainty and equivocality.Uncertainty refers to the absence of answers to well-defined questions: for example, alack of knowledge about future events or the consequences of specific actions. Equivo-cality refers to a deeper level of ambiguity and confusion concerning the nature of the
 questions asked, the goals pursued and the problems to be solved Daft and Lengel,
.1986 . While the collection of hard factual data can help resolve uncertainty, it may be
of relatively little help under equivocality when the nature of the task itself is unclear.

Rather, the participants in the situation will need to exchange ideas and interpretations to
develop a common understanding of the problem. Equivocality and uncertainty oftenoccur together. However, it is also conceivable that a project may involve a relatively
. small number of simple and familiar components i.e, low uncertainty and yet be
difficult to get off the ground because of ambiguities concerning what is to be
.  accomplished high equivocality . Similarly, a project may be well-defined low equivo-
.cality but require the collection of a large amount of information from a variety of
. sources to proceed high uncertainty .
R&D projects clearly involve variable levels of uncertainty and equivocality depend-
. ing on their size, complexity and novelty to the organization Schrader et al., 1993 .
More specifically, as explained in the next paragraphs, several previous researchers on
. innovation have noted that Perrow's 1967 technological dimensions of task variety and
task analyzability can be considered to reflect the uncertainty and equivocality dimen-
 sions of R&D projects Tushman and Nadler, 1978; Daft and Lengel, 1986; Gales et al.,
. 1992; Keller, 1994 .
Task variety is defined as the number of unrelated tasks or unexpected events
 associated with the accomplishment of a project Withey et al., 1983; Daft and Lengel,
. 1986; Gales et al., 1992 . Greater task variety creates greater uncertainty because there
are more sources of unpredictability and more potential disruptions associated with thecoordination of interdependent pieces of the project. Thus, there is an ongoing need tocollect and share information to work out how to execute the next task, find out what is
 going on elsewhere, deal with disruptions and ensure that projects are on track Daft and
. Macintosh, 1981; Keller, 1994 . In contrast, when task variety is low, work can be
pre-programmed, people know what others are doing and there is less need to collect
. information Daft and Macintosh, 1981 . Task variety is related to what Emmanuelides
.1993 called ``internal uncertainty'' stemming from the non-routine nature of R&D
tasks and the high levels of technical and organizational interdependence involved in
. their execution. As Gales et al. 1992 note, dimensions related to uncertainty and task
variety have been proposed in many innovation studies although operationalizations vary
.e.g., Withey et al., 1983 . In this research, task variety at the project level is
operationalized as a function of the number of people involved and the degree of
. administrative and technological complexity see Section 4 .
Task analyzability is defined as the degree to which a computational process can be
. used to develop solutions to problems Daft and Lengel, 1986; Gales et al., 1992 , and is
usually associated with familiarity and the use of established procedures and practices
.Withey et al., 1983 . Incremental projects that involve known techniques and applica-
tions will be relatively analyzable as it will be possible to decompose them into a seriesof stages or activities based on past experience. Conversely, more radical projectsinvolving unfamiliar technology and unclear application requirements will be low inanalyzability because of the lack of reference points for defining the steps to be followed
.Fischer, 1979; Gales et al., 1992 . Therefore, there will be ambiguity surrounding the
nature of the tasks to be accomplished and the problems to be solved. Thus, low
. analyzability is closely associated with high equivocality Daft and Lengel, 1986 .
 Following other researchers Tushman and Nadler, 1978; Gales et al., 1992; Green et al.,
.1995 , we operationalize analyzability in terms of the inverse of the degree of radicality

2.2. Information processing requirements and integration mechanisms
As we noted in the introduction, successful innovation requires the collaboration of
many different specialists whose work must be integrated to create a coherent whole. Avariety of different integration mechanisms can be used to achieve this. From aninformation processing perspective, these mechanisms can be seen as means for
. reducing either uncertainty or equivocality. Daft and Lengel 1986 examine the types of
communication media and structural mechanisms that favor each or these objectives.They argue that while formal rules and regulations, information systems and specialreporting procedures are capable of handling large quantities of factual information and,thus, of reducing high levels of uncertainty, these mechanisms will be inadequate underconditions of high equivocality. In such situations, ``rich'' information media such asface-to-face meetings and personal contact are required to provide opportunities forpeople to share understandings, debate issues, and develop consensus on problem
.  . definitions Daft and Lengel, 1984, 1986 . Specifically, Daft and Lengel 1986 identify
group meetings and integrator roles as structural mechanisms that can permit thereduction of equivocality through close interpersonal contact.
2They see formal planning
systems as intermediate mechanisms that may assist in both the reduction of uncertaintyand equivocality. For example, they argue that planning begins with face-to-facemeetings to establish objectives and a general course of action, thus reducing equivocal-ity. Subsequently, plans become a data processing and control device more closelyassociated with uncertainty reduction.
. Therefore, Daft and Lengel's 1986 theory suggests that the most appropriate
integration mechanisms will vary depending on levels of project uncertainty andequivocality. This research aims to test this proposition. Although some previousresearchers have examined the relationship between uncertainty and equivocality of
 R&D projects and the amounts and richness of information exchanged e.g., Keller,
. 1994; Gales and Mansour-Cole,1995; Gales et al., 1992 , to our knowledge, this is the
first study to relate the theory directly to mechanisms for integration. In this study, weexplicitly consider five possible types of integration mechanisms: horizontal structures,
. formal leadership project management ; planning and process specification; use of
. information technologies, and informal leadership project champions . We also look at
the role of one intermediate indicator of integration effort: horizontal communication.

A set of three hypotheses are formulated for each mechanism. The first hypothesis in
.each set H1a±H6a concerns the overall relationship between the use of the mechanism
and performance. The other two hypotheses are contingency hypotheses concerning theappropriateness of the mechanism in different contexts. Four types of project contexts
. are considered see Table 1 , corresponding to a 2 =2 matrix in which the dimensions
. . represent uncertainty high or low and equivocality high or low . This formulation
. corresponds to that of Daft and Lengel 1986 .
The two contingency hypotheses formulated for each integration mechanism reflect
. two different forms of contingency theory. Van de Ven and Drazin 1985 note that
contingency theories can be founded on several different conceptual approaches. One
. approach called the ``selection'' approach Van de Ven and Drazin, 1985 is based on
the idea that organizations will tend to modulate their behavior depending on thespecific context. This may occur because managers systematically choose the mostefficient approaches in each situation or because natural selection eliminates organiza-tions that do not make the most efficient choices. In either case, this approach impliesthat behavior±context combinations which are most effective will be more frequent thanineffective combinations. Our second series of hypotheses for each integration mecha-
.nism H1b±H6b reflect this idea. Specifically, when information processing theory
 suggests that a mechanism is more appropriate in some contexts than others defined in
. terms of uncertainty and equivocality , we expect the intensity of use of the mechanism
to be higher in those cells.
An alternative approach to contingency theory is called the ``interaction'' perspective
. by Van de Ven and Drazin 1985 . This approach posits that the closer the correspon-
dence between context and behavior, the higher the performance. In this version of thetheory, managers imperfectly adjust their behavior to the project context either becausethey are unaware of contingent requirements, or because they take into account concernsother than efficiency. The result is that performance varies as a function of the degree offit. In our case, this approach is reflected in the third hypothesis presented for each
. mechanism H1c±H6c . Specifically, when information processing theory indicates that
a mechanism is more appropriate in a given context, the degree of association betweenthe use of this mechanism and project performance is also expected to be higher. Wenow present our specific arguments for each of the selected integration mechanisms,
. drawing on information processing theory Daft and Lengel, 1986 and on related
innovation literature.

3. Research hypotheses
3.1. Horizontal structures
Horizontal structures overlay traditional ``functional'' structures. They lie on a
continuum ranging from informal mechanisms such as temporary assignments and taskforces in which functional authority continues to be dominant to matrix organizations inwhich dual lines of authority are permanently embedded to organizations in which
 cross-functional project teams are the dominant unit and functions play a staff role Ford
. and Randolph, 1992 . These mechanisms have long been associated with effective
. innovation in the organization theory literature Galbraith, 1973; Mintzberg, 1979 . They
are intended to counteract the centrifugal forces created by high levels of functionalspecialization among participants in innovation projects by forcing face to face contact,
 mutual adjustment and lateral communication e.g., Hitt et al., 1993; Keller, 1994;
. Adler, 1995 . By improving the amount and quality of information exchange at the
project level, these mechanisms also enable resources to be quickly reallocated as needs
. change over time Larson and Gobeli, 1988 . The use of cross-functional teams has been
 shown in prior research to be strongly related to project performance Zirger and
. Maidique, 1990; Brown and Eisenhardt, 1995 . Therefore, as a working hypothesis, we
predict that the relationship between the use of horizontal structures and performancewill be positive.
 These structures are not without disadvantages, however Ford and Randolph, 1992;
. Hitt et al., 1993 . While intended to improve communications and coordination, they can
. be costly and time-consuming Larson and Gobeli, 1988 . Dual lines of authority can
. lead to new forms of stress Nonaka, 1990 including ambiguity about responsibilities,
and conflict between functional and project managers on issues such as project priorities,
. schedules and resources Barker et al., 1988; Ford and Randolph, 1992; De Laat, 1994 .
Thus, these structures may not always be beneficial. Potential communication benefitsmust be traded off against the costs involved.
. According to Daft and Lengel 1986 , the communication benefits of mechanisms
. that favor face to face contact such as horizontal structures are greatest under high
equivocality. Face-to-face contact allows immediate feed-back and adjustment and can
 incorporate a wide variety of informational cues including content, verbal tone, body
. language, etc. . This is an effective approach when people need to work together to
. clarify what they are doing Daft and Lengel, 1984 but may be less necessary when
.  . project parameters are familiar and clear i.e., under low equivocality . Adler 1995 also
argues that interactive mechanisms such as teams are optimal for highly novel projectsbut too costly for more routine ones. Therefore, we expect that horizontal structures willbe more frequently used and more effective in the case of projects of high equivocalityand they will be less prevalent and less effective in contexts of low equivocality. Thefollowing three hypotheses summarize the arguments of the last three paragraphs.
Hypothesis 1a. The use of horizontal structures will be positi ˝ely related to project
performance.

3.2. Formal leadership
Formal project leadership plays a key integrative role in innovation projects both
within the project team and between the team and outsiders. As Brown and Eisenhardt
.1995 indicate in their extensive review of the product development research literature,
powerful project leadership is strongly associated with effectiveness. In their internalintegrator role, project leaders can help motivate team members to work together toresolve differences and work out problems, relying on their authority, expertise andpersonal credibility. In their external integrator roles, formal project leaders act as a
 ``bridge between the project team and senior management'' Brown and Eisenhardt,
.1995 . The greater the power accorded to formal project managers, the greater will be
 their capacity to mobilize resources and create a cohesive team Ancona and Caldwell,
. . 1990, 1992; Clark and Fujimoto, 1991 . In addition as Emmanuelides 1993: 378
. suggests and as Ancona and Caldwell 1992 confirm in their research, ``Effectiveness
of a product development team often hinges on its ability to buffer certain designactivities against outside interference.'' Leaders with authority over all aspects of theproject will be better able to manage the interface with top management as well asinternal team dynamics.
. Formal project leaders, as ``integrators'' in Daft and Lengel's 1986 terminology,
contribute to both uncertainty and equivocality reduction. They reduce uncertainty byacting as a formally recognized conduit for data transmission between team membersand between the team and top management. However, their most important role is likelyto be in equivocality reduction. They are in a privileged position to interpret topmanagement concerns and to organize and arbitrate face-to-face discussions and negotia-tions among team members concerning the project. Therefore, the reliance on formalproject leadership is likely to be more prevalent and more crucial for performance forprojects involving both high uncertainty and high equivocality. The following hypothe-ses summarize our arguments here.

performance.
Hypothesis 2b. The use of formal project leadership will be highest for projects of high
equi˝ocality and high uncertainty and lowest for projects of low equi ˝ocality and low
uncertainty.
Hypothesis 2c. The association between the use of formal project leadership and
performance will be strongest for projects of high equi ˝ocality and high uncertainty and

3.3. Planning and process specification:
Many writers have noted that careful planning and process specification can improve
project performance. Indeed, several project management techniques have been designedbased on the idea that by breaking down projects into their component parts andprojecting timelines and milestones for each element, it is possible to better coordinate
 the whole Moenaert and Souder, 1990; Von Hippel, 1990; Wheelwright and Clark;
. 1992; Cooper, 1996 . It can be argued that when everyone knows exactly what they
have to accomplish and when, the need for ongoing mutual adjustment is lessened,improving communication and performance. Thus, planning and process specification isa potentially powerful integration mechanism and several empirical studies have con-
 firmed its role in improving project performance Cooper and Kleinschmidt, 1987;
. Zirger and Maidique, 1990 . On the other hand, another current of literature is less
. enthusiastic about the benefits of planning. For example, Mintzberg 1994 suggests that
the creative process of innovation and the constraints of formal planning are to some
. extent incompatible and Eisenhardt and Tabrizi 1995 found that planning tended to
slow down product development. This might lead us to question the role of formalplanning, especially under conditions of equivocality where radically new innovationsare being considered. Based on data concerning the design±manufacturing interface in
. 13 firms, Adler 1995 argued that planning mechanisms are optimal at moderate levels
of novelty but less appropriate for extremely novel or for routine projects.
. Daft and Lengel 1986 suggest that ``planning is a dynamic process that includes
elements of both equivocality reduction and data sharing.'' As noted above, they viewthe initial stages of the planning process as a means to reach consensus through theexchange of rich information, with the more bureaucratic and uncertainty reducingelements dominating later in the process. Based on this logic and encouraged by theweight of empirical evidence, we adopt the working hypothesis suggested by Daft andLengel: that planning and process specification is generally beneficial, but most usefulunder conditions of high uncertainty and equivocality. An alternative hypothesis consis-
. tent with Mintzberg's 1994 position would associate it mainly with uncertainty, while
.Adler's 1995 proposal would suggest a curvilinear relationship with equivocality. We
return to these issues in the conclusions.

Hypothesis 3b. The use of formal planning and process specification will be highest for
projects of high equi ˝ocality and high uncertainty and lowest for projects of low
equi˝ocality and low uncertainty.
Hypothesis 3c. The association between the use of formal planning and process

3.4. Information technology
The use of information technology is expected to facilitate coordination during
innovation projects by increasing the speed of communication, reducing its cost,widening the scope of the information network and allowing access to communal
 information Dean and Snell, 1991; Hitt et al., 1993; Adler, 1995; Fulk and DeSanctis,
.1995 . Useful technologies in this respect may include electronic mail, voice mail, fax,
decision support systems, CAD rCAM, and electronic data management. For example,
 CAD rCAM technologies allow easy shared access to ongoing product designs Adler,
.1995 , while electronic communications facilitate horizontal communications across
intra-organizational and inter-organizational boundaries, lessening the need for physical
. proximity Fulk and DeSanctis, 1995 .
. Daft and Lengel 1986 view information technology in general as a medium highly
appropriate for uncertainty reduction, but less useful for rich communication and,therefore, not strongly associated with equivocality reduction. This is the form of thehypothesis that we have adopted here. It should be noted, however, that there isconsiderable controversy in the literature over the role of newer technologies such aselectronic mail and voice mail. For example, while some authors have confirmed the
. low rating of electronic mail for equivocality reduction Schmitz and Fulk, 1991 , others
 have suggested that it can be used to communicate richer information Kiesler, 1986;
. . Rice and Gail, 1989 . Markus 1994 argues that newer communication media are not
inherently ``rich'' or ``lean'' but that different social groups can construct them so thatthey are used in richer or more structured ways. For example, she showed that whileelectronic mail does not, as such, appear to be open to the flexible formulation ofthoughts and immediate feedback that can be gained in face-to-face conversation, certainorganizational groups may come to adopt them for use in this way. Thus, the potentialfor equivocality reduction via the use of technology may be socially constructed and
. context-dependent see also Zack and McKenny, 1995 . Even so, these asynchronous
technologies do not seem to be inherently favorable to rich exchanges to the same extent
. as telephone calls or personal and group meetings Hinds and Kiesler, 1995 . Therefore,
we view them here mainly as tools for uncertainty reduction.
Hypothesis 4a. The use of information technologies will be positi ˝ely related to project
performance.
Hypothesis 4b. Information technologies will be used to a greater extent for projects of
high uncertainty than for those of low uncertainty.
Hypothesis 4c. The use of information technologies will be more strongly related to
project performance under high uncertainty than under low uncertainty.

3.6. Horizontal communication
The importance of effective horizontal communication for innovation success is
implicit in most of the discussion so far. It has also been firmly established in a major
 stream of previous research Tushman, 1979; Allen, 1986; Gales et al.,1992; Brown and

Following previous work, we argue that the effectiveness ofhorizontal communications is an intermediate variable that reflects the extent to whichsatisfactory integration has been achieved between the various organization membersassociated with a project.
Although effective communication may be more difficult to achieve for more
uncertain projects because of the greater number of people and types of expertiseinvolved, it is likely that it will be particularly critical for such projects, given thegreater need for integration of diverse viewpoints. Similarly, we expect that high

4. Methods
4.1. Research site, sampling and data collection
A cross-sectional survey design was used to test the hypotheses. The data were
 collected from a single research center of a large corporation. Within this site called
() H. Sicotte, A. Langley rJ. Eng. Technol. Manage. 17 2000 1±37 12
. Centerlab , the sample consisted of 121 different R&D projects from 13 research units
.sample sizes are less than 121 in some analyses because of missing data . The research
units differed in terms of clients, products and functional specializations based on a
 variety of fundamental disciplines i.e., molecular chemistry, robotics, mechanical
. engineering, nuclear physics, etc. . The projects included technical studies, process
development projects and product development projects for both internal and externalclients. The choice of a single research site simplifies data collection and controls for anumber of possible confounding variables associated with the industry and environment.It does, however, potentially limit the variance in project management proceduresincluding integration mechanisms. Prior to formal data collection, we conducted anumber of preliminary interviews to examine the potential diversity of projects, proce-dures and clients. These appeared to be more than sufficient to render the proposedresearch meaningful.
The sample of projects was constituted by a snowball sampling method aimed at
including all the most important recently completed projects as well as an equal numberof relative successes and failures. To be included, projects also had to involve severalresearchers. At each research unit, the first author began by contacting the Director andadministrative assistant. Based on a list of research personnel, all the most seniorresearchers were then contacted and asked to supply information on one successful andone less successful project during a personal interview. At the end of the interview, theresearcher was asked to provide the names of all others who had worked on one or bothof these projects. These individuals were then contacted and asked for information onadditional projects. Through this process, 116 personal interviews were completed. Aquestionnaire was left with the respondent after the interview. A further 17 peoplecompleted this instrument without a formal interview for a total of 133 questionnaires.
The interview and the questionnaires tapped different variables and all the question-
naires were filled out under similar conditions. Respondents usually completed the
. questionnaire for two projects one success and one failure . Most of the researchers
excluded from the final sample were younger and less-experienced and were unable toidentify two completed projects that fit the inclusion criteria. Ninety percent of thoseasked to fill out a questionnaire agreed to do so. The high response rate was no doubtassisted by the availability of the first researcher on site for several months. More than
 one response was obtained for 48% of the project sample biased towards the larger and
. more complex projects . In addition, for each project, certain additional information
concerning project characteristics was obtained in an interview with the Director of theunit responsible. Table 2 provides some descriptive statistics on the projects in thesample.

4.2.1. Information processing context
Measures of uncertainty and equivocality were constructed to reflect the dimensions
 of task variety and analyzability described earlier and used in previous research see
. Tushman and Nadler, 1978; Gales et al., 1992; Keller, 1994 . These variables were
derived from items in the questionnaire combined with responses to questions raised ininterviews with senior researchers and unit directors.
The uncertainty dimension reflects the number of information bases and the technical
and administrative complexity of the project. It combines three indicators:
ﬂThe logarithm of the number of project participants. The number of participants was
obtained from the senior researcher for each project by asking him or her to list theirnames. For 83 projects, this indicator was checked in an interview with the Director
. of the research unit the correlation between the two measures was rs0.57 .
ﬂAssessments of the administrative complexity and technical complexity, respectively
of the project on two 7-point scales obtained from the Director of the research unit.
. This indicator of uncertainty or task variety is constructed somewhat differently
. from that proposed by Withey et al. 1983 . The items in their scale in fact tend to
reflect individual-level task variety rather than task variety across an entire project
.Withey et al., 1983: 59 . Our measure has more similarity with that of Gales et al.
.1992 in the sense that it reflects the number of information bases involved at
project-level. This approach applies best to a R&D context where by definition projectparticipants do non-routine tasks and where variety will be reflected more in themultiplicity and diversity of components within the project team than in measures of
 non-routineness for individual members. In fact, averaging individual perceptions of
. personal task-variety may produce little variation between projects . Technical complex-
ity, administrative complexity and the number of research team members provide abetter proxy for project-level task variety. Multiple researchers is both a reflection ofhigh complexity and a factor that in itself leads to diversity in bases of information.
The equivocality dimension reflects the newness of the project and its degree of
 radicality see also Gales et al., 1992; Gales and Mansour-Cole 1995; Green et al.,
.1995 . It is a composite of five indicators:
 ﬂAn assessment of whether the project is considered to be ``radical'' both objec-
. tives and technological methods are not known , ``significant'' the product to be
. developed is unknown but technologies are familiar or vice-versa , or ``incremental''
considered as a recombination of well-known methods and techniques towards a
. well-defined objective . This assessment was obtained from the senior researcher
involved in the project.

ﬂFour Likert-type items measuring the novelty of the project for the organization
. based on Chang and Yong's 1991 measure of technical risk. The items are listed in the
Appendix and were included in the main questionnaire to all project participants.
To verify the plausibility of the two distinct dimensions, a factor analysis including
all eight items listed above was carried out. Two orthogonal factors with Eigenvaluesgreater than 1 emerged, explaining 28.8% and 23.7% of the variance, respectively. Thelast five items all loaded onto the first factor, while the first three all loaded onto thesecond. Each of the individual indicators was normalized and means were calculated tocompose the two scales. Cronbach alphas for uncertainty and equivocality were 0.68 and
. 0.66, respectively. The scale means at zero because of normalization were then used as
natural cut-off points to create the four-cell matrix of Table 1. To avoid informationloss, the continuous versions of the variables were also used in hypothesis testing.

4.3. Analysis
Multiple regression was used to test the hypotheses concerning the overall association
between the integration variables and performance and to test the mediating effect of
. horizontal communication H1a±H6a; H6b . Multiple regression was also used to
examine the effects of information processing context on the use of each integration
. mechanism H1b±H5b . Moderated regression, correlational analysis and Fisher tests for
differences in correlations were used to test hypotheses concerning differences in thedegree of association between integration mechanisms and performance for the four
.  . cells of the uncertainty±equivocality matrix H1c±H5c; H6b . As Venkatraman 1989
notes, the use of subgroups and comparisons of correlation coefficients is preferred tomoderated regression when the objective is to test the strength rather than the form ofmoderating effects, as in our hypotheses. We added moderated regression analyses tovalidate the existence of moderating effects and to provide a test based on all the
. information available since subgroup analysis results in information loss .

We expected that horizontal structures would be used more frequently for projects
. with high equivocality H1b . However, the analysis in Tables 5 and 6 suggests that

We predicted that both uncertainty and equivocality would affect the use of planning
. and process specification H3b . The analysis shows a small positive effect for uncer-
tainty, suggesting that planning and process specification is seen by Centerlab princi-pally as a means to cope with uncertainty rather than equivocality.
. The use of informal leadership project champions was expected to be lower for
projects of high uncertainty given the difficulties of exerting influence with little formalauthority in these circumstances. The data suggest the opposite: project championship isin fact more likely for more uncertain projects.
Finally, there are no significant differences in the use of formal leadership or
information technology among the different cells. It appears that Centerlab paysrelatively little attention to the project context in determining management approaches
. despite considerable variance in project, size and complexity see Table 2 .
It is also worth noting that uncertainty and equivocality do not appear to have a
. significant negative effect on performance overall see Tables 3 and 5 . Comments made
by managers during interviews tend to suggest that while more complex and novelprojects are indeed more risky, the increased attention accorded to them compensatespartly for this effect. Smaller and more incremental projects may be easier to handle, butthey can also suffer from relative neglect.

This research aimed to contribute to a better understanding of the role of integration
 mechanismsin R&D project success. The informationprocessingperspective Galbraith,
. 1973; Tushman, 1979; Daft and Lengel, 1986; Gales et al., 1992 was adopted as a
coherent theoretical framework for understanding the contingency relationships affectingthe appropriateness of different mechanisms. In this section, we highlight the main conclusions, discusscertain surprising results, note the limitations of this research, and examine the implica-tions of our study for research and practice.
6.1. Integration mechanisms and project performance
First, we confirmed that several integration mechanisms discussed in the literature
can contribute to project success. In particular, there is evidence that empowered projectleadership is critical. This supports empirical observations by Ancona and Caldwell
. .1990 and Clark and Fujimoto 1991 . However, our data suggest that the importance of
strong formal leadership is not restricted to projects of high uncertainty or highequivocality as we predicted based on information processing theory. This means that
. ``integration'' of many diverse specialists Daft and Lengel, 1986 is probably not the
only important role of these leaders. We may speculate, for example, that in the case ofsmaller and simpler projects, powerful leaders may contribute to success through theircapacity to attract resources to projects that might otherwise be neglected. The onlycontext in which formal leadership was not significantly related to performance was thelow uncertainty, high equivocality condition. This result is more difficult to explain.

6.2. Degree of support for the information processing perspecti ˝e
Over and above specific observations concerning the performance effects of various
integration mechanisms, how much overall support did we find for the informationprocessing perspective? Hypotheses H1b±H5b reflect the ``selection'' approach to thetheory, which suggests that integration mechanisms will be used more extensively whenthey are ``appropriate'' to the context. We found rather limited support for thesehypotheses. Centerlab did not tend to modulate its use of integration mechanisms as a
 function of project equivocality. Moreover, its adjustments for project uncertainty for
. planning, horizontal structures and informal leadership were not always in the predicted
directions. These results tend to suggest that R&D laboratories may have somedifficulty in adjusting their project management approaches to a wide range of differentsituations. Internal rules and traditions may tend to lead to some uniformity. Whenadjustments are made, they may be more readily justified by tangible factors such as the
 complexity of the project and the number of different specialties involved factors
. related to project uncertainty rather than by less tangible considerations such as the
. novelty of the product or process being developed project equivocality . One hypothesis
worthy of future research is that while task uncertainty is treated as an organizationalproblem, equivocality is more of an individual problem which will be handled byindividual-level rather than organizational-level strategies.
() H. Sicotte, A. Langley rJ. Eng. Technol. Manage. 17 2000 1±37 30
 The alternative approach to contingency theory is the ``interaction'' perspective Van
. de Ven and Drazin, 1985 which posits that the closer the fit between context and
behavior, the higher the performance. This approach was reflected in hypothesesH1c±H6c. We find a modest level of support for this form of the theory. In fact, there isevidence that equivocality moderates the performance effects of integration devices to agreater extent than uncertainty. Formal leadership and horizontal communications arethe two mechanisms that show the strongest effects here, despite the fact that their usedid not emerge as significantly affected by context in the ``selection'' approach analysis.
This suggests the possibility of a degree of complementarity between the two
approaches to contingency theory. When the firm adjusts its approach to the context,performance effects may be undetectable because unpropitious cases are eliminatedbeforehand. When it does not do so but should, significant performance effects are morelikely to emerge. This argument would lead one to expect that the ``selection'' approachwill be more predictive for variables largely under the control of management while the``interaction'' approach will be more predictive for less controllable variables. In our
. case, the variables horizontal structures more controllable , planning and process
.  specification more controllable and horizontal communication effectiveness less con-
.trollable fit the pattern. However, the pattern of relationships for informal leadership
.  .less controllable and formal leadership more controllable appear at first to contradict
this supposition. Clearly, this could be an interesting avenue for future research.
Another observation is that integration mechanisms are in general least useful for less
uncertain and equivocal projects. This means that the factors affecting the performanceof less complex and more incremental projects lie elsewhere. In fact, paradoxically, wecan explain more of the variance in the performance of complex, large and radicalinnovations than of smaller and more incremental ones. Again, this suggests a need formore research.
6.3. Strengths and limitations of the research
Research on technological innovation has often been criticized for its largely atheoret-
. ical nature e.g., Brown and Eisenhardt, 1995 . The information processing perspective
. provides one approach to filling this theoretical gap Gales et al., 1992 . A number of
recent studies have adopted this perspective for the study of R&D project success. For
. . example, Gales et al. 1992 and Keller 1994 focused on the effects of task variability
andanalyzabilityontheamountsofinformationprocessedwhileGalesandMansour-Cole
.1995 examined the relationship between client involvement, project uncertainty and
performance. In this study, we attempted to take a further step forward by examining therole of integration mechanisms in relation to uncertainty and equivocality and the
. mediating effect of horizontal communication Daft and Lengel, 1986 .
Like any other study, ours is not without limitations. Our focus on a single research
laboratory allowed the control of certain contextual variables and enabled us to gatherdata on a fairly large sample of projects through personal interviews and questionnairesfrom several sources. The use of multiple respondents for each project is relativelyunusual in this type of research and enhances the quality of the data base. However, a
() H. Sicotte, A. Langley rJ. Eng. Technol. Manage. 17 2000 1±37 31
single site does limit the generalizability of the research and may have restricted the
. range of certain variables e.g., horizontal structures limiting our capacity to detect
significant relationships. Future work should also use measures of performance based onindependent client assessments. Our use of researcher assessments of performance mayraise a concern about common method bias. To mitigate this, we made an effort to
. measure this variable using factually formulated questions see Appendix . Another
source of bias could arise from the pooling of data which included more than one projectrating from each respondent. This concern is somewhat mitigated by the process ofaggregation which took responses from people involved in evaluating different projectpairs. Finally, as a cross-sectional field study, this research provides evidence ofassociation, but not causality.
6.4. Implications and directions for research and practice
This study suggests several directions for future work, several of which have been
mentioned above. First, there is a need to verify these results in other contexts usingsimilar and different methodologies. For example, client assessments of performancewould be useful, while longitudinal methods would help establish causal linkages. Inaddition, there is a need to test richer explanatory models that incorporate networks ofvariables. For example, we have seen that horizontal structures may well have counter-vailing effects: better communication on the one hand, but higher cost on the other,
 producing an ambiguous effect on overall performance Ford and Randolph, 1992; Hitt
. et al., 1993 . These relationships could be modeled and tested explicitly in different
types of contexts using intervening variables and structural equations modeling.
. Curvilinear models relating particular integration mechanisms e.g., planning to
 information processing context are also theoretically defensible e.g., Boynton et al.,
. 1993; Adler, 1995 and should be tested further. Certain integration mechanisms studied
also warrant finer definition. For example, formal and informal leaders may have avariety of different styles that could be more or less appropriate in different contexts.Finally, the role of uncertainty and equivocality as significant moderating variables alsounderlines the need for research on innovation to take these conditions into accountwhen selecting samples for study. The pooling of diverse sets of projects into a single
. sample potentially contributes to instability in results Downs and Mohr, 1976 .
Our research also offers lessons for managers. First, we note that smaller and simpler
projects performed no better than larger and more complex ones despite the head-startthey had in principle. Managers should beware of neglecting simpler projects thatnevertheless make up a large proportion of the R&D budget. Our data suggest thatformal leadership appears to be as important in this context as in others, though probablyfor different reasons. Here its effects are likely to be related to the capacity to mobilizeresources and attention to get the job done. For more uncertain and equivocal projects,the integrative role will be more significant.
Secondly, we note a tendency to adjust project management practices to uncertainty
but not to equivocality. This is perhaps partly because uncertainty is more obvious andmore easily detectable. Managers need to ensure that the special needs of highlyinnovative projects are recognized. Another reason for this may be that equivocality is
() H. Sicotte, A. Langley rJ. Eng. Technol. Manage. 17 2000 1±37 32
simply less manageable by formal mechanisms alone. Our results suggest that while
formal planning is not without merit in these circumstances, success may be moredependent on individual behavior and the resulting spontaneous chemistry that develops
. between project participants see also Dougherty and Hardy, 1996 . To handle this, ways
need to be found to stimulate effective horizontal communications beyond what can beachieved simply with formal structures, procedures and technologies. This might involvethe selection of people to take part in projects taking into account not only theirtechnical prowess, but also their capacity to collaborate, their compatibility with otherproject participants and their informal leadership potential. This is a task that requiressubtlety and foresight, or possibly some form of mutual self-selection. In conclusion, wesuspect that softer and more unpredictable management skills need to be combined withformal integration mechanisms to optimize the innovative capability of R&D projectgroups.
