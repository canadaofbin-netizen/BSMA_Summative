Abstract
Purpose – Despite the increasing importance of boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects, 
the research on how leader anger expressions impact employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors remains 
unclear. This study aims to investigate the impact of leader anger expressions on employees’ boundary-
spanning behaviors in construction projects while exploring the mediating effect of work hope and the 
moderating effect of power distance orientation through the lens of social information processing theory.
Design/methodology/approach – The empirical data were collected from a questionnaire survey of 235 
employees in construction projects, and the hypotheses were tested using the PROCESS program developed 
by Hayes.
Findings – This study demonstrated that leader anger expressions negatively affect employees’ work hope, 
and work hope mediates the relationship between leader anger expressions and employees’ boundary-
spanning behaviors. Besides, the stronger the employees’ power distance orientation, the stronger the negative 
relationship between leader anger expressions and employees’ work hope, and the above mediating effect was 
also stronger.
Research limitations/implications – The use of cross-sectional data was unable to entirely infer a causal 
relationship between leader anger expressions and employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors. Additionally, 
the reliance on self-reported data inevitably introduced some common method bias.
Originality/value – Applying social information processing theory, this paper enriches the discourse on 
factors influencing employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects. It also offers valuable 
insights for leaders in construction projects to manage their teams effectively.
Keywords Leader anger expressions, Work hope, Boundary-spanning behaviors, Power distance orientation

Introduction
As a cornerstone of global economic development, the construction industry typically 
operates on a project-based model (Liu et al., 2021). Construction projects are temporary 
organizational endeavors characterized by clear time constraints, high uncertainty, and 
diverse talent (Bakker and Janowicz-Panjaitan, 2009; de Blois et al., 2016; Liyanagamage 
et al., 2022). With the integration of advanced technologies, compliance with stringent 
regulations, and the adoption of sustainable practices, construction projects have become 
increasingly complex and multifaceted (Regona et al., 2022). These demands must be met 
within tight deadlines and budget constraints, posing significant challenges. To address 
these challenges effectively, employees in construction projects must engage in close 
interdisciplinary collaboration (Mell et al., 2022b). In this context, it is vital for employees of 
construction projects to engage in boundary-spanning behaviors to dismantle knowledge 
barriers, enhance inter-member communication, and secure external support to address 
internal resource deficits and external challenges (Yan et al., 2023). Employees’ boundary-
spanning behaviors predominantly involve interfacing with external entities to attain 
organizational goals (Zheng and Ahmed, 2024). Research has indicated that employees’ 
boundary-spanning behaviors would foster knowledge integration (Mell et al., 2022b), 
individual performance (Liu et al., 2018), and creative behavior (Liu et al., 2022). Hence, 
investigating the determinants influencing employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors in 
construction projects is essential.
Existing research has examined how individuals’ characteristics, organizational contexts, 
and leaders’ behaviors influence employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors in construction 
projects (Karrbom Gustavsson and Gohary, 2012; Cao et al., 2021). Regarding individual 
characteristics, scholars have posited that individuals’ nationality (Di Marco et al., 2010), 
personal skills (Du and Pan, 2012), and richness of interpersonal networks (Brion et al., 2012) are 
likely to affect individuals’ boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects. On 
organizational factors, research has suggested that organizational fraternization 
(Ramalingam and Mahalingam, 2011), organizational culture (Di Marco et al., 2010) and 
organizational collaboration (Karrbom Gustavsson and Gohary, 2012) would influence 
members’ boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects. Given their roles as 
performance evaluators and resource custodians, leaders are pivotal in shaping employees’ 
boundary-spanning behaviors (Marrone et al., 2022). It is indicated that shared leadership (Cao 
et al., 2021), vertical leadership (Bolden and Petrov, 2014), and leaders’ supportive coaching 
behaviors (Marrone et al., 2022) may also have an impact on employees’ boundary-spanning 
behaviors in construction projects. However, current research on the impact of leader emotional 
expressions on employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors is limited.
Compared to traditional organizations, project managers, as leaders, exhibit increased 
interaction frequency and enhanced relational proximity with employees (Rafique et al., 2023). In 
construction projects with defined time constraints, employees lack the opportunity to deeply 
understand project managers’ traits (Liyanagamage et al., 2022). Their emotional expressions, due 
to the immediacy, directly influence employees’ perceptions and behaviors (Silard and 
Dasborough, 2021). The high-stress nature of construction projects makes project managers’ 
anger expressions common (Jiang et al., 2023). Research in the realm of project management has 
found that leader anger expressions would reduce project members’ proactive career behavior and 
leadership effectiveness while increasing their work alienation (Kang et al., 2024; Lindebaum and 
Fielden, 2011). Yet, the effect of leader anger expressions on employees’ boundary-spanning 
behaviors remains unclear. Therefore, this study aims to examine the impact of leader anger 
expressions on employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects, while further 
investigating the mediating mechanisms and boundary conditions of this effect.

their work hope and boundary-spanning behaviors.
This research aims to achieve three objectives. The first is to examine the impact of leader 
anger expressions on employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects. 
Through this objective, this study will expand the research on the factors impacting 
employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects. The second objective is to 
investigate the mediating role of work hope in the relationship between leader anger 
expressions and boundary-spanning behaviors. By exploring this objective, this study will 
enrich the mediating mechanisms through which leader anger expressions influence 
employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors through the lens of social information processing 
theory. The final objective is to explore the moderating role of power distance orientation in 
the relationship between leader anger expressions and work hope. In fulfilling this objective, 
this study will broaden the boundary conditions in which leader anger expressions operate. 
Meanwhile, the results will also offer practical insights for organizations and leaders in 
construction projects on optimizing employee management strategies. The hypothetical

Employees rely more on social information, including leaders’ emotions, to frame their

The pervasive and destructive nature of leader anger expressions in construction projects

Leader anger expressions in construction projects
Anger is the most readily discernible emotion, especially in construction projects where 
employees have limited time to deeply comprehend a leader’s traits (Naderpajouh et al., 2020). 
As direct responses to employees’ behaviors and workplace events, leader anger expressions 
manifest not only through verbal articulations but also include non-verbal behaviors like 
expressive changes and physical gestures (Forgays et al., 1997). Studies indicated that leader 
anger expressions are contingent upon the leader’s personality traits and situational 
assessments (Shao and Guo, 2021). Stringent timelines, complex collaboration requirements, 
and pervasive safety imperatives of construction projects may precipitate heightened leader 
anger expressions (Irfan et al., 2023).
Leader anger expressions constitute a critical research domain. Existing studies on leader 
anger expressions primarily focus on the realm of general management, while studies in 
construction projects only recently probed the mechanisms of their impacts on employees’ 
cognition and behaviors. In the realm of general management, research has investigated the 
influence of leader anger expressions on employees’ emotions (anger and anxiety, Eberly and 
Fong, 2013), cognition (perceived leader effectiveness, Yun et al., 2020; perceived leader 
integrity, Shao and Martin, 2020), and behaviors (organizational citizenship behaviors, 
Koning and Van Kleef, 2015; innovative behaviors, Lee and Chang, 2014; voice behaviors, 
Song et al., 2019). In the realm of construction projects, limited research has explored how 
leader anger expressions influence employees, and the discourse on leader anger expressions 
within project management remains underexplored. For example, Lindebaum and Fielden 
(2011) investigated the influence of leader anger expressions on employees’ perceived 
leadership effectiveness in construction projects. Besides, Kang et al. (2024) explored the 
influence of leader anger expressions on newcomers’ proactive career behavior and work 
alienation in construction projects. Considering the above, this study will further explore 
their impact on employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors and the mechanisms of action.
Boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects
Boundary-spanning behaviors denote employees’ proactive efforts in transcending the 
demarcations of industry, organization, and knowledge, interfacing with the external 
environment to acquire resources and address challenges (Marrone et al., 2007). Research 
revealed that employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors are pivotal within construction 
projects for two reasons. Firstly, boundary-spanning behaviors will enhance inter-
disciplinary communication within construction projects characterized by diverse 
personnel (Wang et al., 2022). Secondly, boundary-spanning behaviors will motivate 
project members to secure external support to navigate internal resource scarcity and 
external challenges (Cao et al., 2021). Research has indicated that boundary-spanning 
behaviors in construction projects significantly enhance inter-project communication

Investigating the determinants influencing boundary-spanning behaviors holds

Research hypothesis
Leader anger expressions and employees’ work hope
Work hope is the belief that individuals can find suitable ways to achieve their work goals, 
which determines their attitudes and behaviors (Snyder et al., 1991). Given the high-pressure 
work environments in construction projects, cultivating work hope among employees helps 
them navigate stress effectively and fosters positive behaviors (Sundar and Bhaskar, 2021). 
Research has demonstrated that work hope is closely linked to interpersonal interactions 
(Stephanou and Athanasiadou, 2020). Due to intimate leader-employee contact, studies on the 
impact of leader attitudes and behaviors on employees’ work hope are constantly expanding. 
Studies have verified how abusive, despotic, and destructive leadership behaviors affect 
employees’ work hope (Avey et al., 2022; Calheiros, 2018; Ahmad et al., 2023).
Social information processing theory posits that leaders’ emotions and behaviors are 
crucial information sources that employees process to develop their cognitions and behaviors 
(Salancik and Pfeffer, 1978). Leader anger expressions serve as pivotal social cues for 
employees, informing their interpretation of leaders’ feedback and shaping subsequent 
perceptions (Shao and Guo, 2021). This research indicates that leader anger expressions 
impact work hope in two ways. Firstly, the social messages conveyed by leader anger 
expressions prompt employees to perceive a deficiency in achieving work goals 
(Liyanagamage et al., 2022), thereby reducing their work hope. Leader anger expressions 
in construction projects underscore the gravity and immediacy of issues, as minor errors 
during operations can precipitate significant negative repercussions (Peralta et al., 2020). 
Once employees receive and internalize this social message, they may perceive that they are 
failing to attain their work goals (Stephanou and Athanasiadou, 2020), which may be 
detrimental to their work hope. Secondly, leader anger expressions also lead employees to 
perceive diminished worth and attenuate their work hope. Specifically, leader anger 
expressions convey messages to employees that they are perceived as lacking value to the 
organization (Avey et al., 2022). Upon assimilating this message, employees will feel 
undervalued and unconfident (Emmerling et al., 2023), thereby reducing work hope. In 
summary, the following hypothesis is proposed.
H1. Leader anger expressions negatively affect employees’ work hope.
The mediating role of work hope
Boundary-spanning behaviors refer to activities undertaken by individuals beyond their 
formal roles or responsibilities (Marrone et al., 2007). This research indicates that low work 
hope reduces employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors. Firstly, employees with low work 
hope will curtail boundary-spanning behaviors by reducing the willingness to engage in 
searching, socializing, and task coordination. Individuals with low work hope often

According to social information processing theory, leader anger expressions transmit 
information to employees. After interpreting the information, employees perceive 
themselves as lacking the capability to accomplish the work goal, which decreases their 
work hope. Low work hope reduces boundary-spanning behaviors. In summary, the 
following hypothesis is proposed.
H2. Work hope mediates the relationship between leader anger expressions and 
employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors.
The moderating effect of power distance orientation
Power distance orientation is a personality trait that reflects individuals’ beliefs regarding 
the appropriateness of questioning authority (Dorfman and Howell, 1988). Individuals with 
varying power distance orientations perceive leaders’ behaviors differently, resulting in 
distinct responses (Zhang et al., 2023). Specifically, employees with high power distance 
orientation are distinguished by their adherence to hierarchical distinctions and readiness to 
align with leadership directives (Dorfman and Howell, 1988). While employees with low 
power distance prioritize equitable power distribution and advocate for parity between 
leaders and employees (Vitolla et al., 2019).
This study proposes that employees’ power distance orientation would strengthen the 
negative effect of leader anger expressions on employees’ work hope. According to social 
information processing theory, power distance orientation, a critical personality trait, 
would lead to varied interpretations of leader anger expressions among employees, 
culminating in distinct perceptions and behaviors (Shao and Guo, 2021). Employees with 
high power distance orientation are highly attuned to hierarchical dynamics and exhibit 
heightened sensitivity to negative messages from leaders, such as anger expressions 
(Peng et al., 2021). Such employees might construe leader anger expressions as heightened 
disapproval or discontent (Sundar and Bhaskar, 2021). This may exacerbate employees’ 
feelings of skepticism towards their competency in meeting job expectations (Stephanou 
and Athanasiadou, 2020), consequently diminishing their work hope. Compared to high 
power distance orientation, employees with low power distance orientation exhibit less 
sensitivity to authority (Peng et al., 2021). They have less concern for the social cues of 
leader anger expressions, indicating that leader anger expressions exert little negative 
influence on their work hope. In summary, the following hypothesis is proposed.
H3. Employees’ power distance orientation positively moderates the relationship 
between leader anger expressions and employees’ work hope. The higher the 
employees’ power distance orientation, the stronger the negative relationship 
between leader anger expressions and employees’ work hope.

Furthermore, employees’ power distance orientation positively moderates the relationship 
between leader anger expressions and work hope. When employees’ power distance 
orientation is high, leader anger expressions show stronger negative effects on work hope.

Methods
A quantitative approach with the questionnaire survey was adopted in this study. The 
questionnaire’s items were sourced from extensively validated academic publications. Then, 
we used convenience sampling to distribute questionnaires via the WeChat platform. After 
data collection, we utilized Mplus 7.0 to conduct confirmatory factor analysis for examining 
discriminant validity and SPSS 26.0 for descriptive analysis and regression analysis.
Measurement
The scales for this study were chosen based on the existing literature. Participants rated all 
items for leader anger expressions, work hope, boundary-spanning behaviors, and power 
distance orientation using a five-point Likert scale, with scores ranging from 1 (strongly 
disagree) to 5 (strongly agree).
Leader anger expressions. We applied a 5-item scale developed by Forgays et al. 
(1997) to measure leader anger expressions. One sample item is “At work, the project 
manager shows anger toward us”. The Cronbach’s α value was 0.91.
Work Hope. An 8-item scale developed by Snyder et al. (1991) was utilized to gauge 
work hope. One sample item is “I am capable of devising numerous strategies to resolve a 
difficult situation in the workplace”. The Cronbach’s α value was 0.91.
Boundary-spanning behaviors. Boundary-spanning behaviors were assessed using 
a six-item scale developed by Marrone et al. (2007). One sample item is “I will endeavor to 
convince external stakeholders, such as faculty and clients, to support project decisions”. 
The Cronbach’s α value was 0.86.
Power distance orientation. A six-item scale designed by Dorfman and Howell (1988) 
was used to assess power distance orientation. One sample item is “When making decisions, 
project managers do not need to seek my opinion”. The Cronbach’s α value was 0.73.
Control variables. Participants’ age, gender, education, organizational tenure, tenure 
with the leader, and project type were included as control variables. These variables would 
affect participants’ experiences, psychological perceptions, and coping behaviors (Wang 
et al., 2018; Mell et al., 2022a; Lee et al., 2020), thereby influencing the assessment of 
employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors and perceptions of leader anger expressions. 
Therefore, we incorporated these variables as controls.

Analytical approach
After collecting the data, we performed the analysis with Mplus 7.0 and SPSS 26.0. Firstly, 
we utilized Mplus 7.0 to conduct a confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) to evaluate the 
discriminant validity of the measurements. Secondly, SPSS 26.0 was used to carry out 
descriptive analysis. Thirdly, we examined our hypotheses utilizing the PROCESS 
procedure of SPSS26.0 outlined by Hayes (2013). Hypothesis 1 and 2 were verified using 
PROCESS Model 4, and hypothesis 3 was verified using PROCESS Model 1. To mitigate 
potential multicollinearity concerns, we mean-centered leader anger expressions and 
employees’ work hope before testing hypothesis 3 (Aiken and West, 1991). Due to the non-
normal distribution of indirect effects, scholars have recommended using bias-corrected (BC) 
bootstrap resampling to estimate the confidence intervals for the test of mediating effect 
(Hayes, 2009). Hypothesis 4 was verified using PROCESS Model 7. Quantification of 
moderated mediation effects involves a mathematical algorithm known as the “index of 
moderated mediation” (Hayes, 2015). Figure 2 illustrates the specific flow of research 
methods.

Theoretical implications
Firstly, this study expanded the findings regarding determinants of employees’ boundary-
spanning behaviors in construction projects. Previous studies have uncovered the influence 
of individual characteristics (Du and Pan, 2012; Brion et al., 2012) and organizational contexts 
(Ramalingam and Mahalingam, 2011; Karrbom Gustavsson and Gohary, 2012) on project 
members’ boundary-spanning behaviors. There are also studies that have explored the 
impact of leaders’ behaviors (Cao et al., 2021; Bolden and Petrov, 2014; Marrone et al., 2022) on 
individuals’ boundary-spanning behaviors in construction projects. This study extended the 
aforementioned findings by highlighting that leader anger expressions would significantly 
influence employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors. Due to the pressing timelines and 
recurrent safety issues in temporary projects, leader anger expressions are commonplace and 
often yield negative behaviors for employees (Peralta et al., 2020). Based on the above, we 
further demonstrated that leader anger expressions would influence employees’ boundary-
spanning behaviors, providing valuable theoretical insights for project management.
Secondly, this study delved into how leader anger expressions impact boundary-
spanning behaviors through work hope from social information processing theory. Some 
scholars have used emotions as social information model (Shao, 2019a), self-determination 
theory (Schwarzm€ 
uller et al., 2018), conservation of resources theory (Wang et al., 2021), and 
leader-member exchange theory (Fisk and Friesen, 2012) to explain the effects of leader anger 
expressions on employees’ behaviors. They respectively elucidated the mediating roles of 
members’ anger intensity, follower-inferred negative intention, emotional exhaustion, and 
leader-member exchange quality. According to social information processing theory, we 
indicated that leader anger expressions signal to employees a deficiency in achieving 
objectives (Bartel-Radic and Munch, 2023), diminishing their work hope and in turn reducing 
their boundary-spanning behaviors.
Thirdly, we delineated the boundary conditions on the effect of leader anger expressions. 
Scholars have indicated that follower interdependence (Eberly and Fong, 2013), leader 
gender (Keck, 2019), vertical collectivism (Shao and Martin, 2020), and trust in the leader 
(Shao, 2019b) would moderate the impact of leader negative emotional expressions on 
employee cognition and behaviors. Construction projects facilitate close communication 
between leaders and employees, implying that employees’ power distance orientation can 
significantly influence their perception of leader anger expressions (Sydow and Windeler, 
2020; Yan et al., 2020). This study indicated that employees with high power distance 
orientation are more susceptible to leader anger expressions, thereby amplifying the 
negative impact of leader anger expressions on employees’ work hope.
Practical implications
The study revealed the negative impact of leader anger expressions on employees within 
construction projects. Organizations and leaders may implement the following measures to 
mitigate these adverse outcomes.
Firstly, organizations are advised to ensure leaders recognize the adverse impacts of their 
anger expressions and leaders should implement measures to mitigate the damage. In the 
construction industry, where projects are temporary and high-stress (Bakker and Janowicz-
Panjaitan, 2009), leaders can establish “cooling-off periods” (Dorison et al., 2020), meaning

they should step back when problems arise and calmly assess whether the situation warrants

anger management workshops tailored for high-pressure environments which include role-
playing scenarios common in construction projects to help leaders practice coping strategies 
(Sadagheyani et al., 2021).
Secondly, leaders should enhance employees’ meaningfulness at work in order to elevate 
their work hope, particularly in project contexts involving leader anger expressions. In 
construction projects, implementing team-based learning and exchange programs is crucial 
for leaders. These not only help employees acquire a broader skill set, reducing monotony but 
also maintain high engagement levels (Mlekus and Maier, 2021), thereby enhancing their 
work hope. Additionally, leaders can introduce new devices such as real-time progress 
dashboards to provide live updates on project milestones, individual contributions, and 
overall project status. Such transparency allows employees to see the immediate impact of 
their work (Gara et al., 2021), fostering a sense of achievement and enhancing work hope.
Finally, organizations are advised to screen individuals with high power distance 
orientation during recruitment and provide training for existing employees with such 
orientation. In the recruitment process, organizations should assess candidates’ ability to 
cope with leaders’ negative emotional expressions, selecting those who can effectively 
manage such situations. This can be achieved through psychological assessments and 
scenario simulations (Adamovic et al., 2022). In training, organizations should provide 
specialized stress management programs, teaching high power distance employees 
techniques. These include deep breathing, temporarily stepping away from the situation, 
and seeking peer support to help them remain rational in the face of leader anger expressions 
(Toussaint et al., 2021), thereby mitigating negative impacts.
Research limitation
There are several limitations that merit consideration in this study. The first is the utilization 
of a cross-sectional research design. As the data were gathered contemporaneously, it was 
not possible to fully infer the causal relationship between leader anger expressions and 
employees’ boundary-spanning behaviors. Future research should prioritize developing 
longitudinal studies or experimental methods to examine the effects of leader anger 
expressions on boundary-spanning behaviors. Secondly, the model’s variables were 
evaluated based on self-reported data from employees, inevitably introducing common 
method variance and confounding the findings. Future investigations are suggested to 
employ a multi-source data collection strategy to reduce the common method variance.
Thirdly, the data collection of leader anger expressions primarily relies on reports from 
employees, which may reflect their subjective perceptions rather than an accurate 
representation of the actual leader emotional expressions. Future research could employ a 
more objective data collection approach, encompassing multiple subordinates’ perceptions of 
leader anger expressions to obtain a more objective measurement.
