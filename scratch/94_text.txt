A B S T R A C T   
The current study examined the relationships among role stress, proactive personality, employee creativity, and 
customer-oriented boundary spanning behaviors (COBSBs) for frontline service employees (FSEs). This study 
collected data from 382 FSEs in international hotels in Taiwan to test the research model. The empirical results 
revealed that employee creativity was positively affected by proactive personality but negatively affected by role 
conflict and role ambiguity. FSEs’ creativity positively affects COBSBs, including external representation, in­
ternal influence, and service delivery. Internal influence also positively affects service delivery and mediates the 
relationship between FSEs’ creativity and service delivery.   
1. Introduction

for the birth of new products and services (Akgunduz, Alkan, & G¨o). The 
hospitality industry is a customer-oriented service industry involving 
frequent interactions between customers and frontline service em­
ployees (FSEs). FSEs, by dealing with customers’ requirements and 
collecting fist-hand information of customer service, are in an optimal 
position to generate creative ideas to improve the customer service 
offered (Ruiz-Palomino & Zoghbi-Manrique-de-Lara, 2020; Wang, Wen,

in hotel service has been recognized by researchers and practitioners (e. 
g. Wang, Wen, Pas¸amehmeto˘glu, & Guchait, 2021). 
With increasing competition, FSEs’ creativity becomes a source of 
competitive advantages because using creative ideas can rapidly solve 
customers’ problems, delight customers and then to enhance service 
quality and customer satisfaction. Here is a story of how two FSEs, work 
at Dove Mountain Resort, use creative ideas to solve customers’ problem 
as follows (Solomon, 2017). While a guest family packed up to leave for 
an airport, they found that their son lost his favorite Thomas toy. 
Without the permission from management, the two FSEs purchased a 
Thomas toy and composed a tale about how a Thomas toy extended 
vacation, including adorable pictures of Thomas exploring Dove 
Mountain Resort. And then the gift of Thomas toy with the note arrived 
by FedEx at the guest family. 
FSEs are viewed as boundary-spanning workers with responsibilities 
to both internal (managers and co-workers) and external (customers) 
entities (Agnihotri, Rapp, Andzulis, & Gabler, 2014). FSEs’ creativity 
may provide excellence service delivery to enhance customer satisfac­
tion, increase impressions toward the company, and internally share 
their creative ideas with co-workers (Bettencourt & Brown, 2003; Chang 
& Teng, 2017; Kim, Hon, & Lee, 2010). In view to this, FSEs’ creativity 
may simultaneously provide benefits to customers, co-workers, and 
managers. 
Previous research on employees’ behavior role behavior at the FSEs’ 
level includes in-role and extra-role behavior. Especially, extra-role 
behavior is also viewed as organizational citizenship behavior (OCB) 
(Schepers & Van der Borgh, 2020). The hospitality literature has dis­
cussed various OCBs, but the OCBs are not simultaneously directed to 
the organization, co-workers, and customers. For example, traditional 
OCBs are directed to the organization itself (e.g. Kim, McGinley, Choi, & 
Agmapisarn, 2020), interpersonal citizenship behaviors are directed to 
the co-worker itself (e.g. Khliefat, Chen, Ayoun, & Eyoun, 2021), 
customer-oriented citizenship behaviors (e.g. Garba, Babalolab, & Guo, 
2018) or service-oriented citizenship behaviors (e.g. Kloutsiniotis & 
Mihail, 2020) are directed to the customer itself. Due to the FSEs’ role of

customer-oriented boundary spanning behaviors (COBSBs), which the 
behaviors are directed to the organization, co-workers, and customers. 
According to Bettencourt and Brown (2003), enhancing COBSBs is a 
promising avenue for FSEs’ seeking to achieve excellence in three as­
pects: external representation, internal influence, and service delivery. 
That means that external representation refers to present a positive 
impression of the company; internal influence refers to internally share a

Since FSEs delivery customer service in their role as boundary 
spanning, the need to interact with a wider set of customers, co-workers, 
and superiors who have various demands and expectations may allow 
them to experience high level of role stress (Hodari, Waldthausen, & 
Sturman, 2014; Grobelna, 2015). FSEs may follow job descriptions to 
perform standardized operational routine tasks to meet customer re­
quirements. However, because service production and consumption are 
performed in the dynamic process of customer-FSE interaction, FSEs are 
often asked to meet the uncertain needs of customers without a clear 
standard, even deviate from the standard service procedure (Wang et al., 
2021). On the other hand, since FSEs often take on multiple roles in 
conflict as boundary spanners of both the company’s and customer’s 
interests (Kim, Murrmann, Lee, Kim, 2009), they receive incompatible 
job demands from customers, co-workers, and superiors, but are not able 
to satisfy all their demands simultaneously (Karatepe, 2010). The con­
tradictory and conflict inherent to boundary-spanning FSEs results role 
ambiguity and role conflict (Hodari et al., 2014), which generate the 
depletion of energy and then impede their generation of creative ideas 
(Hon, Chan, & Lu, 2013). Prior research suggests that role ambiguity 
and role conflict negatively affect employee creativity (Eatough, Chang, 
Miloslavic, & Johnson, 2011). 
Since services are produced by means of employees’ personal in­
teractions with customers, employee creativity is attributed to person­
ality traits (Shalley, Zhou, & Oldham, 2004; Akgunduz et al., 2018). 
Proactive personality is described as one who is likely to equip capa­
bilities of showing initiatives, taking actions, and identifying opportu­
nities (Horng, Tsai, Hu, & Liu, 2016). While responding to uncertain 
customer needs and requests, passive employees may follow routine 
standard operations, but proactive employees tend to find opportunities 
to make changes in the standard operation and then seek new and better 
solutions (Alikaj, Ning, & Wu, 2020). Companies that wish to emphasize 
creativity may be more successful if they hire and retain individuals with 
proactive personality (Kim, Hon, & Crant, 2009). 
In summary, the outcomes of FSEs creativity may provide benefits to 
the organization, co-workers, and customers, hotel organizations may 
need to understand how the key factors obstruct and facilitate FSE 
creativity. Prior research suggest that individual (e.g., affective or 
cognitive states, and individual personality) and contextual (e.g., work 
environment) factors affect the development of creativity (Amabile, 
Conti, Coon, Lazenby, & Herron, 1996). Based on the job 
demands-resources (JD-R), job demands decrease employee well-being, 
but job resources increase employee well-being (Bakker & Demerouti, 
2007). Creativity-related studies in the hotel industry argued that hin­
drance stressors as work demands negatively affect employee creativity 
(Hon et al., 2013), but personal traits, such as proactive personality as 
personal resources positively affect employee creativity (Horng et al., 
2016). Despite the importance of understanding how role stress and 
proactive personality affect employee creativity, empirical research has 
yet to identify the relationships among role ambiguity, role conflict, 
proactive personality, and FSEs’ creativity. 
The current study aims to provide two significant contributions. 
First, we explore how FSEs’ creativity as a trigger facilitates the devel­
opment of COBSBs. Prior research in the hospitality industry revealed 
that employee creativity generates positive performance, such as 
including employee job performance (e.g. Chang & Teng, 2017), 
customer satisfaction (e.g. Wang et al., 2021), hotel performance (e.g. 
Dabrowski, Brzozowska-Wo´s, Gołąb-Andrzejak, & Firgolska, 2019). 
However, these concepts view the organization or its employees rather 
than customers as the beneficiary of discretionary behavior (Schepers & 
Van der Borgh, 2020), few research proposes customer-oriented OCB 
that are assumed to be beneficial to not only organizations and its em­
ployees, but also to customers (Jung & Yoon, 2020). The current study 
focuses on COBSBs to engage in exploring how creativity affects external 
representation, internal influence, and service delivery. Furthermore, 
the current study examines how internal influence mediates the rela­
tionship between creativity and service delivery. Second, few studies 
have examined how both role stress and proactive personality affect 
employee creativity (Liu & Shi, 2014), especially in the hospitality in­
dustry. Hon and Lui (2016) reviewed creativity-related studies in the 
hospitality industry and noted that job stress and unwillingness to 
change are critical determinants hindering employee creativity in the 
workplace. With the ongoing debates on the JD-R as a backdrop, we 
propose a broader perspective of what obstructs and facilities of FSEs’ 
creativity by simultaneously investigating the negative effect of role 
ambiguity and role conflict and the positive effect of proactive 
personality. 
For this study, a survey was conducted of 382 FSEs working in in­
ternational hotels in Taiwan in the Fall of 2019. The number of foreign 
tourists visiting Taiwan hit a record high in 2019 (Huang, 2020) and the 
importance of international hotels is evident. In 2019, there were 79 
international hotels in Taiwan catering for foreign nationals, with a total 
of 21,968 rooms, but only 48 local hotels, providing just 6,922 rooms 
(Tourism Bureau, 2019). It is thus imperative for hotels to cultivate 
employee creativity and promote COBSBs if they are to satisfy these 
visitors. 
2. Literature review and hypotheses development 
According to Amabile et al. (1996) creativity can be defined as the 
process of generating useful and new ideas with regard to practices, 
procedures, services, or products for an organization. In the service 
context, FSEs are an important source of employee creativity because 
they are the people tasked with identifying customer needs, dealing with 
more challenging tasks, solving service problems creatively, and deliv­
ering a customized service (Coelho, Augusto, & Lages, 2011). Employee 
creativity can generate positive outcomes for individuals and firms, 
including innovation (Slåtten, Svensson, & ), career success (Kim, Hon, 
& Crant, 2009), high job performance (Agnihotri et al., 2014; Chang & 
Teng, 2017), and new service development (Yang, Lee, & Cheng, 2016). 
According to the job demands–resources (JD-R) model, work char­
acteristics can be classified in terms of two categories, namely job de­
mands and job resources, both of which are relevant to employee well- 
being (Bakker & Demerouti, 2007). Job demands incur physiological or 
psychological costs, while job resources reduce job demands and ach­
ieve work goals. This contrasts with the two assumptions of conservation 
of resources (COR): (1) the primacy of resource loss, where efforts are 
made to protect these resources, and (2) resource investment, where 
resources are invested to gain additional resources (Hobfoll, Halbesle­
ben, Neveu, & Westman, 2018). Combining resource investment with 
the motivational aspect of job resources, Xanthopoulou, Bakker, 
Demerouti, and Schaufeli (2007) suggested that personal resources can 
lead to an accumulation of resources that subsequently improve positive 
outcomes. Based on the above concepts, role stress is considered to arise 
from job demands that lead to resource depletion, and a proactive per­
sonality is a willingness to invest job resources to support goal 
achievement. 
Previous research on employees’ role behavior can be classified as 
examining in-role behavior and extra-role behavior (Schepers & Van der 
Borgh, 2020). In-role behavior refers to fulfill the formally required 
tasks to meet organizational standards, and also be viewed as 
role-prescribed behavior (Bettencourt & Brown, 2003). Extra-role con­
cerns discretionary behaviors that go beyond job requirements, and also

boundary spanning behaviors (COBSBs), including external represen­

2.1. Role conflict, role ambiguity, and employee creativity 
Role stress tends to be generated when the expected and perceived 
roles differ (Hartenian, Hadaway, & Badovick, 1994). Role stress in­
cludes role ambiguity, role conflict, and role overload. In the challenge 
stressor–hindrance stressor framework, compared to role overload as a 
challenge stressor, both role ambiguity and role conflict are viewed as 
hindrance-related stressors (Akinola, Kapadia, Lu, & Mason, 2019). 
Role conflict and role ambiguity are the two major types of role 
stress. Role conflict refers to incompatible job expectations and can 
originate from multiple roles or a single role (Dubinsky & Skinner, 
1984), while role ambiguity is caused by a lack of knowledge and in­
formation on how to execute the job correctly (Dubinsky & Mattson, 
1979). Perhaps not surprisingly, role conflict and role ambiguity are 
negatively related to work outcomes, typically by reducing organiza­
tional commitment, job satisfaction, and work involvement (Eatough 
et al., 2011). 
According to the principle of COR (Hobfoll, 1989; Hobfoll et al., 
2018), when FSEs are under stressful situations, in order to minimize 
resource loss, they might not be willing to give additional energy and 
time to creative thinking (Ng & Feldman, 2012). Hindrance stressors 
possess a negative impact on performance regardless of the effort an 
individual exerts, thus making it much less likely that new ideas will 
arise (Seery, 2011). Combining COR and the concept of hindrance 
stressors, Akinola et al. (2019) argued that threat appraisals are asso­
ciated with constrictive physiological stress responses that allow the 
excessive release of dopamine in prefrontal regions, so threat physiology 
actually reduces creativity performance. 
A meta-analysis conducted by Eatough et al. (2011) asserted that 
both role ambiguity and role conflict are hindering stressors that lead to 
negative job performance. In the hospitality industry, Geng, Liu, Liu, 
and Feng (2014) asserted that hindrance stress can affect FSEs’ crea­
tivity negatively in the local restaurant industry; Hon et al. (2013) also 
indicated that hindrance stress has a negative impact on hotel em­
ployees’ creativity. Because role conflict increases the cognitive 
perception of incompatible job expectations, FSEs experiencing role 
conflict may feel threatened by the risk of resource loss and a subsequent 
resource-depleting experience, which may be accompanied by signifi­
cant threat physiological responses. In order to prevent this loss of re­
sources, role conflict might therefore inhibit employee creativity 
(Montani, Setti, Sommovigo, ) as FSEs may limit the resources they 
devote to their work engagement, reducing the level of employee 
creativity (Gichohi, 2014). Role ambiguity can be related to the defi­
ciency of role clarity, referring to the extent to which an individual 
obtains and realizes information about his or her job requirements 
(Knight, Kim, & Crutsinger, 2007). It seems logical to assume that if FSEs 
feel uncertainty or lack adequate information about what is expected of 
them and how to perform their jobs, FSEs tend to experience frustration 
at work (Coelho et al., 2011; De Clercq, 2019). Uncertainties such as 
unclear job descriptions may cause FSEs to suffer from energy depletion 
and threat physiological responses (Akinola et al., 2019; De Clercq, 
2019). When FSEs experience frustration due to this lack of role clarity, 
they will also tend to minimize the associated depletion of their re­
sources, which will in turn adversely affect their creative behavior (De 
Clercq, 2019). 
As such, this research proposes the following hypotheses: 
H1.
Role conflict negatively affects FSEs’ creativity. 
H2.
Role ambiguity negatively affects FSEs’ creativity. 
2.2. Proactive personality and employee creativity 
Proactive personality refers to individual’s inclination to “scan for 
opportunities, show initiative, take action, and persevere until they 
reach closure by bringing about change” (Bateman & Crant, 1993, p. 
104). Put differently, a proactive person tends to take the initiative by 
seeking information, identifying and acting on opportunities, and 
reshaping the current environment or moving to a more desirable situ­
ation (Crant, 1995, 2000) in order to bring about meaningful change in 
their work environment. On the contrary, non-proactive people prefer to 
await opportunities passively, and not incline to actively shape their 
surroundings (Crant, 1995, 2000). Proactive personality is relevant to

In comparison with non-proactive FSEs, FSEs with proactive per­
sonalities are more likely to exhibit creativity for a number of reasons. 
First, by their very nature, a proactive personality will use their initia­
tive to change their environment to create a more desirable situation 
(Fuller & Marler, 2009; Bergeron, Schroeder, & Martinez, 2014). Prior 
research indicates that proactive employees tend to look for better so­
lutions that go beyond the expectations of their formal responsibilities 
because they want to reshape their current situations (Crant, 2000). For 
example, Jiang and Gu (2015) suggested that proactive employees may 
feel responsibility for change because they gain available resources from 
the organization, and are expected to be able to use the resources 
responsibly and make work-related improvements to achieve organiza­
tional strategic objectives. 
Second, entrepreneurial intention and behavior are more likely to 
occur for proactive people (Paul, Hermel, & Srivatava, 2017). Creativity 
requires experimentation, trial and error, or even failure, thus creativity 
is associated with psychological risk (Powell, 2008). Highly proactive 
employees can tolerate failure, and are thus willing to take greater risks 
to explore new pathways (Coelho et al., 2011). This is because they tend 
to think ahead to evaluate possible outcomes, anticipate possible fu­
tures, and act in advance (Jiang & Gu, 2015). 
Based on the COR perspective, proactive FSEs who have stronger 
inner thoughts and feelings are likely to adopt positive strategies as they 
engage in problem-solving (Bateman & Crant, 1993) and generate new 
ideas, and as a result, generate further resource gain. The current study 
therefore proposes the following hypothesis: 
H3.
Proactive personality positively affects FSEs’ creativity. 
2.3. Employee creativity and COBSBs 
2.3.1. Employee creativity and external representation 
External representation involves presenting a positive impression of 
the firm’s image, products, and services to outsiders (Bettencourt & 
Brown, 2003). Social exchange theory reflects employees’ evaluation of 
rewards from the company (Emerson, 1976) and it has therefore been 
suggested that social exchange theory should be utilized to investigate 
why employees’ job satisfaction fosters external representation (Bet­
tencourt, Brown, & MacKenzie, 2005; Lages, 2012; Yoo, 2013; Limpa­
nitgul & Jirotmontree, 2012). When they experience positive effects 
from their organizations, FSEs may become more motivated to carry out 
extra-role behaviors to reciprocate their feeling of satisfaction (Lages,

2012; Yoo, 2013). Barnes, Ponder, and Hopkins (2015) argued that 
external representation is a form of employee referral that is viewed as 
the most credible and trustworthy of all information sources. According 
to Parish, Berry, and Lam (2008), satisfied employees deliver positive 
word-of-mouth and job satisfaction positively affects employee referrals. 
In their examination of the employee referral concept, Barnes et al.

Prior research on creativity has shown that creativity improves job 
satisfaction (Mishra & Shukla, 2012). Kim, Hon, and Crant (2009) found 
that employees who create new and useful ideas tend to improve their 
job environment and therefore experience job satisfaction. Combining 
these arguments, this study assumes that while FSEs experience job 
satisfaction originating from their creativity, they may increase their 
referral intention, and thus enhance the image of the firm through FSEs’ 
advocacy of the firm’s products or services. Thus this present research 
proposes the following hypothesis: 
H4a.
FSEs’ creativity positively affects external representation. 
2.3.2. Employee creativity and internal influence 
Internal influence refers to the degree to which an individual takes 
the initiative in sharing information and communicating with their 
colleagues and the organization as a whole regarding changes in 
methods or processes that lead to service improvements (Bettencourt 
et al., 2005). In this context, FSEs, as important boundary spanners, can 
be viewed as information processing units and critical buffers that 
confirm whether a proposed change will benefit the organization or not 
(Andersen & Kragh, 2015). Thus, Woisetschl¨ager, Hanning, and Back­
haus (2016, p. 110) redefined internal influence as “disseminating in­
formation about creative solutions to customer problems and suggesting 
possible service improvements”. They went on to suggest that frontline 
employees with internal influence may gather creative ideas during 
their interactions with customers and then engage in idea dissemination 
in the company. 
Following this logic, as filters or gatekeepers of information, creative 
FSEs may provide novel and useful ideas regarding customer needs and

FSEs can internally disseminate information within the organization,

ideas with their co-workers and then make suggestions about further 
improvements in service delivery. As such, this study proposes the 
following hypothesis: 
H4b.
FSEs’ creativity positively affects internal influence. 
2.3.3. Employee creativity and service delivery 
Service delivery is based on job descriptions in which employees are 
required to serve customers in a conscientious, responsive, flexible, and 
courteous manner (Bettencourt et al., 2005). Service delivery behavior is 
viewed as a key element of service quality provision that influences 
customer relationship quality and customer satisfaction (Yoo, Arnold, & 
Frankwick, 2014). 
However, when the standard available scripts are insufficient to 
solve customer problems, frontline employees may instead deploy more 
creative strategies to satisfy customers’ needs and attain a higher level of 
role-prescribed performance. For example, creative FSEs may create 
new ways to deal with customer problems and complaints, developing 
new procedures that support the delivery of better service (Geng et al., 
2014), make immediate improvements to successfully meet customers’ 
expectations, and provide high-quality customer service for hospitality 
management (Coelho et al., 2011). Employee creativity positively af­
fects role-prescribed customer service performance (Liu, Liu, & Geng, 
2013) and improves marketing performance because new service con­
cepts can meet customer needs and build customers’ trust in new ser­
vices (Yang et al., 2016). As such, this study proposes the following 
hypothesis: 
H4c.
FSEs’ creativity positively affects service delivery. 
2.3.4. Employee creativity, internal influence, and service delivery 
Internal market orientation involves obtaining and circulating in­
telligence of employee needs and then responding appropriately to these 
needs (Lings & Greenley, 2005). Based on the concept of internal market 
orientation, this research assumes that employees with internal influ­
ence are likely to improve their service delivery. According to Lings and 
Greenley (2010), enacting appropriate internal market responses en­
ables employees to deliver a service more effectively, especially when 
followed by organizational strategies that enhance employee identifi­
cation with the organization, thus fostering employees’ in-role behavior. 
Tortosa, Moliner, and S´anchez (2009) asserted that frontline employees’ 
perceptions of internal market orientation have a positive impact on the 
quality of service perceived by external customers. Internal influence is 
considered to be a relatively extra-role activity due to its discretionary 
nature, but service delivery behavior is viewed as being more 
role-prescribed because it is likely to be specified in employees’ job 
descriptions (Bettencourt et al., 2005). FSEs’ organizational citizen 
behavior may lead to altruistic behaviors that then foster a higher level 
of service delivery behavior (Netemeyer, Boles, McKee, & McMurrian, 
1997). 
Therefore, given this background, it is posited that FSEs’ internal 
influence affects service delivery positively. Further, as discussed pre­
viously, FSEs’ service delivery and internal influence are outcomes of 
FSEs’ creativity, suggesting that internal influence mediates the link 
between employee creativity and service delivery. This research there­
fore proposed the following hypotheses: 
H5.
FSEs’ internal influence positively affects service delivery. 
H6.
FSEs’ internal influence mediates the relationship between 
employee creativity and service delivery. 
3. Methodology 
3.1. Data collection 
This study collected data from FSEs in international hotels in Taiwan 
to test the research hypotheses. There were 79 international hotels in 
Taiwan in September 2019 (Tourism Bureau, 2019). The human 
resource managers of all 79 hotels were contacted and asked if they 
would be willing to help collecting the data for this research; 25 agreed 
to do so. Thirty questionnaires were sealed in envelopes and sent to 
those human resource managers at 25 international hotels (750 sealed 
questionnaires in total). Then the mangers distributed questionnaires to 
their FSEs and FSEs returned the completed questionnaires in another 
sealed envelope to his/her managers for sending them back to us. The 
incentives of gift certificates (150 NTDs) were provided to thank to re­
spondents of this study and a 1000 NTDs worth gift certificate was given 
to each manager for appreciating his/her kind assistance. Research data 
were collected from October 15th to December 15th in 2019. 382 valid 
samples were collected, for a response rate of 50.9%. The responses of 
the late respondents (51 persons) were compared to those of the early 
respondents (332 persons) to examine non-response bias. The results 
showed no differences in any variable and hence non-response bias is 
unlikely to be a problem (Armstrong & Overton, 1977). The final sample 
consisted of 249 female (65.2%) and 133 male (34.8%) staff. Of these, 
29.3% were below 30 years of age, and 23.8% were aged 41–50. Most 
respondents were under 5 years of tenure (39.5%) and 51.8% had a 
university degree.

3.2. Measures 
All measures were developed and/or adapted based on previous 
research. For role ambiguity and role conflict, a 6-item scale (3 items 
each) from Rizzo, House, and Lirtzman (1970) was employed. The 
measurement items also were adopted in the hotel setting (e.g. Karatepe, 
2010). A 3-item scale from Seibert, Kraimer, and Crant (2001) was 
utilized to assess proactive personality. The measurement items also 
were adopted in the hospitality industry (e.g. Horng et al., 2016). For the 
measure of employee creativity, Zhou and George’s (2001) 4-item scale 
from was employed. Studies in the hospitality industry such as Hon et al. 
(2013) have used the measurement items. For the measure of COBSB, 
this research used a 9-item scale from Bettencourt et al. (2005); internal 
influence, external representation, and service delivery were assessed 
with 3 items each. The measurement items also were used in the hos­
pitality industry (e.g. Jung & Yoon, 2020).A 7-point Likert scale, ranging 
from 1 for “strongly disagree” to 7 for “strongly agree” was used in all 
scales. A pre-test with a sample of 50 hotel FSEs was conducted before 
collecting data formally. The pre-test results confirmed the Cronbach’s α 
value of each variable was greater than 0.6 and thus measurements for 
all variables were deemed reliable. 
3.3. Common method variance 
The empirical results of this research might be affected by common 
method variance (CMV) since data were collected employing self-report 
questionnaires. The Harman’s one-factor test was thus utilized to test 
this threat (Podsakoff, MacKenzie, Lee, & Podsakoff, 2003). The fit 
indices of the one-factor model are as the following: χ2 (209) = 2324.79, 
p < .001, GFI = 0.64; AGFI = 0.56; RMR = 0.19), indicating a poor 
model fit. The significant chi-square change (Δχ2 (21) = 1852.3, p <
.001) showed that the seven-factor model used for this research had a 
better fit than the single-factor model tested. The results suggest that 
CMV was not an issue for this study.

4.1. Scale validity and reliability 
Whether or not items of questions in the survey questionnaire 
appropriately stand for the latent variables can be tested by conducting a 
confirmatory factor analysis. Amos 21.0 was therefore used to test 
reliability and validity of the latent variables. As shown in Table 1, χ2/df 
= 2.513, RMSEA = 0.063, RMR = 0.059, and IFI, NFI, TLI, CFI, GFI were

A Cronbach’s α value ranging from 0.5 to 0.7 is acceptable and over 
0.7 indicates high reliability, according to Nunnally (1978). The Cron­

that the questionnaire used in this research was acceptably reliable. The 
factor loadings for all the observed items and their latent variables were 
also calculated. As illustrated in Table 1, all factor loadings were greater 
than 0.50, indicating a good item reliability (Hair, Black, Babin, & 
Anderson, 2010). Additionally, Composite reliability (CR) and average 
variance extracted (AVE) were used to assess internal consistency. CR 
and AVE values should be above 0.60 and 0.50, respectively (Fornell & 
Larcker, 1981). CR and AVE values of this research, as shown in Table 1, 
met the requirements except for role conflict (AVE = 0.354), indicating 
an appropriate internal consistency. Discriminant validity was assessed 
by comparing the square of the correlation parameters with the AVE 
value. The square root of the AVE value of every variable is greater than 
the inter-construct correlations (Hair et al., 2010), as shown in Table 2, 
indicating a good discriminant validity and that variables are distinct 
from one another.

5. Discussion and conclusion 
Several meaningful contributions are provided in the present 
research. First, the research results indicate that role conflict and role 
ambiguity should be viewed as hindering stressors that limit the re­
sources available for engaging in creative activities through a resource 
depletion process. On the other hand, a proactive person invests per­
sonal resources in creative activities through an active motivational 
process. These findings are in line with COR and JD-R model. With re­
gard to role stress, the study’s findings confirm the negative influence of 
role conflict and role ambiguity on employee creativity. This is consis­
tent with previous studies which assert role conflict (e.g. Wang, Huang, 
Davison, & Yang, 2020) and role ambiguity (e.g. Coelho et al., 2011) 
negatively affect employee creativity. Hon and Lui (2016) contended 
that time pressure and workload hinder employee creativity in the 
hospitality industry. These results extend Hou and Liu’s (2016) work 
and suggest that role conflict and role ambiguity can also be obstacles to 
FSEs’ creativity in the hotel industry. These findings also confirm that 
proactive personality directly improves employee creativity, which is in 
accordance with previous research (e.g. Kim, Hon, & Crant, 2009). 
Because FSEs with higher proactive personality traits actively look for 
opportunities of improving their current environments, they are most 
likely to be the ones who come up with new ideas. 
Second, this study focused on a specific and detailed examination of 
how FSEs’ creativity affects COBSBs. Although past research has sug­
gested that employee creativity leads to positive individual and orga­
nizational outcomes (Hon & Lui, 2016), no evidence had been presented 
to show conclusively that employee creativity leads to COBSBs. The 
research results provide this evidence and confirm that FSEs’ creativity 
positively affects external representation, internal influence, and service 
delivery. When FSEs serve their customers in a creative way, they 
deliver excellent service that satisfies customers, gaining a better 
external impression from the customers, and take the initiative in 
communicating information about improvements in service delivery to 
their co-workers and organizations. In particular, the research findings 
confirm that internal influence has a mediation effect between employee 
creativity and service delivery. These findings also reveal that employee 
creativity improves role-prescribed behaviors (service delivery) through 
extra-role behaviors (internal influence). Thus, the present study adds 
new knowledge to employee creativity literature by extending the 
consideration of COBSBs as consequences of FSEs’ creativity in the 
hospitality setting. 
5.1. Managerial implications 
The hotel industry is a high-contact service industry, where service 
quality and customer satisfaction depend on FSEs’ customer-oriented 
behavior (Barnes et al., 2015; Hon & Lui, 2016). Faced with increas­
ingly competitive environments, customer-oriented hotel firms must put 
customers’ interest first, highlighting the need for their FSEs to actively 
seek out new ways to satisfy their customers. Boosting FSEs’ creativity 
may generate beneficial customer-oriented behavior such as better 
external representation, internal influence, and service delivery. Espe­
cially, FSEs’ creativity affects service delivery through internal influ­
ence. Internal influence focuses on sharing, communicating, and 
disseminating information with both the organization and their 
co-workers when suggesting possible service improvements. Karlsson 
and Skål´en (2015) argued that frontline employees who have been 
involved in developing an improvement in service delivery can inter­
nally disseminate information on the new service to their co-workers 
and facilitate its market launch. Thus, hotel managers may foster the 
development of FSEs internal influence. 
If managers are to encourage creativity among their FSEs effectively, 
they need to understand what obstructs and facilitates this creativity. 
First, the results suggest that role conflict and role ambiguity often un­
dermine employees’ creativity. If employees are uncertain of their job 
expectations and requirement, or experience role incongruities, they 
tend to focus on meeting performance standards and preventing 
resource loss, avoiding the need to come up with new ways to solve 
customer problems. In order to decrease role ambiguity and role con­
flict, hotel managers may empower FSEs to revise some job descriptions 
under the company’s policy, and provide training programs in 
communication skills for minimizing problems related with role conflict 
(Karatepe, 2010). Second, our findings suggest that a proactive per­
sonality may improve employee creativity. The hospitality industry 
needs proactive employees who are passionate about their work if it is to 
be able to cope with diverse and rapidly changing customer demands 
(Horng et al., 2016). Thus, organizations may consider reviewing the 
selection procedures they utilize to assess job applicants and hire more 
proactive candidates from the labor market. 
5.2. Limitations and future research 
Some limitations of this research should be noticed. First, this study 
examined the relationships among role stress, proactive personality, 
employee creativity, and COBSBs. However, this study used cross- 
sectional data and was unable to establish causality given that the 
data are not experimental or longitudinal in nature. Future research 
could consider employ experiments and provide additional insights by 
exploring the potentially moderating and mediating effects of other 
factors such as person-job fit, work engagement. Second, self-reporting 
scales were used to measure role stress, proactive personality, crea­
tivity, and COBSBs, as perceived by the FSEs who participated in the 
study. Harman’s one-factor test result suggests that CMV is not an issue 
for the current study; however, perceptions of supervisors and customers 
can be included in future research. Third, women comprise the majority 
of FSEs in the hotel sector in Taiwan (Buffon Digital and Technology, 
2019); future studies could explore the different impacts due to gender 
among the variables. Fourth, the data for this study was collected in the 
hospitality industry in Taiwan, raising the question of generalizability of 
the findings to firms in other industries and countries. Thus, testing this 
model in different service settings or in different countries would in­
crease its generalizability.
