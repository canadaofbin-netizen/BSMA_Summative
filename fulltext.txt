Knowledge sharing of inpatriates
Empirical evidence from an
ability –motivation –opportunity perspective
Tassilo Schuster
Ludwig-Maximilians-Universität München, Munich, Germany, and
Dirk Holtbrügge and Franziska Engelhard
Department of International Management,
Friedrich-Alexander-Universität Erlangen-Nürnberg, Nuremberg, Germany
Abstract
Purpose –The purpose of this study is to analyze the effects of inpatriates ’abilities, motivation and
opportunities on knowledge sharing and the moderating role of boundary spanning in this context.
Design/methodology/approach –By integrating the ability –motivation –opportunity framework with the
concept of boundary spanning four hypotheses are developed, which are tested against the data of 187
inpatriates working in Germany.Findings –The study reveals that inpatriates ’motivation and certain opportunities are positively related to
knowledge sharing, whereas inpatriates ’abilities do not show a positive effect. Moreover, it is shown that
inpatriate boundary spanning has a moderating effect on this relationship.Originality/value –Based on the results, the study enhances the current literature by introducing the
concept of reputation asymmetry. Moreover, requirements of how inpatriates ’assignments should be
designed and implications for further research are outlined.
Keywords International assignments, Ability-motivation-opportunity framework, Knowledge sharing,
Boundary spanning, Inpatriation, Reputation asymmetry
Paper type Research paper
1. Introduction
International assignees are important means of knowledge transfers within multinational
corporations (MNCs) as they are able to translate culturally instilled knowledge from one
context to another (Iles et al., 2004; Minbaeva and Michailova, 2004). While MNCs had mainly
focused on headquarters ’(HQs) delegates who transfer knowledge to foreign subsidiaries
(expatriates) (Chang et al., 2012; Harzing, 2001; Hocking et al., 2007; Minbaeva et al., 2014), they
have increasingly recognized the importance of knowledge creation capabilities of their
foreign subsidiaries (Colakoglu et al. , 2014) to withstand the hyper-competitive nature of
global markets (Moeller and Reiche, 2017). To exploit those knowledge creation capabilities,
MNCs have progressively introduced alternative staffing options, including flexpatriation
(Mayerhofer, Hartmann and Herbert, 2004; Mayerhofer, Hartmann, Michelitsch-Riedl and
Kollinger, 2004), international business travels (Welch et al. ,2 0 0 7 ) ,v i r t u a la s s i g n m e n t s
(Welch et al., 2003) and inpatriation (Harvey et al., 2011; Harvey, Speier and Novicevic, 1999;
Harvey and Buckley, 1997). In particular, inpatriation –assignees recruited from MNCs ’
subsidiaries or other third countries to work in HQ locations over varying timeframes for
various purposes and on varying levels of management (Moeller et al., 2016; Reiche, 2011) –is
of particular relevance for disseminating knowledge created in the MNC ’s foreign subsidiaries
because these employees are not only familiar with the subsidiary ’s products, processes and
customers, but often possess a dense personal network in their home organization and society
(Moeller et al., 2016). This makes inpatriates valuable resources regarding reverse innovationsEmployee Relations: The

While the global mobility literature is replete with research on expatriates and their
value to MNCs, research on inpatriates is still very fragmented and has only received limited
attention (Moeller and Reiche, 2017). During the past two decades, several researchers have
broadened the perspective of global mobility by studying the inpatriate phenomenon, which
reflects the increasing practical relevance of this employee group (Collings et al. , 2010;
Gertsen and Søderberg, 2012; Harvey, 1997; Harvey, Novicevic and Speier, 1999; Harvey,
Speier and Novicevic, 1999; Harvey and Buckley, 1997; Maley and Moeller, 2018; Moeller
et al. , 2010; Reiche, 2006, 2011; Tungli and Peiperl, 2009).
These researchers consistently outline that despite similarities between expatriates and
inpatriates, differences along numerous dimensions and additional challenges, compared to
their expatriate counterparts, are apparent (Mäkelä et al. , 2012; Maley and Moeller, 2018).
For instance, Harvey et al. (2005) emphasize a deep-rooted resistance toward the integration
of inpatriates in the home-country organization, which is often transmuted into silent but
concerted customs of ethnocentric stigmatization and stereotyping. This “collective
reservation ”toward inpatriates (Harvey et al. , 2005) particularly occurs in HQs when the
inpatriates come from peripheral units, technologically less-advanced countries, developing
economies and less important markets (Moeller et al., 2016). Moreover, inpatriates may also
face organizational barriers, such as language problems or a lack of management attention,
which may reduce their efforts to share knowledge (Chen et al. , 2010; Peltokorpi, 2015).
Finally, inpatriates frequently receive less organizational support in the form ofintercultural training or mentoring, which impedes their cultural adjustment to the host
country and host organization (Maley et al. , 2015).
Given these opportunities and challenges of this valuable group of assignees, MNCs seek
to understand how inpatriates ’efforts to share knowledge from the subsidiary to the HQ can
be increased. While previous literature has already provided initial evidence that inpatriates
and expatriates should be treated differently (Maley and Moeller, 2018) and outlined
distinctive features of inpatriates as agents of knowledge transfer (Reiche, 2006, 2011;
Reiche, Kraimer and Harzing, 2009), we still have a limited understanding whether
inpatriates ’efforts to share knowledge are primarily promoted by individual characteristics,
such as inpatriates ’international work experience, motivation or by organizational support
offered by the home and host organization –factors that have been frequently mentioned in
the expatriation context. Moreover, although boundary spanning is seen as an important
aspect of inpatriation (Reiche, 2011) and knowledge sharing (Barner-Rasmussen et al., 2014),
the role of inpatriate boundary spanning as a moderator in this context is yet to beexamined. Boundary spanning is defined as the activity to link and establish relationships
between previously unconnected individuals at different MNC units (Barner-Rasmussen
et al. , 2014; Monteiro and Birkinshaw, 2016; Reiche, 2011). As inpatriates often serve as
boundary spanners in the context of knowledge transfers –it is argued in this study that
inpatriates abilities, motivation and opportunities may have different effects on knowledge
sharing depending on the level of their boundary spanning activity. Therefore, this study
extends on previous studies and aims to explore and provide empirical evidence on the
question of how inpatriates ’abilities, motivation and opportunities affect knowledge
sharing and how inpatriate boundary spanning moderates this relationship. By doing so,
the study responds to recent calls for empirical research on inpatriates as the emerging body
of literature on inpatriation remains largely conceptual (Maley and Moeller, 2018).
As a theoretical foundation, the study applies the ability –motivation –opportunity (AMO)
framework. This framework is considered to be highly suitable for analyzing inpatriates ’
knowledge sharing because it addresses both individual characteristics (e.g. host-country
language skills) and organizational processes (e.g. organizational support) which have been972ER
41,5revealed as main determinants of knowledge sharing in previous studies (Feldman and
Bolino, 1999; Peltokorpi, 2015; Reiche, 2011). However, as the AMO framework mainly
focuses on the agents of knowledge transfer (i.e. inpatriates and employees of the home and
host organization), the relationships between these agents are not explicitly conceptualized.
Therefore, this study extends previous research by combining the AMO framework with the
concept of boundary spanning.
To realize our research objectives the study proceeds as follows: first, the current state of
research on inpatriation, knowledge sharing and boundary spanning will be illustrated as
well as the theoretical framework will be outlined. Next, our research model and hypotheses
are to be presented. Subsequently, we outline the methodological procedure before the
results are presented and discussed. Finally, we are reflecting on contributions, limitations
and implications of our study.
2. Theory and hypothesis
2.1 Theoretical background
Inpatriation among other staffing options plays an important role in the creation of a
beneficial global mobility mix of MNCs, which helps them to withstand the hyper-
competitive nature of global markets. However, inpatriates and the management of this
global staffing mechanism are still insufficiently explored and companies need a better
understanding of the value that such individuals can bring and the challenges they face
(Moeller and Reiche, 2017). Inpatriates are defined as assignees who are recruited from
MNCs ’subsidiaries or other third countries to work in HQ locations over varying
timeframes for various purposes across different management levels (Moeller et al. , 2016;
Reiche, 2011). This broad definition reflects the different conceptualizations that exist in the
extant inpatriate literature (Maley and Moeller, 2018; Reiche, 2011). Whereas Harvey et al.
(2005) and Harvey, Novicevic and Speier (1999) view inpatriation as semi-permanent to
permanent relocations with the objective to build global management teams at the HQ, other
scholars consider inpatriation as assignments of limited duration (three to five years) mainly
for boundary spanning and knowledge transfer purposes (van der Heijden et al. , 2009;
Peterson, 2003; Reiche, 2006, 2011; Shaffer et al., 1999). It becomes apparent that these two
streams look at different management levels when speaking of inpatriation. While the
former consider experienced subsidiary managers or senior employees that joined the
middle or top management in the HQ and who are responsible for long-term strategic
decisions of the MNC (Maley and Moeller, 2018), the latter focus on lower management levels
and specialized technical positions that are filled with foreign employees and managers whojoin the HQ for several years but will most likely return to their home units after they have
completed their assignments. Analogously, Moeller et al. (2016) accentuate that the presence
of inpatriates is widespread and covers varying levels of management, rather than being
restricted to the top management. Consistent with our objectives and in line with previous
research on inpatriate knowledge sharing (Harvey et al., 2011; Reiche, Kraimer and Harzing,
2009), this study focuses on the latter and defines inpatriates as assignees from MNCs ’
subsidiaries, who are relocated to the HQ for a limited timeframe mainly for boundary
spanning and knowledge transfer purposes.
Despite many similarities between inpatriates and parent country expatriates, the body
of literature on inpatriation outlines differences between expatriates and inpatriates and
specify additional challenges of inpatriates that might impede knowledge sharing (for an
overview, please see Moeller et al., 2016; Reiche, Kraimer and Harzing, 2009). First, whereas
expatriates carry with them the status and reputation associated with their role as HQ
representatives, inpatriates are perceived as outsiders or peripheral members by the HQ
staff, which limit their influence and credibility in the host unit. Likewise, Harvey et al.
(2005) refer to a “collective reservation ”toward inpatriates of the HQ staff by outlining973Knowledge
sharing of
inpatriatescustoms of ethnocentric stigmatization and stereotyping, causing that inpatriates will have
more difficulties and might need different abilities to prove their unique value for the MNC
and to successfully share knowledge with the HQ staff. Second, regarding dissimilar
adjustment processes between inpatriates and expatriates, previous inpatriate studies argue
that inpatriates are not only confronted to adjust to the new national culture but also need to
be socialized into the MNC ’s HQ corporate culture (Moeller et al., 2010), which might cause
additional barriers to knowledge sharing.
Despite the outlined differences that might have an impact on knowledge sharing
processes, the inpatriate literature outline fundamental parallels between these employee
groups concerning knowledge transfer processes. First, inpatriate scholars (e.g. Reiche,2011; Reiche, Kraimer and Harzing, 2009) argue that knowledge is rooted in individuals
(Felin and Hesterly, 2007) and that the individuals need the ability and willingness to
engage in knowledge sharing (Minbaeva and Michailova, 2004). Second, it is argued that the
process of transferring knowledge between individuals depends on social interaction, which
means that individuals need opportunities to share their knowledge with others (Reiche,
2011). These two rationales are strongly reflected in the AMO framework, which suggests
on a general level that ability, motivation and opportunity are the primary antecedents of
successful task performance (Blumberg and Pringle, 1982; Campbell et al. , 1993). Ability
covers an individual ’s knowledge, skills and experience that are needed to perform a task.
Motivation refers to the individual ’s willingness to perform it. Opportunity represents the
organizational mechanisms that enable task performance.
The AMO framework is well-established as a th eoretical basis for the explanation of
work performance (Blumberg and Pringle , 1982) and has been subsequently used to
explain firm-level decision making (Wu et al., 2004) and firm performance (Kim et al., 2015).
More recently, it has also been used to anal yze knowledge-management practices in
MNCs as knowledge has become more an d more people-dependent (Chang et al. , 2012;
Minbaeva et al., 2003; Reinholt et al., 2011). In line with the argumentation in the inpatriate
literature and in accordance with the AMO fra mework it can be concluded that just as the
successful performance of any task depends on t he ability, motivation and opportunity,
knowledge sharing is determined by the assignee ’s ability, motivation and opportunity
(Argote et al. , 2003).
While the AMO framework is useful for conceptualizing antecedents of inpatriates as
knowledge agents, the current literature on knowledge flows within MNCs argues that the
relationships between foreign assignees and employees of the home and host organizations
should also be considered. In particular, the role of boundary spanning in form of linking
previously unconnected individuals and facilitating information flows between differentorganizational units has received large attention in this context (Au and Fukuda, 2002;
Barner-Rasmussen et al., 2014). Although boundary spanning is seen as a central influencing
factor for successful knowledge transfers as it makes knowledge more widely available in an
organization, little empirical evidence is available on this topic (Barner-Rasmussen et al., 2014).
To give more clarification on this issue, this study takes an alternative approach to
Reiche (2011) by arguing that the effects of inpatriates ’abilities, motivation and opportunities
on knowledge sharing will vary as a function of inpatriate boundary spanning. In this study,
we define boundary spanning as the activity to link and establish relationships between
previously unconnected individuals at different MNC units (Barner-Rasmussen et al. , 2014;
Monteiro and Birkinshaw, 2016).
To deepen our understanding in this matter, this study combines the AMO framework
with the literature on boundary spanning. In the next section, the direct relationships
between the three dimensions of the AMO framework and knowledge sharing will be
elaborated ( H1a –H3b), followed by the conceptualization of the moderation effects of
inpatriate boundary spanning on these relationships ( H4a –H4c).974ER
41,52.2 Hypotheses
Ability as antecedent of inpatriate knowledge sharing . Ability reflects an individual ’sg e n e r a l
capacity to perform in specific situations (Cummings and Schwab, 1973). In the context ofMNCs ’knowledge management, ability refers to the knowledge, skills and experience
needed to solve difficulties in transferring knowledge between different organizational
units across national borders. The inpatriate literature regards international workexperience and host-country l anguage proficiency as crucia l abilities that facilitate
knowledge sharing (Froese et al. , 2016; Harvey et al. , 2000; Peltokorpi, 2015; Reiche, 2006;
Shaffer et al. , 1999).
International work experience can support knowledge transfer of MNCs in numerous
ways. Internationally experienced employees have been exposed to different cultures, marketsand organizational settings (Maley and Kramar, 2014; Takeuchi et al. , 2005), making them
aware of knowledge developed in one country that may be useful in other host countries of the
MNC (Kogut and Zander, 1993). Thus, international work experience enables employees,among others, to transfer knowledge about HQ-subsidiary relations, world-wide business
structures, complexities of global operations and characteristics of national markets from one
organizational unit to another (Lazarova and Tarique, 2005). While these arguments hold truefor expatriates and inpatriates alike, it can be argued that international work experience is of
particular relevance for subsidiary employees who work as inpatriates at the HQ. As outlined
above, inpatriates are often perceived as less qualified and have less credibility in particularwhen they come from a technologically and economically less-advanced country (Harvey,
Novicevic and Speier, 1999). This ethnocentric perspective of local employees may be less
pronounced toward inpatriates with extensive international work experience, a more facetedunderstanding of diverse organizational processes and a broader set of problem-solving
capabilities (Harvey, Novicevic and Speier, 1999; Minbaeva and Michailova, 2004). At the
same time, international work experience evokes a higher cultural self-awareness and allows
for the selective incorporation of foreign values and practices (Levy et al., 2007). This, in turn
may have a positive impact on the inpatriates ’respect for and the adaption to foreign cultures
and their openness for knowledge exchange (Harveston et al., 2000).
Finally, international work experience will facilitate the cultural adjustment process of
the inpatriate to the HQ and thus will hamper the loss of self-efficacy (Harvey, Novicevic andSpeier, 1999; Shaffer et al. , 1999). Harvey, Novicevic and Speier (1999) outline that in the
situations, in which inpatriates experience a devaluation of self-esteem, they may be less
able to efficiently share their knowledge regarding culturally and economically distant
countries and operations. Based on these considerations, internationally experienced
inpatriates will be regarded as more valuable in terms of acquired knowledge and skills andthus, more attractive as partners of knowledge exchange. Thus, it is hypothesized:
H1a. International work experience has a positive effect on inpatriates ’knowledge sharing.
A large portion of knowledge in MNCs is embedded in organizational structures, processes
and systems and is often only implicitly shared, for example by participation of subsidiary
employees in international task forces or by the direct transfer of employees (Rabbiosi,
2011). In order to transfer this knowledge from one unit of the MNC to others, it has to becodified and externalized (Nonaka, 1994). This requires a common language between the
sender and receiver of the knowledge transfer (Peltokorpi and Vaara, 2014; Zhang and
Peltokorpi, 2015).
Language represents not only an important means of communication, but is also used as
an informal source of expert power (Tenzer and Schuster, 2017). For example, assignees ’host-
country language proficiency is an important prerequisite of successful cultural adjustment
(Moeller et al., 2010) and acceptance by host-country employees (Moeller et al., 2016). At the975Knowledge
sharing of
inpatriatessame time, subsidiary employees who speak the national language of the HQ are often
perceived as more competent and occupy more central roles in transnational communication
networks (Harzing et al., 2011).
Inpatriates often have a lower social status, face higher adjustment challenges and need
to invest more time in order to become adequately socialized into the HQ corporate culture(Moeller et al., 2016; Reiche, 2006). Host-country language proficiency enables inpatriates to
more quickly adjust to the local environment, to better communicate with their colleagues,
and also strengthens the inpatriates ’understanding of the subtleties of the HQ corporate
culture (e.g. specific practices and routines). Thus, inpatriates who are fluent in the
host-country language can estimate better which knowledge is valuable and acceptable for
the HQ. Moreover, local employees will view them as a more valuable source of knowledgethan those without host-country language skills (Harzing and Pudelko, 2014). Thus, this
study argues that this ability will have a positive influence on inpatriates ’knowledge
sharing and therefore proposes:
H1b. Host-country language proficiency has a positive effect on inpatriates ’knowledge
sharing.
Motivation as antecedent of inpatriate knowledge sharing . Beyond ability, the AMO
framework highlights the importance of motivation as a driver of behavior and recognizes itas a decisive factor for knowledge sharing (Argote et al., 2003; Minbaeva et al., 2014; Zhang
and Peltokorpi, 2015). Motivation determines the direction, intensity and persistence of any
action and can be of intrinsic or extrinsic nature (Ryan and Deci, 2000). Osterloh and Frey(2000) highlight that intrinsic motivation is especially important when sharing tacit
knowledge, whereas Argote et al. (2003) and Kankanhalli et al. (2005) point out that
organizational rewards such as a higher salary, bonus or promotion are positively related tothe frequency of knowledge contribution.
Intrinsic motivation refers to performing an action or behavior for its own sake, out of
interest or for enjoyment derived from the experience (Ryan and Deci, 2000). For instance,
employees can be satisfied by enhancing their knowledge self-efficacy or confidence by
transferring knowledge to colleagues that is useful to the organization (Lin, 2007). Moreover,knowledge-sharing behavior can be regarded as a form of prosocial behavior which is an
important aspect of intrinsic motivation (Gagné, 2009).
In the context of international assignees, personal challenge is often considered as an
important intrinsic motivation (Reiche, 2006; Stahl et al. , 2002). International assignments
are more demanding, include a broader task spectrum, and present greater learning
opportunities, thus requiring intensive knowledge transfers. Therefore, it is expected that
intrinsically motivated inpatriates will more actively engage in knowledge sharing
activities. Thus, it is proposed:
H2a. Intrinsic motivation has a positive effect on inpatriates ’knowledge sharing.
Extrinsic motivation refers to performing an action or behavior for known or anticipated
external rewards, such as a higher salary, bonus or promotion (Ryan and Deci, 2000). In fact,many employees are willing to accept an international assignment because they believe it
will help advance their career (Bolino, 2007; Harvey, Novicevic and Speier, 1999; Reiche,
2007; Stahl et al. , 2002). Previous research reveals that career development opportunities
positively influence knowledge sharing (Lin, 2007). O ’Reilly and Pondy (1979) indicate that
the probability that organizational members will route information to other members is
positively related to the rewards that they expect to gain from knowledge sharing.
Subsequent studies have supported the positive relationship between extrinsic motivation
and knowledge sharing in MNCs (Gupta and Govindarajan, 2000). Based on these976ER
41,5considerations, it is argued that inpatriates with strong extrinsic motivation will engage in
knowledge sharing. Thus, it is proposed:
H2b. Extrinsic motivation has a positive effect on inpatriates ’knowledge sharing.
Opportunity as antecedent of inpatriate knowledge sharing . Opportunity broadly refers to
organizational mechanisms in contrast to ability and motivation, which are individual-levelfactors that support individuals in acquiring, retaining and transferring knowledge.
According to Chen et al. (2010), only including individual-level antecedents of knowledge
sharing is not sufficient, as organizational contexts may have a great effect on the creationand exchange of knowledge as well. While opportunities are often conceptualized as
constraints (Bendoly and Hur, 2007; Mathieu et al. , 1992; Siemsen et al. , 2008), this study
focuses on organizational mechanisms that could positively affect knowledge transfersacross national borders. In the inpatriate literature, mentoring has been described as an
important organizational mechanism that is highly influential for knowledge dissemination
as it facilitates information processing and internal communication (Feldman and Bolino,1999; Harvey et al. , 2011; Reiche, 2011).
Mentoring can be defined as a one-to-o ne relationship between a mentor, with
advanced experience and knowledge, and a m entee with less experience and knowledge.
In the context of international assignments, both home-country and host-country mentors
have been proven to positively affect knowledge sharing (Carraher et al., 2008; Mezias and
Scandura, 2005; Schuster et al. , 2017).
A home-country mentor prepares the mentee for the international assignment and
provides on-site vocational and psychosocial support. Staying in permanent contact with a
home-country mentor helps inpatriates reduce feelings of isolation, while strengthening the
positive attitudes toward the organization ( Jassawalla et al., 2006). Moreover, time and effort
invested by the home-country mentor may help to increase the assignee ’s belief that the
home organization values them (Carraher et al. , 2008). Home-country mentors keep their
mentees up to date about developments at home and may also monitor potential jobopportunities for the assignee upon his or her return. This in turn increases the inpatriates ’
commitment to the sending organization. As a result, inpatriates with home-country
mentors are likely to more strongly identify themselves with their home organization andthus take pride in sharing their knowledge about this entity with the HQ (Mezias and
Scandura, 2005). Based on these considerations it is assumed:
H3a. Home-country mentoring has a positive effect on inpatriates ’knowledge sharing.
The main task of host-country mentors is to provide inpatriates with information about the
receiving organization, workplace, job requirements, etc., and thus improve their
understanding of the new work environment (Carraher et al. , 2008). Moreover, they may
help inpatriates with personal and family-related issues such as authorizations, housing or
schooling. This form of organizational support is likely to increase their identification with
the host organization (Mezias and Scandura, 2005). Due to the reciprocal character ofmentoring, inpatriates may feel the obligation to repay the support provided by the
host-county mentor by sharing their experiences with the host-country organization. Thus,
inpatriates with host-country mentors are more likely to engage in knowledge sharing. Thisstudy therefore proposes:
H3b. Host-country mentoring has a positive effect on inpatriates ’knowledge sharing.
Moderating effects of inpatriate boundary spanning . While the AMO framework focuses on
the agents of knowledge transfer (i.e. inpatriates and employees of the home and host
organization), the relationship between these agents is not explicitly conceptualized.977Knowledge
sharing of
inpatriatesBoundary spanning refers to the fact that MNCs consist of various geographically dispersed
units that operate under idiosyncratic economic, technical and cultural conditions and that
organizational knowledge that exists in a particular unit of the MNC is often instilled with
context-specific meaning (Bhagat et al., 2002). Harzing et al. (2011) argue in this regard that
inpatriates play an important role as bridge individuals as they can overcome languagebarriers between the HQ staff and the employees in the subsidiaries. They can hereby
create linkages between groups in different units that enable future transactions on a
broader scale (Reiche, 2011). Inpatriate boundary spanning may therefore function as acatalyst for knowledge sharing in MNCs and amplify the effects of ability, motivation and
opportunity on inpatriates ’knowledge sharing because of its brokerage function by linking
previously unconnected individuals at different units of the MNC (Barner-Rasmussen et al.,
2014). Reiche (2011) argue that inpatriates can act as broker between social ties they have
established at the HQ and the social ties they maintain at their home unit. By doing so,
employees from both the HQ and the subsidiary can subsequently leverage these cross-unitties for further knowledge exchange. In order to connect previously disconnected
individuals, inpatriates ’abilities, such as international work experience and host-country
language proficiency, are beneficial to share required information. For instance, HQemployees will require information about the subsidiary context and about cultural norms
and accepted cultural behaviors to establish a trustful relationship with employees of the
foreign subsidiaries (Reiche, 2011). Previous international work experience will helpinpatriates to understand which information are useful and required for the HQ employees.
Thus, it is expected that inpatriates ’abilities are even more useful for knowledge sharing
when leveraged by boundary spanning activity. As outlined above, inpatriates can act as
bridge individuals to overcome language barriers between previously disconnected
individuals and therefore can help to build a trustful relationships between different MNC ’s
units (Harzing et al. , 2011; Zhang and Peltokorpi, 2015). We therefore argue that
host-country language skills can be even more beneficial for knowledge sharing in
situations of high boundary spanning activities.
As motivation turned out to be a crucial factor in the context of boundary spanning
(Holtbrügge and Engelhard, 2016), it can be expected that this brokerage has a reinforcing
effect on knowledge sharing of motivated inpatriates. Also, organizational support in theform of mentoring should have a stronger effect on knowledge sharing when the inpatriates ’
possibility and opportunity in form of easier communication and larger social network
channels to link previously unconnected individuals at different units of the MNC is high(Kostova and Roth, 2003). Based on these considerations it is proposed:
H4a. Boundary spanning amplifies the relationship between inpatriates ’abilities and
knowledge sharing.
H4b. Boundary spanning amplifies the relationship between inpatriates ’motivation and
knowledge sharing.
H4c. Boundary spanning amplifies the relationship between inpatriates ’opportunity and
knowledge sharing.

3. Methodology
3.1 Sample and procedure
Our hypotheses and overall model proposed above was tested through unique data set
obtained from a survey among international assignees working in the HQ of German
MNCs. More precisely, the study focused on MNCs headquartered in the Nuremberg978ER
41,5Metropolitan Region. This region has a high economic growth and is home to many world-
renowned MNCs such as Adidas, Schaeffler and Siemens, as well as medium-sized MNCs
including Playmobil, GfK and Faber-Castell. These companies have a high degree of
internationalization, operate in a diverse set of industries and employ many foreign
employees. This enabled us to approach a large number of inpatriates in various
organizational settings and functions. Moreover, this region provides homogeneous working
and living conditions for inpatriates, thus potential location effects can be excluded.
The authors sourced contact addresses of inpatriates in the Nuremberg Metropolitan
Region in two different ways. First, the authors searched on respective online platforms and
social media sites, such as XING, LinkedIn and Just Landed. Second, they contacted the HR
departments of firms in the region and asked them to provide us with contact addresses of
their inpatriates. This procedure led to 1,005 identified inpatriates. The authors
subsequently contacted all identified inpatriates, kindly asked them to participate and
provided a token-based link to our online questionnaire. After two weeks, the authors sent
out a reminder to all those who had not yet started the survey. In total, 187 usable data sets
were received –equaling a response rate of 18.6 percent. The majority (15.3 percent) of the
respondents were from India, followed by respondents from the USA (9.1 percent), UK(9.1 percent) and China (8.0 percent). In total, our sample was comprised of inpatriates from
46 different countries. Consistent with our study objectives, we largely focused on
inpatriates who are relocated for a limited timeframe of three to five years to the HQ
location. Concerning this matter, 80.4 percent of the respondents of our sample indicated
that they are on a long-term assignment (three to five years), whereas 19.6 percent indicated
that they are either on a short-term assignment, localized or on other assignment type
( flexpatriation, commuter assignment, etc.).
The authors followed the recommendations of Armstrong and Overton (1977) and
compared returns from early and late respondents (65.2 percent who responded to the first
mailing vs the 34.8 percent who responded after the reminder) on all dependent and
independent constructs, as well as several control variables. Only one significant difference
between early and late respondents with respect to the position of the inpatriate ( po0.05)
was found. More specifically, a significant number of inpatriates with higher ranking
positions only answered the questionnaire after having received a reminder. Based on the
result of the non-response test, non-response bias is determined to not be problematic and
used the data without further correction.

inpatriatesTo reduce potential common-method bias, the study followed several recommendations
suggested by Podsakoff et al. (2003) and Chang et al. (2010). From an ex ante perspective, the
authors ensured all respondents complete anonymity and separated independent and
dependent constructs in the questionnaire. Moreover, the authors varied different scale
endpoints and formats for the predictor and criterion measures in order to minimize
potential consistency. From a post hoc perspective, the authors computed an exploratory
factor analysis and examined the unrotated factor solutions by means of the commonly used
Harman single factor test (Harman, 1976; Podsakoff and Organ, 1986). Neither a single
factor, nor a general factor accounting for the majority of the covariance emerged in this
study based on the unrotated factor solutions. Moreover, the effects of an unmeasured latent
method factor were controlled by using AMOS 23.0. The authors introduced a single
unmeasured first-order latent method factor to the theoretical model and measured the
common-method variance by subtracting standardized regression weights without the
common latent factor from standardized regression weights with the common latent factor.
Based on these results it is concluded that common-method variance does not play an
important role in our findings.
3.2 Measures
All items were measured on the individual l evel of analysis. Our dependent variable,
knowledge sharing, was adopted from R eiche (2011). This was regarded as more
appropriate than alternative concepts, such as knowledge transfer performance or
effectiveness, because the latter depend on the ty pes (e.g. explicit vs tacit) and conditions
(e.g. absorptive capacity) of knowledge sharing, while knowledge sharing is mainly a
function of their individual characteristics and organizational con ditions (Szulanski,
2000). The measure consists of four items referring to the extent inpatriates shared
different types of knowledge with colleagues at the HQ. These include local market
knowledge such as formal and informal business norms, knowledge on the political,
economic, and social environment of the home c ountry, knowledge on the local subsidiary
context and specific knowledge about potential contact people at the home unit (Harvey,
Novicevic and Speier, 1999). The four items w ere measured on a seven-point Likert scale
(1¼low effort –7¼high effort). Since the internal reliability of the construct is very high
(α¼0.92) (Nunnally and Bernstein, 1978), a composite factor was created by calculating
the arithmetical average of all four items.
International work experience was operationalized by using a well-established single
item measure in which the respondents indicated the total amount of time they have workedabroad (Brewster et al. , 2005; Okpara and Kabongo, 2011).
For host-country language proficiency th e authors applied the measure developed by
Caligiuri (2000) in which the respondents wer e asked to describe their ability to speak
the language of the host country (German ) on a five-point scale ranging from 1 ¼Id on o t
know the language of this host country to 5 ¼I am fluent in the language of this
host country.
The measurement of extrinsic and intrinsic motivation was based on Stahl et al. (2002).
The respondents were asked to rate the importance of their motives for accepting the
international assignment (1 ¼not important at all –7¼extremely important). In line with
previous research, “personal challenge ”was considered as intrinsic motivation, whereas
“future opportunities for advancement ”was regarded as extrinsic motivation to accept the
international assignment (Haines et al. , 2008).
For mentoring, the respondents were asked whether they were assisted by a
home-country mentor (a mentor from the foreign subsidiary) or a host-country mentor
(a mentor from the German HQ). Both variables were binary coded with 1 ¼yes and 0 ¼
no

new knowledge (technical , managerial, etc.) in the local unit in Germany ”
and “to apply
knowledge (technical, managerial, etc.) to new contexts. ”Again, all three items were
averaged to create a composite factor ( α¼0.86).
Corporate language policy was measured by a binary variable. The respondents were
asked to indicate whether their MNC had implemented English as its official corporate
language (with 1 ¼English as formal language policy –0¼other or no formal language

5. Discussion
InH1a andH1b we argued that international work experience and host-country language
proficiency have positive effects on inpatriates ’knowledge sharing. Contrary to the
hypothesized direction, the results of the regression analysis reveal negative effects of
the two abilities on knowledge sharing, albeit on a non-significant or marginal significant
level. Hence, H1a –H1b have to be rejected.
The body of inpatriate literature provides potential explanations why the basic rationale
proposed by the AMO framework may not hold true in the inpatriate context. It is argued
that inpatriates lack in reputation and credibility because they are perceived as outsiders or
peripheral members by the HQ staff and because of the ethnocentric stigmatization and
stereotyping toward them (Harvey et al. , 2005; Reiche, Harzing and Kraimer, 2009). The
rationale behind this logic is that inpatriates in contrast to their expatriate counterparts are983Knowledge

strategically relevant for the entire MNC than the HQ. In fact, the inpatriate literature
outlines that expatriates typically carry with them the status and reputation associated with
their role as representatives of the HQ, which is generally the center of decision making and
the most important unit of the MNC. Thus, being identifying and associated with the HQ
will enhance the expatriate ’s individual reputation in the foreign subsidiary (reputational
gain) (Kostova and Roth, 2003), which in turn leads to the expatriate being more inclined to
share knowledge on the HQ with the foreign subsidiary staff (Inkpen and Tsang, 2005;
Lazarova and Tarique, 2005). This is because engaging in knowledge sharing will result in a
stronger association with the HQ, so that the expatriate can benefit from the reputational
status of the HQ.
On the contrary, inpatriates are delegated to the HQ from a foreign subsidiary that is
regularly less strategically relevant for the entire MNC. It is smaller than the HQ and
possesses a lower level of resources and capabilities. Thus, being associated with the less
powerful home subsidiary will lower the inpatriates ’individual reputation and social capital
in the HQ (reputational loss) (Harvey et al. , 2005; Millar and Choi, 2009). The reputational
gain of expatriates, e.g. benefiting from being associated with the powerful HQ and the
reputational loss of inpatriates, e.g. being penalized by being associated with the less
powerful home unit can be understood as a reputation asymmetry between these two
employee groups, which influence their behavior concerning knowledge sharing.
Based on our findings, we speculate that this reputation asymmetry between expatriates
and inpatriates influences the effect of the assignees ’abilities on knowledge sharing.
Inpatriates with extensive international work experience have spent a longer period of time in
other countries and are thus aware of cultural and organizational differences. This
cosmopolitan socialization is likely to reduce their identification with a particular country or
MNC unit (Black et al. , 1991). However, sharing knowledge about their home market will
increase the association with the less powerful subsidiary and will cause the reputational loss.
The reputation asymmetry between HQs and subsidiary may also explain the significant
negative effect of language proficiency on inpatriates ’knowledge sharing. Host-country
language proficiency not only facilitates inpatriates ’communication with HQ employees,
but also allows them to adapt more easily to the host-country organization (Black et al. ,
1991). Thus, fluency in the local language reduces the inpatriates ’psychic distance to the
HQs and the local employees ’ethnocentric attitudes toward them. Inpatriates fluent in the
host-country language perceive a higher acceptance, reputation and social capital in the HQ.
Thus, with a high host-country language fluency of the inpatriate, the HQ staff may less
strongly associate the inpatriates with the less powerful foreign subsidiary. In order to notrisk this perception (e.g. being less associated with the home unit), inpatriates may reduce
their engagement in sharing knowledge about their home unit and market.
InH2a andH2b, it is proposed that intrinsic and extrinsic motivation is positively related
to knowledge sharing. As only the extrinsic motive, career advancement, shows a
significant effect, H2b can be confirmed, while the coefficients for personal challenge
reflecting intrinsic motivation are insignificant in all models. Again, this finding may be
explained by the reputation asymmetry between the home and host organization.
Inpatriates who are strongly motivated by future career opportunities in the MNC are
inclined to maintain close relationships with colleagues in both the HQ and the subsidiary
and will therefore engage in knowledge sharing between the two units. On the contrary,
strong ties with their home organization are less valuable for inpatriates who are primarily
motivated by personal challenges. Close relationships with the home-country may even
reduce their reputation and social capital in the HQ. Thus, inpatriates with strong intrinsic
motivation will less engage in knowledge sharing compared to inpatriates with strong
extrinsic motivation.985Knowledge

Based on the concept of reputation asymmetry, we speculate that organizational support
by a host-country mentor (e.g. a mentor of the HQ) gives inpatriates the opportunity to adjust
more quickly to the HQ culture (Maley et al., 2015). Moreover, the attention they receive from
the HQ in form of being mentored by experienced HQ managers signals their value for the HQ
and reduces the HQ staff ’s ethnocentric attitudes toward the inpatriates (Reiche, 2011). Thus,
being connected with the host-country mentor may lead to a reputational gain of the
inpatriates and lowers the association with less powerful foreign subsidiary. As a
consequence, the inpatriates ’reputation at and identification with the HQ increases, while
their identification with the subsidiary decreases. This in turn decreases their propensity to
engage in knowledge as this will increase the association with the less powerful subsidiary.
InH4a, it is argued that inpatriate boundary spanning has a reinforcing effect on the
relationship between inpatriates ’abilities and knowledge sharing. While a significant
moderating effect of inpatriate boundary spanning for international work experience was
found, the effects of German language proficiency on knowledge sharing are not affected.
Figure 2 illustrates the significant moderating effect of boundary spanning on the relationship
between international work experience and knowledge sharing and shows that high inpatriate
boundary spanning reduces inpatriates ’knowledge sharing with increasing international
work experience, whereas for low boundary spanning a positive interaction effect is revealed.
Based on these results, it seems plausible to argue that in order to connect individuals
from different organizational units and cultures, inpatriates have to exchange information
concerning the unique context of their home units and markets. Reiche (2011, p. 366)
outlines in this regard that inpatriate boundary spanning requires to “provide HQ staff with
context-specific knowledge about the subsidiary environment to ensure that HQ employees
can benefit from future exchanges with subsidiary staff. ”Inpatriates with international
work experience will know more precisely which contextual knowledge is necessary and
which not. Being more efficient in providing contextual knowledge may explain the
observed lower engagement in knowledge sharing.
InH4b, it is argued that boundary spanning will reinforce the relationship between
motivation and knowledge sharing. The results show no differences between low and high
boundary spanning. Intrinsic and extrinsic motivations have similar positive effects on
knowledge sharing for high and low boundary spanners.

context analyzed, for example, the effect of different functional areas and categories of
international assignees or the influence of language and cultural intelligence (Froese et al. ,

knowledge sharing and the impact of inpatriate boundary spanning. Furthermore, this
study extends on current research by employing the AMO framework in combination with
boundary spanning which has only recently been applied in the expatriate literature (Chang
et al., 2012; Reinholt et al., 2011) and has never been transferred to the inpatriation context.
Our study provide empirical evidence and support the arguments in the extant body of
literature that expatriation and inpatriation in the context of knowledge sharing needs to be
differentiated due to differing challenges and mechanisms between these groups of
international assignees. In fact, the study reveals that certain inpatriates ’abilities do not
positively affect knowledge sharing, whereas motivation and certain opportunities have a
positive influence. By introducing the concept of boundary spanning as a moderator, the
study is able to outline divergent effects of abilities (international work experience) and
opportunities (mentoring) on knowledge sharing. More generally, as certain aspects of the
AMO framework have divergent effects on knowledge sharing, the study reveals that it may
be too simplistic to transfer the results of the expatriate literature to inpatriates and
emphasize to more specifically consider the differences between these two groups of
international assignees.
An important theoretical contribution in this context is the introduction of the reputation
asymmetry concept. While expatriates perceive a reputation gain when delegated to a
foreign subsidiary, the identification of inpatriates with their home organization is likely to
reduce their reputation at the HQ. Thus, inpatriates ’abilities, such as international work
experience and host-country language proficiency may negatively impact knowledge
sharing. Similarly, organizational support by a host-country mentor increases their
reputation at the HQ and reduces the inpatriates ’propensity to engage in these transfers.
Our second theoretical contribution lies in the identification of inpatriate boundary
spanning as contextual variable. Therefore, our study extends on Reiche (2011) by
incorporating a moderating role of inpatriate boundary spanning in the context of reverse
knowledge transfer. Furthermore, the authors identify inpatriate boundary spanning solely
as a linking function in this study, while other functions such as knowledge exchanging,
intervening or facilitating remain to be examined by further research. In a more general
sense, the introduction of the reputation asymmetry concept contributes to the current
debate on distance measures in international management (Harzing and Pudelko, 2016;
Shenkar, 2012). In line with research scholars who advise caution when using the national
cultural index as proxy for differences between units from different countries (Ambos and
Håkanson, 2014; Mezias et al., 2002), it is emphasized that distance measures should not only
include cultural, geographical and language differences, but also reputation differences.
Particularly in the context of international assignments, the reputation of countries and
organizational units is likely to influence assignees ’attitudes and behavior (Håkanson et al.,
2016). Second, reputation –like any other perception concept –should be measured on an
individual level and take individual differences into account. As our study shows, the
transfer from a subsidiary to the HQ may have different reputation effects for inpatriates as
compared to expatriates. Third, future conceptualizations of reputation should distinguish
between the reputation of countries and those of organizational units. While the reputation
of countries includes factors, such as advanced economy, appealing environment
and effective government (Reputation Institute, 2018), the reputation of organizational
units is more a function of their relevance for the entire MNC and their level of autonomy.988ER
41,5Finally, reputation should be conceptualized as an asymmetric phenomenon. As our study
shows, the reputation effect of a delegation from the HQ to a peripheral unit may be
asymmetric to the reputation effect of the reverse delegation.
6.2 Managerial implications
Our findings have important practical implications as well. MNCs should be aware that
inpatriates with strong abilities are not automatically inclined to engage in knowledge
sharing. In contrast, they may hide abilities which identify them with their home
organization because this reduces their reputation at the HQ. Therefore, MNCs should signal
to HQ staff that inpatriates can be valuable sources of knowledge in order to reduce
collective reservation and to prevent ethnocentric stigmatization and stereotyping. One way
to increase the status and social capital of the inpatriate at the HQ is the provision of a
host-country mentor. Moreover, MNCs should actively support the inpatriates propensity to
share their personal contacts in the subsidiary with managers in the HQ.
Another important practical implication relates to the selection of inpatriates. Our
study shows that inpatriates who primar ily accept delegations to HQ for extrinsic
motivations have a higher tendency to engage in knowledge sharing. Thus, MNCs that
seek to increase these transfers should select su bsidiary employees who have particularly
strong career-related motives. On the contrary, intrinsically motivated inpatriates are less
suitable for this task.
6.3 Limitations and future research
The study is not without limitations. First, while the study outlined the importance of
reverse knowledge transfers for MNCs, the authors actually measured knowledge sharing.
While knowledge sharing is a required antecedent of successful knowledge transfers, the
latter also depends on the knowledge characteristics (teachability, tacitness, codifiability
and complexity), the relational aspects between knowledge sender and receiver (trust,
power, position and reliability), and the characteristics of the knowledge receiver (absorptive
capacity, past experiences and cultural intelligence). Thus, future research would benefit
from taking these conditions of knowledge transfer into account. Similarly, distinguishing
between the sources of transferrable knowledge (e.g. internal knowledge and network
knowledge) could be an alley for future research (Foss and Pedersen, 2002). Moreover, as
Barner-Rasmussen et al. (2014) already pointed to more faceted roles of boundary spanning
(e.g. exchanging, linking, facilitating and intervening), another avenue for future research
could be to split up the boundary spanning concept into different dimensions and to analyzetheir relevance for inpatriates ’knowledge sharing.
Second, our sample consists of inpatriate s who are temporarily relocated to the HQ in
one specific metropolitan region in Ger many (Nuremberg Metropolitan Region).
Comparing our sample with the conceptualizat ion of inpatriates in the extant literature,
it is important to mention that the inpatriat es in our sample may not be representative for
the broad and diverse population of inpatriates described in the literature. In fact, our
sample is widely restricted to relatively youn g and less experienced inpatriates, a fact that
may partly explain findings that contradict the arguments in the inpatriate literature.
Therefore, one needs to be cautious when tra nsferring the findings to experienced
inpatriate managers in top management pos itions. Moreover, while focusing on one
specific region comes along with the important advantage of keeping possible location
influences constant, the findings may not be transferable to other contexts. Therefore,
future studies should include a more div erse set of inpatriates (experienced vs
unexperienced, top management position vs sp ecialists) and home and host countries with
different technological, economic and cultural conditions. This would also allow for
analyzing the proposed reputation asymmetr y effect in more detail. For similar reasons,989Knowledge

inpatriatesfuture research should include subsidiaries with different size, resources and strategic

knowledge sharing efforts of inpatriates as well. Finally, it would be interesting to

expatriates. While our study indicates sever al important differences, our sample only
included inpatriates and therefore did not a llow us to formally test these differences.
