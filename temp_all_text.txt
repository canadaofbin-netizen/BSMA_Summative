ABSTRACT
The present study explores the antecedents of frontline employ­
ees’ boundary-spanning behaviors in the hospitality industry. 
Based on social exchange theory and role theory, a conceptual 
model was built to explore how three dimensions of perceived 
organizational support (perceived supervisory support, internal 
communication, and training) cultivate frontline employees’ 
boundary-spanning behaviors through job satisfaction and 
organizational commitment. Based on the analysis of 597 hos­
pitality frontline employees in the United States, which were 
recruited through Amazon Mechanical Turk, this study shows 
the differential impacts of organizational support types on driv­
ing boundary spanning staff to effectively operate on the 
boundary. Theoretical and practical implications of the findings 
are articulated.

Introduction
The hospitality industry has seen an increasingly popular trend that more and 
more hotels acknowledge and encourage above and beyond employee beha­
viors, since delivering high-quality service and generating satisfied customers 
is fundamental to businesses’ success (Madera et al., 2017). Unlike non-service 
industries and other service settings, the hospitality industry is distinguished 
by the delivery of an experience that extends beyond the provision of tangible 
goods during irregular and long hours and thus requires high experiential 
qualities. Thus, the success of hospitality businesses mainly relies on customer- 
contact employees’ services in creating and delivering high-level, in-person 
customer service and handling multiple responsibilities simultaneously (e.g., 
Tsaur & Tang, 2012; Zeithaml et al., 2017).
High-contact frontline employees, as the first and foremost representative 
of service organizations, are an important nexus between hospitality firms and 
customers (Yoo & Arnold, 2016; Zablah et al., 2012). Frontline employees not

only create service (Choi et al., 2014), but also represent the organization to 
outsiders as ambassadors, building up customer relationships (Choi et al., 
2014) and advocating the firm’s image (Yoo et al., 2014). On the other hand, 
they also act as “envoys” sharing information internally for improvements in 
service delivery (Johlke et al., 2002).
Although different types of hospitality businesses may have their own 
performance scales, frontline employees in this sector generally need to span 
the firm’s boundary by interacting with its external stakeholders in their daily 
work (Kim et al., 2009). Their behaviors have been encapsulated in the concept 
of boundary-spanning behaviors, which contribute to products and service 
excellence through boundary spanning employees’ exceeding their job respon­
sibility, promoting the organization’s products and service, advocating an 
outstanding image for the organization, and differentiating from competitors 
(Bettencourt & Brown, 1997; Yoo et al., 2014). For example, during peak hours 
in a restaurant, frontline associates may help the customers who are unfamiliar 
with the menu to order, while acknowledging other customers’ time-sensitive 
needs. On the other hand, the frontline employees may also be in a position to 
share customers’ complaints with the organization to improve the service 
process and experience in the future. Boundary-spanning behavior is distinct 
from organizational citizenship behavior because the former highlights 
a heterogeneous social landscape where employees interact with external 
stakeholders possibly diverging from the firm’s goals, whereas the latter refers 
to the proactive collaboration and assistance among all types of coworkers, 
largely within an organization (Williams & Anderson, 1991).
The hospitality industry exhibits a fitting boundary-spanning context where 
frontline employees engaged with both external and internal environments on 
the boundary of a firm. Oftentimes facing not only conflicting requests from 
different parties but also unclear and ambiguous expectations, all while per­
forming as boundary spanners, hospitality frontline employees may easily get 
stressed due to divergent expectations from organizations and customers, 
thereby creating cross-boundary issues (Kang et al., 2016; Yang & Lau, 
2019). However, studies on how to cultivate boundary-spanning personnel 
have been limited in hospitality literature until recently (Adam, 2019; Hu et al., 
2017; Kim & Qu, 2019; Yang & Lau, 2019).
What factors lead to boundary-spanning behaviors and how to promote 
such behaviors among frontline employees have aroused interests in the 
marketing literature (Bettencourt & Brown, 2003; Wintericht et al., 2013). 
However, previous research tended to focus on personal motivational factors 
such as individual differences (i.e., attitude, personality, psychology) 
(Bettencourt et al., 2001) and contextual variables (i.e., role recognition, 
symbolization, internalization) (Grant, 2012; Wintericht et al., 2013), neglect­
ing other important organizational supportive factors such as how manage­
ment interventions can motivate frontline employees’ boundary-spanning

behaviors since boundary spanners are likely to be affected by the social 
environment, especially social support, within the corporation (Bettencourt 
et al., 2005; Goolsby, 1992; Korschun, 2015).
To address the call for more attention to situational predictors of boundary- 
spanning behaviors (Malhotra & Ackfeldt, 2016) and sustaining more extra- 
role service among hospitality employees (Castro-Casal et al., 2019), this 
research investigates how organizational support predict boundary-spanning 
behaviors of hospitality frontline employees in the United States. It aims to 
explain human behavior in a social exchange relationship. For example, when 
hospitality frontline employees perceive they are getting support or benefits 
from the organization, they are more likely to reciprocate with positive 
organizational attitude and behavior (Cropanzano & Mitchell, 2005). It pro­
vides the basic premise for the current study that frontline employees may 
devote to more boundary-spanning behaviors when they perceive more orga­
nizational support via job satisfaction and organizational commitment.
Literature review and conceptual model
This study is anchored in two theoretical streams: social exchange theory and 
role theory. According to social exchange theory (Blau, 1964), denoting a two- 
sided rewarding process between two or more parties (Mitchell et al., 2012; 
Rasoolimanesh et al., 2015), employees trade commitment and efforts to the 
organization in exchange for tangible incentives and socio-emotional benefits 
(Blau, 1964; Eisenberger et al., 1986). Moreover, drawing upon role theory 
(Walker Jr. et al., 1975), an individual’s role is shaped by their perceived 
support or influences from role senders (supervisors or customers). 
Presumably, organizational support would prompt boundary spanners to 
have a clear understanding of their roles and increase their satisfaction and 
effective performance (Walker Jr. et al., 1975). When employees recognize that 
they are provided with the support needed, they feel more obliged to meet 
expectations for work performance. Thus, frontline employees may devote to 
more boundary-spanning behaviors when they acknowledge job satisfaction 
and organizational commitment as a result of organizational support. 
Integrating these two theoretical schools and taking into account the unique 
characteristics of the hospitality industry, this study intends to the dynamic 
relationships between three dimensions of perceived organizational support, 
frontline employees’ job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and bound­
ary-spanning behaviors.
Boundary-spanning behaviors
Defining organizations in terms of boundaries between members and non­
members, the boundary-spanning theory postulates that boundary spanners

are personnel that engage in social and economic exchanges between an 
organization and its external environment and ensure the smooth functioning 
of such interactions (Aldrich & Herker, 1977). Therefore, boundary-spanning 
behaviors occur through “job-related interactions with a person who is con­
sidered part of the environment” and “not a member of the organization” 
(Robertson, 1995, p. 75). Boundary spanners not only operate within the 
confines of the firm but interact with external stakeholders whose objectives 
may diverge from those of the business (Korschun, 2015). As a result, bound­
ary-spanning employees may perceive role stress due to divergent 
expectations.
Unlike organizational citizenship behaviors that are widely applicable 
across positions and contexts, boundary-spanning behaviors focus on the 
unique nature of boundary crossing in organizational functioning 
(Robertson, 1995). This requires that boundary spanners are not only subject 
to the expectation of the parent system but also that of a boundary interactive 
system (Wall & Adams, 1974). That is, boundary-spanning employees not 
only have the responsibility of presenting products or services in a quality way 
and fulfilling the organization’s promise through service delivery (Hartline & 
Ferrell, 1996; Mulki et al., 2012), they also perform value-added functions such 
as establishing a desirable service environment, extensively communicating 
with customers, and influencing their preferences toward organizational goals, 
while crossing the organizational boundary (Manev & Stevenson, 2001; Owen 
et al., 2015).
Therefore, boundary-spanning behaviors are distinguished, by which cus­
tomer-organization relationship benefits in terms of the quality of exchange 
relationships through boundary spanners’ ties (Huang et al., 2016). A key 
aspect of boundary-spanning behaviors is employees’ interpersonal interac­
tion with the external environment, which is of strategic importance for 
organizations. Specifically, in the hospitality firms, frontline employees are 
considered typical boundary-spanners who are constantly involved in transac­
tions with various influencing parties (e.g., supervisors, coworkers, and cus­
tomers) (Coelho et al., 2011). They not only follow the in-role service 
procedures but also promote service delivery through interpersonal relation­
ships (Huang et al., 2016), monitoring customer preferences and demands 
(Peng & Heath, 1996), and introducing innovations to products and services 
(Manev & Stevenson, 2001). To some extent, the relationship between custo­
mers and hospitality organizations is attributed to boundary spanners who are 
instrumental in bridging communication in consumptive scenarios, resulting 
in favorable ties and mutual trust between the internal and external 
environments.
Existing literature suggests that boundary-spanning behaviors manifest in 
three major dimensions, namely external representation, internal influence, 
and service delivery (Bettencourt & Brown, 2003; Bettencourt et al., 2005;

Bowen & Schneider, 1985; Parasuraman et al., 1988; Schneider & Bowen, 
1984). Specifically, (1) external representation denotes employees’ promotion 
and advocacy of the organization’s products and service to consumers; (2) 
internal influence happens when employees take initiatives to communicate 
with the organization and/or colleagues for service improvement; and (3) 
service delivery refers to providing responsive, courteous, and flexible service 
to customers. For most frontline employees, external representation and 
internal influence behaviors tend to be relatively more extra-role whereas 
service delivery behaviors are more role-prescribed (Bettencourt et al., 2005). 
Boundary spanning behavior is a significant contributor to hospitality firms’ 
service excellence since it not only provides a mechanism for quality service 
and settling customers’ complaints effectively, but also transmits information 
about customers’ requirements, complaints, and expectations of the service to 
the firm (Karatepe, 2012).
Given the importance of such behaviors, scholars have been striving to 
understand the factors that lead to more boundary spanning. A major stream 
of studies focused on employees’ attitudes, personal features, and psychologi­
cal states as potential triggers of boundary-spanning behaviors (J. Wang et al., 
2020; Ying & Norman, 2017; Yoo et al., 2014). Yoo et al. (2014) revealed how 
bank employees’ perception of positive sentiments (i.e., trust, commitment, 
and satisfaction) motivates them to work, which may spur boundary-spanning 
behaviors for positive work outcomes. Similarly, service employees who have 
a strong sense of membership are likely to exhibit boundary-spanning beha­
viors in the hospitality context (Bayighomog & Arasli, 2019). Nevertheless, few 
studies in the hospitality context explored the organizational motivators of 
boundary-spanning behaviors. Presumably, boundary spanners such as sales 
personnel who perceive being greatly supported by the organization tend to 
show a high level of job satisfaction (Stamper & Johlke, 2003). As a way to 
reciprocate perceived organizational support, they may also exhibit higher 
levels of organizational commitment and more boundary-spanning behaviors 
(Srivastava & Tang, 2015).
Perceived organizational support
Being apt to face challenges and complexity in their daily work, boundary- 
spanners are easy suffering stress and exhaustion (Kang & Jang, 2019), which 
has a significant negative effect on task performance (Cropanzano et al., 2003). 
Extant research has focused on investigating the impact of personal differences 
and environmental factors (e.g., person-organization fit) on boundary- 
spanning behaviors in service settings. Empirical research found that internal 
undermining (such as supervisor undermining and coworker undermining) in 
retail banks and insurance companies negatively impacted boundary-spanning 
employees’ service behaviors toward their organizations (Yoo, 2013). On the

contrary, desirable work resources and error-tolerance helps to promote the 
formation of employees’ positive psychological states and well-being, which 
lead to the successful execution of their work assignments (Schaufeli & Van 
Rhenen, 2009; X. Wang et al., 2020). Nonetheless, few studies attempt to 
discover the influence of favorable organizational support on hospitality 
boundary-spanners’ attitudinal and behavioral outcomes.
Perceived organizational support is defined as employees’ perception of the 
extent to which their employers value their contribution and care for their 
well-being (Eisenberger et al., 1986; Shanock et al., 2012). Usually, it is pre­
sented as the employees’ perceptions of organizations’ commitment toward 
their employees (Abou Hashish, 2017). Perceived organizational support 
develops as a result of the social exchange relationship between employees 
and organizations (Baran et al., 2012). It instills a sense of ownership among 
the employees, leading to positive performance and attitudes toward the 
organization (Marchand & Vandenberghe, 2016).
A review of the literature endorsed that a few factors are believed to relate to 
employees’ perceptions of organizational support, such as organizational 
rewards and work environment (Baran et al., 2012). Employees assess the 
amount of assistance the organization provides to help them complete assign­
ments, as well as the training and development experiences in areas that are of 
interest to employees (LaMastro, 2000). Besides, management support, cow­
orker support, supervisor support, learning culture, job design, and job auton­
omy(Islam & Ahmed, 2018) are evaluated as the environmental factors which 
are likely to influence employees’ attitudinal and behavioral outcomes 
(Chiaburu et al., 2013).Therefore, in accordance with previous studies(Castro- 
Casal et al., 2019; Chiaburu et al., 2013; Islam & Ahmed, 2018), organizational 
support is manifested through perceived supervisory support, internal com­
munication, and training. Supervisory support refers to the level to which 
employees recognize the support, encouragement, and concerns received from 
supervisors (Krishnan & Mary, 2012; Rhoades & Eisenberger, 2002). Since the 
supervisor is the one whom employees contact regularly and with whom they 
maintain an intimate working relationship, he/she is considered instrumental 
in pushing frontline employees’ organizational commitment (Qureshi & 
Hamid, 2017) and strengthening their task-related behaviors (Chen & Wang, 
2017). Working with a supportive supervisor, boundary spanners’ are more 
receptive to their leader’s positive attitude and their work-related motivation 
and enthusiasm are more likely to be aroused or activated (Chen & Wang, 
2017). Supervisory support can lead to job satisfaction of hotel frontline 
employees (Kang et al., 2015), and employees’ affective attachment and orga­
nizational commitment (Kang et al., 2015). Hence, this research proposes the 
following hypotheses: 
H1a. Perceived supervisory support positively influences job satisfaction.

Another relevant aspect of organizational support is internal communica­
tion (Hume & Leonard, 2014; Welch, 2012). As a service sector, the hospitality 
industry relies on effective internal communication among employees to 
ensure quality service delivery (King & Lee, 2016). Work-related information 
redundancy may induce a “bystander effect”, which hardly benefits employees’ 
job involvement (Hussain et al., 2019). High levels of internal communication 
help solve problems, achieve goals, and benefit service and product innova­
tion, which leads to higher employee satisfaction (Huang et al., 2016). Internal 
communication also increases employees’ psychological attachment to their 
organization, as “it encourages employees to perceive themselves as core 
members of the organization” (Ng et al., 2006, p. 477). Moreover, reinforcing 
communication channels and valuing external information exchange and 
sharing help to create a team consensus. According to role theory, effective 
internal communication would reduce the uncertainties about roles and 
responsibilities perceived by boundary-spanners and enhance their satisfac­
tion and organizational commitment (Malhotra & Ackfeldt, 2016). Thus, H2a

Last but not least, quality training represents a common means of support­
ing employees in organizations (Johlke et al., 2002), promoting employees’ 
positive attitudes and behaviors (Chen, 2017). Training programs are integral 
to perceived organizational support, promoting employees’ skills, product and 
customer knowledge, and career development. Both employees and organiza­
tions perceive training as an important element for quality assurance, certainly 
in the case of the hospitality industry (Costen & Salazar, 2011). Meanwhile, 
training has been linked to greater employee satisfaction, commitment, and 
positive behaviors, such as role and extra-role performance (Bhat, 2013; 
Karatepe et al., 2018). Trained boundary spanners are capable of better tying 
customers and the organization (Huang et al., 2016), gaining a sense of being 
valued and trusted (Karatepe et al., 2018), and performing work effectively and 
efficiently (Hanaysha & Tahir, 2016). Empirical study also provided evidence 
that in the hospitality industry, a high level of training received by frontline 
employees can affect their extra-role service behavior via affective commit­
ment (Castro-Casal et al., 2019). In comparison to other job benefits, training 
more likely shows the organization’s willingness to invest in employees,

Job satisfaction and organizational commitment
Job satisfaction is conceptualized as a pleasurable emotional state resulting 
from the assessment of one’s job experience (Locke, 1969), which plays 
a critical role in the mechanism of social exchange theory, performing as 
a psychological construct mediating the relationship between workplace envir­
onment and behaviors (Crede et al., 2007). High levels of job satisfaction are 
the precursors of many positive outcomes such as organizational commitment 
(Valaei & Rezaei, 2016) and better performance (Bowling et al., 2015). 
Adams’s (1963) equity theory has been used to help understand job satisfac­
tion and its outcomes (Sean et al., 2015). Simply put, individuals compare their 
input in work (e.g., effort, knowledge, and skills) with what is gained from 
such input (e.g., salary, benefits, and intrinsic rewards). Job satisfaction is 
related to boundary spanning effectiveness (Owen et al., 2015) and used to 
be measured as a motivator of boundary spanners’ performance (Liu et al., 
2018; Marrone, 2010). In the hospitality industry, frontline employees’ job 
satisfaction is even more crucial, as an essential contributor to experience 
quality on the boundary and customer satisfaction (W. G. Kim et al., 2005; 
Karatepe & Sokmen, 2006).
Organizational commitment indicates the attachment between employees 
and organizations (Angle & Perry, 1981), implying employees perform in 
accordance with organizational values or goals (O’Reilly & Chatman, 1986). 
Employees with high levels of organizational commitment are driven to 
achieve superior task performance (Huang et al., 2016). In the hospitality 
industry, both emotional satisfaction and organizational commitment can 
predict how customer-oriented employees repay the organization and/or 
represent it to others. Employees who are highly attached to organizations 
can better handle work stress and make both in-role and extra-role contribu­
tions (K. Y. Kim et al., 2016). Based on social exchange relationships such as 
when employees feel satisfied and committed to their organizations, they tend 
to reciprocate through supportive attitude and behavior toward the outside 
audience in a boundary-spanning situation. Moreover, during the process of 
interpersonal interactions with outside stakeholders, boundary-spanning 
employees with a higher level of job satisfaction/organizational commitment 
are typically more involved, acting as ties between the organization and

Methodology
Questionnaire design
The questionnaire was designed as a result of a critical literature review. First, 
job-related questions (e.g., work context and job position) were asked. Two 
screening questions were implemented at the beginning of the questionnaire, 
“Do you work in the hospitality industry (e.g., hotels, restaurants, resorts) in the 
United States?” and “At work, do you directly interact with guests daily?” to 
recruit frontline employees in the hospitality industry only. Next, the respon­
dents were directed to describe their perceived organizational support, job 
satisfaction, organizational commitment, and boundary-spanning behaviors. 
The survey closed with demographic questions including gender, age, educa­
tion level, ethnic background, and annual income. The major constructs were 
assessed with seven-point Likert-type scales from “1 = strongly disagree” to 
“7 = strongly agree”.

Multi-item indicators from previous studies were employed to operationa­
lize the major constructs. Perceived organizational support consists of per­
ceived supervisory support, internal communication, and training. Perceived 
supervisory support was measured by a five-item shortened version of Kottke 
& Sharafinski (1988); internal communication was assessed by a four-item 
scale from Carrière & Bourque (2009); training was evaluated by three items 
by Johlke et al. (2002). To evaluate job satisfaction, this study used a scale with 
five items adapted from W. G. Kim et al. (2005). A four-item scale measuring 
organizational commitment was developed by Mowday et al. (1979). Lastly, 
the measurement of boundary-spanning behaviors in this study is based on the 
conceptual definitions by Bettencourt et al. (2005). Particularly, external 
representation was assessed by four items, internal influence was measured 
via four items, and service delivery was tested through five items. The mea­
surement items were adapted to fit the hospitality context and face and content 
validity were assessed through a panel of academic and industry experts.
Data collection
To evaluate the questionnaire, a pilot test was conducted through Amazon 
Mechanical Turk in December 2018and the target population of this study was 
frontline employees of the hospitality industry who live in the United States. 
M-Turk was used as data collection tool in this study because it served as an 
online pool with large and diverse workers completing the required tasks 
(Paolacci & Chandler, 2014) and proved useful with regard to reaching 
samples with specific characteristics reliably and validly (Buhrmester et al., 
2011).
Among 230 distributed questionnaires, 195 complete questionnaires were 
usable, for a valid response rate of 84.8%. All dimensions and the total scale 
showed good internal consistency of the scale with Cronbach’s Alphas value 
from 0.801 to 0.973 (Nunnally, 1978). After making some alterations to the 
wording of items based on the pilot test, the research team launched the formal 
survey in January 2019. Participants in the pilot test were excluded from re- 
taking the survey. Respondents from the worker pool who completed the 
questionnaires were paid a small honorarium. Out of 680 distributed ques­
tionnaires, 83 were excluded due to incompletion, resulting in a response rate 
of 87.5%. The large number of sample helps ensure a high level of statistical 
validity (McQuitty, 2004).
Data analysis
Data were analyzed with the aid of SPSS 25.0 and AMOS 25.0. First, descrip­
tive analysis was performed to offer baseline information of the study con­
structs. Next, confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) was applied to verify the

factorial structure and the reliability and validity of the proposed model. Both 
first- and second-order models were examined to validate the structure of 
boundary-spanning behaviors which contain three sub-dimensions (external 
representative, internal influence, and service delivery). The first-order model 
is usually used to evaluate the relationship between latent and observed 
variables (Bollen, 1989), while the second-order model confirms the latent 
variables and their sub-dimensions (Jarvis et al., 2003). Third, this study 
performed Harman’s single factor test to check whether common method 
variance was a serious problem. Harman’s single factor test is a principal 
component factor analysis using varimax rotation (Podsakoff & Organ, 
1986), suggesting that common method variance is problematic if the result 
of one latent construct accounting for more than 50% total variance of the 
measures. The results of this study supported multiple factors with eigenvalues 
over the cutoff value of one with the first factor representing 22.55% of the 
variance. In addition, a structural equation modeling (SEM) technique was 
conducted to assess the hypothesized paths and the associated model fit 
indexes (Anderson & Gerbing, 1988).
Findings
Sample profile
The sample contains 325 males and 270 females. More than half of the 
respondents were between the ages of 25–34 (54.94%) and had a white ethnic 
background (82.07%). Respondents with a 2-year college education are the 
largest proportion (35.51%). The median of respondents’ annual pretax 
income is 30,000 USD-$39,999. Respondents represent a variety of job posi­
tions across the hospitality industry, including waiter/waitress, bartender, 
front desk clerk, receptionist, concierge, bellman, housekeeper, cashier, coor­
dinator, supervisor, director, duty/shift manager, and so on. Among them, 
those working in the front office department (i.e., front desk clerk, reception­
ist, concierge, bellman, etc.) constitute the largest share (36.68%), followed by 
the category of waiter/waitress, bartender, and attendant (32.50%). The results 
showed no critical non-normality with all items’ skewness and kurtosis scores 
below 2, which are under the thresholds of 3 and 10 respectively (Kline, 2015).
Measurement model
The measurement model of 34 items included three sub-dimensions of bound­
ary-spanning behaviors together with job satisfaction and organizational 
commitment as endogenous constructs and three exogenous constructs (i.e., 
perceived organizational support, internal communication, and training) was 
assessed via AMOS 25.0. The first-order model indicated an acceptable fit

Discussion and implications
Discussion
Boundary spanners operate at the boundary or periphery of a service firm, 
managing communication, negotiating and resolving issues, and advocating 
the organization’s image (Bettencourt et al., 2005). However, hardly any 
research has examined the antecedents to boundary-spanning behaviors in 
the hospitality industry, despite it being a highly relevant field. Taking a social 
exchange and role theory perspective, the current research addresses the gap in 
the literature by investigating the organizational supportive antecedents and 
psychological constructs as mechanisms of boundary-spanning behaviors. 
With most of the hypotheses supported, the results demonstrated that the 
more organizational support customer-contact employees perceived, the more 
job satisfaction and organizational commitment they demonstrated which, in 
turn, led to boundary-spanning behaviors. A closer look at the findings 
uncovers several valuable insights.
First, all three dimensions of perceived organizational support lead to 
boundary-spanning behaviors, via job satisfaction and organizational commit­
ment, with varied influences, corroborating the existing findings in the general 
marketing field that management interventions/supervisor support (Ackfeldt 
& Wong, 2006), internal communication (Malhotra & Ackfeldt, 2016), and 
training (Johlke et al., 2002) promote boundary-spanning behaviors of high- 
contact employees. Overall, such findings harmonize with (1) social exchange 
theory (Blau, 1964) since employees who perceive organizational support and 
acknowledge their satisfaction and commitment would reciprocate their

Second, frontline employees react to the level of perceived support of the

Implications
This study offers both theoretical and practical implications. Theoretically, 
first, this paper contributes to past literature by developing a new conceptual 
mechanism anchored in social exchange theory and role theory, which con­
siders three distinct forms of organizational support as antecedents to custo­
mer-oriented boundary-spanning behaviors. Compared to organizational 
citizenship behaviors, boundary spanning is a more pertinent concept to the 
hospitality industry, possessing unique boundary-crossing features such as 
making both in-role and extra-role contributions to satisfy the expectations 
of both the parent system and the interactive system. Considering the unique 
role of boundary spanners, this study advances the research on frontline 
employees by going beyond the management of distress and burnout and 
mitigation of unfavorable service encounters (e.g., Adam, 2019; Yang & Lau, 
2019), and taking a more proactive stance in creating desired “ambassadors” of 
the organizations. Second, this study identified differentiated impacts of dif­
ferent organizational supportive dimensions on job satisfaction, organiza­
tional commitment, and boundary-spanning behaviors, shedding light on 
the important role of perceived supervisory support. It informs the literature 
that support from the direct supervisor is considered as more valuable to 
frontline employees’ job satisfaction, compared to other organizational sup­
portive dimensions. Those who work as boundary spanners and frequently 
face conflicting expectations seem to value the supervisor’s support when 
assessing the costs and benefits of performing the extra-role, indicating that 
as a hygiene factor, interpersonal relationship between boundary spanners and 
their supervisors promote boundary spanners’ job satisfaction and their go 
above and beyond behaviors, which amplifies the insights of the previous 
research on the outcomes of favorable relationship between supervisor- 
subordinate (e.g., Major & Morganson, 2011; Tse et al., 2018). Moreover, 
training presents its relatively stronger contribution to organizational com­
mitment among the three organizational supportive forms. It enriches the 
literature showing that training is not only critical for in-role performance 
(e.g., Chen, 2017; Woodside et al., 2020) but also for sustaining affective 
commitment and extra-role in hospitality firms. Finally, the paper highlights 
the role of attitudinal constructs between organizational supportive dimen­
sions and boundary-spanning behaviors.

Boundary spanning employees serve as ambassadors in hospitality organi­
zations, resolving issues between the internal and external environment. 
Meanwhile, they represent a group oftentimes plagued by staff shortage, 
work overload, irregular work shifts, role stress, and role ambiguity, and 
then high turnover rate, on the boundary. Therefore, a thorough consideration 
of frontline employees’ work conditions and related hygiene factors, such as 
clear job descriptions and reasonable hours, need to be given. Since a high level 
of job satisfaction and organizational commitment lead to frontline employ­
ees’ boundary-spanning behaviors, it is important to boost employees’ satis­
faction and commitment so that they want to repay the organizations through 
positive behavior and/or word-of-mouth to outside audiences.
Moreover, it is of strategic importance for organizations to foster 
a supportive organizational culture to align boundary-spanning behaviors 
with managerial goals, encouraging the frontline employees to create and 
deliver quality service especially when they face role stress and conflicting 
demands. Hospitality organizations should implement multi-form internal 
supportive policies and management intervention strategies that elicit 
a greater sense of belonging and attachment among high contact employees 
since employees who feel connected to their employer may take extra mea­
sures of recommending the organization to others and advocating the orga­
nization’s offerings. For example, on-the-job training at hospitality firms may 
not only enable hotel frontline employees to become more competent in 
completing tasks but also help them to internalize the brand and reinforces 
their values and behaviors that reflect the brand promise. Such internal 
branding initiatives make the frontline employees the believers first, and 
then prompt them to perform boundary-spanning behaviors and deliver 
excellent service.
Last but not least, hospitality organizations wishing to engage their frontline 
employees in boundary-spanning behaviors should leverage multi-level sup­
ports. The findings showed that support perceived from the direct supervisor 
is considered more valuable than other aspects. For supporting hospitality 
high-contact employees who constantly face stressors and conflicting 
demands, the supervisory support when time is tough is especially precious, 
such as bringing the manager or supervisor to help high-contact employees to 
handle demanding customers. To support boundary spanners and promote 
boundary-spanning behaviors, democratic leadership or participative leader­
ship style should be adopted to recognize, understand and respond to the 
frontline employees’ needs and struggles. Especially, favorable leader beha­
viors such as charismatic and supportive leadership can evoke positive affect 
and behaviors among employees. Considering the uniqueness of a hospitality 
job, supervisors should develop rapport with and show concerns for customer- 
contact employees’ needs especially considering the long and irregular work 
hours along with the work overload. This can be done by showing willingness

them some extra ownership over a task. Besides, to enhance in-role and extra- 
role behavioral performance in a boundary-spanning customer-contact role, 
necessary training in different scenarios and quality internal communication 
should be warranted in hospitality organizations.
Limitations and directions for future research
Several limitations are associated with the current study, which offers sugges­
tions for future research. First, the proposed conceptual framework will benefit 
from the incorporation of other variables for further exploring the triggering 
mechanism of boundary-spanning behaviors. Further research can introduce 
more organizational antecedents into the framework, such as empowerment 
and social justice. The moderating effect of job satisfaction, organizational 
commitment, and the mechanism of internal branding in the mechanism are 
also worth testing. Training and its possible dimensions, considered as 
a booster for individual development, also deserve further exploration. 
Second, the research context of this study was the hospitality industry, 
which is a typical high-contact service environment, but different sectors 
(lodging, foodservice, tourism, etc.), positions (reservationist, housekeeper, 
tour guide, etc.), and personality may play a role in the predictive mechanism 
and thus deserve further exploration. Third, boundary-spanning behaviors 
could be considered as a protective strategy used by employees to reduce 
resource depletion at the workplace. Therefore, the conservation of resources 
theory (Hobfoll, 1989) is worth applying to develop future research. Fourth, 
this current study was conducted in the United States only; therefore, the 
respondents’ understanding of boundary-spanning behaviors may be influ­
enced by their respective social and cultural contexts. Thus, it is deemed 
necessary to cross-validate the study findings across different service work­
force settings. Also, a mixed-methods study can be designed to refine the 
mechanism and its associated scales. For example, future studies can conduct 
interviews with frontline employees to unearth items accounting for job- 
specific characteristics. Besides, though M-Turk is considered as an effective 
and valid data collection platform, caution still should be exercised when 
interpreting the generalizability of the findings. Additionally, the results in 
this current research are based on self-reported data, which may raise concerns 
about common method bias, despite the efforts to minimize the threats: (1) the 
research team informed the respondents of the anonymity of their responses 
and there was no right or wrong answer to a question, which helped reduce 
social desirability bias, (2) the researchers adapted the measurements of scales 
from different empirical studies, removing unclear and ambiguous expres­
sions, and (3) measurements of the predictor and outcome variables were not 
placed in sequential orders, to reduce estimations of the relationships
