Mindfulness ABSTRACT  
Enterprise social media (ESM) provides convenient channels for employees to access enterprise-wide work re-
sources but also exposes employees to potential distractions. Prior studies have confirmed this “two-sided ” role of 
ESM usage on employee work performance. Ongoing studies are exploring possible methods to alleviate side 
effects of ESM usage. Drawing on boundary management literature and information processing theory, we 
propose the concept of individual boundary spanning to capture employees ’ information-obtaining behaviors via 
ESM and identify two mediating mechanisms —mindfulness and information overload —through which an in-
dividual ’s boundary spanning affects work performance. We also propose the concept of individual boundary 
buffering to capture protective actions by individuals that prevent them from becoming disturbed or overloaded 
with ESM requests. We further investigate the interaction effects of these two boundary activities in the ESM 
context. We conducted a survey and collected data from 202 ESM users from a research and development en-
terprise in China. The results suggest that individual boundary spanning triggers mindfulness in individuals that 
benefits their work performance but also increases information overload, harming work performance. Individual 
boundary buffering can enhance the positive relationship between individual boundary spanning and mindful -
ness, and mitigate the positive relationship between spanning activity and information overload.   
1.Introduction 
Companies are increasingly deploying enterprise social media (ESM) 
as internal information and communication tools (ICTs) and resource- 
sharing platforms to support employees ’ work (Cai, Huang, Liu, & 
Wang, 2018; Leonardi, 2014; Shang & Sun, 2020 ). However, recent 
studies have shown that organizations have vague guidelines regarding 
the use of these tools that enable employees ’ work to benefit from 
enterprise-wide resources (Lu & Pan, 2019 ). One investigation showed 
that roughly half of the companies surveyed could not effectively use 
ESM tools (Li, 2015 ). Since social media has been considered a signifi -
cant source of distraction and waste of work resources, managers are still 
uncertain as to whether ESM is a “productivity killer ” (Archer-Brown & 
Kietzmann, 2018 ). Therefore, how organizations can better leverage the 
advantages of ESM and alleviate the potentially adverse effects of ESM 
use has become an important and urgent issue (Liang, Xin, Yan, & 
Jianxiang, 2020 ). 
One main advantage of ESM is that it offers unprecedented opportunities for employees to break the work boundary formed by 
work or department divisions, enabling them to conveniently access and 
share information-based resources across the company (Ellison, Gibbs, 
& Weber, 2015; Leonardi, Huysman, & Steinfield, 2013 ). According to 
boundary management theory, behaviors that solicit external resources 
through action and communication are recognized as boundary span-
ning (Dey & Ganesh, 2017; Tushman & Scanlan, 1981; Yan & Louis, 
1999 ). 
Scholars have provided evidence that ESM can support boundary 
spanning within organizations. For example, Pee (2018) found ESM ’s 
various affordances (e.g., visibility) can alleviate codification efforts for 
cross-domain or complex knowledge transmission. Rode (2016) and Liu 
and Bakici (2019) recognized ESM as a valuable tool to help individuals 
search for work-related information. Aboelmaged (2018) found that 
employees can acquire knowledge from individuals both within and 
outside organizations via ESM. Scholars posit that work completion 
generally requires employees to have access to adequate work resources 
(Bacharach & Bamberger, 1995; Tatikonda & Rosenthal, 2000 ),

environment overwhelmed, consuming their limited work resources and 
ultimately leading to information overload (Chen & Wei, 2019; Zhang,

into our understanding of the positive and negative effects of ESM usage. 
Unfortunately, how organizations can alleviate the potential negative 
effects of ESM use on individuals and improve employees’ usage of ESM 
has still not been answered. 
Scholars have suggested that individuals take protective actions such 
as neglect and avoidance behaviors to protect themselves from infor-
mation overload and prevent interruption from computers and 
communication tools during work time (Russell, Jackson, & Banks, 
2019; Zhang Y, 2020). According to boundary management theory, 
behaviors individuals take to protect themselves from becoming 
disturbed and overloaded from the outside can be defined as “boundary 
buffering” (Faraj & Yan, 2009). However, when researching boundary 
spanning and boundary buffering, extant studies mainly focus on the 
effects of these two kinds of activities individually (e.g., Faraj & Yan, 
2009) or only consider one of them (e.g., Schotter, Mudambi, Doz, & 
Gaur, 2017; Van Osch & Steinfield, 2018). There is currently a lack of 
empirical investigation into the interaction effects between these two 
types of boundary activities at the individual level. In their review of 
team boundary management, Dey and Ganesh recommended that 
scholars pay attention to the interaction effects of both boundary 
spanning and boundary buffering. They suggested that “buffering pro-
vides teams with slack, which gives team members an opportunity for 
quite reflection within the team. This fuels learning and innovation in 
the team by letting team members synthesize the knowledge they have 
gathered through boundary-spanning activities” (Dey & Ganesh, 2017). 
This study argues that individual boundary spanning and boundary 
buffering can work together to achieve interaction effects for in-
dividuals’ work for several reasons. First, within the ESM context, the 
objects of boundary spanning and boundary buffering are both 
information-type content (i.e., spanning for needed work-related infor-
mation and buffering for non-work-related information), the two ac-
tivities are compatible while being implemented. Second, ESM activities 
are information-based. Since individuals possess limited information- 
processing resources, the effectiveness of boundary spanning depends 
on whether individuals have adequate information processing resources 
(e.g., cognition) to process the work materials they acquire from span-
ning and processing efficiency. Third, individual boundary buffering can 
be practiced to ensure continuity of ongoing information processing 
procedures and save resources from unnecessary activities, which acts as 
an effective approach to strengthen the effectiveness of boundary 
spanning (Dey & Ganesh, 2017). This study also resonates with the call 
for the need to provide empirical studies to examine the effectiveness of 
protective actions for coping with information overload (Saxena & 
Lamest, 2018). We therefore propose our research question as: What role 
does ESM boundary buffering play when considering the effects of ESM 
boundary spanning on individual work performance? 
We apply information processing theory as the theoretical lens 
through which to understand how humans process information to 
answer research questions (Miller, 1956). The theory suggests that 
human brains receive information from various sources and store it in 
working memory for further cognitive processing (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 
1968; Simon, 1978). The information processing yielding two outcomes: 
mindfulness or information overload. Mindfulness is a mind mode in 
which individuals are open to new experiences and display active 
awareness of novel distinctions. In this mode, individuals tend to reflect 
on their situations, consider actions from various perspectives, and think 
of different possibilities, etc. (Frese & Zapf, 1994a; Thatcher, Wright, 
Sun, Zagenczyk, & Klein, 2018; Wei, Zhu, & Chen, 2020). However, because humans have limited information processing capacity (e.g., 
working memory) (Miller, 1956), individuals who are continually 
accessing information will quickly reach their limits, leading to infor-
mation overload (Swar, Hameed, & Reychav, 2017). This study con-
siders mindfulness and information overload as two mediators that 
mediate the impact of individual boundary spanning on individual work 
performance. 
Furthermore, we consider individual boundary buffering as a 
mechanism to strengthen individuals’ information processing efficiency 
by avoiding unnecessary information processing and enhancing the 
levels of mindfulness. Based on a survey of an important research and 
development company in China, the results support the idea that ESM 
enabled individual boundary spanning to promote users’ work perfor -
mance by motivating mindfulness, but it also harmed work performance 
by increasing information overload. The results also support that prop-
erly utilizing individual boundary buffering can help employees 
enhance mindfulness and mitigate information overload. These findings 
significantly contribute to our understanding of ESM usage in several 
ways. They suggest individual boundary buffering, in addition to indi-
vidual boundary spanning, as another necessary activity in effective 
ESM usage in the ESM literature. They confirm that information over-
load and mindfulness are two critical explanatory factors of the rela-
tionship between ESM-enabled individual boundary spanning and 
individual work performance. Finally, they provide new insight into the 
boundary management literature by revealing the interaction effects of 
boundary spanning and boundary buffering in the ESM context. 
The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 summa -
rizes prior literature on individual boundary spanning and individual 
boundary buffering. Section 3 introduces the theoretical background 
and hypothesis. Section 4 elaborates on our research method. Section 5 
presents the results of the data analysis. Section 6 discusses theoretical 
and practical implications of the findings, and specifies the study’s 
limitations. Section 7 concludes this study. 
2.Literature review 
Boundary management literature recognizes a series of activities in 
which individuals interact with the external environment, such as when 
communicating with external actors to obtain resources beyond their 
boundaries or maintaining boundaries to protect themselves from being 
negatively influenced by the outside environment (Dey & Ganesh, 2017; 
Wu, Huang, Jiang, Klein, & Liu, 2020). The scope of boundaries can be 
different when considering diverse analytical units and research con-
texts. For example, when the boundary is at an organizational level, the 
organization’s scope can be classified as within or outside the company 
(Schotter et al., 2017). In the context of this study, ESM is typically 
implemented within the company and in the company we surveyed, 
project teams generally carry out the job responsibilities. Therefore, we 
define the scope of individual boundaries as that formed by the acces -
sibility of resources the project team officially provides. For example, 
suppose one developer engages in a project with three other teammates. 
In that case, resources provided by the members within the project team 
are resources within the work boundary. At the same time, the knowl -
edge of colleagues in the same department/organization who are not 
members of the project is a resource outside the work boundary. 
2.1. Individual boundary spanning 
In boundary management literature, individual boundary spanning 
refers to individual behaviors to solicit external work resources through 
actions and communication relationships (Tushman & Scanlan, 1981). 
Boundary spanning can play an important role in employees’ work 
performance as it supplements their work resources (Bacharach & 
Bamberger, 1995; Tatikonda & Rosenthal, 2000; Wang et al., 2019). In 
an organization, coworkers are reliable sources for supplementing work 
resources, and prior research has found that employees’ sharing J. exchanging knowledge, can broaden the resources employees possess, 
improve the details of their work as well as their decision-making ca-
pabilities (Aboelmaged, 2018; Anand & Singh, 2011; Mantym aki & 
Riemer, 2016; Teigland & Wasko, 2003 ). ESM has been suggested as 
effective tools for transferring information-based work resources among 
employees within organizations (Aboelmaged, 2018; Laitinen & Sivu-
nen, 2020 ). 
In particular, ESM connects every employee and equips multiple 
sharing and communication functions to support users in breaking 
boundaries established by structures such as work divisions, de-
partments, or individuals ’ social networks so that they can access 
enterprise-wide resources (Leonardi et al., 2013; Van Osch & Steinfield, 
2016 ). Prior studies have implicitly suggested that ESM supports in-
dividuals across work boundaries to acquire external resources to aid 
their work. 
According to our summary, as shown in Table 1, studies that embody 
the connotation of individual boundary spanning via ESM can be 
divided into two streams: the knowledge management stream and the 
information management stream. In the former, scholars found that ESM 
can facilitate employees ’ knowledge sharing and knowledge creation. 
Pee (2018) found that ESM ’s various affordances alleviate the diffi-
culties of sharing knowledge with others. Qi and Chau (2017) found that 
an employee ’s ESM usage is positively related to his/her knowledge 
sharing and knowledge creation, which benefits organizational learning. 
In regard to information management, scholars mainly focus on the 
antecedents of ESM information sharing (Laitinen & Sivunen, 2020 ) and 
information searching (Van Osch & Steinfield, 2016, 2018 ), and only 
one study has explored the relationship between information manage -
ment and individual work performance (Lu & Pan, 2019 ). 
However, there are significant differences between the above per-
spectives and the distinct boundary spanning perspective. First, extant 
studies do not explicitly discriminate whether knowledge or information 
management activities occurred within the work boundary (e.g., within 
the project team) or outside the work boundary but within the organi -
zation. Such lack of discrimination fails to recognize the benefits of 
ESM ’s broadening individuals ’ access to work resources across work 
boundaries. Second, neither the knowledge management perspective 
nor the information management perspective represent the complete 
range of individual boundary spanning activities. Presently, we only 
vaguely understand how ESM users can benefit from accessing the 
broader range of enterprise work resources outside their conventional 
work jurisdiction. We argue that boundary management theory provides 
a research lens to capture such behavior and investigate different 
mechanisms influencing an individual ’s work. 
Besides the topic of ESM, many studies have investigated the influ-
ence of individual boundary spanning activities on other issues, such as 
the relationship between boundary spanning and managers ’ influence 
(Manev & Stevenson, 2001 ) or boundary spanning in multinational 
corporations (Barner-Rasmussen, Ehrnrooth, Koveshnikov, & Makela, 2014; Reiche, 2011; Schotter et al., 2017 ). These studies found that 
individual boundary spanning can broaden the channels for acquiring 
work resources (Barner-Rasmussen et al., 2014; Reiche, 2011 ) while 
providing extra cues for decision-making (Floyd & Wooldridge, 1997 ), 
thus benefiting work performance. However, when comparing 
ESM-facilitated boundary spanning to these conventional individual 
boundary spanning studies, several differences remain. First, they 
examined different actors. ESM allows every employee to access the 
enterprise resource pool, thus everyone can be a boundary spanner 
(Schotter et al., 2017 ). However, conventional boundary spanning 
literature has shown that only a limited number of individuals possess 
key resources and can mediate activities across the boundary as 
boundary spanners. For example, inpatriates who can connect their 
home unit and the headquarters (Reiche, 2011 ), or IT employees who 
have unique knowledge and serve multiple departments ( Wang et al., 
2019 ) can be boundary spanners. Second, the resources boundary 
spanners can access are different. Every spanner can access the 
enterprise-wide resources pool in the ESM context, such as project re-
ports and Q&As on the ESM. If spanners find it necessary, they can 
contact relevant colleagues for help through the tool even if they do not 
know one another. However, their work and relational network limit the 
resource pool of conventional boundary spanners. Lastly, motivations or 
expectations under spanning activities might differ since ESM users 
engage in boundary spanning activities mainly out of their own interests 
(i.e., acquiring support for their work). However, because of their 
unique identities (roles), conventional boundary spanners might be ex-
pected to contribute benefits beyond the individual level, such as pro-
moting knowledge transfer between the home unit and headquarters 
(Reiche, 2011 ) or negotiating with external stakeholders on behalf of the 
team (Faraj & Yan, 2009 ). Inappropriate management of this conven -
tional boundary spanning could lead to side effects such as role overload 
(Wang et al., 2019 ). As ESM becomes more prevalent in businesses, 
these differences indicate that ESM boundary spanning is a topic that 
warrants further investigation. In this study, we investigate the mech -
anisms through which ESM-based individual boundary spanning could 
affect individuals ’ work performance. 
2.2. Individual boundary buffering 
Boundary buffering generally refers to behaviors that shield the en-
tity from unwanted exposure to the environment (Faraj & Yan, 2009 ). In 
the ESM context, individual boundary buffering can be defined as ac-
tions to insulate individuals from unwanted interference and overloaded 
demands from the ESM. In contrast to boundary spanning, which can be 
characterized as a form of engagement activity, boundary buffering is a 
form of disengagement (Faraj & Yan, 2009 ). Boundary buffering de-
creases outside influences, strengthening the effectiveness of rational 
action inside (Scott, 1998 ). 
Researchers have thus far paid attention to team-level boundary 
buffering (Dey & Ganesh, 2017 ). For example, Chang, Wu, Discenza, and 
Klein (2021) found that team boundary buffering can improve software 
development project performance. Faraj and Yan (2009) found that 
team boundary buffering can enhance team members ’ psychological 
safety and team performance. Wu et al. (2020) found that supplier 
teams ’ boundary buffering in the supply chain management context can 
protect teams from suffering the environmental uncertainties driven by 
buyers ’ requirements changes. Team boundary buffering activities are 
often carried out by team leaders dedicated to creating a well-protected 
working environment. In the ESM context, ESM makes it viable and 
convenient for individuals to practice individual-level boundary buff-
ering. Except for conventional buffering approaches such as negotiation 
or coordination (Faraj & Yan, 2009 ), the use of ESM functionalities can 
be regarded as a new, technology-based method of buffering, which is 
complementary to extant buffering approaches. Specifically, conven -
tional buffering activities generally require interaction engagement, 
such as refusing a non-work invitation during work time. An individual Table 1

coping with information overload (Saxena & Lamest, 2018). Since 
contextualization is essential to improving the theory’s generalizability 
(Saxena & Lamest, 2018), this study aims to investigate the influence of 
individual boundary buffering in the ESM context. 
In short, boundary management studies have suggested that different 
boundary activities have different motivations, targets and objects, and 
functions (Dey & Ganesh, 2017; Marrone, 2010). We summarized the

Different motivations drive ESM users to practice different boundary 
activities. Specifically, individuals who practice boundary spanning aim 
to obtain more resources that once were difficult or unable to be 
accessed (Leonardi et al., 2013). Individuals engage in boundary buff-
ering because they want to remain undisturbed and not be overloaded 
with ESM requests (Faraj & Yan, 2009). Targets of boundary spanning 
are external colleagues who possess information, knowledge, or tech-
nology that spanners need to aid them in finishing their work, and 
external work resources stored on ESM. The targets of boundary buff-
ering are external ESM users who may interrupt individuals or give them 
an overload of requests during work time. Along with the different 
boundary targets, boundary objects for boundary spanning are 
task-related documents, knowledge, ideas, feedback, and conversations 
enabled by ESM. Simultaneously, non-work-related posts, notifications, 
chats, or overloaded work requests are objects that ESM users buffer 
during work time (Leonardi et al., 2013). 3.Theoretical background and hypotheses development 
3.1. Information processing theory 
Given that information processing theory (IPT) has been proposed as 
one important theoretical lens through which to explore the influence of 
boundary spanning on individuals’ work (Carlile, 2004; Wang et al., 
2019), our study adopts it to formulate the hypothesized relationships. 
Information processing theory provides scholars with a theoretical lens 
to understand how humans process information (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 
1968; Miller, 1956). According to theory, the human brain receives in-
formation from various sources and stores it in working or short-term 
memory. There, it is subject to further cognitive processing (Atkinson 
& Shiffrin, 1968; Simon, 1978). Prior studies have suggested that in-
dividuals can engage in two cognitive processing states to process the 
information stored in working memory: mindfulness and mindlessness 
(Langer, 1989, 1992). Mindfulness is defined as a state of cognitive 
functioning in a specific situation under which individuals exhibit 
conscious awareness and alertness of their actions and contexts, open-
ness to new experiences (Addas & Pinsonneault, 2018; Langer, 1989), a 
tendency to consider actions from various perspectives, reflect on their 
situations, think of different possibilities, etc. (Frese & Zapf, 1994a; 
Hacker, 2003). When individuals are in a mindless state, their thoughts, 
emotions, and behaviors rely on well-structured routines based on dis-
tinctions and associations learned in the past (Bodner & Langer, 2001; 
Haigh, Moore, Kashdan, & Fresco, 2011). Mindlessness requires less 
effort, but people are also more likely to neglect the nuances of new 
information in such a processing mode. Scholars posit that individual 
work performance is determined by whether employees can access 
adequate work resources such as information, knowledge and expertise 
(Bacharach & Bamberger, 1995; Tatikonda & Rosenthal, 2000), which 
stresses the importance of how individuals process the 
information-based work resources. 
3.1.1. Theorizing the relationship between boundary spanning and 
mindfulness 
Scholars have adopted the information processing perspective to 
investigate the influence of boundary spanning activities on boundary 
spanners (Carlile, 2004; Wang et al., 2019). They proposed that the 
objects of boundary spanning activities are generally information flows, 
which convey work-related information and knowledge. ESM users 
consciously span boundaries to acquire task-related information that 
usually contains information outside the spanner’s routine experience 
that could update or complement the spanner’s understanding of the 
work. Under such situation, mindfulness is validated as an appropriate 
information processing mode that allows individuals to effectively 
leverage the information obtained to benefit their current work (Addas 
& Pinsonneault, 2018; Jett & George, 2003). Therefore, we use mind -
fulness to capture the potential positive influence of boundary spanning 
activities on an individual’s work. 
3.1.2. Theorizing the relationship between boundary spanning and 
information overload 
Information processing theory acknowledges individuals possess 
limited information processing capacity (Gao, Liu, Guo, & Li, 2018; 
Miller, 1956). Considering boundary spanning brings in external 
work-related information and knowledge for spanners (Ratcheva, 2009; 
Teigland & Wasko, 2003; Wang et al., 2019), reflecting the need for 
information processing, we argue that intensive boundary spanning 
activities will import too much information to spanners’ working 
memory over a short period, causing an individual’s information pro-
cessing capacity to quickly reach its limits (Swar et al., 2017) and 
resulting in information overload (Gao et al., 2018; Jacoby, Speller, & 
Kohn, 1974). Table 2

Information processing theory emphasizes that the fit between the

will determine individual performance, such as decision making quality 
( Gao, Zhang, Wang, & Ba, 2012 ). We first map the boundary spanning 
to determine the quantity of information to be processed. Further, 
boundary buffering can shield individuals from unwanted interference 
and overloaded demands (Wu et al., 2020 ), maintaining their informa -
tion processing capacity and promoting information processing effi-
ciency (Dey & Ganesh, 2017 ). Therefore, information processing theory 
supports our argument for positive interaction effects between individ -
ual boundary spanning and boundary buffering.

3.2. Hypotheses development 
By drawing on information processing theory and the relevant 
literature, this study proposes a research model in which individual 
boundary spanning is positively related to mindfulness and overload. 
Mindfulness could promote individual performance, but overload would 
decrease individual performance. We further investigate the moderating 
effects of individual boundary buffering, which is hypothesized to 
strengthen the positive effect of boundary spanning on mindfulness 
while mitigating the positive effect of boundary spanning on overload.  
Fig. 2 presents the research model. The hypotheses are developed in the 
paragraphs that follow. 
As discussed, boundary spanning is a conscious activity that aims to 
acquire useful resources to aid spanners ’ work, such as finding novel 
task solutions or filling knowledge gaps to develop individuals ’ under -
standing of their work. So individuals have the motivation and will-
ingness to keep an eye on the link between external resources and their 
current work. In the ESM context, resources obtained from boundary 
spanning are information-based, which requires effective information 
processing procedures. This specific requirement aligns with the prac-
tice of spanners ’ mindfulness because individuals in the mindful state 
are alert, lively, aware of current experiences (Baer, 2003; Roberts, 
Thatcher, & Klein, 2007 ), more willing to explore alternatives and 
novelties brought by external resources (Frese & Zapf, 1994b; Hacker, 
2003 ) and more sensitized to the subtleties of context (Roberts et al., 
2007 ). This mindful state enables them to capture the similarities and 
differences between external resources and current work and identify 
the formation factors behind them. Therefore, mindfulness is a pro-
cessing mode that can help individuals fulfill the goal of boundary 
spanning. 
Prior research suggests that task-related events can trigger an in-
dividual ’s mindful processing mode (Addas & Pinsonneault, 2018; Jett 
& George, 2003; Langer, 1989 ). In the ESM context, external resources 
acquired from boundary spanning via ESM may contain novelties or 
discrepancies about spanners ’ work, serving as external clues to trigger 
individuals ’ mindful processing to help them better integrate external 
resources. Hence, we posit the following hypothesis: 
H1a.Individual boundary spanning is positively related to 
mindfulness. ESM activities are information-based. Individuals engaging in 
boundary spanning activities generally need to go through a series of 
information processing processes, including collecting potentially useful 
resources (either in proactive ways such as searching on the ESM or in 
passive ways such as receiving relevant feedback) and recognizing 
whether these resources match their needs. If the resource fits their 
needs, substantial cognitive efforts must be made to incorporate such 
external resources to aid the spanner ’s work (Addas & Pinsonneault, 
2018; Carlile, 2004 ). The more boundary spanning activities there are, 
the more information processing capabilities and resources are required 
(Swar et al., 2017 ). However, information processing theory and related 
studies have proposed that individuals have limited work resources 
(Miller, 1956 ), and intensive boundary spanning activities could drive 
information processing resource deficiency and lead to information 
overload (Cao & Yu, 2019; Sun et al., 2021 ). 
On the other hand, individuals face various work-related activities 
aside from boundary spanning via ESM during work time. Then when an 
individual ’s work resources are rapidly depleting due to boundary 
spanning, few resources are left to process the other necessary tasks. 
Therefore, an incompatibility between the limited resources one pos-
sesses and the information processing required is aggravated, leading to 
information overload (Gao et al., 2018 ). Hence, we propose the 
following hypothesis: 
H1b.Individual boundary spanning is positively related to informa -
tion overload. 
Through mindful processing, an individual can incorporate relevant 
external resources gathered from ESM into contextual-fit internal re-
sources to assist their work (Addas & Pinsonneault, 2018 ). For example, 
a developer can search a particular development flow from a completed 
project report (e.g., similar to the one he is currently working in) 
uploaded by other colleagues on the ESM. Even though two projects may 
have different aims, objects, and personnel, some test procedures can be 
referenced, and the developer can compare and analyze the differences 
and commonalities between the two projects and recognize and acquire 
the key knowledge to benefit his work when in a state of mindfulness. 
The mindful process can also improve individuals ’ work details 
(Addas & Pinsonneault, 2018 ). In this mindful state, individuals are 
sensitive to discrepancies and abnormalities in their work (Frese & Zapf, 
1994a ) and possess the motivation to improve their performance (Roe, 
1999 ). Therefore, they are efficient and effective in intellectual en-
deavors to solve their problems and are open to changing routinized 
behaviors such as altering existing plans or developing new approaches 
based on the current situation, correcting their work process, or 
applying new approaches to work. Hence, we posit the following 
hypothesis: 
H2.Mindfulness is positively related to individual work performance. 
Information overload could decrease individuals ’ work performance. 
Bounded rationality emphasizes that individuals ’ cognitive limitations 
inhibit their decision-making (Karr-Wisniewski & Lu, 2010 ). Being 
overwhelmed by ESM information could render users unable to effec-
tively recognize, interpret and integrate external resources, resulting in 
decreased decision-making efficiency (Jackson & Farzaneh, 2012; Keller 
Fig. 1.Information processing model of ESM boundary spanning.  J. Zhang et al. 6& Staelin, 1987; Schick, Gordon, & Haka, 1990 ) and increased errors 
(Eyrolle & Cellier, 2000 ). 
In addition, when workers ’ minds are occupied with a large amount 
of ESM information, especially non-work-related information, it be-
comes harder for them to recall prior information and resume inter-
rupted activities (Baethge & Rigotti, 2013; Schick et al., 1990 ). This 
predicament requires them to consume more work resources during the 
transition process and generate issues due to resource deficiency, such as 
work delays and decreased work performance (Adler & Benbunan-Fich, 
2012 ). Hence, we posit the following: 
H3.Information overload is negatively related to individual work 
performance. 
3.2.1. Moderating effects 
As Table 2 shows, both boundary spanning and boundary buffering 
objects are information-based content. The core rationale of their 
interaction is that individuals possess limited information processing 
resources (Miller, 1956 ). Specifically, on the one hand, achieving the 
benefits of boundary spanning requires individuals to process external 
information-based work materials obtained from spanning activities 
effectively (Carlile, 2004 ). Therefore, the more information processing 
resources (e.g., attention, time cognitive effort, etc.) available, and the 
higher the resource efficiency, the better the effectiveness of the 
boundary spanning. On the other hand, ESM boundary buffering can 
protect individuals from being exposed to an interruptive environment 
during work time and spending information processing resources on 
unnecessary ESM activities (Faraj & Yan, 2009; Galluch, Grover, & 
Thatcher, 2015 ). Boundary buffering guarantees the continuous running 
of information processing procedures and saves individuals ’ limited 
information processing resources. Therefore, it is reasonable to infer that 
the information processing procedure dynamics driven by boundary 
buffering can affect the outcomes of boundary spanning. Below, we 
discuss the moderating effects of boundary buffering concerning the 
relationship between boundary spanning and two mediators. 
Engaging in mindful processing requires an individual ’s sustained 
and focused attention and intellectual efforts to analyze and synthesize 
the information acquired from ESM boundary spanning with current 
work details (Bishop et al., 2004 ). For example, a product manager may 
need to consider the underlying factors that cause discrepancies in the 
product evaluations collected from various department colleagues on 
ESM to leverage its report and experience better to adjust the develop -
ment plan. Boundary buffering activities support such engagement by 
protecting themselves against unrelated activities to form a less 
disturbed working environment. This behavior ultimately enhances the 
continuity of their mindful process and rational behaviors (Scott, 1998 ). 
On the other hand, boundary buffering provides individuals with more slack to fuel mindful processing of information from boundary 
spanning (Dey & Ganesh, 2017 ). Due to an individual ’s limited working 
memory capacity and other work resources (Miller, 1956 ), subsequent 
unnecessary resource consumption is avoided when individuals block 
unrelated activities ’ involvement during work time (Faraj & Yan, 2009 ). 
Therefore, users can invest more work resources in processing infor-
mation gathered from boundary spanning activities in a mindful mode. 
Hence, we posit the following: 
H4a.Individual boundary buffering can strengthen the positive rela-
tionship between individual boundary spanning and mindfulness. 
The convenient communication feature of ESM exposes employees to 
a broader interaction environment, aggravating the risk of receiving an 
overload of requests either out of work or non-work purposes (Chen & 
Wei, 2019 ; Guo, Lu, Kuang, & Wang, 2020 ). Engaging in appropriate 
boundary buffering activities can effectively defend ESM users from 
getting too much information processing pressure by ESM requests in 
two ways. First, individuals can refuse unrelated or overloaded requests 
from others to reduce information processing needs and avoid unnec -
essary consumption of work resources (e.g., working memory) related to 
this information and follow-up activities (Dey & Ganesh, 2017; Faraj & 
Yan, 2009 ). Secondly, individuals can take measures to decrease the 
disturbances from ESM information (Russell et al., 2019 ; Zhang Y, 
2020 ). For example, users can turn on the ESM silent mode or mute 
unrelated notifications to avoid interruption or be reminded to check 
new messages every time they are received. Instead, they can handle 
these unread messages when they are free. In addition, checking 
collected unread messages at fewer time points means fewer transition 
processes between current work and ESM activities (Galluch et al., 
2015 ), which helps employees save their work resources (e.g., time, 
cognition efforts). 
Since individuals have limited resources to process information from 
both boundary spanning and other activities, they practice boundary 
buffering to decrease the expenditure of resources on unnecessary ESM 
information processing, leading to a decrease in the perception of in-
compatibility between boundary spanning and information processing 
resources. Hence, we posit the following: 
H4b.Individual boundary buffering can mitigate the positive rela-
tionship between individual boundary spanning and information 
overload. 
4.Methodology 
4.1. Construct measurement 
Multi-item scales operationalized constructs in this study. Items were 
adapted from validated existing studies; we modified some items to

Zhang et al. 7ensure that they fit our context. A seven-point Likert scale was used as an 
anchor to measure all items. Specifically, individual boundary spanning 
and individual boundary buffering are adapted from Faraj and Yan 
(2009). Individual boundary spanning is measured by four reflective items 
and individual boundary buffering is measured by three reflective items, 
both of them are adjusted to fit the context of an individual’s ESM use. 
Information overload is defined as the condition induced by the large 
amount of information generated on ESM that exceeds the capacity an 
individual can process (Jacoby et al., 1974). Five reflective items of the 
construct were adapted from Zhang et al. (2016). Mindfulness is defined 
as a mode of cognitive functioning in a specific situation that increases 
individuals’ alertness to discrepancies, openness to newness, orientation 
in the present, and awareness of multi-angle thinking while performing 
key activities (Addas & Pinsonneault, 2018). The construct was 
measured with six reflective items adopted from (Addas & Pinsonneault, 
2018; Langer, 1989). Individual work performance was evaluated by 
the ESM user’s section leader, and four items from Venkatesh, Rai, and 
Maruping (2018) were used to reflect an individual’s work performance 
in a particular project. 
Since the study was implemented in China, the existing construct 
items were first translated into Chinese by three researchers fluent in 
English and Chinese. After minor corrections, we conducted two inde-
pendent card sorting analysis rounds with eight Ph.D. students for initial 
validity (Moore & Benbasat, 1991; Tsai, Wu, Klein, & Jiang, 2021; 
Zhang, Guo, Jiang, Wu, & Jiang, 2021). Four students classified items 
according to their definition similarity and descriptions of construct 
categories in each round. First round classification accuracy was 93%. 
After minor corrections based on feedback, modified items were classi -
fied by the other four students in a second round following which the 
accuracy of classifications increased to approximately 96%. Improve -
ments indicated adequate face and convergent validity. 
At the company surveyed in this study, work is generally carried out 
by project teams. One can legitimately access resources in the project 
team, freely contact teammates, and respond promptly to requests made 
to ensure the project’s progression. Therefore, we specified the 
“external” (the connotation of boundary) of the respondents as the 
demarcation formed by the accessibility officially provided by the 
project team that they are in. 
In addition, items for boundary management constructs were 
adapted from Faraj and Yan (2009). In their study, items were used to 
measure team-level boundary activities in a general context. Compared 
to this study, both the research context and research level are different. 
Therefore, we conducted interviews with three employees at the com-
pany we surveyed who were experienced in using ESM to refine the 
wording of these items to ensure they were consistent with ESM users’ 
actual use scenario. After incorporating their comments, we made 
several necessary amendments to these items, including specifying that 
all boundary activities are enabled by ESM and practiced by ESM users 
themselves. The statement sentences were also revised to fit our research 
purpose. For example, one item of boundary spanning in Faraj’s study is 
“To what extent does the team encourage its members to solicit infor-
mation and resources from elsewhere in and/or beyond the division?” 
We revised this item as “I use ESM to solicit information and resources 
from external channels.” To measure boundary buffering, we repeated 
these steps but added more materials (i.e., use of typical ESM func-
tionalities, which could reflect the connotation of ESM boundary buff-
ering) to some of the items to aid respondents’ comprehension. For 
example, one item of boundary buffering in Faraj’s study is “To what 
extent are outside pressures deflected or absorbed so that the team can 
work free of interference?” We revised the item and added relevant 
materials, to “I take measures (e.g., muting nonwork-related notifica -
tions) to ensure that I can work free of ESM interference.” 
To further ensure the questionnaire’s content validity, we invited ten 
experienced employees using ESM to participate in the pretest. They 
reviewed every item to assess precision and clarity as well as appropriate 
meaning in the ESM context, rating each item as “1-not matched,” “2- somewhat matched,” “3-quite matched” and “4-highly matched.” We 
then calculated the content validity index for each item. The results 
show that the index for each item is no less than 0.9, indicating good 
content validity (Polit & Beck, 2006). Several items were slightly 
modified according to feedback. The results from the pretest suggested 
that the measurement items were valid. 
4.1.1. Control variables 
Our study included seven control variables. Polychronicity orien -
tation refers to the extent to which one prefers to be engaged in two or 
more tasks or events simultaneously. Two items were adapted from A. 
Chen and Karahanna (2018). Prior studies have validated that people 
with different polychronicity orientations show variations in resource 
allocation and performance when they face multiple tasks (Bluedorn 
Allen, Kalliath Thomas, Strube Michael, & Martin Gregg, 1999; Cameron 
& Webster, 2012). Demographic factors, such as age, gender, and edu-
cation were also controlled in this study since they have been found to 
influence workload perception and work performance in the informa -
tion and communication tool context (Ragu-Nathan, Tarafdar, 
Ragu-Nathan, & Tu, 2008; Tams, Thatcher, & Grover, 2018). We include 
work experience as a control, because more experienced workers could 
have more social and work ties, which could further influence infor-
mation overload and work performance (Peng, Heim, & Mallick, 2015). 
Moreover, ESM use experience is controlled given that individuals with 
more experience in ESM would manage their ESM use more appropri -
ately and utilize ESM work resources better. Finally, we included 
non-work ESM use (e.g., social-related ESM use) as a control because 
extant studies have validated that such use can affect information 
overload ( Chen & Wei, 2019) and further influence work performance 
(Kuegler, Smolnik, & Kane, 2015). 
4.2. Data collection 
We collected data from a large Chinese telecommunications and 
information technology corporation with over 10,000 employees in 
Xi’an, China. The firm uses an ESM application that runs on personal 
computers and mobile devices for internal communication and other 
employee activities. The software is similar to other popular enterprise 
social media platforms in China, such as DingDing and WeCom, which 
integrate various functions, including basic communication functions (e. 
g., instant messaging and online meetings), social features (e.g., posts) 
and work-support features (e.g., documentation management). 
The formal survey used a multi-informant design to eliminate the 
risk of social desirability bias and common method variance. Section 
members are expected to answer most of the questionnaire, while sec-
tion leaders are required to evaluate their own section members’ per-
formance. In addition, collecting all items in one survey wave could lead 
to common method bias (Podsakoff, MacKenzie, Lee, & Podsakoff, 
2003). To help alleviate these issues, our study conducted a two-wave 
survey, which has been widely implemented (e.g., Wang, Wang, Lin, 
& Abdullat, 2021). Specifically, section members are required to answer 
their questionnaires over two waves. The first wave includes measure -
ment of ESM boundary spanning and buffering, and polychronicity 
orientation. One week later, respondents finish the questionnaire, with 
measurements of mindfulness and information overload, and 
demographics. 
With the help of the company’s administrator, we finally sent 400 
questionnaires and received 284 responses. After deleting responses 
with missing data, we analyzed 202 usable responses to test the pro-
posed model. We compared the early and late respondents for all the 
constructs and found no significant differences between them, suggest -
ing that nonresponse bias was not a problem (Armstrong & Overton, 
1977). Table 3 shows respondents’ demographic information. J. 5.1. Measurement model 
We used the partial least squares (PLS) method (SmartPLS 2.0) to test 
the measurement model and assess each construct ’s reliability and val-
idity. PLS is best suited for our research because it does not require 
multivariate normality of the data (Teo, Wei, & Benbasat, 2003 ). Sec-
ond, this method is suitable for explaining a relatively complex research 
model (Sarstedt, Ringle, & Hair, 2017 ). Third, PLS is capable of calcu -
lating reasonable latent variable scores of each construct. Rather than 
assume equal weights for all indicators of a scale, PLS allows every in-
dicator to contribute differently to the composite score of the latent 
variable (Chin, Marcolin, & Newsted, 2003 ), which avoid fixed-scale 
construction (Lowry & Gaskin, 2014 ), the obtained latent variables 
support subsequent analysis (Hair, Hollingsworth Carole, Randolph 
Adriane, & Chong Alain Yee, 2017 ). The PLS algorithm follows a 
two-step approach to test the measurement model. The first step aims to 
calculate the convergent latent variable scores through iteration. The 
scores are then used to estimate the measurement models (Henseler, 
Ringle, & Sarstedt, 2012 ). 
As shown in Table 4, the Cronbach ’s alpha score of each construct 
was above the recommended value of 0.70. Each construct ’s composite 
reliability was above the recommended value of 0.70 (Chin et al., 2003 ). 
Every construct ’s average variance extracted (AVE) was above 0.50, and 
item loadings on each latent variable were over 0.50, most of which 
reached higher than 0.7, indicating good convergent validity (Chin 
et al., 2003 ). To assess discriminant validity, we compared the square root of each 
construct ’s AVE with the absolute value of corresponding correlation 
coefficients in Table 5. The results indicated that the square roots of all 
AVEs were greater than their correlations with other constructs, 
showing satisfactory discriminant validity (Fornell & Larcker, 1981; 
Gefen & Straub, 2005 ). 
The variance inflation factor (VIF) was calculated, and the values 
were all below 3.3, indicating that there was no multicollinearity issue 
(Diamantopoulos & Winklhofer, 2001 ). We employed Harman ’s 
one-factor test to determine whether there was a common method bias 
issue. The results showed that no factor accounted for over half of the 
variance, suggesting that common method bias was not a problem 
(Harman, 1970; Mattila & Enz, 2002 ).

6.Discussion and implications 
The advantages and potential negative effects of ESM technologies 
that facilitate employees ’ online interactions and access to the 
enterprise-wide resources pool provide the impetus for this empirical 
investigation. This study applies an information processing theoretical 
lens to explore how individuals ’ boundary spanning and boundary 
buffering can collectively affect work performance in the ESM context 
(Dey & Ganesh, 2017 ). The results of this study support our proposed 
research hypotheses, which confirmed two distinct mechanisms (i.e., 
mindfulness and information overload) that could explain the positive 
and negative effects of ESM-based individual boundary spanning ac-
tivities. Furthermore, the results indicated that individual boundary 
buffering could strengthen the positive effect of individual boundary 
spanning on mindfulness and alleviate the positive influence of indi-
vidual boundary spanning on information overload. This study links to 
existing research in several ways. 
The present study takes the position that perceives ESM as broad -
ening an individual ’s resource sources within the organization (Ellison 
et al., 2015; Leonardi et al., 2013 ), and introduces the concept of 
boundary spanning from boundary management theory to explicitly 
capture the behaviors of individuals obtaining external work resources 
in the ESM context. Our research validates the positive mediating effect 
of mindfulness on the relationship between individual boundary span-
ning and work performance, indicating mindfulness is an effective in-
formation processing mode for taking advantage of external 
work-related resources obtained from individual boundary spanning 
via ESM. The finding is consistent with a prior study stating that em-
ployees ’ mindfulness can help individuals incorporate task-relevant in-
formation obtained from e-mail interruptions during work time (Addas 
& Pinsonneault, 2018 ). The revealed mechanism (i.e., mindfulness) adds 
knowledge to extant boundary spanning research, which recognizes that 
boundary spanning can broaden resource access and promote work 
performance (Barner-Rasmussen et al., 2014; Levina & Vaast, 2005; 
Reiche, 2011 ). 
This study found that individual boundary spanning has a positive 
linear relationship with information overload, indicating that intensive 
boundary spanning activities constantly consume individuals ’ informa -
tion processing resources, aligning with a prior study ’s notion that 
boundary spanning is taxing (Wang et al., 2019 ). It is worth noting that 
this finding seems inconsistent with one previous study that found an 
inverted curvilinear relationship between work-related ESM use and 
information overload (X. Chen & Wei, 2019 ). Chen and her colleague 
argued that as employees become familiar with ESM, they can better 
manage ESM activities; therefore, information overload decreases. 
However, the validated moderating role of individual boundary buff-
ering provides a perspective to address such inconsistency. Specifically, 
an individual who is more familiar with ESM will better understand how 
to prevent overloading oneself with ESM information (i.e., individual 
boundary buffering). Our study adds knowledge to the understanding of 
the relationship between ESM use and information overload. 
Lastly, this study examines the moderating role of individual 
boundary buffering. Previous literature on other information technolo -
gies’ use behavior suggested taking measures such as queuing (Wilson, 
1995 ), filtering, and withdrawing (Savolainen, 2007 ) to protect oneself 
from receiving too much information, thus enhancing one’s information 
processing efficiency. Nevertheless, scholars have pointed out a lack of 
empirical studies to statistically investigate the effectiveness of ap-
proaches for coping with information overload (Saxena & Lamest, 
2018 ). This study echoes this call and provides empirical evidence to 
consider the protection mechanisms on the effects of ESM on individual 
performance. Table 6

6.1. Research contributions and implications 
First, this study contributes to the ESM literature by introducing 
individual boundary spanning to capture individuals ’ obtaining external

dividual boundary spanning can affect individual work performance. 
Extant studies have validated the fact that ESM ’s integration of conve -

ESM users ’ obtaining and exchanging work resources (Qi & Chau, 2017; 
Rode, 2016 ). For example, Mantym aki (2016) found that ESM encour -
ages employees to exchange novel work ideas and gather opinions on 
work issues by reading colleagues ’ postings on ESM. However, it is a pity 
that we still know little on whether these resources obtaining and 
exchanging activities are occurring within the work boundary (e.g., 
within the project team) or out of the work boundary but within the 
organization. Such lack of discrimination does not allow us to explicitly 
capture the advantage of ESM as a tool to broaden individuals ’ access to knowledge across their work boundaries as proposed by prior scholars 
(Ellison et al., 2015; Leonardi et al., 2013 ). Based on boundary man-
agement literature, this study proposed the concept of boundary span-
ning to explicitly capture individuals ’ behaviors of obtaining work 
resources outside the boundary of their work. The study results reveal 
ESM boundary spanning activities trigger spanners into a mindful pro-
cessing mode through which they effectively incorporate external work 
resources obtained to aid their work. These findings echo prior studies ’ 
findings that receiving work-related information can benefit one’s work 
(Addas & Pinsonneault, 2018 ). Simultaneously, due to an individual ’s 
limited information processing capacity, intensive boundary spanning 
could lead to resource deficiency (i.e., information overload) and harm 
an individual ’s work performance. This finding is consistent with prior 
research findings that information overload is a critical negative 
consequence in ESM use activities (Chen & Wei, 2019 ; Gao et al., 2018 ). 
Second, the significant moderating effect of ESM boundary buffering 
suggests that it effectively enhances the value of ESM use. Prior litera -
ture presents conflicting views on the relationship between ESM use and 
work performance. The favorable perspective observed that ESM 
implementation is an excellent approach to breaking down knowledge 
silos in organizations and facilitating individuals ’ sharing and accessing 
of cross-boundary resources (Ellison et al., 2015; Kwayu, Abubakre, & 
Lal, 2021; Leonardi et al., 2013 ). In contrast, the opposing side posited 
that ESM use depletes individuals ’ limited resources and negatively 
influenced them through information overload (Chen & Wei, 2019 ; Sun 
et al., 2021 ). However, limited knowledge exists about alleviating ESM ’s 
negative effects on individuals and improving employees ’ efficiency of 
resources on ESM (Liang et al., 2020 ). The results of this study support 
the prior suggestion of taking measures such as queuing (Wilson, 1995 ), 
filtering and withdrawing (Savolainen, 2007 ) to protect oneself from

Finally, we contribute to the boundary management literature by 
theoretically proposing and empirically validating that individual 
boundary spanning and boundary buffering can be strategically com-
bined to effectively benefit an individual’s work. Extant studies have 
found that individual boundary spanning can broaden the channels for 
acquiring more work resources (Barner-Rasmussen et al., 2014; Levina 
& Vaast, 2005; Reiche, 2011), facilitating knowledge flow (Pawlowski & 
Robey, 2004), and providing extra cues for decision-making (Floyd & 
Wooldridge, 1997), thus benefiting work performance. However, those 
studies focused solely on the effects of boundary spanning, without 
in-depth discussion of strengthening the effectiveness of boundary 
spanning. Our study applied information processing theory and argued 
that individual boundary spanning via ESM is information-based; such 
activities can influence individual work performance through two 
mechanisms—mindfulness and information overload. We further 
considered the moderating role of individual boundary buffering in the 
relationship between individual boundary spanning and the two 
mechanisms. The results revealed that individual boundary buffering 
activities could strengthen the positive relationship between boundary 
spanning and mindfulness and alleviate the negative relationship be-
tween boundary spanning and information overload, thus, strength -
ening the effectiveness of individual boundary spanning. Our study 
echoes Dey and Ganesh’s call for studies that do not only consider the 
influence of separate boundary activities, as this would neglect the 
interaction effects of boundary activities (Dey & Ganesh, 2017). To the 
best of our knowledge, this is one of the first few, if not the first, 
empirical studies on the effects of combining both individual boundary 
spanning and boundary buffering activities on individual performance. 
6.2. Implications for practice 
Our study provides significant evidence that employees can enhance 
work performance by accessing external work resources via ESM. 
Managers need to realize that employees’ boundary spanning activities 
rely mainly on unofficial communications and interactions. In Chinese 
culture, co-worker guanxi is crucial to interpersonal workplace in-
teractions (Zhang X, 2020), and high-quality co-worker guanxi can 
reduce interaction conflict (Yen, Abosag, Huang, & Nguyen, 2017), 
facilitate communications, and motivate employees’ organizational 
citizenship behaviors (Lin & Ho, 2010). Therefore, managers can 
develop a fun and friendly atmosphere in the workplace (Yang, 2018) or 
organize more joint departmental team-building activities to enhance 
familiarity among employees, thus strengthening co-worker guanxi. On 
the other hand, team or project leaders can also encourage their mem-
bers to proactively acquire external work resources via ESM (Faraj & 
Yan, 2009). 
Second, it is notable that the effectiveness of individual boundary 
spanning also depends on the quality of the external resources. Orga-
nizations can take approaches to enrich high-quality work resources 
documented on ESM (Dang, Zhang, & Chen, 2018), such as providing 
incentives for employees to encourage ESM content creation and sharing 
(Beranek, Klein, & Jiang, 2014), setting sharing norms for project teams 
and departments to require them to upload and maintain the work log 
and report on the ESM. 
Third, although employees can benefit from individual boundary 
spanning, managers should be aware that employees need to search, 
recognize work-related information from massive content on ESM, and 
then incorporate useful information into their work. Such a series of 
procedures is costly and inefficient. Since individuals possess limited 
information processing resources, managers are expected to reduce the 
cost of individuals’ boundary spanning activities. Examples include 
providing a series of content tags, allowing users to customize content 
tags, and encouraging ESM users to tag the content they create or upload on the ESM to facilitate other ESM users’ content searching. Managers 
can also establish a reasonable content management directory to better 
arrange content on the ESM, or increase the communication visibility of 
ESM to help users develop meta knowledge (i.e., knowledge about who 
knows what and who knows whom) to assist them in locating the source 
of needed work resources (Leonardi, 2014). 
Fourth, the positive moderating effects of individual boundary 
buffering show their value during individuals’ boundary-spanning ac-
tivities, especially as ESM functionalities provide various technology- 
based approaches to support individuals in engaging in these activ-
ities. Managers should therefore conduct ESM training courses for em-
ployees to clarify and emphasize the use and effects of different 
functionalities in different typical contexts so that ESM users can have 
better control over ESM. These practices would ultimately achieve more 
effective ESM boundary management. The results also imply that the 
intrusiveness and disturbance of non-work-related activities via ESM 
during work time are not negligible in the ESM context. Managers 
should set norms or rules to reduce employees’ unnecessary ESM use 
activities during work time. 
Finally, the findings of boundary buffering activities provide di-
rections for ESM developers. They can provide more buffering types of 
ESM functionalities to improve user experience. For example, by 
providing tags for information flows to enable ESM users to classify 
whether the specific information flow is work or non-work-related, such 
functionality can help ESM manage notifications automatically (e.g., 
ESM can mute notifications from non-work-related information sources 
during work time). 
6.3. Limitations and future research direction 
This study has limitations that present opportunities for future 
research. First, this study focuses on one typical interaction of boundary 
management activities. Prior boundary management literature has 
identified different boundary activities, such as boundary reinforcement 
(Faraj & Yan, 2009). Future research could follow this direction and seek 
a new portfolio of boundary management activities. Second, this study 
considered limited mechanisms regarding the interaction effects of 
boundary spanning and boundary buffering in the ESM context. Future 
researchers could investigate other potential mechanisms, such as job 
burnout or stress, in this specific context (Voydanoff, 2005). Third, 
although we measured ESM boundary buffering, we did not further 
discriminate whether the information that individuals’ buffer is 
work-related, which could neglect some of the potential effects of such 
boundary behaviors. For example, if one buffers work-related informa -
tion (though he may feel overloaded at that time), such behavior could 
negatively influence the relationship between him and his colleagues (e. 
g., collaboration or mutual trust) and work performance. Future 
research could therefore continue to explore these behaviors to provide 
finer-grained insights. Fourth, this study provides insights into the ef-
fects of individual boundary spanning. However, according to the 
summary of boundary spanning in the ESM context, there is a lack of 
research on what factors influence individuals to engage in ESM 
boundary spanning. For instance, scholars have confirmed different 
psychological conditions (i.e., psychological meaningfulness, psycho -
logical availability, psychological safety) as indispensable factors that 
affect work activities in the ESM context (Cai et al., 2018). Future studies 
could follow this direction and explore the relationships between psy-
chological conditions and individual boundary spanning in the ESM 
context. Finally, the data in this study was collected from one firm in 
China. Whether the results and discussions are applicable to other 
countries remains unverified. Different cultures might affect ESM users’ 
behaviors and interactions. Prior studies have suggested differences 
between Chinese guanxi and Western relationship norms (Yang, 2018; 
Yen et al., 2017). Future studies can apply our model to other cultural 
contexts to develop more comprehensive research. J. 127.Conclusion 
This study adopts an information processing theoretical lens to 
empirically identify two mechanisms through which individual bound -
ary spanning affects work performance. Along with these two mecha -
nisms, we also explored the moderating effects of individual boundary 
buffering via ESM. Our results found that boundary buffering can 
enhance the positive effects of individual boundary spanning on mind -
fulness and reduce the side effects of individual boundary spanning on 
information overload. Our findings emphasize that necessary boundary 
buffering helps employees better reap the benefits of boundary 
spanning.
