Social exchange in buyer-supplier
relationships and innovation speed: the
mediating and moderating role of information
sharing and knowledge channels
Divesh Ojha, Mumin Dayan, Beth Struckell, Amandeep Dhir and Terrence Pohlen
Abstract
Purpose – This study recognizes service as the majority contributor to global and US gross domestic
product and the importance of innovation speed to service innovation. Generating innovative products
and services at a faster rate generates advantages for business-to-business (B2B) service organizations
in keeping up with and moving ahead of rivals. This study aims to introduce the concept of capacity for
social exchange (CSE) in buyer –supplier relationships, which reflects the degree to which individuals
possess competencies that enable the exchange of information, and this study also explores how CSE
affects knowledge sharing and innovation speed within a supply chain organization.
Design/methodology/approach – The sampling frame of this research consisted of service businesses
in the USA. The data were collected through Zoomerang, an online survey research firm where the B2B
panel of Zoomerang formed the sampling frame. The data was collected from 264 B2B service sector
executives.
Findings – The key findings are as follows: CSE facilitates knowledge sharing; knowledge sharing is
positively related to innovation speed; and the relationship between CSE and innovation speed is fully
mediated by knowledge sharing. Additional analysis reveals that knowledge channels which are
designed as boundary-spanning strategies used to transfer and create knowledge between business
units, directly increase– and positively moderate– the relationship between CSE and innovation speed.
Originality/value – This study addresses the gap in literature focused on micro-level influencers on
innovation. This study sets out by presenting the concept of CSE, and this study addresses limitations in
prior work by examining the research questions.
Keywords Capacity for social exchange, Knowledge sharing, Knowledge channels, Innovation speed
Paper type Research paper
1. Introduction
Service firms, which currently represent more than 70% of global gross domestic product
(Chen et al. , 2016 ), operate in an increasingly dynamic environment that subjects them to
intense pressure to be highly responsive to competitive service offerings and continuously
launch novel offerings ( Berry et al. ,2 0 0 6). Business-to-business (B2B) service businesses
face unique challenges in delivering innovation in a landscape inhabited by traditional
product manufacturers. Easily copied by competitors, B2B service innovation demands
continuous and rapid bursts of innovation activity ( Kessler and Chakrabarti, 1996 ).
Competitive advantage in this environment is therefore crucially dependent on a company’s
embedded innovation speed ( Kessler and Chakrabarti, 1996 ; Chen et al.,2 0 1 2); however,
faster service innovation is not always a straightforward process for B2B firms ( Thomas,
2013). This has led to service innovation becoming a process that is interconnected with

Given the importance of innovation speed, researchers have explored multifarious mechanisms
to influence innovation speed within organizations. Although several studies have examined
antecedents to innovation speed, much of the interest has been centered on macro-level
variables.
Kessler and Chakrabarti (1996) , for example, explore how a firm’s strategic
orientation, goal alignment and organizational capabilities affect innovation speed.Chen et al.
(2010) and Struckell and Ojha (2017)focus on the role of leadership, bricolage and operational
flexibility in enabling innovation. However, there is a dearth of studies on how individual-level or
micro-level phenomena affect innovation speed. Further, those studies focus on innovation and
knowledge sharing which do include individual-level variables and neglect broader, more
generic conceptualizations to focus instead on specific concepts such as self-efficacy, which
can hamper theory-building efforts. Despite revealing the importance of theorizing the actions
and interactions of individuals in buyer–supplier relationships (
Liu et al., 2017; Forkmannet al.,
2018), and given the wide recognition of the role played by individual capabilities, such as
relationship termination capability in B2B relationships and networks (Zaefarianet al., 2017), it is
striking that recent research has yet to fully examine this phenomenon. According toFoss and
Lindenberg (2013), micro-foundations involve theorizing in terms of the actions and interactions
of individuals. Lack of emphasis at this level is concerning, as it is these variables that have the
greatest potential to generate novel conclusions and suggest informed interventions in
buyer–supplier relationships. For instance,
Alinaghianet al.’s (2020) case study reveals that joint
practices in a B2B network are noticeable only when both the socially and cognitively
embedded conditions accurately become established in a given relationship. Specifically,
without joint optimistic attitudes and motivation at the micro-level, buyer–supplier relationships
can hardly be beneficial to both parties.
To address these limitations as well answer numerous calls for studies on these issues
(
Forkmann et al. ,2 0 1 8; Alghababsheh and Gallear, 2020 ), we introduce the notion of
“Capacity for Social Exchange” (CSE) in buyer–supplier relationships, which we define as the
degree to which individuals possess a set of competencies that allow them to share and
exchange knowledge with others. By using the lens of social exchange theory, we examine
how CSE affects the sharing of knowledge and interacts with the organizational context so as
to influence speed of innovation. Social exchange theory has been recognized as a promising
perspective within the knowledge management literature and is uniquely useful because it
provides holistic arguments for when such exchanges are likely to occur (
Wu et al., 2014).
This contribution builds upon our conceptualization of CSE. We set out by presenting the
concept of CSE, and we address limitations in prior work by examining our research
questions. We first question how CSE affects knowledge sharing in our B2B service
innovation context (i.e. between business units of an organization and its supply chain
partners). Second, we ask how knowledge sharing affects innovation speed and whether,
as expected, sharing entirely mediates the effect of CSE on innovation speed. Finally, by
using structural equation modeling (SEM) to test our hypotheses, we explore how the use of
integrating mechanisms (i.e. knowledge channels [KC] that allow for exchange and creation
of knowledge) enhances knowledge sharing and innovation speed in organizations. We
conclude by discussing our study’s contributions as well as its limitations.

While the development of knowledge as a resource within organizations is undeniably
influenced by a broad range of factors, organization theorists have lamented the overt
emphasis that has been placed on studying more macro and higher-level phenomena,
such as competitive intensity, environmental turbulence, absorptive capacity and strategic
orientation, among others (
Foss et al. ,2 0 1 0). The dearth of studies that focus on micro-
foundations (i.e. individual-level phenomena and their interaction within organizational
contexts) is concerning because, ontologically, the development of knowledge as a
resource is contingent on individual attitudes, behaviors and actions. Prior work suggests
that neglecting micro-foundations poses a potentially serious deficit for the knowledge
management literature, to the extent that “understanding what happens in organizations
requires beginning from their fundamental constituents, namely individuals” (
Foss et al. ,
2010, p. 457), as well as generating insight at the level of individuals (i.e. organizational
members) and their mutual interaction within their context. Emphasizing micro-levels means
identifying the true mechanisms that produce observed correlations, and an understanding
of such generative mechanisms generates the basis for informed intervention (
Felin and
Foss, 2005; Brenton and Levin, 2012).
It follows that the limitations in the available knowledge-based literature affect the definition
of the level and domain of the concept at hand, and therefore we define the CSE as a broad
set of competencies which enable individuals to interact with others and share or exchange
knowledge. The level of the CSE concept as an individual micro-foundation remains
important because, ultimately, all organizational action and intervention occurs at the level
of the individual. The individual level domain of CSE is crucial, for the sharing of knowledge,
which has ultimately been associated with improved organizational performance (
Della
Peruta et al., 2014; Chang, 2017; Yang et al.,2 0 1 9; Rungsithong and Meyer, 2020). Sharing
knowledge (at the individual level) can lead to increased absorptive capacity and the
development of other organization level capabilities. Previous studies have proposed that
understanding how knowledge is shared is important, as it is through interaction between
organizational members that an overlap of knowledge sets occurs and absorptive capacity
is increased, which, in turn, improves the identification, reconciliation and prompt
responses to problems (
Forkmann et al. ,2 0 1 8; Kohtama¨ ki et al. ,2 0 1 8; Argote and
Miron-Spektor, 2011).

organizational performance can only be fully understood by examining action at this level
(Wasko and Faraj, 2005 ). A number of studies have focused on individual-level
determinants of knowledge sharing and performance. For example, Hendriks (1999) posits
that sharing is motivated by the desire to be successful, whereas Eze et al. (2013) examine
how reward systems, confidence and leadership qualities encourage individuals to share
knowledge with others. Although a wide range of theoretical perspectives and individual
constructs have provided valuable research contributions, for reasons of practicality and
necessity, these have focused on specific, individual-level variables based upon the
specific theoretical paradigm used and their relationship to knowledge sharing and
performance. A few studies have emphasized the role of extrinsic or intrinsic rewards in
defining attitudes toward knowledge sharing; for instance, Jeon et al. (2011) use the theory
of planned behavior to explore how both “enthusiasm to help” and “perceptions of
reciprocity,” motivate individuals and affect knowledge sharing. Although it is important to
understand how enjoyment and reciprocity facilitate sharing, it becomes increasingly clear
that these factors alone do not suffice to share knowledge ( Blau, 1964; Serenko and Bontis,
2016). Additionally, individuals need to believe that the knowledge they hold is valuable,
accurate and acceptable within the organization. Without confidence in the knowledge they
hold, motivation may not suffice and, thus, may limit the will to share and diminish the
perceived benefits in helping organizations improve performance.
A further issue pertaining to knowledge management studies at the level of the individual, derives
from the diversity in the use and conceptualization of constructs, as well as the inconsistency in
study findings. While Kim and Lee (2006) found that reward systems significantly impact
knowledge-sharing capabilities,Lin (2007) reported that organizational rewards did not have an
effect on employees’ willingness to share knowledge with their colleagues. Such contradictory
findings are problematic, as they limit practitioner recommendations and impede theoretical
interpretation, synthesis and theory development for research.
Considering these observations, yet while recognizing the value of prior research, our
objective here is to enrich the knowledge management literature and aid in further theory
development by introducing the broad, higher-order concept of individual CSE. CSE
reflects the set of individual competencies necessary to engage and interact with others,
with the intent to share knowledge. Specifically, we draw upon the framework introduced by
Maclnnis and Jaworski (1989) in the context of marketing and use the three dimensions of
motivation, opportunity and ability (MOA) to reflect the dimensions of CSE. These three
dimensions have been insinuated in numerous studies, as well as in theoretical
perspectives, that explore the role of individual-level factors in knowledge sharing and
organizational innovation. Here, these dimensions are appropriate for two reasons. First,
they allow us to step back and adopt a broader and more abstract perspective to avoid the
contradictions and problems of inconsistency that often surface when theorizing from more
specific variables (such as rewards). As Foss and Lindenberg (2013) contend, and
because all phenomena are mediated through micro-foundations (e.g. organizational
culture > affects motivation > which affects performance), putting the spotlight on micro-
foundations of interest can alleviate difficulties related to the use of the divergent concepts
and inexplicable contradictions that have appeared in similar studies. Second, the
motivation, opportunity and ability dimensions allow us to propose a simple yet holistic
concept that includes those variables necessary to facilitate knowledge sharing – as
suggested in prior research.
2.2 Composition of capacity for social exchange: theoretical perspectives and
empirical studies
2.2.1 Social exchange theory. The knowledge management literature predominantly adopts
the perspective of social exchange theory, where individual behavior and individual
PAGE 1512 jJOURNAL OF KNOWLEDGE MANAGEMENT jVOL. 27 NO. 6 2023
Downloaded from http://www.emerald.com/jkm/article-pdf/27/6/1509/1768174/jkm-04-2022-0280.pdf by University College London user on 30 May 2026interactions with others are based upon a self-interest analysis of the costs and benefits of
the interaction ( Lambe et al. , 2001 ). Individuals seek to maximize benefits and minimize
costs when interacting with others. While initially framed as a strictly economic exchange,
later developments in social exchange theory have illuminated that benefits and costs can
be intangible and may be realized in either a short- or long-term manner. The theory
suggests that individuals build relationships and exchange knowledge with others to
maximize benefits with expectations of future returns. For example,
Davenport and Prusak
(1998) suggest reciprocity, promotions and status as potential intangible and longer-term
motivators for behavior. In a more recent B2B study, Cortez and Johnston (2020) explain
social exchange theory to encompass economic and or social outcomes which can
manifest in both near-term and longer-term outcomes.
On the other hand, Knowledge sharing and exchange have been found to have costs along
with the associated benefits. The cost represents the risk associated with knowledge
exchange. Individuals may be discouraged if they perceive risk in interacting with others.
Organizational cultures that emphasize outcomes may discourage sharing because
individuals perceive that knowledge-sharing interactions may result in the loss of a personal
competitive edge, the possibility of being made redundant and losing work or the risk
management interpretation of individuals having “excess time on the organizational dime.” If
these issues lead management to perceive an individual in a negative way, the
consequences are viewed as significant: loss of work or benefits.
Prior literature covers some aspects of the importance of individual motivations to share
knowledge.
Roodbari et al. (2016a) provide a small yet representative sample of individual-
level studies on individual cognition in knowledge management. For instance, motivators
such as job satisfaction and organizational commitment ( Teh and Sun, 2012 ), increasing
credibility (Wu and Zhu, 2012), success and promotion (Hendricks, 1999), reward system
(
Eze et al. ,2 0 1 3), positive attitude ( Roodbari et al. , 2016b ), reputation and enthusiasm to
help (Anitha, 2006) and learning and rewards (Yee, 2010; Jolaee et al., 2014), each suggest
potential motivating factors and support motivation for sharing as a key aspect of building
knowledge repositories in an organization. These studies (alongside others) have also
addressed the risks of knowledge sharing – negative motivators, albeit indirectly.
Beyond individual motivations to share knowledge, other studies recognize the importance
of ability to share knowledge. For example, Eze et al. (2013) note the influence of
confidence in interacting with colleagues, whereas Fathi et al. (2011) and Jolaee et al .
(2014) mention self-efficacy as barriers to interaction.
Besides the motivations of knowledge sharing (benefits and risks) and ability to share
knowledge, a particularly crucial factor in studies of individual cognition and knowledge
management are situational constraints placed on individuals who wish to share knowledge.
While constraints are barriers to interacting with others, they are distinct in that they do not
directly refer to individual attributes and abilities such as self-efficacy. One previously
suggested and particularly essential element, especially within the organizational context, is
time constraint. Although prior research suggests that having the motivation and the
requisite knowledge is necessary to support knowledge sharing (
Trada and Goyal, 2020 ),
numerous studies suggest that lack of time can inhibit knowledge sharing in organizations.
Because exchanging, internalizing, combining or co-creating knowledge can be a
demanding endeavor, individuals may understandably focus solely on work demands for
which they have primary accountability (
Connelly et al., 2009).
These studies suggest that individual motivations alone are insufficient to influence knowledge
sharing and as such provide support for the use of motivation –opportunity–ability as a three-
dimensional framework for the CSE.
The common theme emerging from our understanding of the knowledge management
literature is that three primary aspects exist (motivation, opportunity and ability), which

2.3 Defining dimensions of capacity for social exchange: motivation/opportunity/
ability
The MOA framework, originally introduced by MacInnis and Jaworski (1989) in the context
of marketing communication effectiveness, has been found to be an important antecedent
of knowledge sharing (
Ardichvili, 2008; Siemsen et al.,2 0 0 8; Kettinger et al., 2015; Radaelli
et al.,2 0 1 4). In the marketing context, motivation implies audience interest and openness to
advertising communication. Opportunity relates to the audience ability to focus and to
allocate attention to the advertising vehicle (i.e. television, print ad, etc.). And finally, ability
suggests skills, experience with the product or service and knowledge to understand and
interpret the messaging (
MacInnis et al.,1 9 9 1).
The MOA framework was extended to knowledge sharing research by Siemsen and
Colleagues (2008). The authors established individual employee MOA to gauge the degree
of knowledge sharing that can occur within an organization. Later, and relevant to our study,
Turner and Pennington (2015) use the MOA framework as antecedent to organizational
learning and ultimately to innovation success. Even more recently, Ahmad and colleagues
support the MOA framework with social exchange theory and show that the three
dimensions moderate the relationship between charismatic leadership and service –sales
performance (
Ahmad et al.,2 0 2 1).
2.3.1 Motivation. Although the specific elements that enhance motivation in the MOA
framework vary across different studies, there is consistency in the importance of motivation
in explaining knowledge sharing (
Gruen et al.,2 0 0 5; Siemsen et al., 2008). We leverage the
definitions from the MOA framework’s elements introduced by Siemsen et al. (2008) to
define motivation as the primary driver of individual behavior, that is, the willingness to act.
Motivation explains the successful practice of knowledge sharing among employees
(
Siemsen et al. ,2 0 0 8 ). For instance, personal ambition ( Hendriks, 1999 ), trust in
organizations (Ardichvili, 2003), enjoyment ( Hsu and Lin, 2008 ), rewards, self-efficacy ( Lin,
2007), willingness ( Lam and Lambermont-Ford, 2010 ) and altruism are elements of
motivation that have been positively associated with knowledge sharing. Based on past
studies, we assume motivation to be a key dimension that influences knowledge sharing.
2.3.2 Opportunity. Opportunity refers to constraints that affect an individual’s ability to act,
that is, to transfer knowledge. A common constraint in organizational culture is the
availability of time to allow employees or groups to interact and create, or share, knowledge
(
Siemsen et al.,2 0 0 8). Other examples of situational impediments to opportunity discussed
in the literature include fear of organizational criticism, fear of misleading organizations,
extreme security environments (
Ardichvili et al ., 2003 ), norm sharing ( Gagne´ , 2009 ) and
open egocentric networks ( Reinholt et al. , 2011 ; AlHussan et al. , 2017 ; La Rocca and
Snehota, 2014 ). Here, having the “right opportunity” is an important precondition for
knowledge sharing. Following MacInnis et al. (1991) and Siemsen et al. (2008),w e
conceptualize opportunity as the availability of time to allow individuals to share knowledge.
Opportunity (time) is considered one of the most crucial constraining factors in the
knowledge management literature.
2.3.3 Ability. The third element of the MOA framework refers to the skill, proficiency and
knowledge of the parties at hand in enabling effective exchange (
MacInnis et al. , 1991 ).
Ability represents an individual’s skills or knowledge base in relation to action ( Rothschild,
1999; Siemsen et al. ,2 0 0 8). Having the ability or necessary knowledge is an underlying

helps to build new knowledge, which can be absorbed in the workplace through
experience, formal education and training, and is thus a prerequisite for interacting with

Reinholt et al.,2 0 1 1). Gruen et al. (2005) included past experiences, subjective

dimension to successful innovation ( Lee, 2001 ), and we follow prior research by defining
ability as the “skills or knowledge related to the action” ( Siemsen et al.,2 0 0 8, p. 427).
2.4 Mutual reinforcement of motivation, opportunity and ability elements: capacity
for social exchange as a second-order latent concept
According to Siemsen et al. (2008) and Kettinger et al. (2015), motivation is considered to
be the most vital of the three dimensions – although the remaining two elements (ability and
opportunity/time) are by no means unimportant. For example, when motivation levels are
high, ability has a positive effect; yet, if motivation levels are low, increasing ability has no
effect (
Clark et al. ,2 0 0 5). Further, highly motivated groups can make poor decisions
(Hackman, 1990 ; Janis and Mann, 1977 ), and the lack of opportunity can impede the
productivity of even the most highly motivated employees ( Rothschild, 1999 ). Turner and
Pennington (2015) tested each dimension of MOA as antecedents to knowledge sharing in
the context of corporate entrepreneurship and found motivation to be the strongest
influencer and the other two dimensions to be highly correlated. The authors did not test the
holistic association. Likewise, and more recently,
Ahmad et al. (2021) studied the
moderating effect of the three dimensions – MOA – between charismatic leadership and
service to sales ambidexterity in a service to sales context. Motivation was found to have the
strongest moderating influence, but all three dimensions were positive and significant.
These scholars also did not test the holistic moderating influence of the MOA model.
It follows that each of the three dimensions should be considered as important to the overall
framework, as has been found in previous studies. In the present contribution, we go
beyond the individual dimensions and refer to the mutually reinforcing elements of
motivation, opportunity and ability as the CSE. As
MacInnis et al. (1991) suggest, while
motivation is the most significant factor in the framework, each of the elements is unique,
important and interrelated within the MOA framework. Therefore, we posit that CSE is a
second-order construct with three mutually complementary first-order dimensions:
motivation, opportunity and ability. Prior studies using the MOA framework fail to fully
investigate the holistic influence of the three dimensions in addition to the importance of
each dimension on its own.

3.1 Capacity for social exchange and knowledge sharing
Knowledge sharing refers to the activities of transferring knowledge between individuals or
groups (
Lee, 2001 ). Chiu et al. (2006) found that a lack of social capital could negatively
affect knowledge sharing in virtual communities, which bears important implications for this
study because the supply chain within a service industry operates both face-to-face and
virtually. We follow the definition of knowledge sharing introduced by
Siemsen et al. (2008)
in that knowledge sharing involves at least two people: a sender (here, an employee who
attempts to share knowledge) and a recipient (the coworker who is intended to acquire it).
In this context, we seek to understand and focus on what motivates a sender to transfer
expertise to others.
VOL. 27 NO. 6 2023 jJOURNAL OF KNOWLEDGE MANAGEMENT j PAGE 1515
Downloaded from http://www.emerald.com/jkm/article-pdf/27/6/1509/1768174/jkm-04-2022-0280.pdf by University College London user on 30 May 2026The core emphasis in social exchange theory lies in individual cognition, which includes the
perceived benefits and risks of interacting with others and sharing knowledge. The former
of these are the subjective gains that accrue to an individual from interacting with others,
whereas perceived risks are the subjective losses incurred from sharing knowledge.
According to
Blau (1964), individuals are more willing to share expertise when they believe
that it will not distract them from their primary work and will result in tangible rewards
(bonuses) or intangible rewards (status, enjoyment and promotions), over time.
Davenport
and Prusak (1998) argue that motivation to share knowledge is a result of perceived
benefits, and Wasko and Faraj (2005) go on to note that the possibility of improving
reputation (a perceived benefit) motivates advice-giving and affects the frequency of
interaction.
Thus, CSE – the measure of the degree to which individuals are motivated, capable and
have the opportunity to interact – should positively relate to knowledge sharing. Having the
necessary motivation suggests that organizational mechanisms have been put in place to
encourage knowledge sharing. For example, Siemens uses a points-based rewards system
to recognize individuals who meaningfully contribute to eliminating re-work. The provision of
adequate time – hence, opportunity – in which to share knowledge is also suggestive of
organizational priorities.
Once provided with the appropriate incentives and opportunity, capable individuals might
perceive the personal benefits in sharing as outweighing its risks. Additionally, depending
upon the strategies used to augment opportunities (such as flexible work arrangements),
individuals may also perceive management as genuinely acting in the best interest of
employees, thereby identifying or bonding with the organization and reciprocating goodwill
by sharing expertise to the benefit of all. It stands to reasons that if the separate dimensions
of MOA have been found to be positively associated with knowledge sharing, then the
holistic construct of CSE should be strongly associated with knowledge sharing. Therefore,
we propose the following hypothesis:
H1. CSE is positively related to knowledge sharing.

Innovation speed is defined as the period of time between the launch of a new idea and the
placement of a product (or service) in a market ( Kessler and Chakrabarti, 1996). According

In other contexts, knowledge sharing between individual team members has been found to
be positively related to team-level innovation ( Hu and Randel, 2014 ). Previous studies have
shown that innovation can be improved when an organization encourages individuals to
share knowledge freely ( Spencer, 2003 ). Innovation performance and knowledge sharing
have a positive relationship, especially when gaining external knowledge from partners is
crucial, for example in supply chains ( Ritala et al. , 2014 ), where collaborative innovation
activities are also positively affected by knowledge sharing ( Wang and Hu, 2020 ). Thus,
given the overwhelming evidence, we propose the following hypothesis:
H2. Knowledge sharing is positively related to innovation speed.
3.3 Capacity for social exchange and innovation speed
Based on prior research, we would expect CSE to improve personal innovativeness (e.g.
the number of innovative ideas proposed by individuals); however, our context suggests
that CSE may not enhance innovation speed at the organizational level. Specifically, we are
interested in innovations that are implemented with the purpose of achieving organizational
objectives and improving work processes and services. Thus, the emphasis here lies on
innovations that affect not only the individual but also groups that make use of services and
work processes. As
Van de Ven (1986) suggests organizational innovations do not take
place in isolation: executives, close working colleagues and other important stakeholders
such as strategic customers are affected by innovation and attempt to steer novel ideas in a
direction that is acceptable to everyone. Similarly, Scott and Bruce (1994) have stressed the
importance of interacting with colleagues and eliciting support for new ideas. Amabile
(1988) proposed a model of creativity that highlights the importance of a work environment
that includes top management support and the discussion of ideas among colleagues. This
implies that while motivation, opportunity and ability may indeed result in the development
of novel ideas, in organizational contexts, these three dimensions alone do not suffice to
convert ideas into practice. Therefore, unless discourse on new ideas and frequent
interactions take place, generating support from other significant stakeholders becomes
difficult and may restrain or slow the implementation of innovative ideas. In an organization,
innovation does not happen without the collaboration of multiple organizational units and
individuals.
Therefore, we believe that in the absence of knowledge sharing, any benefits of CSE are
likely to emerge in form of new ideas and thoughts that do not translate into value creation
for the company. In the absence of interactions with others, motivation, opportunity and
ability are, by themselves, not strong enough to generate support – without which the
perception can be created that an idea generator is “going against the crowd,” thereby
risking sanctions should the proposed idea be deemed inadequate. Furthermore, when
others, especially higher executives, show indifference to fresh ideas, the benefits of

Accordingly, we believe in the following hypothesis:
H3. Knowledge sharing entirely mediates the relationship between CSE and innovation

3.4 Knowledge channels as boundary objects to facilitate knowledge sharing
In this study, we also consider KC as an organizational support mechanism and explore
their influence on both sharing as well as speed of innovation.
KC refer to the boundary-spanning strategies used by organizations that permit the transfer,
recombination and/or creation of knowledge among supply chain partners (Rosenzweig and
Roth, 2007). According to Rosenzweig and Roth, who outline the concept, “KC enables the
cross organizational integration required for creating formal and informal connections and also
the potential for deep integration among partners” (p. 1317). KC therefore may be thought of as
a coordinating or integrating mechanism used by partner organizations to bring together the
various pockets of knowledge that reside within a supply chain’s diverse functional units and to
leverage shared expertise and interactions so as to identify opportunities and innovate across
products, services or processes which help them stay competitive (
Uzzi and Lancaster, 2003).
KC may be formal (e.g. the deployment of electronic interfaces) or informal (e.g. periodic
informal gatherings or cross-organizational points of contact, where functional teams from
different partners may be co-located to facilitate exchange and the sharing of expertise).
Regardless of the specific form of deployed KC, the literature generally concurs that KC allow
knowledge to converge, thereby providing a forum that enables the clarification, debate and
enactment of knowledge among partners.
KC are important for the sharing of information because boundaries between organizations can
adversely impact information flow and knowledge. For instance, the extent of information shared
between partners can be influenced by the extent to which parties mutually depend upon each
other for their business needs. Furthermore, boundaries may act as barriers to that helps
organizations stay competitive. Beyond economic considerations, the literature suggests that
people belonging to different organizations possess different knowledge sets and, often, non-
overlapping mental schemas (
Carlile, 2002, 2004 ), which can impede the exchange of
information between partners due to limited absorptive capacity (Cohen and Levinthal, 1990).
Thus, integrating and coordinating mechanisms in the form of KCs are crucial to enabling
members of different organizations with unique mental schemas to understand, interpret and
make sense of novel information (
Claro and Claro, 2010). Indeed, Carlile (2004)argues that KC
are critical to sustaining the intense sharing of knowledge because the very act of constructing
these boundary-spanning channels requires the creation of a shared commitment and the
establishment of routines, stories, models or a common language that allows members from
distinct organizations to contribute to shared objectives (
Kellogget al., 2006).
In the context of this study, KC capture the informal mechanisms that an organization’s
individual managers may use to share expertise. In the governance branch of organization
theory, these informal practices of co-locating team members or participating in frequent
informal gatherings where knowledge can be shared are often labeled as “clan controls”
(Ouchi, 1979). Knowledge sharing has been found to enable sharing and increase
innovative activity because they allow organizations to overcome the three types of
boundaries outlined by
Carlile (2004) that prevent the exchange and coordination of
dispersed knowledge in supply chains. Shared mental schemas that arise from frequent
interactions may overcome syntactic boundaries (differences in information and knowledge
content) and semantic boundaries (differences in viewpoints and priorities). Finally, time
spent together engaging with partners also builds quasi-clan types of working relations not
exclusively dominated by economic interests but which also emphasize cooperative,
mutually beneficial goals important for overcoming pragmatic boundaries (
Homans, 1958;

by generating a greater breadth of individuals amongst whom knowledge may be
exchanged, as well as more frequent opportunities in which this can occur. Accordingly, we
hypothesize as follows:
H4. KC positively moderate the relationship between CSE and knowledge sharing.
3.5 Knowledge channels as boundary objects to facilitate innovation speed
We expect KC to moderate the relationship between CSE and innovation speed for several
reasons. The literature agrees that communication between individuals who possess
diverse opinions, viewpoints, and information is a key factor driving innovation. Although
prior work suggests that communication among individuals with diverse viewpoints and
knowledge can be a source of conflict and may be counterproductive, y et al so that the
conflict process may prove to be fruitful in facilitating innovation. Conflict can bring hidden
assumptions and agendas to the fore (
Flaatin, 2007), and productive debate may result in a
broader discovery of ideas and resolutions to potential problems and disagreements
(
Tjosvold et al. , 2014 ). As argued above, by instituting informal meetings and co-locating
partners, KC open opportunities for individuals with diverse pockets of knowledge and
mental schemas to come together and collaborate. Although individual creativity and talent
are certainly important, by themselves these remain insufficient as conditions for innovation
for they do not present fruitful opportunities for collaboration. KC augment creativity and
talent by providing realistic opportunities for knowledge sharing and innovation (
Del Giudice
et al. ,2 0 1 4, 2015; Scuotto et al. ,2 0 1 7). Moreover, in the context of knowledge sharing
among supply chain partners, KC may be crucial for organizing both financial and non-
financial resources during the implementation phase. For instance, KC may serve to provide
social capital resources (adoption of ideas by stakeholders) that increase the likelihood of
gaining access to additional material resources. Therefore, we hypothesize as follows:
H5. KC positively moderate the relationship between CSE and innovation speed.
4. Methodology
4.1 Participants and procedure
The sampling frame of this research consisted of service businesses in the USA, and the
data were collected through Zoomerang, an online survey research firm; the B2B panel of
Zoomerang formed the sampling frame. Other studies have successfully used data from
Zoomerang and suggest that the data provided there are valid for research purposes
(
Iskandar et al. ,2 0 1 5; Pham et al. ,2 0 1 5; Trzesniewski and Donnellan, 2014 ). The
respondents were screened to ensure a sampling of only full-time and mid-to-high-level
managers and executives in the service industry. A total of 1,500 respondents were
contacted, and 264 complete responses were received. The response rate of 17.6% is
similar to other B2B studies using the Zoomerang panel (
Glenn Richey et al. , 2010 ). The
sample included 97 (37%) public and 167 (63%) private firms. Service industries included
were 70 (27%) health-care and social work industries; 50 (19%) finance, banking and
insurance; and 45 (17%) retail trade; remaining responses stemmed from the information,
transportation and telecommunication industries. The respondents’ job titles encompassed
144 (53%) managers and assistant managers, and 179 (53%) respondents worked for
small- and medium-sized companies that employed fewer than 500 individuals (
Allocca and
Kessler, 2006). In addition, 217 (82%) respondents worked at organizations with total sales
of less than US$100m. No significant statistical differences were observed regarding job
levels, total sales, number of employees or industries. No non-response bias was found
when comparing early and late respondents in the targeted demographics. Furthermore,
there were no missing data, as we used forced responses in a Web-based survey.

All the constructs in our research model ( Figure 1) have reflective items, where the causal
arrow points from the latent construct to the observed variables ( Jarvis et al. , 2003 ). The
constructs measured in this study include CSE, knowledge sharing, KC and innovation
speed. The operationalization is provided in Table 1 . The CSE construct was
conceptualized as a second-order construct with three first-order dimensions: motivation,
opportunity and ability, the items for which were adopted from Siemsen et al. (2008).T h e s e
items were measured on a five-point Likert scale (1 = “Strongly disagree,” 5 = “Strongly
agree”) and captured employees’ willingness, time availability and proficiency to share
knowledge within a supply chain.
Innovation speed measurement items were modified from Nassimbeni (2003) to fit the study
at hand. The innovation speed items captured the ability of a firm to develop new services –
or add new features to existing services – at a higher rate than its competitor. The items of
innovation speed used a five-point Likert scale to assess a firm’s position relative to its
competitors (1 = “Far behind,” 5 = “Far ahead”).
The knowledge channel items were adapted from Rosenzweig and Roth (2007) and
captured the boundary-spanning activities used in the supply chain to create, recombine
and transfer knowledge. The items for KC used a five-point Likert scale (1 = “Strongly
disagree,” 5 = “Strongly agree”).
The knowledge sharing scale was adapted from Siemsen et al. (2008) and captured the
efforts and attempts made by supply chain partners to share knowledge. The knowledge
sharing construct is measured on a five-point Likert scale (1 = “Strongly disagree,” 5 =
“Strongly agree”). See item/scale detail in Appendix.
4.3 Control variables
We included three control variables in our research model: number of employees, sales
and industry. Number of employees is a measure of firm size and allows partially
canceling out the effect of economies and dis-economies of scale. Sales as a control
allows controlling for differences in resources among firms. Finally, industry as a control
variable allows controlling for difference in innovation levels across different industries.
Some combination of these three control variables is commonly used in related
research ( Johnson and Sohi, 2017 ; Wang and Wang, 2012; Zimuto et al. , 2018 ;
Kownatzki et al. , 2013 ).

5.1 Convergent and discriminant validity
We assessed the convergent and discriminant validity of the measurement instrument by
using the procedure suggested by Hair et al. (2016) (see Table 2 ). The average variance
explained (AVE) for each of the constructs in our research model was above 0.5, thus
indicating convergent validity. Moreover, the square root of the AVE was larger than all inter-
construct correlations for all the scales, thus indicating discriminant validity. CFA analysis
was also used to assess the validity of the constructs. Item loadings for all the constructs
were highly significant, thus providing further evidence of convergent validity.
5.2 Hypotheses testing
The hypotheses test was performed through SEM by using AMOS 21 software. The overall
fit of the structural model was quite satisfactory ( x2 (df) = 247 (135), p /C20 0.001; CFI = 0.976;
RMSEA = 0.056; SRMR = 0.058), indicating the nomological validity of our hypothesized
model.
Our implicit hypothesis that CSE is a second-order construct with three first-order
dimensions – motivation, opportunity and ability – was supported. The overall fit indices of
the hypothesized second-order factor structure were excellent ( x2 (df) = 57 (24), p /C20 0.001;
CFI = 0.986; RMSEA = 0.073; SRMR = 0.034). The path loadings of the three first-order
factors – motivation, opportunity and ability – were 0.963 (p /C20 0.001), 0.251 (p /C20 0.001) and
0.840 (p /C20 0.001), respectively.
The direct effect hypotheses were evaluated by using the path loadings obtained through
the SEM analysis. H1, which states that CSE is positively related to knowledge sharing,
was supported ( b = /C0 0.144, p /C20 0.308). H2, which states that knowledge sharing is
positively related to innovation speed, was supported ( b = 0.673, p = 0.008). H3, which

While H3 was not supported, the first-order correlation between CSE and innovation speed
was highly significant ( r= 0.407, p /C20 0.001). This indicates a net suppression effect, where

cannibalizes the relationship between CSE and innovation speed, thus making it
insignificant.
We also evaluated the impact of three control variables (number of employees, sales and
industry) on the research model. The effects for all three control variables were not
significant. Also evaluated was the effect of an a priori marker variable borrowed from
leisure literature on the research model. The inclusion of the marker variable had no
significant effect on the results.

We find that CSE is positively related to knowledge-sharing behavior ( H1 is supported),
hence suggesting that when organizations provide the right conditions (additional time or
reduced workload), individuals may perceive that an organization values the exchange of
knowledge. In particular, motivation (0.963) and ability (0.840) appear to be critical to
knowledge sharing. Second, we find that knowledge sharing is positively related to
sustaining innovation speed in service contexts ( H2 is supported). For instance, through its
consulting business, International Business Machine Corporation builds better relationships
with its customers and improves their ability to innovate (
Papailiou et al. , 2007 ). Third, as
expected, we find an insignificant relationship between the CSE and innovation speed,
thereby implying that knowledge sharing entirely mediates this relationship. It appears that
capable and motivated individuals may fall short in either producing novel ideas or selling

can result in increased speed of innovation.
Our result for the interaction between KC and CSE can be interpreted in terms of limits on
rationality, which is consistent with the hypothesis that CSE increases the sharing of
knowledge. Innovation usually takes place in two stages, and the mere generation of a
novel and promising idea does not ensure its implementation and value generation. Even
highly motivated and capable individuals are subject to their own bounded rationality –
without the input of colleagues, an individual may not independently be able to sense
opportunities to innovate. Furthermore, and due to limited rationality, individual employees
may possibly perceive their own ideas as lacking value, or they may fear failure and resist
aggressively pushing their ideas up the ranks or horizontally to partners within the supply
chain. Accordingly, it makes sense that CSE increases knowledge sharing yet may not
necessarily increase the rate of innovation. For innovation speed to increase, organizations
must institutionalize strategies such as KC for boundary spanning and actively
communicate their vital role in remaining competitive.

Second, we introduce a second-order latent construct – the CSE – to the extant literature.
Weick et al. (2005) propose that a broad multidimensional construct with specific
dimensions such as CSE can be used as a platform for further theory building, as well as for
making sense of some of the perplexing and contradictory findings in prior research. Past
research has clearly established the role of knowledge sharing in buyer –supplier
relationships on innovation performance, with varying success. Some studies find that
supplier integration helps firms accelerate the development process of new products,
enhance their quality and reduce the costs of innovation (
Lau et al. ,2 0 1 0). Other studies

problems) and jeopardize firms’ valuable knowledge (
Wagner and Hoegl, 2006 ). Our CSE
construct sheds light on these contradictory findings that suffuse the extant literature.
Third, by introducing the idea of CSE and applying the MOA framework, we contribute by
exploring how individual-level variables relate to the sharing of knowledge and the
organizational competencies that encourage and facilitate this exchange of knowledge,
which, in turn, affect a firm’s speed of innovation. Prior research has not investigated how
employees’ motivation, opportunity and ability to share knowledge relate to knowledge
sharing attempts and innovation speed. Our findings suggest that information exchange in
buyer–supplier relationships is not resolved only by the readiness but also the capacity, for
social exchange. Past research has argued that tacit knowledge sharing could be difficult
without the assistance of clarification and description (
Hansen, 1999 ). A strong CSE in
buyer–supplier relationships thus plays a role in helping the receiving party to understand
and use tacit knowledge better, thereby simplifying the relationship and, hence, smoothing
the transfer of tacit knowledge that leads to faster innovation (
Reagans and McEvily, 2003 ;
Rowley et al.,2 0 0 0).
Finally, this study incorporates KC as an informal mechanism and investigates how it
interacts with CSE in terms of knowledge acquisition and innovation speed. Commonly,
manufacturers attempt to cut costs and enhance delivery performance by working with
suppliers (
Hoegl and Wagner, 2005 ). However, suppliers are increasingly regarded as
important sources of information during the innovation process ( Fliess and Becker, 2006 ),
therefore emphasizing the importance in this process of identifying the conditions under
which suppliers are most willing to exchange information with manufacturers.
6.2 Managerial implications
B2B firms should engage in knowledge sharing with their suppliers during the innovation
process. This study offers valuable implications for managers on how to collaborate with
their suppliers and develop effective communication and knowledge-sharing practices that
help firms develop innovations more rapidly. Our results suggest that, prior to launching
knowledge-sharing practices, managers should ensure the presence of motivation,
opportunity and ability among employees involved in the knowledge-sharing process.
Without the existence of these three elements of CSE, bilateral routines of knowledge
sharing can hardly be initiated. Strong CSE in buyer –supplier relationships helps buyers to
obtain and use fresh information and generates opportunities for them to improve their
products or services faster than those with weak CSE. With a high level of CSE, buying firms
are more capable of learning and experimenting with various alternatives, which can
enhance their ability to adapt effectively and promptly to market changes. Moreover, our

7. Limitations and future research
The interpretation of our findings should be considered alongside our study’s limitations.
The research at hand employed a subjective self-reported measure for innovation speed –
although we sampled knowledgeable executives, we cannot rule out the possibility of bias.
Future studies may wish to include objective measures for innovation speed, for example by
examining patent databases. A second limitation of this study is that we left aside aspects
of knowledge sharing that could conceivably contribute to rapid innovation; for example,
according to
Kessler and Bierly (2002) , quality of ideas is an important antecedent for
organizational innovation speed. Beyond this, our measure of CSE could be enriched by
including other kinds of opportunities. Due to considerations of the limited space available
here, we measured the availability of time. However, material and social capital resources
may be included as additional measures to enhance this construct in further research.
Knowledge management and organizational performance are rewarding and significant
topics for study, for motivating employees to share knowledge is essential if organizations
want to innovate and remain competitive. Our study proffers a small yet vital step in this
direction. Further, in the study we followed prior organizational research in selecting our
controls. Future research could consider controls at the individual as well as organizational
levels.
