ABSTRACT 
Nonprofit organizations have historically been seen as a 
vanguard in U.S. civil society, supplying places and spaces for innovation and change to flourish. According to the Competing 
Values Culture Framework (CVCF), a developmental organiza
-
tional culture, characterized by innovation and risk-taking, may 
help organizations respond to changes in their operating environments and be more effective at boundary-spanning 
activities, such as fostering external support, acquiring 
resources, and spurring growth. Despite its importance, the culture–
effectiveness relationship has not been studied suffi -
ciently in nonprofit organizations. The exploratory study 
described in this article examines the links between develop -
mental culture and nonprofit organizational effectiveness. 
Results suggest that nonprofit executive directors see organiza -
tional culture as more than a phenomenon to be experienced. Specifically, findings indicate that executive directors perceive there to be a positive and significant relationship between developmental culture and how effective their organization is at 
performing boundary-spanning activities. The practical and 
theoretical implications of this finding are discussed. KEYWORDS  
nonprofit management; 
nonprofit organizations; 
organizational effectiveness; 
organizational theory and behavior  
Nonprofit organizations have long been thought of as an integral part of the 
fabric undergirding civil society in American life. Often acting as agents of democracy, they encourage participation and act as stewards of the public interest. In addition, these organizations help to build the social capital that democratic societies rely on to function effectively. As such, understanding the factors influencing the effectiveness of nonprofits becomes an issue critical not only to theory building and program improvement, but also to democratic accountability and social change more broadly. Today, the operating environ
-
ments of nonprofit organizations are more complex than ever. Reductions in philanthropic donations, cuts in government spending, increased compe
-
tition, and an expanded need for human services have challenged nonprofits to search for new ways to respond to changing environmental demands (Young, Salamon, & Grinsfelder, 2012 ). 
Consequently, the historical role of 
nonprofits as innovators that pioneer unique approaches to the delivery of

One important factor thought to be associated with organizational

Dynamic and innovative cultures that 
help to build an organization’s adaptive capacity have also been linked to the long-term survival of nonprofit organizations, because they help them to meet environmental demands, take advantage of opportunities, and use resources effectively (Kanter & Summers, 1987). 
Though an organizational culture centered on flexibility, adaptability, and

present article were not able to identify any other empirical study in the nonprofit context that examined the links between culture and effectiveness. 
Given the historically important role of nonprofits as flexible organizations 
that are ready and able to act as innovators and entrepreneurs and the lack of empirical research examining the culture–effectiveness relationship in nonprofit organizations, the present article addresses this important question from the perspective of nonprofit executive directors: To what extent are developmental organizational cultures that reflect values of adaptability, innovation, and entrepreneurship associated with nonprofit effectiveness at fostering support, growth, and resource acquisition? A framework for understanding organizational culture 
Organizations are more than just structure. Academics and practitioners alike 
know that “much more goes on in the process of organizing than the charts, boxes, dotted lines, position descriptions and matrices can depict” (Waterman, Peters, & Phillips, 1980, p. 14). As sector blurring becomes the 
norm rather than the exception in all areas of organizational life, and conventional organizational forms are dismantled, nonmaterial cognitive structures such as organizational culture, that define what the organization stands for and where it intends to go, are of increasing importance (Albert, Ashforth, & Dutton, 2000). While there are many competing definitions 
and approaches to understanding organizational culture (Denison, 1996; 
Denison & Mishra, 1995; Taylor, 2014), most researchers agree that the 
construct can best be understood as a bounded set of shared values, beliefs, assumptions, and cognitions (Denison, 1996; Denison & Spreitzer, 1991; 
O’Reilly, Chatman, & Caldwell, 1991; Schein, 2010). These underlying beliefs 
and assumptions direct organizational behavior by indicating to members what attitudes and behaviors are appropriate and important (Schein, 2010). 
Unlike organizational activities, which can frequently change in response to environmental demands, organizational culture is seen as deeply embedded, stable, and enduring (Schein, 2010). For example, while an organization 
may decide to use social media as a way to connect with stakeholders, this activity change will not necessarily change the overall culture of the organization. As such, organizational culture provides organizations with a sense of identity and offers dynamic, open systems stability (Smircich, 1983). 
Today, the environments that nonprofit organizations exist within are 
inextricably linked to internal systems (Adams & Perlmutter, 1995). Conse
-
quently, like most organizations, nonprofits are viewed as open systems whose survival depends on their ability to attend to complex and diverse environ
-
mental demands (Miller & Friesen, 1983). 
Multiple changes to the operating 
environments of nonprofits mean that they must be willing and able to adapt and develop innovative capacities in order to survive (Eikenberry & Kluver, 2004; Jaskyte, 2004; Jaskyte & Dressler, 2005). Jaskyte (2004) suggests that 
nonprofit managers can help their organizations respond to these environmental demands effectively by recognizing the ways in which cultures of innovation influence organizational effectiveness (Jaskyte, 2004). A more PUBLIC PERFORMANCE & MANAGEMENT REVIEW
 459 robust understanding of the practical aspects of organizational culture may 
help nonprofit managers account for the role that culture plays in helping organizations cope with uncertainty and navigate difficult waters in times of environmental change. 
One popular framework that has been widely used by organizational 
theorists (Lumpkin & Dess, 2001; Miller & Friesen, 1983; Moynihan et al., 
2012; Pandey & Garnett, 2006; Pandey & Moynihan, 2006; Quinn & Spreitzer, 
1991) to examine the practical aspects of organizational culture, and the specific relationship between a developmental organizational culture and organizational effectiveness, is the Competing Values Culture Framework. The two main underlying assumptions of the CVCF are (a) that all organiza
-
tions can be characterized by common cultural traits, and (b) that these traits direct basic assumptions about organizational elements such as decision- making, compliance mechanisms, leadership, motivation, and effectiveness (Denison & Spreitzer, 1991 ; 
Kimberly & Quinn, 1984). Simply stated, the 
CVCF is a framework that managers and theorists can use to explore practical 
aspects of organizational culture, such as the relationship between organiza -
tional culture and organizational outcomes (e.g., effectiveness). 
The four culture types proposed as part of the CVCF (hierarchical, rational, 
group, and developmental) fall along two main dimensions that represent the 
competing values of change versus stability and an internal versus external organizational focus (Denison & Spreitzer, 1991 ). 
After a brief explanation 
of the core components of each culture type, developmental culture, the focus of this study, will be explicitly defined and explained. Figure 1 identifies the 
two main dimensions of the CVCF, and the factors that define an organiza
-
tion’s culture and guide the construction of its conceptualization of effectiveness. 
As highlighted by Figure 1 (for a full review, see Denison & Spreitzer, 1991 ; 
Kimberly 
& Quinn, 1984), group cultures are primarily concerned with human 
development. The primary characteristics of a group culture include loyalty, tradition, cohesion, and morale. Thus, its compliance mechanisms include affiliation and attachment between individuals or groups, and its effectiveness values include discussion, participation, and openness. In hierarchical 
cultures, which are characterized by formalization and structure, emphasis is placed upon internal consistency and uniformity, and thus compliance is rule-based. Effectiveness values include stability, control, and continuity. Rational cultures are characterized by a production orientation, and empha
-
size efficiency and output maximization. Effectiveness is measured by productivity, profit, and accomplishment. Therefore, compliance is contrac
-
tual and based on the maximization of output. 
The focus of this article is on the fourth culture type identified by the 
CVCF, developmental cultures , 
which are characterized by entrepreneurship, 
innovation, and risk-taking. Developmental cultures place a premium on 460
 LANGER & LEROUX transformation and change. The focus of the developmental culture is on how 
well the organization can manage its environment and meet new demands (Moynihan & Pandey, 2005). Organizational members comply with needs 
based on task importance and ideological appeal, and the primary needs of individuals are for stimulation, innovation, and variety. Thus, it is not rules, relationships, or contracts that characterize the motivations of individuals within organizations that display dominant developmental cultures (Moynihan & Pandey, 2005), but rather the “ideological appeal of the task 
being undertaken” (Zammuto & Krakower, 1991, p. 87). A developmental cul
-
ture will signal to members that appropriate behaviors and attitudes reflect values of innovation and entrepreneurship. Consequently, fostering external support, growth, and resource acquisition are the primary effectiveness criteria in organizations with developmental cultures. Simply stated, the CVCF posits that organizations with developmental cultures will determine how effective they are, in part, by their ability to grow and perform bound
-
ary-spanning activities, such as the gaining of adherents, support, or resources. 
Given that exploring the link between developmental culture and organiza -
tional effectiveness is the topic of interest, one might hypothesize, in applying the CVCF to nonprofit organizations, that developmental culture is associated with an organization’s ability to effectively perform activities such as influen
-
cing policy or government decisions, raising public awareness of its cause, or

Developmental culture and organizational effectiveness 
In general, effectiveness can be understood as the degree to which a desirable 
result is successfully achieved. However, despite this rather straightforward definition, consensus on what constitute the criteria of effectiveness in non
-
profit organizations, which must often balance competing social and financial demands, remains elusive (Sowa, Selden, & Sandfort, 2004 ). 
As such, prior to 
proceeding with a review of relevant theoretical and empirical work investi -
gating the links between developmental organizational cultures and effective -
ness, it is necessary to explain the scope of the terms’ use in the research reported in this article. 
While notions of effectiveness may vary across organizations and programs 
(Herman & Renz, 1999 ; 
Quinn & Rohrbaugh, 1983; Sowa et al., 2004), models 
highlighting generalizable dimensions of organizational effectiveness can be used to further an understanding of the construct and its determinants. The CVCF is a model that highlights generalizable dimensions of organiza
-
tional effectiveness from a cultural perspective. The understanding of effectiveness in this article is bound by using the CVCF. This means that the conceptualization of organizational effectiveness is predefined and very specific. According to the CVCF, effectiveness in organizations with develop
-
mental cultures will, in part, be judged by the degree to which the organiza -
tion can foster support, grow, and acquire new resources. This is not to say that these are the only desired results in organizations with developmental cultures or that other culture types will not influence how effective an organi
-
zation might be at boundary-spanning activities. It simply means that when developmental cultures are dominant, these boundary-spanning criteria may be more salient when judging organizational effectiveness. 
Organizational effectiveness can be defined in many ways, but for the 
purposes of the present research organizational effectiveness is bound by the CVCF and refers only to effectiveness at fostering external support, growth, and resource acquisition, here collectively termed “boundary- spanning” activities. More specifically, boundary-spanning can be understood as externally oriented organizational activities that “link organizational structure to environmental elements whether by buffering, moderating or 462
 LANGER & LEROUX influencing the environment” (Aldrich & Herker, 1977, p. 218). For example, 
the boundary-spanning activities of a government organization might include 
building interdependencies with other governments, citizens, and public and private organizations in order to pursue public purposes (LeRoux, 2014). In 
nonprofit organizations, boundary-spanning activities might include actions geared toward representing the organization to external constituencies or developing relationships that increase the flow of resources, broadly construed (Miller-Millesen, 2003). 
Before proceeding with a review of the literature, it should be noted that 
some executive directors are required to balance many competing cultures that may signal the importance of diverse measures of effectiveness. The research presented here is exploratory in that it simply takes a first step at examining one specific and important relationship proposed by the CVCF, the link between developmental culture and effectiveness in a nonprofit con
-
text. While much has been learned about this specific relationship in public (Moynihan et al., 2012 ; 
Pandey & Garnett, 2006; Pandey & Moynihan, 
2006) and for-profit organizations (Lumpkin & Dess, 2001; Miller & Friesen, 
1983), it has not yet been addressed in the nonprofit literature. The theoretical and empirical evidence linking developmental organizational cultures to organizational effectiveness will now be examined, taking a comprehensive view and drawing from literature that spans the for-profit, public, and nonprofit sectors. 
The primacy of developmental culture in for-profit organizations and its 
links to organizational effectiveness have been relatively well established theoretically and empirically (Covin & Slevin, 1991; Ireland, Hitt, & Sirmon, 
2003; Morris, Coombes, Schindehutte, & Allen, 2007). “A central precept of 
the for-profit literature is that environmental threats force firms to become more strategic, adaptable and innovative” (Morris et al., 2007, p. 13) in order 
to survive and obtain a competitive advantage in the marketplace. Overall the adoption of a developmental culture is seen as a strategy to stimulate growth, enhance economic performance, and foster an increased sense of legitimacy among stakeholders (Covin & Slevin, 1991). Empirical studies consistently 
link developmental culture to performance and effectiveness in for-profit organizations. Hardcopf and Shah (2014) find that performance quality and flexibility in lean manufacturing firms is dependent on the organization’s having a developmental culture. Miller and Friesen (1983) find that an organization’s ability to manage system influences is more effective when innovation is a key component of the organization’s culture. Further, Lumpkin and Dess (2001) find a positive link between proactiveness, a key tenet of developmental cultures, and performance of for-profit firms. While the introduction of developmental cultures in public organizations is a newer phenomenon, similar associations to those in for-profit organizations have been found. newer trend that reached a tipping point during the reinventing government 
movement (Kamarck, 2013). One charge of the reinvention movement was to 
fundamentally alter the culture of government organizations by replacing hierarchical cultures with cultures of innovation and entrepreneurship (Kamarck, 2013; Posner & Rothstein, 1994). It is difficult to determine what 
the long-term effects of a fundamental culture shift will mean for democracy and the structure of government organizations in the United States, but empirical evidence shows that the adoption of developmental cultures is asso
-
ciated with positive organizational-level outcomes. Pandey and Moynihan (2006
) find that developmental cultures mitigate the negative effect of 
human-resources red tape on mission effectiveness. Developmental culture was also found to be an antecedent of organizational performance (Park, Lee, & Kim, 2016) and performance information use (Moynihan & Pandey, 
2010), and, further, to mediate the relationship between transformational leadership and performance information use in government organizations 
(Moynihan et al., 2012). In addition, enhanced interpersonal communication 
in the workplace (Pandey & Garnett, 2006), executive well-being (Quinn & 
Spreitzer, 1991), trust, morale, and leadership credibility (Cameron & 
Freeman, 1991) are all believed to be positively related to the adoption of a 
developmental culture by public organizations. 
Despite the links between developmental culture and effectiveness in 
for-profit and government organizations, and the historical importance of 
nonprofit organizations as the vanguard and incubator of experimentation, innovation, and transformation, the link between developmental cultures and organizational effectiveness has not been examined in the nonprofit con
-
text. When culture is addressed in nonprofit studies, with but a few exceptions (Jaskyte, 2004 ; 
Jaskyte & Dressler, 2005), it is often incorporated as part of lar -
ger studies on organizational characteristics or behavior (Allen, Smith, & Da Silva, 2013 ; 
Kim, 2016) rather than as the key construct of interest. The 
present discussion draws both on insights from entrepreneurship theories of nonprofit existence and on theories of organizational innovation to exam
-
ine the link between organizational culture and effectiveness in the nonprofit context. This literature is then tied to the propositions outlined by the CVCF to assess the generalizable dimensions of nonprofit organizational effective
-
ness from a cultural perspective. 
Entrepreneurship theories explain the existence of nonprofit organizations 
from a supply-side perspective as entities engaged in continuous innovation, focused on creating social value through the pursuit of new opportunities (Anheier, 2005 ). 
These theories recognize that the primary interest of a non -
profit may not be in profit or the simple provision of services, but rather the achievement of some other ultimate objective (Anheier, 2005 ). 
To achieve this 
ultimate objective, the nonprofit must be able to acquire both financial 464
 LANGER & LEROUX resources and believers, adherents, or members who further the larger aims of 
the organization (James, 1987, 1989; Rose-Ackerman, 1996). Similarly, in 
organizations with developmental cultures (see Figure 1), an organization is 
judged effective according to whether or not it can acquire the support and resources it needs in order achieve its ultimate objective of transformation or change. Simply stated, entrepreneurial theories of nonprofit organizations portray them as inherently developmental from the perspective of the CVCF. However, it should be noted that developmental cultures embody much more than the notion of entrepreneurialism. For its part, entrepreneurialism is sim
-
ply one facet or activity that might be deemed acceptable by the members of an organization with a developmental culture. 
It is clear that nonprofits today are engaging in entrepreneurial activities, 
such as adopting fee-for-service structures (Chetkovich & Frumkin, 2003 ), 
launching 
earned-income and commercialization strategies (Eikenberry & 
Kluver, 2004; Foster & Bradach, 2005), entering into shared-value initiatives 
(Porter & Kramer, 2011), and forming cause-related marketing alliances 
(Liu & Ko, 2011). What these activities do not tell us is whether they have 
a meaning for members that manifests in deeper cultural structures that drive how the organization judges its own effectiveness. Whether entrepreneurial practices such as these will have a positive or negative overall effect on the spirit and values of the nonprofit sector is an area of debate among both scholars and practitioners (Eikenberry, 2009; Eikenberry & Kluver, 2004). 
However, preliminary findings have linked similar yet distinct constructs such as social enterprise and entrepreneurial orientation to positive organizational outcomes. Specifically, research suggests that entrepreneurial nonprofits perform better on financial measures of effectiveness, receive greater social recognition, and improve managerial efficiency, social performance, and customer satisfaction (Barrett, Balloun, & Weinstein, 2005; Coombes, Morris, 
Allen, & Webb, 2011; Felicio, Gonçalves, & Conceição Gonçalves, 2013; Liu, 
Takeda, & Ko, 2012; Shoham, Ruvio, Vigoda-Gadot, & Schwabsky, 2006). 
In addition to entrepreneurialism, developmental cultures also highlight 
the importance of adaptability and innovation for effectively managing the organization’s operating environment (Denison & Spreitzer, 1991). The 
nonprofit sector has been characterized as inherently innovative and as “a laboratory for experimentation and risk-taking” (Frumkin, 2002, p. 32) 
Further, scholars have stressed the important role of the nonprofit sector as an experimenter and innovator responsible for developing pioneering approaches to service delivery (Gummer, 1989). Drucker (1990) contends 
that innovation may be more important for nonprofit organizations than for business organizations because of the great challenges and opportunities that today’s rapidly changing society places upon nonprofits; and Hasenfeld (1983) suggests that the capacity to innovate is a key factor in determining the long-term survival of nonprofit human service organizations. In a case PUBLIC PERFORMANCE & MANAGEMENT REVIEW

tally disabled, Grabowski, Neher, Crim, and Mathiassen ( 2014
) found that 
the organization was able to survive and thrive over its 50-year history by 
innovating and adapting to its environment, through the implementation either of structural changes, service changes, or management changes. This flexibility and readiness to adapt and grow is central to developmental organizational cultures, as outlined by the CVCF. 
Taken together, theories of entrepreneurship and innovation suggest that 
many nonprofits exist to fulfill entrepreneurial roles in civil society. Further, those organizations with developmental organizational cultures that stress the values of innovation may have a leg up when it comes to effectively managing their environment (Jaskyte, 2004). As organizations continue to manage an 
evolutionary phase characterized by the transformation of conventional orga
-
nizational forms, nonprofits must often draw upon all available resources, both traditional and nontraditional, in order to capitalize upon social value creation opportunities (Morris, Webb, & Franklin, 2011 ). 
Today, whether 
intentionally or unintentionally, nonprofits are increasingly reflecting upon the role that developmental organizational cultures play in helping to capitalize on such opportunities and foster effectiveness. 
As is evidenced by the theoretical and empirical links between entrepre
-
neurialism and innovation in nonprofits and an organization’s ability to grow and acquire new resources, when organizations increasingly emphasize developmental values, effectiveness measures associated with developmental cultures are likely become more important. Consequently, in line with theories that suggest nonprofit organizations have a proclivity toward entre
-
preneurial activity, and the links posited between innovation and effectiveness in nonprofit organizations, the following hypothesis is proposed, based on the competing-values culture framework: 
H1:  A positive association will exist between nonprofit organizations 
with developmental cultures and managerial perceptions of effectiveness at 
boundary-spanning activities.

Findings and discussion 
Insight drawn from the CVCF suggests that effectiveness values in organiza -
tions emphasizing a developmental culture will center on fostering external 
support, growth, and acquiring resources in order to affect change. The purpose of the study was to determine whether these contentions hold in a nonprofit setting. In line with the CVCF, it examined, from the perspective of the executive director, whether a developmental organizational culture is associated with how effective the nonprofit is at performing boundary- spanning activities. The results of the ordinary least squares model, presented in Table 3,

This finding in itself expands our understanding of the CVCF and provides empirical support for the framework’s applicability in a nonprofit 
setting. Yet, perhaps more importantly, what this finding may indicate is that 
nonprofit executive directors see organizational culture as something more than a phenomenological construct that is only to be experienced. Rather, perceiving there to be a significant link between developmental culture and organizational effectiveness at boundary-spanning activities suggests that executive directors, in this instance, may also see view organizational culture as a practical element of organizational life that helps to shape and promote specific types of organizational outcomes. As such, executive directors who examine their own organization’s culture–effectiveness relationship may be able to gain valuable insight into what behaviors the organization’s beliefs and values signal as appropriate to members, and in turn whether or not these behaviors help the organization to achieve the ends it deems effective.

2012), thus helping the organization to be more effective at activities seeking to attract additional external support and resources. Lastly, to the extent that the composite measure of effectiveness captures advocacy and engagement activities of nonprofits, this finding also provides support for earlier research which finds an increase in government funding to be positively and signifi

Conclusion 
Before discussing the implications of the findings, it is important to note the 
limitations of this study. First, the analysis relies on cross-sectional survey data, which enables an exploration of the associations between variables, but limits the ability to make causal claims. It is important to note that there is no assertion of causation here, because the study is simply an exploratory analysis that looks at the association between developmental culture and effec
-
tiveness at boundary-spanning activities in the nonprofit context. Second and perhaps most obvious, while it is important to study constructs like culture and effectiveness, which are partially socially constructed, from the viewpoint of the executive director, it is acknowledged that the analysis is subject to the challenges that come with data that rely on the self-reporting of perceptions. Future work can improve upon this study by examining these relationships PUBLIC PERFORMANCE & MANAGEMENT REVIEW
 471 using data that are not fully reliant on respondent perceptions. Third, while 
the focus is solely on examining the relationship between the existence of developmental organizational cultures in nonprofits and effectiveness at boundary-spanning activities, some organizations may embody multiple cultures (Quinn & Kimberly, 1984, p. 300), and different cultures might influ
-
ence their effectiveness. Future studies examining the links between organiza -
tional culture and effectiveness could build upon this exploratory study by investigating the associations between other culture types and salient effectiveness criteria, and by examining the ways in which organizations balance competing cultures and how competing cultures influence effective
-
ness criteria. Further, while the CVCF provides a parsimonious way to investigate the culture–effectiveness relationship, one might ask whether or not developmental cultures are associated with other measures of effective
-
ness, such as organizational stability or profit/impact. In other words, can the framework itself be improved upon? Despite the limitations, the study presented in this article takes an important initial step at testing the CVCF 
and the links between developmental culture and culturally bound effec
-
tiveness values in nonprofit organizations in order to better understand 
managerial perceptions of the links between organizational culture and effectiveness. 
Overall, the study reveals that executive directors perceive a positive and 
significant link between developmental culture and effectiveness at bound
-
ary-spanning activities. Entrepreneurial theories of nonprofit organizations contend that one of the primary reasons for the existence of nonprofit organizations is to act as a space for the innovative pursuit of ideological transformation or change (Rose-Ackerman, 1996 ). 
In part, ideological trans -
formation and change are thought to be achieved when nonprofits are able to maximize both monetary and nonmonetary returns, such as gaining believers, adherents, or members to their cause (James, 1987 , 1989
; Rose-Ackerman, 
1996). Developmental cultures, as outlined by the CVCF, support these contentions and link them with an organization’s ability to foster external support, grow, and acquire resources. To the extent that effectiveness at increasing organizational funding, influencing policy and government decisions, raising public awareness of the organization’s, cause and communi
-
cating with stakeholders through social media represents the ability to acquire resources, grow, and cultivate external support by gaining adherents or believ
-
ers, the findings support both the entrepreneurial theories of nonprofits and the propositions set forth by the CVCF. Further, to the extent that these boundary-spanning activities help an organization to take advantage of opportunities and resources in the environment, this study also supports theories of innovation and helps us to understand how executive directors view organizational culture and its important role in helping to manage environmental demands and promote organizational effectiveness. 472
 LANGER & LEROUX Long recognized for the vanguard role that they play in U.S. civil society, 
nonprofit organizations act as places and spaces that supply the innovative 
means necessary for addressing complex social problems (Anheier, 2005; 
James, 1987, 1989; Rose-Ackerman, 1996). Jaskyte (2004) posits that nonprofit 
organizations that fulfill this vanguard role and develop a culture of inno -
vation will be more responsive to changes in their external environment and thus will become more effective. In recent years, the environment in which many nonprofits operate has become increasingly turbulent due to increased demands for services, shifts in institutional structures, and a changing funding landscape, perhaps highlighting the importance of develop
-
mental cultures in a nonprofit setting. However, the complexity of nonprofit organizations and the often-competing demands of their operational elements, such as mission, money, clients, and funders, have given rise to institutional structures that often must manage diverse cultures. This means that, while the study confirms that a developmental culture may help nonpro
-
fits to be effective in many respects, such as fostering support and resource 
acquisition, it is also important to recognize and balance developmental 
cultures with other culture types in order to meet the varied expectations of diverse stakeholder groups. This research takes an important first step at investigating these important facets of organizational life.

Description/coding 
Dependent variable 
Effectiveness at performing 
boundary-spanning activities that foster external support, growth, and resource acquisition Composite scale comprised of four items asking executive director 
“How would you describe the effectiveness of your organization with regard to” (1) influencing policy or government decisions (local, state or national) that pertain to the organization’s mission, (2) raising public awareness of your cause, (3) communicating with 
stakeholders through social media, and (4) increasing the 
organization’s funding. Item was measured on a 6-point scale, with 1 = not at all effective, 2 = marginally effective, 3 = somewhat effective, 
4 = largely effective, 5 = completely effective. 
Independent variable Developmental culture Summed index of five survey items. Executive directors were asked to 
indicate their agreement with the statements (1) My organization is a 
dynamic and entrepreneurial place. (2) Staff are encouraged to stick 
their necks out and take risks. (3) The glue that holds my organization together is a commitment to innovation and development; there is an emphasis on being best. (4) My 
organization places a premium on growth and acquiring new 
resources. (5) Readiness to meet new challenges is of utmost importance in my organization. The original items were measured on a 5-point scale, with 1 = strongly disagree, 2 = somewhat disagree, 
3 = neutral, 4 = somewhat agree, and 5 = strongly agree. Measures 
were adapted from Cameron & Freeman (1991). 
Control variables Executive–board relationship: 
Gap between actual board involvement and executive director’s preferred level of 
board involvement in 
mission-setting and oversight Created from two sets of survey items. The first captured executive 
director’s preferred level of board involvement in: determining purpose and services of the organization, identifying strategies for future development, budget process, setting long-term financial 
goals, developing annual program/service goals, evaluating 
accomplishments. Next, executive directors were asked to rate their board’s actual involvement in each of these tasks. All items were measured on a 5-point scale, with 1 = none, 2 = low, 3 = some, 
4 = somewhat high, 5 = high. The difference between executive 
directors’ preferred vs. actual board involvement was calculated for 
each of the items, and these differences summed to create the dependent variable.
