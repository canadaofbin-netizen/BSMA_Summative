In an increasingly global research landscape, it is impor-
tant to identify the most proliﬁc researchers in variousinstitutions and their inﬂuence on the diffusion of knowl-edge. Knowledge diffusion within institutions is inﬂu-enced by not just the status of individual researchersbut also the collaborative culture that determines sta-tus. There are various methods to measure individualstatus, but few studies have compared them or exploredthe possible effects of different cultures on the statusmeasures. In this article, we examine knowledge diffu-sion within science and technology-oriented researchorganizations. Using social network analysis metricsto measure individual status in large-scale coauthor-ship networks, we studied an individual’s impact on therecombination of knowledge to produce innovation innanotechnology. Data from the most productive and high-impact institutions in China (Chinese Academy of Sci-ences), Russia (Russian Academy of Sciences), and India(Indian Institutes of Technology) were used. We foundthat boundary-spanning individuals inﬂuenced knowl-edge diffusion in all countries. However, our results alsoindicate that cultural and institutional differences mayinﬂuence knowledge diffusion.

Scientiﬁc publications are important indicators of inno-
vative research, which has been deﬁned as discoveringsolutions to problems via the novel recombination of exist-ing knowledge (Fleming, 2001). The diffusion of existingknowledge can be traced by this recombinatory process andthe factors that inﬂuence it. Researchers have a choice inselecting knowledge that is recombined to produce newinnovations, and studies have suggested that this choice isinﬂuenced by the status of individuals within knowledge net-works (Nerkar & Paruchuri, 2005; Singh, 2007). Individualsusually value and recombine innovations from high-statusresearchers.
Previous research has used various social network anal-
ysis (SNA) measures to establish the status of individualsand examine their role on knowledge diffusion. Patterns ofcollaboration at various departmental, organizational, andnational granularities also have been studied. However, therehas been limited comparative examination of social networkmeasures to establish individual status. In addition, there hasbeen little research on whether cultural differences inﬂuenceindividual status and the recombinatory knowledge-diffusionprocess. We believe that in an increasingly competitive globalresearch landscape, identifying the most proliﬁc individualsin various countries and disciplines based on the mosteffective measures is important.

Literature Review
In this section, we discuss important previous research
on coauthorship-based knowledge networks. We character-ize knowledge networks as social networks with authors asnodes and coauthorship collaborations as links between them.
A summary of the social network measures to determine
individual status also is presented.
Knowledge Networks
Since the ﬁrst collaborative scientiﬁc paper was published,
coauthorship has been increasing steadily over time across allﬁelds. Innovation by an individual in a knowledge network isgreatly inﬂuenced by his or her collaboration and interactionpatterns. To understand the diffusion of knowledge in thesenetworks, it is necessary to identify the factors leading to theselection of a “focal innovation.” Podolny and Stuart (1995)deﬁned the focal innovation as the knowledge that othersbuild upon to produce novel outcomes. Figure 1 shows theprocess of evolution of knowledge (time increasing on the x-
axis) and the emergence of a focal innovation. An arrow fromone innovation to the next indicates that the second innova-
tion built upon the ﬁrst. This process of knowledge diffusioncan be traced using citations in research literature. Indeed,citation analysis has been widely used in coauthorship net-works to study various network and diffusion phenomena(Bornmann & Daniel, 2008; Davis, 2009; Leydesdorff & Sun,2009).
Knowledge networks can be studied at different levels of
granularity, with emphasis on individual, within-group, orintergroup collaboration. Previous studies have examined thepatterns of collaboration between individual authors in vari-ous disciplines (Kretschmer, 2004; Moody, 2004; Newman,2001; Newman, 2004; Yoshikane & Kageura, 2004). Otherresearch also has focused on within-group collaboration(Arunachalam & Doss, 2000; Arunachalam, Srinivasan, &Raman, 1994; Luukkonen, Persson, & Sivertsen, 1992; Zitt,Bassecoulard, & Okubo, 2000) and intergroup collabora-tion (Leydesdorff & Sun, 2009; Singh, 2007; Wagner &Leydesdorff, 2005; Yamashita & Okubo, 2006; Zucker &Darby, 1996).
Though there is a difference in granularity, the primary
research focus of previous studies are twofold: (a) networkstructure, which analyzes the topological structure, its evo-lution, and the effect of global measures on knowledgegeneration, diffusion, and recombination within or acrossthe organizations; and (b) individual status, which analyzestrends or patterns in an individual’s collaborative activity andlinks to determine status. Social Network Measures
Various measures to identify high-status nodes have been
proposed in prior work (Bonacich, 1987; Burt, 1992; Free-man, 1979; Newman, 2005). These measures are usuallyknown as centrality orprestige measures and can be broadly
classiﬁed into three categories based on their methods of cal-culation: degree centrality, closeness centrality, and between-ness centrality. The degree centrality measures refer to thoseemphasizing the immediate ties of a node and its neighborsand include the Freeman’s degree centrality (Freeman, 1979),Bonacich power (Bonacich, 1987), and structural holes (Burt,1992), among others. Closeness centrality (Freeman, 1979)emphasizes the distance of a node to all others in the network.Betweenness centrality views a node as being in a favoredposition to the extent that it falls on paths between otherpairs of nodes in the network. Primary betweenness cen-trality measures include Freeman’s betweenness centrality(Freeman, 1979) and the more recent random-walk between-ness centrality (Newman, 2005). Each of these measuresmakes different assumptions about the ﬂow of knowledge ina network. For instance, degree-based measures emphasizedirect links, closeness-based measures are based on the speedof transfer, and betweenness measures emphasize access todiverse parts of the network. Thus, picking the right mea-sure to determine individual status becomes paramount, andcomparison becomes important.Previous studies have analyzed the effect of individual
status and knowledge diffusion based on two theories:
Centrality and its impact on knowledge diffusion: Centrality
is usually regarded as a signal of a high level of collaborationwithin the network. Previous empirical studies have indicatedthat an individual’s centrality measured by degree centralityhas a positive effect on the diffusion of his or her knowledge(Patrakosol & Olson, 2007; Podolny & Stuart, 1995). In addi-tion, separate studies have shown that Bonacich power andbetweenness centrality also had a positive effect (Nerkar &Paruchuri, 2005; Newman, 2001).
Boundary spanning and access to diverse knowledge: Indi-
viduals who perform boundary-spanning roles are consideredinﬂuential as gatekeepers to diverse knowledge bases. Pre-vious empirical studies have provided mixed results: Nerkarand Paruchuri (2005) used the structural holes measure todetermine centrality and found that a higher centrality hasa positive effect on knowledge diffusion; in another studyon structural holes, it was found to have negative effect oninnovation (Ahuja, 2000).
However, none of the previous studies have compared
these measures to determine which is more effective. Inaddition, coauthorship networks have not been studied tosee the effect (if any) of organizational or cultural environ-ments on the determination of individual status. To addressthese concerns, we implemented degree centrality (Freeman,1979), structural holes (Burt, 1992), and betweenness central-ity (Freeman, 1979) to model knowledge diffusion patternsin coauthorship networks based on their use in previous lit-erature. In addition, random walk betweenness (Newman,2005) also is included since this measure is largely differentin basis because it assumes random knowledge diffusion andhas rarely been used to study knowledge networks.

Data Acquisition and Research Testbed
We selected Thomson Reuters’ Science Citation Index
(SCI) as our database because it provides broad coverage
of journals in more than 150 disciplines, which can be usedto extract coauthorship networks and citation information.
We narrowed our focus to nanotechnology-related literature,
as it promises to impact various engineering and computingdisciplines and forms an ideal testbed for a large-scale studyon global research outputs. The keyword-searching methodwas used to collect nanotechnology papers (for more details,see Huang, Chen, Chen, & Roco, 2004). Since SCI papers
are in XML format, we developed parsers to extract relevantauthorship and citation data. Our ﬁnal testbed spans from1976 to 2004, with a total of 213,847 papers published by231,561 individuals.
We focused on China, Russia, and India because these
countries are active in paper publications and are havingan increasing impact on global research, especially in nan-otechnology (Huang et al., 2004; Li et al., 2008; Liu et al.,2009). China’s nanotechnology R&D investment is estimatedto have been about 250 million U.S. dollars in 2008 (Guan &Ma, 2007). In Russia, it has been reported that approximately20 to 25 million U.S. dollars in funding have been investedin nanotechnology research annually since 2002 (Tretyakov,2007). In India, the government planned to spend 23 millionU.S. dollars on nanotechnology between 2004 and 2009, andthe investments’new research programs and centers increasedsigniﬁcantly after 2007 (Hassan, 2005).
SCI papers also serve as a good testbed to study the effect
of different collaborative cultures and work environments onknowledge diffusion. Since we focus on the status of individ-uals within organizations, we selected the Chinese Academyof Sciences (CAS), the Russian Academy of Sciences (RAS),and the Indian Institutes of Technology (IIT) as our test group.
These were picked because they are the most prestigious insti-
tutions within the target countries and are the most productivein nanotechnology research (Liu et al., 2009). Table 2 showsthe basic statistics of publications in nanotechnology for theseorganizations.
Network Extraction
A knowledge network based on coauthorships was
extracted for authors from all three institutions. The networkswere constructed such that each node is an author, and twoauthors are linked to each other if they have coauthored apaper. Research has shown that such an observed collabora-
tion marks the beginning of a strong tie that generally lastsbeyond the collaboration date. Uniquely determining authoridentity posed a challenge, as the SCI database uses only
the last name and the ﬁrst initial to identify authors. Thisproved to be insufﬁcient to disambiguate most authors fromChina. Thus, we used names, afﬁliations, addresses, and e-mail addresses to identify authors. Two static snapshots ofthe coauthorship networks in CAS are shown in Figure 3. Ascan be seen, the number of authors in the connected com-ponents increased signiﬁcantly from 1995 ( n=152) to 2004
(n=3,114).
Network Measurement
The following social network measures were used to
determine the status of authors in the network:
•Betweenness centrality (BC): A node has a high betweenness
centrality if a large number of shortest paths between othernodes pass through it.
•Degree centrality (DC): This is based on the intuition that
actors who have more direct ties than do other actors may bein advantaged positions.
•Structural holes: Structural holes measures identify boundary-
spanning individuals. There are two different approaches tocalculating structural holes, constrained based (CONS) andredundancy- or efﬁciency-based (EFF).
•CONS is a summary measure that determines the extent to
which a node’s connections are to others who are connected toeach other. EFF for a node measures the proportion of its linksthat are nonredundant. We implement both these approaches.
•Random walk betweenness (RW): This measure is similar to
Freeman’s betweenness centrality; however, it includes con-tributions from all paths between nodes (not just the shortestpaths). We included this measure because intuitively, thismeasure may ﬁt the real knowledge diffusion process incoauthorship networks.

The three kinds of variables in our study are discussed in
the following sub-sections and summarized in Table 3.
Dependent variable. Paper citation was used as the depen-
dent variable because it is considered an excellent measureof technological impact as well as knowledge ﬂow (Ahuja,2000; Nerkar & Paruchuri, 2005; Podolny & Stuart, 1995).
To include time into the analysis, the life of a paper (from
publication year until the end of the dataset) was divided intotime periods called spells (Nerkar & Paruchuri, 2005). The
strategy of dividing time into spells effectively measures theeffect of network measures on the citation of a patent throughits entire life. Each time period was used as a data point todetermine the effect of explanatory and control variables onthe dependent variable in that spell. In line with prior research(Nerkar & Paruchuri, 2005), spells of a maximum of 1 yearwere created for each paper. The ﬁrst spell begins at the pub-
lication year and ends either with a citation or no citation. Wecalculated the network measures and time-varying covariatesfor each paper in each spell based on the network of authorsin the prior calendar years. This is important because if thesocial network measures are calculated only once (for a singlesnapshot of the knowledge network), then it will not accu-rately reﬂect the author’s status over time. The status of anauthor might change as the size and density of the networkchange.

Explanatory variables. Each of the four social network

Control variables. Control variables are the factors (other

Two kindsof control variables were included based on previous studies(Nerkar & Paruchuri, 2005; Podolny & Stuart, 1995): vari-ables effecting citation rates and variables describing paperattributes. The variables effecting citation rates included thecalendar age , which controls for the improvement in search
technology since the start of the dataset. Information retrievaland search technology have improved dramatically since1976, and papers are easier to ﬁnd and cite. Another time-variant control included is the paper age . This was included
since an older paper may be cited more than are newer papers.

the technological spaces it occupies.

Results also showed that thepresence of self-citation and being published in top journalspositively affect the citation of the paper. The signiﬁcance ofthese control variables generally persisted across all models.
Model 1 adds Freeman’s BC with all the control variables.
As can be seen from Table 5, the BC measure of the author

Using different social network mea-sures, we identiﬁed the mechanism through which knowledgeis selected for recombination. Speciﬁcally, we asked howthe status of an individual in a coauthorship network affectsknowledge diffusion. To answer this, we used Cox propor-tional hazards models to examine the role of author statuson the selection of knowledge that is recombined to pro-duce innovation within an organization. The empirical resultsshowed that individuals with access to diverse parts of theknowledge network were inﬂuential in all three institutions.
Our second research question focused on which measures
could explain the effect of an individual’s status on knowl-edge diffusion. Since Cox proportional hazards models canbe used to compare the effect of a set of variables on afuture event, by measuring individual status in coauthorshipnetworks with different social network measures (BC, DC,structural holes, RW betweenness), we can identify the mosteffective measure to determine author status for a speciﬁcinstitution. For example, our empirical study showed thatfor the CAS in China, structural holes (as measured by theefﬁciency measure) best explained the status of authors inthe knowledge diffusion process in China. Using the mostsuited measures in each institution, we also identiﬁed themost important authors in the nanotechnology ﬁeld in China,Russia, and India.
There are some limitations to this study. Although we
found that cultural and institutional differences may resultfrom the varying knowledge diffusion mechanisms withininstitutions, a further sociological study to understand whichcultural factors might inﬂuence diffusion is warranted. Thus,comparing institutional culture and incentives in these coun-tries and its effect on network structure (and the selectionof knowledge) might be an interesting future direction topursue. We also plan to compare these results to organiza-tions in the United States and European nations to examineany differences.
