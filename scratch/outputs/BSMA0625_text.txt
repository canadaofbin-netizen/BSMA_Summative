Following Edmondson (1999) and Argote et al. (2001),thisstudyadoptsaprocessapproachtoexamininggrouplearning, that is, learning activities such as dialogue(Senge 1990), group reﬂection (Edmondson 1999), andbrainstorming(SuttonandHargadon1996).Locallearn-ing is deﬁned as the interpersonal knowledge acquisi-tion, sharing, and combination activities with members

SimilartoCyertandMarch’s(1963)distinctionoflocaland distant search where the type of search dependson how close or far the search for solutions is fromthe vicinity of the problem, local and distal learningcharacterizewhethergroupmembersengageinlearningactivities with local individuals (i.e., within the groupmembership boundary) or distant individuals (i.e., out-sidethegroupmembershipboundary).

Similarly, the diverseknowledgeacquiredasaresultofdistallearningislikelytotriggerdivergentthinkingand,thus,facilitatethecre-ationofnovelknowledge(e.g.,newideasandsolutions).
Hypothesis 1a. Distal learning is positively related
to group innovativeness.
Unlike the differentiation-enhancing nature of dis-
tal learning, local learning is likely to be coherencebuilding—creatingandmaintainingsharedgroupknowl-edge (i.e., knowledge about tasks, group processes,groupmembers)thatpromotesgreatergroupefﬁciency.Forexample,greaterlearningwithintheteamcancreategreatersharedunderstandingthatalignscollectiveaction(Fiol1994,Senge1990),builda“communitymemory”of task knowledge that facilitates speedy diagnosis andresolutionofproblems(Orr1990),anddevelopa“trans-activememorysystem”orsharedknowledgeabouteachother’s expertise that enhances more efﬁcient coordi-nation of members’ expertise (Liang et al. 1995). Bydevelopingsharedgroupknowledgethatimprovescoor-dination and reduces misalignments, local learning islikely to enhance a group’s ability to execute its taskson schedule and within budget. Although the combi-nation of different members’ knowledge in local learn-ing creates the potential for novel understanding to bediscovered, the similar task and team mental modelsof individuals within the same group suggest that any“new” understanding that is produced is likely to be intheneighborhoodofthecurrentsolution.Consequently,local learning is likely to deepen and develop greatersharedunderstandingaboutcurrenttasksandhowtasksarecurrentlyexecutedinthegroup.
Hypothesis 1b. Local learning is positively related
to group efﬁciency.
In the case of group innovativeness, I hypothesize
that distal and local learning complement one anotherto enhance group innovativeness (i.e., a positive inter-action effect). As a group engages in a greater level ofdistal learning, more divergent thinking and combina-torial possibilities are likely, thereby leading to highergroupinnovativeness(e.g.,Hargadon1999).However,asSuttonandHargadon’s(1996)casestudyshowed,groupmembers also have to actively engage in mutual learn-ing(speciﬁcally,brainstorming)toconsiderhowexternalknowledgethatisacquiredcanbecombinedtocreateaninnovative solution. In the case of group efﬁciency, I posit that distal

DiscussionandImplications
Asthefocusofagrowingnumberofstudies,bothlocallearning (e.g., Fiol 1994, Orr 1990) and distal learn-ing (e.g., Ancona and Caldwell 1992a, Hansen 1999)have been demonstrated to be central to group perfor-mance. However, the trade-offs and tensions associatedwith local and distal learning deserve greater theoreti-calandempiricalattentionthantheyhavebeengiventodate. This study contributes to this area of research in
thefollowingthreeways.
First,theﬁndingsreinforceourunderstandingthatdif-
ferent types of group learning have different impactson group performance. Distal learning was positivelyassociatedwithgroupinnovativeness,andlocallearningwaspositivelyassociatedwithgroupefﬁciency,suggest-ing that there are performance trade-offs to selectingone type of group learning over the other. This patternof ﬁndings parallels March’s (1991) theory that whileexploringdistantknowledgebasesimprovesinnovative-ness,exploitingfamiliarknowledgebasesenhancesefﬁ-ciency. Notably, even though local learning involvesengaging in exploratory-oriented behaviors such as try-ing new ideas and reﬂecting, the more circumscribedknowledge space likely to be accessed in local learn-ing (i.e., only sampling the knowledge of individualswithinthesamegroup)servestoimproveonlytheefﬁ-ciency and not the innovativeness of the group. Giventhat distal and local learning are signiﬁcant for dif-ferent performance dimensions, the ﬁndings imply thatlearning-oriented teams with emphasis on developingnew competencies (e.g., Bunderson and Sutcliffe 2003)should engage in greater distal learning, and mastery-orientedteamswithemphasisonperfectingcurrentcom-petenciesshouldengageingreaterlocallearning.
Second, the ﬁndings of this study reframe the com-
mon belief that local and distal learning are mutu-allycompatiblelearningprocessesbydemonstratingthenegative interaction effect of local and distal learningon efﬁciency. Studies on internal and external groupcommunication (e.g., Katz and Tushman 1979) andintra-andintergroupknowledgeexchange(e.g.,Anconaand Caldwell 1992b, Hargadon 1999) have suggestedthat groups that engage in both internal and externalknowledge sharing perform better. However, the ﬁnd-ingsofthisstudyillustratethatdistallearninginterfereswith local learning’s ability to achieve efﬁcient perfor-mance. Particularly, local learning becomes insigniﬁ-cantlyrelatedtogroupefﬁciencywhendistallearningishigh. Therefore, for groups whose performance is pri-marily driven by the efﬁcient execution of tasks, theﬁndings of this study would suggest a focus on locallearningaloneandprovidingabufferforthegroupfromexternalinﬂuences.
Third, this study reﬁnes current understanding of the
positive role of social relationships in promoting col-lective learning by showing that a high level of socialcohesionmaynotalwaysbebeneﬁcialforgrouplearn-ing. Speciﬁcally, the unexpected ﬁnding of an invertedU-shapedrelationshipbetweengroupcohesionandlocallearning suggests that excessive social integration inthe group reduces mutual learning among members.However, in the case of distal learning, a higher levelofgroupcohesionpromotesgreaterexternalknowledgesharing. This counters the popular belief that higherWong: Distal and Local Group Learning

internalgroupcohesivenessfostersgreaterinsularityand
resistance to external ideas. These differential relation-ships of local and distal learning with group cohesionhighlights one tension in fostering both types of grouplearning at the same time (e.g., Argote et al. 2001)by demonstrating that while a high amount of socialcohesion promotes distal learning, it diminishes locallearning. However, in light of the modest and associa-tive nature of the ﬁndings (particularly, the relationshipbetweendistallearningandgroupcohesion),theseﬁnd-ings should be interpreted with caution and it wouldbe worthwhile for future research to further examinehowgroupcohesionisrelatedtodifferentgrouplearningprocesses.
Several insigniﬁcant ﬁndings in this study also bear
further mention. Contrary to expectation, local learn-ing did not complement distal learning to enhancegroup innovativeness. A possible reason is that rangerestriction—local learning scores of the teams in thissample were clustered toward the high end (the scoresranged from 2.7 to 4.2 on a scale from 1 to 5, witha mean of 3.45) with only about 13% of the teamswith local learning scores below 3—reduced the like-lihood of detecting a signiﬁcant interaction effect. Inaddition,taskroutinenesswasnotsigniﬁcantlyrelatedtoeitherdistallearningorlocallearning.Theinsigniﬁcantﬁndings, combined with the signiﬁcant associations ofgroupcohesionwithdistalandlocallearning,maysug-gestthatsocialconditionssuchasgroupcohesion,trust,andclimatearemorepredictiveofinterpersonallearningprocesses(e.g.,Edmondson1999)thantaskconditions.However,givenitsspeculativenature,itwouldbefruit-ful for future research to investigate whether other taskconditions(e.g.,taskcomplexity)affectgrouplearning.
LimitationsandFutureResearchDirections
There are several limitations in this study that open
avenues for future research. First, because this studyfocuses on interpersonal group learning processes, thedifferentialrelationshipsoflocalanddistallearningwithgroupperformanceandgroupcohesionareboundedbytheinterpersonalnatureofthelearningprocessesexam-ined in this study. Knowledge, however, can also beacquiredandsharedintheabsenceofsocialinteractions;forexample,throughaccessingdatabases(Culnan1983)andtransferringtoolsdevelopedinonegrouptoanother(Argote and Ingram 2000). Thus, future research couldexplorewhethernoninterpersonalmeansofdistallearn-ing (e.g., accessing external databases) would have thesame group performance effects as those found in thisstudy.Byadoptingamultifacetedperspectivetolearning(e.g.,examininghowembeddedknowledgeinmembers,tools,andtasksisshared),wemightlearnhowdifferenttypes of knowledge-sharing networks complement oneanother(ArgoteandIngram2000,Arrowetal.2000).Second, different knowledge effects were theorized
to mediate the relationships of distal and local learningwith group performance, but they were not empiricallytested in this study. Given that there is considerableinterest in the types of knowledge that are devel-oped by different group learning processes, it wouldbe worthwhile for future research to systematicallyexamine the knowledge outcomes of distal and locallearning.
Third, the cross-sectional nature of the dataset pro-
hibitsthetestingofcausality.Eventhoughthisresearchposits the inﬂuence of group cohesion on distal andlocal learning, it is likely that distal and local learn-ing impact group cohesion. In the absence of longitu-dinal data, this research can only conclude associative,notcausal,inferencesabouttherelationshipsamongtheconstructs.Hence,itwouldbevaluabletoconductlongi-tudinal research on how group contextual conditionsfostergrouplearningandhowgrouplearningmight,inturn,shapethegroupcontext.
Finally, the self-reported nature of the data increases
the risk of common method variance. In mitigation,usingtheHarmanone-factortest(PodsakoffandOrgan1986), the risk of common method variance is unlikelyto be signiﬁcant in this case because the exploratoryfactor analytic results showed that one general factordid not account for most of the variance. Tests of dis-criminant analyses in the conﬁrmatory factor analysisalso evidenced that the constructs were distinct fromoneanother.Groupperformancevariableswereobtainedfrom the team manager, thereby decreasing the risk ofpercept-perceptbias.Whileitispossiblethatmanagers’performanceratingswereaffectedbytheirevaluationofthe extent of group learning processes, thereby inﬂat-ing the relationship between group learning and groupperformance variables, they are generally more likelyto positively correlate the two when they experiencethese processes as impacting “objective” indicators ofgroup performance. In a review of performance evalu-ations in work settings, Arvey and Murphy (1998) alsofound that there is “increased recognition that subjec-tivitydoesnotautomaticallytranslateintoratererrororbiasandthatratingsaremostlikelyvalidreﬂectionsoftrue performance” (p. 163). However, the risk of per-ceptual biases remains whenever subjective ratings areused,thuspresentingapotentiallimitationofthisstudy.It would be useful for future research to use objectivemeasuresofperformancesoastoprovideamorerobusttestoftherelationshipbetweengrouplearningandgroupperformance.
Conclusion
Responding to Argote and associates’ (2001) call for amore in-depth understanding of the trade-offs and ten-sionsunderlyingdifferentgrouplearningprocesses,thisWong: Distal and Local Group Learning
