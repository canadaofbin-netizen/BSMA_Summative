Research Note
Designing Promotion Ladders to Mitigate Turnover
of IT Professionals
Frank MacCrory
Gabelli School of Business, Fordham University, New York, New York 10023, fmaccrory@fordham.edu
Vidyanand Choudhary
Merage School of Business, University of California Irvine, Irvine, California 92697, veecee@uci.edu
Alain Pinsonneault
Desautels Faculty of Management, McGill University, Montréal, Québec H3A 1G5, Canada, alain.pinsonneault@mcgill.ca
C
hronic excessive turnover among information technology (IT) professionals has been costly to ﬁrms for
decades with annual turnover rates as high as 24% even among Computerworld’s “100 Best Places to Work in
IT.” Prior information systems literature has identiﬁed two key factors affecting turnover: boundary-spanning
roles and low promotability in one’s current ﬁrm. We draw on tournament theory, which is primarily concerned
with inducing effort in employees, to decompose promotability into two distinct constructs: the likelihood of
promotion and beneﬁt from promotion, and demonstrate that each has a distinct role in affecting turnover rates.
Our key result is that a job ladder motivating IT professionals with large, infrequent promotions will lead to
higher turnover than a job ladder with smaller, more frequent promotions. We describe the conditions under
which rearranging the job ladder creates economic value for the ﬁrm. We also offer an explanation for the
observation that jobs characterized by boundary-spanning activities have higher turnover, and show that such
jobs are more sensitive to the effect of likelihood of promotion on turnover. We test our hypotheses on a
detailed data set covering 5,704 IT professionals over a ﬁve-year period. We conﬁrm that likelihood of promotion
has the predicted effects on turnover of IT professionals. A one standard deviation increase in likelihood of
promotion decreases turnover by over 99%, consistent with our prediction. The empirical analysis also conﬁrms
the predicted effects of boundary spanning activities.
Keywords: IT turnover; tournament theory; econometrics; career concerns; management of IT human resources

Introduction
Chronic excessive turnover among information tech-
nology (IT) professionals has been costly to ﬁrms
for decades (Jiang and Klein 2002, Tambe and Hitt
2014), during periods of high and low unemploy-
ment (Quan and Cha 2010) with annual turnover rates
as high as 24% even among the “100 Best Places
to Work in IT” (Computerworld 2015). Prior research
(see Joseph et al. 2007 for a review) has identiﬁed
several factors that impact turnover of IT profession-
als including job satisfaction, organizational commit-
ment, human capital, intrinsic motivation, and exter-
nal labor market conditions, with boundary-spanning
activities and low promotability as key factors that
are under the ﬁrm’s control. Yet a ﬁrm cannot avoid
boundary-spanning jobs, and increasing promotabil-
ity can be costly to the ﬁrm. This leads us to the
following questions: What trade-off does a ﬁrm face
between promotion incentives, turnover, and overall
wage costs? Do the components of promotability affect
IT professionals’ incentives to remain with or leave
their current employer? More speciﬁcally, what is the
impact of likelihood of promotion and the beneﬁt from
promotion on the turnover rate? How do boundary-
spanning activities affect turnover and why?
Our approach to these questions is to incorpo-
rate tournament theory into the information systems
(IS) literature on turnover, and to our knowledge
ours is the ﬁrst study to do so. Tournament theory
explains how relative performance appraisals incen-
tivize effort from knowledge workers (Lazear and
Rosen 1981, Rosen 1986), including IT profession-
als, even if the ﬁrm cannot observe effort levels.
We combine prior research on tournament theory
with economically signiﬁcant job alternatives to gen-
erate novel recommendations for mitigating turnover
among IT professionals.

Turnover often occurs when some job alternative’s
net present value (NPV) is sufﬁciently higher than
the NPV of staying at the current job, particularly if
the NPV of the current job has decreased (Lee et al.
1999).1 This is related to two phenomena, “career
concerns” (Gibbons and Murphy 1992, Gibbons and
Waldman 1999) in which employees exert effort for
rewards in the future, and “promotions as job mar-
ket signals” (Waldman 1984, Lerner and Tirole 2002,
Roberts et al. 2006, Owan 2004, Roberts et al. 2006),
which extend the career horizon beyond the current
employer. A promotion at the current ﬁrm may sig-
nal an employee’s ability to other ﬁrms independent
of signals that she can send herself such as edu-
cation (Spence 1973). Firms may engage in a risky
strategy of delaying promotion to mitigate poaching
(Bernhardt 1995, Owan 2004), but on average promo-
tions decrease turnover among IT personnel (Igbaria
and Siegel 1992).
In the context of tournament theory with economi-
cally signiﬁcant job alternatives, we move beyond cor-
relation between pay dispersal and turnover (Bloom
and Michel 2002) to discuss two key factors that
have been identiﬁed in prior IS literature as im-
pacting turnover: boundary-spanning activities and
promotability.
Boundary-spanning activities are those that require
interactionacrossmultiplebusinessfunctionsordepart-
ments. They can sometimes be a source of power within
the organization (Baroudi and Igbaria 1994) but are
almost always a source of stress (Kahn et al. 1964),
and many IT jobs fall into the boundary-spanning cat-
egory (Baroudi 1985).
Promotability has been deﬁned in prior literature as
the probability of being promoted within one’s cur-
rent ﬁrm (Igbaria and Greenhaus 1992). Prior research
has identiﬁed promotability within the current ﬁrm as
a factor in turnover decisions (Igbaria and Greenhaus
1992, Igbaria and Siegel 1992, Nalbantian and Szostak
2004), but the variety of internal labor market strate-
gies used by IT departments (Ang and Slaughter
2004) and the indeterminate results of these efforts
(Joseph et al. 2007) indicate that promotability’s effec-
tiveness is still an open question.
A key contribution of our work is that promota-
bility should be considered as two distinct constructs
of likelihood of promotion and beneﬁt from the promo-
tion. After all, a promotion from Helpdesk Agent 2
to Helpdesk Agent 3 (with a small pay increase and
similar prospects for promotion) is not as valuable as
a promotion from senior developer to technical man-
ager (with a large pay increase and a shift from a

“[O]pportunities for career mobility outside the
organization have been found to be negatively related
to satisfaction and commitment,” (Mobley et al. 1979;
Bluedorn 1982; Cotton and Tuttle 1986; Igbaria and
Greenhaus 1992, p. 37) which leads to turnover (Meyer
et al. 2002, Paré and Tremblay 2007). The fungibil-
ity of IT skills and the need to acquire updated
IT skills (Gallivan et al. 2002) increases turnover
relative to other professions (Spain 1995, Ang and
Slaughter 2004).
Our results show that incentives from internal pro-
motion opportunities for IT professionals can have
undesirable—but avoidable—effects on turnover. Our
key result is that a job ladder motivating IT pro-
fessionals with the possibility of large, infrequent
promotions will lead to higher turnover than a job
ladder with smaller, more frequent promotions. This
is consistent with the reduced-form results in John-
ston et al. (1993) and Bloom and Michel (2002), but
our detailed microdata allow us to explore the con-
ditions under which adjusting the job ladder is cost-
effective for the ﬁrm. We argue that increasing the
likelihood of promotion can reduce turnover with-
out increasing the NPV of the ﬁrm’s wage cost,
although there is no free lunch: the ﬁrm faces a trade-
off between incentive effects and turnover expenses.
Firms in industries with low turnover costs will be
better served by focusing their job ladder design to
incentivize increased employee effort by having large,
infrequent promotions. However ﬁrms in industries
with high turnover costs should deploy a job ladder
that reﬂects the value of retaining talent. We argue
that high turnover costs should lead to a job ladder
with smaller, more frequent promotions as compared
to the job ladder used by ﬁrms in low-turnover-cost
industries.
Counterintuitively, we also ﬁnd that increasing the
beneﬁt from promotion can increase turnover when
it is coupled with a lower likelihood of promotion.
This result is counter to prior career concern results
that do not take turnover into account. We also ﬁnd
that jobs characterized by boundary-spanning activ-
ities are more sensitive to the effect of likelihood of
promotion.
Our results have important implications for inter-
nal incentives that depart from classic tournament
theory. Misapplying a ladder suitable for a low-
turnover industry in a moderate-turnover industry
creates precisely the type of situation that causes
excessive turnover. Smaller, more frequent promo-
tions reduce turnover even if the beneﬁt from promo-
tion is decreased so much that the NPV of the job is
unchanged. Thus our work contributes to the litera-
ture by showing the existence of a force countering
the incentives created by promotion opportunities.

Hypothesis Development
In this section, we motivate the development of our
hypotheses by combining tournament theory (Rosen
1986) with the future-oriented nature of career con-
cerns (Gibbons and Murphy 1992). Lazear and Rosen
(1981) show that even if the actual effort exerted by
each employee is unobservable, the publicly observ-
able characteristics of a tournament-style job ladder
determine the equilibrium level of effort and the prob-
ability of promotion. The ability to design the job
ladder allows the ﬁrm to induce a speciﬁc level of
employee effort chosen from a fairly broad range. We
argue that the “optimal” level of effort in a job lad-
der that does not consider turnover is not necessar-
ily proﬁt maximizing once turnover-related costs are
considered.
Faced with a trade-off between incentivizing effort
and mitigating turnover, the ﬁrm has three funda-
mental options. We discuss each option in turn.
1. The benchmark case is to induce an “optimal”
level of effort ignoring the cost of turnover.
2. Increase employee compensation to make out-
side job alternatives less attractive.
3. Minimize the pool of at-risk employees and/or
decrease the shock of nonpromotion.
The ﬁrst option may be appropriate if the ﬁrm’s
replacement cost is very low for this job. In this case,
the cost of reorganizing the job ladder may not pay
off for the ﬁrm (Cappelli 2000). Some entry-level IT
jobs may be in this category such as helpdesk agents,
but most IT staff are valuable enough to the ﬁrm to
justify some expenditures on retention.
The second option is appropriate if the ﬁrm needs
to adjust to labor market conditions that change
rapidly relative to the ﬁrm’s ability to adjust its job
ladder. Firms that enjoy a productivity advantage
because of ﬁrm-speciﬁc human capital are in an espe-
cially good position to share some of their surplus
with employees to reduce turnover.
The third option allows for compensation adjust-
ments like option 2 but provides additional ﬂexibil-
ity regarding the job ladder. For example, the ﬁrm
can insert new levels in the middle of the job lad-
der that increase the fraction of employees promoted
but decrease the incremental beneﬁt of each promo-
tion. Such an adjustment could be less costly, but it
would also weaken employees’ incentives for exerting
unobservable effort. This option is appropriate if the
ﬁrm has time to adjust its job ladder such as when
it is facing persistent, long-term turnover issues. We
devise our hypotheses in a way that controls for ﬁrm
actions consistent with the second option, so that we
may focus on the third option, which is novel to our
research.
We expect that employees will seek to maxi-
mize their NPV over their entire careers derived
from wages less the cost of effort and nonpecu-
niary costs associated with switching employers, plus
any nonpecuniary beneﬁts from working at the ﬁrm
(Duncan 1976). The ﬁrm’s NPV, on the other hand,
is the employee’s productivity less the NPV of wages
and the cost of nonpecuniary beneﬁts. Consider an
employee’s NPV just prior to an appraisal for a possi-
ble promotion. Her NPV is a weighted average of the
NPV conditional on promotion and the NPV condi-
tional on nonpromotion. If the employee is promoted
then her NPV increases, otherwise she is not pro-
moted and the NPV decreases. Nonpromoted IT pro-
fessionals have job alternatives that may exceed the
NPV of remaining until the next appraisal, causing
some to leave the ﬁrm. Promoted employees may also
leave the ﬁrm because of job market signaling, but
they do so at a lower rate (Igbaria and Siegel 1992,
Johnston et al. 1993).2
Ceteris paribus, increasing the promotion rate will
put more employees into the promoted group that has
lower turnover, lowering the overall turnover rate,
but increase costs for the ﬁrm. One way to increase
promotion probability at lower cost is to redesign the
job ladder with more rungs. The additional rungs
will slow down the increase in the ﬁrm’s costs, and
it can even be done in a way to keep the NPV
of costs constant while increasing promotion prob-
ability. However, employees are motivated by the
difference in NPV between promotion and nonpromo-
tion (Lazear and Rosen 1981), and increasing promo-
tion probability without increasing the overall NPV
must decrease the difference in NPV between promo-
tion and nonpromotion. This smaller difference will
induce less unobservable effort, creating a trade-off
between promotion incentives and turnover mitiga-
tion. Hence tournament theory incentivizes effort but
also introduces shocks, which lower NPVs whenever
an appraisal does not result in a promotion. Thus, we
have the following:
Hypothesis 1. Controlling for NPV, the turnover rate
is lower for jobs with larger likelihood of earning a
promotion.
As the likelihood of promotion increases, the frac-
tion of employees that are not promoted decreases.
As discussed previously, the turnover rate is lower
for promoted employees (Johnston et al. 1993), so this
leads to a reduction in turnover. Introducing the NPV
of holding the job as a control variable removes the
effect of changes in the average wage (i.e., any use
of the “second option” is ﬁltered out). The ﬁrm can
balance the wage cost implications of the increased

In Hypothesis 2, we test for the effect of beneﬁt
from promotion upon turnover, again controlling for
NPV. It is possible that this control may not fully
ﬁlter out the “second option.” In Hypothesis 2, any
impact from the second option “seeping” through

turnover will increase because of the larger drop in
employee NPV. Therefore, this hypothesis tests the
adequacy of our control and our novel prediction that
an increase in the beneﬁt from promotion will lead to
an increase in turnover.
As discussed above, an appraisal that does not
result in a promotion creates a negative shock to
the employee’s NPV. The higher the potential bene-
ﬁt from the promotion, the larger the negative shock
and the greater the likelihood of the nonpromoted
employee leaving the ﬁrm. If our prediction about the
“third option” is true, then a higher beneﬁt from pro-
motion will tend to occur with a lower likelihood of
promotion, increasing the pool of employees that are
subjected to this negative shock. Thus, we have the
following:
Hypothesis 2. Controlling for NPV, jobs with higher
beneﬁt of promotion will have higher turnover.
Our ﬁrst two hypotheses assume implicitly that
the only things that differentiate jobs are objective
measures like NPV, Likelihood of Promotion, Beneﬁt
from Promotion, and Current Wages, but this obvi-
ously oversimpliﬁes the problem. Some jobs are asso-
ciated with lower commitment to the ﬁrm than others
(George and Bettenhausen 1990), which has impor-
tant implications for turnover. It turns out that many
IT jobs involve boundary-spanning activities that
increase stress (Kahn et al. 1964) and decrease job
satisfaction and organizational commitment (Baroudi
1985, Guimaraes and Igbaria 1992) relative to other
jobs.3 Hence, we expect boundary-spanning activi-
ties to be signiﬁcant drivers of employees’ commit-
ment to the ﬁrm. Ceteris paribus such activities would
tend to increase turnover because on average these
employees are less attached to their current employer,
as stated in Hypothesis 3A and illustrated by how
the solid curve (turnover rate for boundary-spanning
jobs) is higher than the dashed curve (turnover rate
for non-boundary-spanning jobs) in Figure 1.

3.
Empirical Investigation
The focal ﬁrm in our study is a large multinational
outsourcing, systems integration and consulting ﬁrm.
It provides application managed services, IT con-
sulting services, and numerous software solutions.
The ﬁrm is headquartered in North America with
ofﬁces in over 100 cities across four continents. They
have over 30,000 employees. For this study, we based
all employee-level analysis on the ﬁrm’s administra-
tive records to preserve the information asymmetries
present in the workplace and prevent a Hawthorne
Effect.5 As a fortunate side effect, this eases replica-
tion of the results across ﬁrms since most ﬁrms keep
similar records.
Our primary data source is the ﬁrm’s human re-
sources records, which includes demographic infor-
mation and date of hire for each employee. We also
observe time-varying job titles, base salaries, and
scheduled hours for each employee. We supplement
this data with information from timecards that allow
us to observe exits from the ﬁrm. These observations

the observed Beneﬁt. Yet we can exclude this possi-

reveals that the 12% of employees exiting the ﬁrm
each year tend to earn wages in the 40th to 50th
percentile for their jobs at the focal ﬁrm. Second,
we designed our NPV variable to include the exiting
employee’s wages in the average wage to better rep-
resent the Beneﬁt facing the employees in that job.

is endogeneity of a sort that managers intentionally
promote employees that they believe are most likely
to leave the ﬁrm (using signals unobserved by the
researchers). This could be serious because we do not
have access to a good instrument for which employees
should be promoted absent their managers’ discretion.
The best proxy available is the average promotion rate
for that job over the entire sample period, which is the
control we use. If we insufﬁciently control for man-
agers’ preferential promoting, the effect would be to
depress the turnover rate for nonpromoted employ-
ees and bias against us ﬁnding results for Likelihood of
Promotion or boundary spanning.

important job alternatives to explain the turnover
behavior of IT professionals. In this section, we pro-
vide a summary of our key ﬁndings, contributions
to the literature, and managerial implications of our
results.

from prior literature has two distinct components
that impact turnover at the job level in a promo-

turnover because promoted employees are less likely
to leave the ﬁrm, and obviously a higher Likelihood
of Promotion puts more of the employees in the pro-
moted group. Second, the Beneﬁt from Promotion is
highly correlated with the negative shock experienced
by employees who are not promoted,12 with larger
shocks causing a larger fraction of the nonpromoted
employees to leave the ﬁrm. Taken together, these
results demonstrate why plateau-type jobs that offer
a large but less likely path to promotion lead to con-
sistently high turnover. It is important to note that
the mechanisms we describe apply to any knowl-
edge workers facing competitive promotions, but the
high baseline turnover in the IT profession makes
these mechanisms particularly salient because small
changes can have economically important impacts for
the ﬁrm.

boundary-spanning activities. We ﬁnd partial sup-
port for our hypothesis that these jobs have higher
turnover on average, and we ﬁnd full support for our
hypothesis that boundary-spanning IT employees are
more sensitive to the Beneﬁt from Promotion effect. If
small changes are important for IT jobs in general,
even the smallest adjustment to boundary-spanning
IT jobs can have an outsized impact.
For example, consider the customer service repre-
sentative jobs at level 2 of our focal ﬁrm’s hierarchy
(see Table 3). The promotions for a CSR 2a or CSR 2c
have large beneﬁts (to Developer 4 or Consultant 4a)
but relatively low likelihood. The promotion for a
CSR 2b is more modest (to Clerical 3e) but at a much
higher likelihood. We hypothesized that the CSR 2b
job would have a lower turnover rate than CSR 2a or
CSR 2c, and it does.
The intuition behind this result is as follows: just
before they are appraised, each CSR’s NPV is a con-
vex combination of the values of her current job and
the one for which she is competing. Each also has
access to a job alternative with its own NPV. The CSRs

that are promoted now have a higher NPV for remain-
ing at the ﬁrm (namely, the NPV of the new job)
and are less likely to leave for a job alternative. The
CSRs who are not promoted now have a lower NPV
(because they must wait another period for the next
appraisal and promotion opportunity) and are more
likely to leave the ﬁrm for a job alternative. Since the
likelihood of promotion is higher and the beneﬁt of
promotion is smaller for CSR 2b, this job has a smaller
pool of high-risk employees and a smaller fraction of
those employees leave the ﬁrm.
This ﬁnding, which applies much more broadly
than just customer service representatives, has impor-
tant managerial implications for the design of the
job ladder. It suggests that when high turnover is a
problem, ﬁrms can trade off between increased com-
pensation, promotion incentives, and turnover. The
ﬁrm can buy organizational commitment by paying
an information rent to the employees (due to their
private knowledge of their organizational commit-
ment),13 but this can lead to wage inﬂation. This work
provides another way to lower turnover: by increas-
ing the number of rungs on the job ladder. This
is proﬁt improving so long as managers are careful
to maintain enough promotion incentive to induce
effort.
Counterintuitively, we also ﬁnd that increasing
the beneﬁt from promotion can increase turnover.
This result is counterintuitive because one would
expect higher beneﬁts to incentivize employees to
stay at their current employer. This surprising result
is, however, consistent with our hypotheses. We also
predicted that IT jobs characterized by boundary-
spanning activities would exhibit higher turnover.
This result was partially supported by our empiri-
cal analysis, in that most job categories satisﬁed the
hypothesis’s prediction. We also predicted that such
boundary spanning jobs would be even more sensi-
tive to the turnover-inducing effects of beneﬁts from
promotion. This result was fully supported by our
empirical analysis.
Prior literature has identiﬁed promotability as a
crucial factor in IS turnover, and we contribute to
this literature by examining jointly the likelihood of
promotion and the beneﬁt of promotion. Prior ﬁnd-
ings on the negative relationship between likelihood
of promotion and turnover could have been driven by
the increase in NPV that results from an increase in
the likelihood of promotion. By considering the ben-
eﬁt from promotion, we are able to disentangle the
cause of this correlation and determine that it is not
simply the result of paying IT professionals more.

We also contribute to tournament theory by consid-
ering economically important job alternatives for the
employees. IT professionals are an ideal population
to demonstrate the importance of the job alternative’s
impact on tournament theory because IT profession-
als have persistent outside employment opportunities
in a wide variety of economic conditions.
Since the incentives of a classic tournament affect
not only effort decisions but also turnover, it is impor-
tant for researchers applying tournament theory in
future studies to account for the job alternatives when
it is an economically signiﬁcant factor for an indus-
try (such as it is for IT professionals). Furthermore,
for researchers studying turnover, it is important to
take into account the various dimensions of a job’s
NPV identiﬁed by tournament theory when design-
ing surveys or gathering administrative data. That is,
while a single “promotability” survey item captures
an important facet of promotion incentives, a richer
set of items can capture the multidimensional impacts
of internal promotion opportunities upon turnover.
Our results also have important implications for
managers. Job ladders can be designed in a way that
trades off incentive effects and turnover. Managers
can accomplish this by increasing the likelihood of
promotion at each appraisal (and balancing this with
a lower beneﬁt from the promotion to contain the
overall wage cost). This can be accomplished either
through marginal adjustments to existing jobs or by
inserting new levels into the job ladder. Importantly,
since boundary-spanning activities make IT profes-
sionals particularly sensitive to these types of shocks,
even small changes can have economically signiﬁcant
impacts on turnover. Our study provides guidance on
the constraints facing a manager designing (or alter-
ing) a job ladder for IT professionals.
On the other hand, if a ﬁrm wishes to decrease
turnover without muting promotion incentives then
it has the option to increase its compensation to its
employees. This is consistent with SAS Institute’s
retention strategy of spending $6,000 to $8,000 per
employee on perks to prevent $50 to $70 million
of replacement costs (Harvard Business School 2002,
pp. 80–81) and with the use of costly “industrial” in-
ternal labor market strategies to retain employees that
would be expensive or disruptive to replace (Ang and
Slaughter 2004). We believe that even a ﬁrm that is
buying commitment from its employees (Spence 1973)
can take advantage of our results to trade off a small
effect on incentives to save a considerable amount of
compensation.
Classic tournament theory’s guidance to managers
is to motivate employees by increasing the differ-
ence in present values between winning and not
winning a promotion. Since classic tournament the-
ory abstracts away turnover, the guidance may lead

to some steps of the job ladder with high beneﬁts
from promotion and low likelihood of promotion.
We show that this combination increases turnover.
One method for avoiding high-beneﬁt/low-likelihood
steps is to increase the number of rungs in the job lad-
der (without materially affecting the overall wage bill
or the top and bottom wage levels) so that employees
face smaller promotions with higher probabilities of
attaining them.
Our study has limitations that researchers and man-
agers may need to consider. First, we infer turnover
from a premature exit from our panel data set,
although such an exit may represent merely a trans-
fer to a different line of work (e.g., a CSR 3 moving
to sales). Such events create similar replacement costs
for the focal business unit, but may reduce replace-
ment costs or time-to-proﬁciency at the hiring busi-
ness unit. Second, we cannot distinguish voluntary
turnover from involuntary turnover, although from
conversations with managers at the focal ﬁrm, it is
apparent that ﬁrings are rare and there were no lay-
offs during the sample period. Voluntary and invol-
untary turnover impose similar replacement costs, but
our results and guidance apply only to voluntary
turnover. Third, some of the retention actions taken
by the ﬁrm may trigger reactions from other ﬁrms.
Fourth, the framework we test is an extension of tour-
nament theory and is applicable only when tourna-
ment theory itself is applicable (costly or infeasible
to observe individual effort or productivity, and an
expectation that a signiﬁcant number of employees
will not be promoted in each period).
Future work in this area may include deriving
the labor market equilibrium, which should demon-
strate whether broad adoption of our recommenda-
tions would decrease overall competition (and thus
excessive turnover) in the IT labor market. Future
work might also examine the incentive effects of orga-
nizational changes, such as the introduction of off-
shoring. Another opportunity for future research is
to test our framework on a data set spanning multi-
ple ﬁrms. Finally, future work can incorporate human
capital and the signaling role of promotions to more
fully capture the multiple channels through which a
job ladder affects employees’ incentives to leave a
ﬁrm.
While many ﬁrms struggle with annual IT turnover
rates of 24% or higher, managers have the option
to increase pay and beneﬁts to achieve SAS’s rate
of 6% or adjust the job ladder to lower turnover
with a trade-off between promotion incentives and
replacement costs. The guidance in this paper helps
managers make the right decision for their speciﬁc
combination of IT needs and industry characteristics.
