# Hidden Guardrails: Nuanced Exclusion Criteria

This document formally captures the nuanced, "hidden" criteria that human coders historically applied to exclude papers that technically measured boundary-spanning but failed to meet specific contextual requirements. 

These rules must be applied during the Include/Exclude screening phase.

## 1. The Student Sample Ban
**Rule:** Exclude the paper if the sample consists of students (e.g., MBA students, undergraduates) participating in simulations, coursework, or academic team projects.
**Rationale:** The meta-analysis requires real-world organizational employees. Boundary spanning in an academic setting lacks the external validity required for our organizational context.
* **Example Case:** `BSMA0325` (Lanaj & Hollenbeck, 2015). This paper measures boundary spanning in self-managing teams, but the participants were MBA students. **Action: EXCLUDE.**

## 2. The Internal Boundary Spanning Ban
**Rule:** Exclude the paper if the boundary spanning behavior is strictly *internal* to the organization.
**Rationale:** The construct of interest must cross *external* organizational boundaries. Interactions solely between different departments, or between expatriates and local employees within the same office, do not qualify.
* **Example Case:** `BSMA0509` (Salem et al., 2018). Measures boundary-spanning leadership between expatriate and local aid workers within the same humanitarian field office. **Action: EXCLUDE.**
* **Example Case:** `BSMA0127` (Deng & Chi, 2015). Examines interactions between IS workers and end-users entirely within a single organization. **Action: EXCLUDE.**

## 3. The Adaptive Selling Ban
**Rule:** Exclude the paper if the boundary spanning construct is solely and exclusively measured using an "Adaptive Selling Behavior" scale (e.g., Spiro & Weitz, 1990).
**Rationale:** While sales is a boundary-spanning role, adaptive selling (altering sales behaviors during customer interactions) is considered a distinct construct from general boundary-spanning behaviors (e.g., information scouting, external representation, championing) in the context of this specific meta-analysis.
* **Example Case:** `BSMA0471` (Morgan et al., 2014). The only individual-level behavior measured is 'flexibility', which relies entirely on the ADAPTS scale. **Action: EXCLUDE.**
* **Example Case:** `BSMA0126` (DelVecchio, 1998). The sole construct representing a boundary-spanning behavior is "Adaptive Selling Behaviors." **Action: EXCLUDE.**
