Abstract
Purpose – The purpose of this paper is to know that how group resources (internal and external) and the
relationship quality among group members relate to group performance.
Design/methodology/approach – Given the normative nature of group performance, the study is carried
out in a contrived environment. Participants were 204 master of business administration students who were
allocated to 51 study groups. Data were collected in three waves and from two different sources, i.e., students
and instructors. Data analysis was carried out by employing regression analysis and the bootstrapping
procedure, i.e., PROCESS.
Findings – The results of this paper reveal that an individual-level internal resource, i.e. time, positively
predicts group performance, while group-level internal resources, i.e., group composition and group members’
experience, negatively predict group performance. Both external resources (external communication and
instructor’s support) are found to have a positive effect on group performance. The relationship quality
among group members partially relates to group performance. Instructor’s support as an external resource is
found to moderate the relationship between only two aspects of relationship quality and group performance.
Practical implications – This study provides guidance to group members as to how they can utilize
internal and external group resources and their relationship quality for enhancing their group performance.
Managers in varied organizations can also utilize the findings of this study.
Originality/value – This study is unique in that it offers a new insight into internal and external resources
and relationship quality, that is, from the perspective of group performance. The group resources included in
the study are rarely found in the existing literature.
Keywords Relationship quality, External resources, Group performance, Internal resources
Paper type Research paper
Introduction
Mirroring each other, both business schools and business organizations pursue innovative
efforts that could lead them to business excellence (Dahlgaard, 2014). A roller-coaster
relationship exists between business schools and business organizations. There are some
practices which the former learns from the latter and vice versa. However, both undertake
some practices simultaneously, and group work is one of them. Observing how business
organizations impart training to their managers and employees, business schools try to
develop the future managers (students) for using group resources efficiently, maintaining
good relationship quality among group members and thus, attaining high group performance.
According to McGrath (1991), different groups follow different paths and go through
different stages to achieve high group performance. For instance, in order to foster students’
understanding and make them capable of applying what they learn, group-based learning is

business and management tasks are often performed in groups. Group-based learning is an
innovative learning technique that improves group performance both at individual and
group levels. In line with the above, the literature suggests that students’ academic
performance is enhanced, especially when they carry out study projects in groups (Zgheib
et al., 2010). Group-based learning facilitates information sharing and relationship quality

relationship quality helps group members achieve business excellence for their organization

the available internal and external resources.
While accentuating group intelligence, Woolley et al. (2015) called for exploring and
examining the internal and external factors that could ensure high group performance.
Notably, research on group performance thus far has rarely been conducted with respect to
internal and external resources of groups. In view of the above, business education is
considered essential in universities, especially in developing countries, as it is a source of
producing developed human resources for local business organizations as well as for the
international market (Fryer and Ogden, 2014). For this reason, universities have now shifted
from passive learning to a group-based learning approach.
Drawing upon the resource-based view (Barney, 1991) together with resource
dependence theory (Salancik and Pfeffer, 1977), the study includes both internal and
external resources in the research model. Specifically, internal resources are studied at two
levels, i.e., individual level (“time” and “finance” available to an individual group member)
and group level (group formation – males, females or both – and group members’ experience
of working in group projects). External resources include supervisor’s support and external
communication (communication with members of other groups). In addition, four elements
of relationship quality (information sharing, communication quality, long-term relationship
orientation and satisfaction with the relationship) are included in the study.
Being mindful of the significance of efficient resource utilization and relationship quality
for attaining high group performance, our study seeks to increase understanding of the
factors that can influence group performance. More specifically, the study aims to answer
the following questions:
RQ1. Do internal and external resources affect group performance?
RQ2. Does relationship quality among group members predict group performance?
RQ3. Do external resources interplay with the relationship between relationship quality
and group performance?
Bearing in mind that business organizations reflect on business schools, especially with
regard to group-based projects, for empirical testing of the study relationships we have
focused on graduate students enrolled in the master of business administration (MBA)
program of a business school. Since, our study is carried out in an artificial phenomenon, we
discuss the relevant literature in the context of students’ group projects.
Group resources and group performance
Being mindful of sustainability, contemporary educational institutions realize that they
must be competitive. Toward this end, these institutions are restructuring their systems and
policies. Likewise, business schools are preparing themselves for facing up to more
challenges as globalization and change in business trends are on the rise. For instance, in
order to prepare future managers who could truly fulfill the requirements of contemporary
business organizations, business schools are employing advanced instructional designs.
These instructional designs focus more on practicums, wherein students are put in

In relation to group performance, previous studies focused on a variety of areas, inter
alia, intra-group conflict (Jehn and Mannix, 2001), workgroup diversity (Van Knippenberg
et al., 2004), social networks (Sparrowe et al., 2001), group cohesion (Beal et al., 2003), group

also attempted to predict group outputs, and that while testing process-related assumptions
(Davis, 1969; Steiner, 1972). However, the one that gained popularity among researchers is
the research by Steiner (1972). His model focuses on achieving the optimum level of group
performance by using idealized combinations of group members’ resources.
There are different group resources, which according to resource-based view and
resource dependence theory can be combined and orchestrated to produce the desired group
performance. We categorize group resources into two sets, i.e., internal and external
resources. For the accomplishment of group tasks, group members’ reliance on one another
cannot be ruled out. However, each group member’s dependency on other group members
can vary, that is, some group members need to work less dependently while others may
have to work more collectively (Van der Vegt et al., 2000, 2001). Therefore, as a part of their
professional training, students’ academic performance is evaluated based on the collective
work accomplished by them (Zgheib et al., 2010). Better said, the more the group members
are resourceful at both individual and group levels, the better the group performance will be.
Internal resources (individual level)
This study considers that two internal resources at individual level, i.e., time and finance,
can have the potential to positively predict group performance. Here, time refers to whether
a group member is a full-time or a part-time student. According to Astin’s (1984)
involvement theory, there are less chances of success for students employed off-campus, as
their work consumes their time and energy and also keeps them away from their academic
assignments which are to be performed while being off-campus. The theory has gained
empirical support that part-time students’ number of working hours inversely relate to their
degree of involvement in the assigned academic tasks (e.g. Furr and Elling, 2000). In
contrast, students’ number of hours spent on-campus, and that with teachers and peers,
directly relates to their learning (Astin, 1984; Pescosolido, 2001).
Given the prevalent economic and social conditions in countries like Pakistan, where the
present study has been carried out, the scarcity of employment opportunities in business
environment means that employees remain in competition with each other. As a result of
this, the youth continues education along with earning their livelihoods. Like other
economies, muddying the waters of proper school-to-work transition, part-time jobs are
witnessed to have rapid growth (Nothdurft, 2010). Because of divided attention between
school and job, part-time students’ performance is more likely to be different from full-time
students. Generally, it is considered that part-time students have an edge over the full-time
students, i.e., being well conversant with what is happening in the world of work they can
contribute more toward the group accomplishments (Pescosolido, 2001). On the contrary,
students who are enrolled as part-time have less opportunity to meet with teachers and
peers and hence their performance remains low (Lundberg and Schreiner, 2004). However,
the fact is that the performance is not only about knowing but doing as well. Therefore, it is
expected that students devoting full-time to their studies can contribute to their group
performance more than part-time students. Thus, we hypothesize that:
H1a. Full-time students can contribute to group performance more than part-time students.

Finance is another important internal resource. In current times, higher education has become
a privilege that everyone cannot afford, especially in developing countries. From higher
education institutions’ point of view, students are attracted toward education by providing
them with financial support in terms of scholarships and bursaries. This is also because
numerous students leave education due to the lack of funds (Lau, 2003). Therefore, financial
support is considered important not only for students’ retention in academia, but for
improving their academic performance too. Abraham and Clark’s (2006) findings suggest that
students who receive financial assistance tend to remain in an institution and achieve higher
grades than their peers who are given admission to university on a self-finance basis.
Due to high competition in the job market, employees’ desire to achieve excellence in their
respective line of work and attain a competitive advantage over other job seekers. To achieve
these goals, individuals opt for continuing their education, i.e., getting education while
simultaneously doing jobs. However, maintaining equilibrium between the demands of school
and job is a challenge for such university students as they cannot expend the requisite time on
their studies. Therefore, to continue the education, students need funding through earnings or
savings, tuition reimbursements by their employers, parental support and scholarships and
loans and grants for higher education. It is expected that the students who get financial
support can give more time to academic activities that in turn may help them perform more
efficiently in their academic life (Faulk et al., 2012). Generally, in higher learning, and
particularly for group-based learning, from “forming groups” to “performing in groups,”
individuals’ financial resources play a vital role. Building on the above, we hypothesize that:
H1b. The students being awarded a scholarship can contribute to group performance
more than self-financing students.
Internal resources (group level)
According to the extant literature, group composition can have a significant impact on
group performance (Moreland et al., 2013). Given, the between-group differences are under
control, homogeneous groups attain better performance than heterogeneous groups
(Watson et al., 1993). Concerning surface-level differences, a group can consist of males,
females (homogeneous) or both (heterogeneous). In the Pakistani environment specifically,
where the present study has been carried out, people feel reluctant to work with the opposite
gender. A large population in the region maintains that females and males are supposed to
work only inside and outside their homes, respectively. However, considering the growing
awareness, changing social circumstances, expanding dimensions of economics, e.g.,
womenomics, females are now also being increasingly encouraged to work with men. Yet,
there is still reluctance in sharing ideas and point of views among male and female
colleagues, especially when they have to go out for some fieldwork. Likewise, female
students in their study projects face similar problems. Therefore, it is assumed that in class
projects, the more male group members there are, the higher the group performance will be,
and vice versa (Ensley and Hmieleski, 2005). Hence, it is hypothesized that:
H2a. The greater the number of male students in a group is, the higher the group
performance will be.
The second component of internal resources at group level is the experience of working in
groups. It means that individuals have a willingness to explore, consider and tolerate both
new and familiar ideas and experiences (Schilpzand et al., 2011). Per general perception, group
members’ experience is assumed to positively predict group performance. However, there is
also a contrasting viewpoint in the literature (e.g. Homan et al., 2008) which asserts that group
members’ experience can negatively predict group performance. This is likely to happen
when more experienced group members dominate other group members by using their

External resources
There are some external factors that can have a direct impact on group performance. We
consider the following two factors more compelling: supervisor’s support and external
communication. Besides group leader and group members, the supervisor’s support is
instrumental to group-based learning which in turn can improve group performance
(Metheny et al., 2008), although the supervisor is not a member of the group (Black and Deci,
2000). In a group, students can have different backgrounds and different characteristics.
This diversity can be well managed if the instructor support and intervention is at hand
(Duffy and Shaw, 2000). The literature suggests that instructor’s support helps students to
increase their interest in academic activities, competence and self-regulation and it helps
students to diffuse their anxiety while taking up tough tasks in the semester (Black and
Deci, 2000). Therefore, it is hypothesized that:
H3a. Instructor’s support positively predicts students’ group performance.
Being useful in both work and study contexts, external communication, i.e., sharing of ideas
with individuals outside the group, can contribute to group performance (Chermak and
Weiss, 2005). In group-based learning, external communication can help group members
mitigate the effects of misinformation, obtain the quality information and data they require
for completing their study project and seek informal feedback from out-group fellows
(Lovall, 2001). Having the requisite information fetched from outside the group, group
members feel more inclined to share that information within the group that helps them
reiterate the group’s objectives and then compare with the group’s achievements, further
leading to overall group performance (Smidts et al., 2001). Based on the above, it is
hypothesized that:
H3b. External communication positively predicts students’ group performance.
Relationship quality and group performance
The literature suggests a positive relationship between relationship quality and group
performance (see e.g. Curkovic et al., 2000; Rickards and Moger, 2000). However, the
development of effective in-group relations depends on the psychological traits of team
members (Randeree and Ninan, 2011). These traits help them share their knowledge, skills
and cognitive ideas to enhance group performance (Dong et al., 2008). Effective ﬂow of
information, for learning purposes, can be attained through high-quality relationships
among group members (Akgun et al., 2006).
Different determinants of relationship quality have been discussed in the literature. Four
determinants of relationship quality, namely, cultural understanding, cultural interaction,
communication competence and communication interaction have been identified by Grifﬁth
and Harvey (2001). Pressey and Selassie (2003) suggested that some managers perceive that
factors such as language barriers in communication, geographic distance, political barriers,
economic factors, time differences, industry barriers, technology barriers, infrastructure
barriers and legal differences can influence intra-group relationships. Ural (2009) also
identified four elements of relationship quality: information sharing, communication quality,
long-term relationship orientation and satisfaction with the relationship. In the context of

the present study, the dimensions used by Ural (2009) are considered more relevant as
students have to work in groups and communicate, and seek intergroup and intragroup

First, information sharing requires that information should be shared openly within
groups to achieve high group performance (Lages et al., 2004). Information sharing is
important for improving the relationship quality (Ural, 2009), performance (Lages et al.,
2004) and satisfaction of group members by the effective utilization of available information
(Cannon and Homburg, 2001). It is expected that while working in groups, group members
with more information can use their knowledge for better group performance. Second,
communication quality refers to the process that maintains a relationship between different
individuals or groups. While working in groups, communication flow depends on the
amount of information shared within the groups. Notably, two-way information exchange is
necessary for the efficient use of information (Ural, 2009). Both types of communication,
formal and informal, exist within groups. It is an intrinsic element of relationship quality
(Roberts et al., 2003) that results in high group performance.
Third, long-term relationship orientation leads to sustainable competitive advantage for
groups (Keyton, 2000). It involves goal sharing, cooperation and risk sharing while working
in groups. Each group member expects that his/her performance is contingent upon
relationship quality (Ural, 2009). In this way, instead of one group, more groups can benefit
from each other leading to improved group performance. The final, the fulfillment of desired
goals within the groups lead to satisfaction with the relationship (Anderson and Narus,
1990). It is a positive emotional state resulting from the assessment of relationship within
individuals or groups (Katz et al., 2004). More satisfied individuals have a better relationship
quality (Ural, 2009). Based on the above, we hypothesize that:
H4. Relationship quality may positively predict group performance.
Moderating effect of external resources on relationship quality–group
performance
External resources can interplay with the relationship quality, amplifying its effect on group
performance. That is, support from instructors to complete assigned tasks, when combined
with relationship quality, produces more effective group performance (Black and Deci,
2000). Instructor’s support has a strong relationship with both relationship quality
(Mullen and Tallent-Runnels, 2006) and external communication. When information is
gathered and shared outside a group, it has a multiplying effect on relationship quality,
thereby increasing group performance (Chermak and Weiss, 2005). Therefore, external
communication is also considered to strengthen the relationship between relationship
quality and group performance. Hence, it is hypothesized that:
H5a. Instructor’s support moderates the association between relationship quality and
students’ group performance by strengthening the relationship.
H5b. External communication moderates the relationship between relationship quality
and students’ group performance by strengthening the relationship (Figure 1).
Method
Sample and procedures
The sample consisted of 204 students enrolled in an MBA program at a large public sector
university in Islamabad, Pakistan. The study utilized 51 groups developed by the concerned
course instructors on a random basis for group projects. The project assigned to the students
was to be completed in a semester. Data were collected in three waves from two sources.

Measures
While adapting measures, we considered two aspects, i.e., ensuring the consistency between
measures included in the survey questionnaire and the respective construct of interest and
incorporating slight modifications to help participants familiarize with the study context.
Relationship quality. Relationship quality was measured using 14 items from Ural (2009).
This construct comprises four elements, therefore, amount of information sharing had three
items (the sample item is: “Different approaches to complete the given project successfully
were frequently discussed within the group”), communication quality (four items, “Group
members continuously interacted to complete the project”), long-term relationship
orientation (four items, “I focused on long-term goals in this relationship”) and
satisfaction with the relationship (three items, “My association with this group was
successful one”). All items were measured using a five-point Likert type scale, ranging from
1 ¼ strongly disagree to 5 ¼ strongly agree.
Group performance. For measuring group performance, marks given by the instructors
on the basis of group members’ performance in the projects were utilized. These marks were
used as they were actual and more authenticated than perceived group performance.
Internal resources. Internal resources at individual level include time and finance.
Respondents were asked whether they are 1 ¼ full-time students or 2 ¼ part-time students.
Regarding financial support, respondents were asked whether they are 1 ¼ self-financed or
2 ¼ scholarship recipients. Internal resources at group level include group members’
experience and group composition. For measuring the former, respondents were asked that
how many times they remained involved in group projects: 1 ¼ less than three times,
2 ¼ four times, 3 ¼ five times, and 4 ¼ six times or more. Whilst for measuring the latter,
respondents were categorized into three groups: 1 ¼ only males, 2 ¼ only females and
3 ¼ both.
External resources. External resources include instructors’ support and external
communication. For measuring the both these variables, respondents were asked to mention
the level of instructors’ help and external communication on the following scale: 1 ¼ low,
2 ¼ medium and 3 ¼ high.

Main effects
H1a–b pertain to the association between internal resources at individual level and group

Thus, H2a and H2b are accepted.

Moderating effects
H5a–b pertain to the moderating effects of external resources, i.e., instructor’s support and
external communication, on the relationship between relationship quality and group
performance. In order to run moderation analysis, we employed Preacher and Hayes’
(2008) bootstrapping procedure (5,000 iterations, bias-corrected, 95% confidence intervals
(CI)). We ran eight moderated models. In models 1–4, we analyzed the moderating effect of
instructor’s support on each factor of relationship quality, and in models 5–8, we analyzed
the moderating effects of external communication on each factor of relationship quality.
Of eight moderated models only the following two models provided evidence of
moderating effects.
First, the direct paths between information sharing (B ¼ 1.17, po0.001) and instructors’

these are met (see e.g. Faulk et al., 2012).
We studied the role of two group-level internal resources toward group performance, i.e.,
group composition and group members’ experience. The results of our study confirm that
both these resources negatively predict group performance. According to Zhan et al. (2015),
gender-based group composition significantly influences group performance. They have
observed that males perform better in mix-gender groups and females can perform equally
whether group comprises all female members or mixed gender. Moreover, males prefer to
join groups having a mix-gender, while females prefer single-gender groups. Gender
differences occur on attitude rather than learning achievement. For group formation and
improved performance, it is required that group members demonstrate mutual interest and
understanding with each other.
Woolley et al. (2015) studied collective intelligence and found it an ability of groups to
perform multiple tasks. According to them, group composition and group interaction can
potentially influence group intelligence. In contrast, some other researchers suggest that
group composition may lead to loss of trust and compatibility leading to decreased group
performance (see e.g. Ensley and Hmieleski, 2005). According to them, group composition
is important for understanding others’ viewpoints in the group. Groups having
individuals with more experience tend to have decreased performance as they attempt to
impose their ideas on others, and thus, either they make decisions on their own or
influence others for the purpose. Moreover, highly experienced group members attempt to
accomplish tasks without seeking others’ input. This reduces the chances of others’
learning, and may demotivate them, resulting in overall low group performance
(Homan et al., 2008).
External resources include external communication and instructor’s support. The results
of our study confirm that both have a positive significant relationship with group
performance. External communication is the communication with other groups. The more
the external communication is, the more the level of information sharing and knowledge
transfer will be. As a result, group performance increases (Smidts et al., 2001; Lovall, 2001;
Chermak and Weiss, 2005). As regards instructors’ support, if instructors are supportive
and guide students, the group of students can increase their group performance (Black and
Deci, 2000; Duffy and Shaw, 2000).
We examined the relationship between four dimensions of relationship quality and
group performance. All four dimensions were found to have a significant impact on group
performance, but contrary to our expectations, two of them, i.e., information sharing and
satisfaction with the relationship, appeared to have an inverse relationship.
The remaining two, i.e., communication quality and long-term relationship orientation,
confirmed their direct relationship with group performance. This means that when the
quality of communication increases, within or across groups, the understanding of
information becomes clearer. Consequently, the clearer the ideas are, the higher is the
group performance (Roberts et al., 2003). Likewise, when group members intend to develop
long-term relationships amongst them they try to work collectively, share information and
make communications successful. As a result, the group performance increases (Keyton,
2000; Ural, 2009).
While examining the moderating effect of external resources on the relationship between
dimensions of relationship quality and group performance, we found that external
communication did not interact at all. It could be because in our study we assigned every
group a unique project. Maybe because of this, external communication could not moderate
the relationship between relationship quality and group performance, as ideas bought
into each group, from outside the groups, may not be relevant to the respective project
(Katz et al., 2004). However, the other external resource, instructor’s support interacted with

Based on the results of our study, instructor’s support moderates the relationship
between information sharing and long-term relationship orientation. While moderating
information sharing and group performance, the instructor’s support strengthens the
relationship. When instructor’s support is involved in this relationship, information sharing
becomes more effective because of verification of information shared within or across
groups (Cannon and Homburg, 2001; Lages et al., 2004; Ural, 2009). However, if information
sharing already has a negative effect on group performance then it can further increase by
instructor’s support. Therefore, instructor’s support needs to be specific. That is, instructors
should work toward resolving intra-group conflicts. Instructor’s support also moderates the
relationship between long-term relationship orientation and group performance by further
strengthening the relationship. Instructors facilitate this relationship (Black and Deci, 2000)
and hence students are more prone to achieve good group performance due to their
inclination toward long-term relationship. As a result of long-term relationship orientation
together with instructor’s support and guidance, group members help each other in
completing projects by assisting and guiding them throughout their projects and increasing
their performance (Anderson and Narus, 1990; Ural, 2009).
Implications
This paper has implications for both academics and non-academics. That is, it contributes
to the current literature by examining the different effects of internal and external
organizational factors on group performance along with components of relationship
quality. Group performance in higher education has been a slightly ignored area.
Therefore, this study provides an additional insight about how to work in groups. In line
with this, the findings of this study can help academics to consider group formation
(based on gender and experience) for enhancing the learning experience of students.
Moreover, this study will help instructors to improve their supportive role toward the
completion of group projects.
Our findings can also be helpful for instructors who may consider the available resources
both at individual and group levels while making groups. Furthermore, external resources
can be considered while making groups, as these can help group members in order for
working with like people to produce more output and increasing chances of group learning.
This study can help practitioners, e.g., HR managers, line managers, understand the
important role of group formation toward group performance. Moreover, it will also help
them consider the internal organizational resources and external organizational resources
for the achievement of group goals.
Limitations and future research
Alongside the above implications, we realize that the study has some limitations as well.
The study is conducted in a relatively established university. When conducted in less
developed ones, the results might be different. Besides studying the proposed
relationships from business school’s perspective, these can also be tested in some
organizational setting. Parallel to supervisor’s support, overall, administrative support
can also be studied in the future. Moreover, instructors can also be asked about support
provided by them to students.
Conclusions
This research has been conducted to study the role of group resources and relationship
quality toward group performance. Resources at both levels (i.e. internal and external) have
