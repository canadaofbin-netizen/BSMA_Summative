A B S T R A C T   
Despite the enthusiasm for engaging in interorganizational collaboration to enable digital product innovation, 
firms often face challenges in integrating knowledge across organizational boundaries. Our research examines 
how collaborating firms use Interorganizational Systems (IOS) tools and project coordinators to overcome 
knowledge boundaries in different types of ideation tasks. Drawing on boundary-spanning theory, we concep­
tualize IOS tools as boundary objects, project coordinators as boundary spanners, and the interdependence of 
ideation tasks as moderators of the impacts of boundary objects and spanners on knowledge integration. Our 
findings suggest that IOS tools help overcome syntax and semantic knowledge boundaries by transferring and 
sharing information when the interdependence of idea implementation tasks is high, and project coordinators 
help overcome pragmatic knowledge boundaries by building reciprocity of innovation participants when the 
interdependence of idea generation tasks is high. Our research contributes to value co-creation literature by 
developing a boundary-spanning mechanism to explain the roles of boundary objects and boundary spanners in 
overcoming different knowledge boundaries to enable digital product innovation at the inter-firm level.   
1. Introduction 
As digital technologies become highly distributed and change 
frequently, organizations have advanced from single-firm innovation to 
collaborative innovation across multiple firms [1]. Collaborative inno­
vation focuses on the joint development of digital products by 
combining technical and business resources to generate knowledge 
across organizational boundaries [2,3]. Such innovation is exemplified 
in Samsung and Royal Philips teaming up for an intelligent 
Internet-of-Things healthcare platform [4] to Ericsson and Piaggio 
working on cloud-based connected vehicles [5]. Despite the prevalent 
examples in the industry, there is little scholarly investigation of how to 
co-create value for digital product innovation at the interorganizational 
level [6]. 
Interorganizational Systems (IOS) tools are used as “catalysts and 
enablers for collaboration across organizational boundaries and impor­
tant drivers for value creation in inter-organizational networks” ([7], p. 
288). Prior research focuses on the role of IOS tools in information 
sharing to improve operational efficiency between collaborative firms 
[8–10]. As well as sharing information, firms need to integrate 
domain-specific knowledge to create digital products [11,12]. 
Knowledge integration requires organizations to overcome syntactic 
boundaries by transferring information, semantic boundaries by devel­
oping the shared meaning of the information, and pragmatic boundaries 
by integrating information for new knowledge [13]. Research, to date, 
offers limited insights into how to use IOS tools to overcome these 
knowledge boundaries for digital innovation. 
Interorganizational knowledge integration is challenging because 
collaborative firms have different organizational structures and cul­
tures, causing barriers to sharing and synthesizing knowledge. In addi­
tion to IOS tools, firms need project coordinators to help increase the 
belongingness of team members and manage the conflict within 
boundary-spanning teams [14]. Moreover, the effects of IT and com­
plementary resources on value co-creation are contingent on the in­

integration that leads to value co-creation for digital innovation.

Our research focuses on ideation tasks including idea generation and 
idea implementation because they are two important stages in enabling 
digital innovation [17,18]. IOS tools and project coordinators enable 
digital innovation via interdependent tasks of generating and imple­
menting ideas, and their roles may vary across these two types of tasks 
[16,19]. Therefore, we address the research question: how do IOS tools, 
project coordinators, and ideation task interdependence interact to 
integrate knowledge for digital product innovation? 
Drawing on boundary-spanning theory, we conceptualize IOS tools 
as boundary objects and project coordinators as boundary spanners [13, 
20]. Our research model examines 1) the enabling effects of boundary 
objects and boundary spanners on inter-firm knowledge integration that 
leads to high levels of digital innovation quality; and 2) the moderating 
effects of ideation task interdependence on the relationships between 
boundary resources (i.e., boundary objects; boundary spanners) and 
inter-firm knowledge integration. 
We used the survey method to test our research model using data 
from 209 firms in China. The findings suggest that IOS tools and project 
coordinators are co-specialized assets for digital innovation. Specif­
ically, coordinators are important for idea generation tasks because 
humans are effective in tacit knowledge integration, whereas IOS tools 
are critical for idea implementation tasks because these tools are effec­
tive in transferring codified, explicit knowledge. Our findings further 
suggest that IOS tools cannot directly lead to digital innovation quality 
and their effects are fully mediated by inter-firm knowledge integration, 
while boundary spanners can directly influence the quality of digital 
innovation. 
This research makes three contributions to existing knowledge. First, 
it contributes to the IT-based value co-creation literature by developing 
a boundary-spanning mechanism from a knowledge development 
perspective [21,15]. The boundary-spanning mechanism provides a 
systematic understanding of how IOS tools, humans, and ideation tasks 
interact to integrate knowledge. IOS tools help organizations overcome 
syntactic and semantic knowledge boundaries when the interdepen­
dence of idea implementation tasks is high, while project coordinators 
help overcome pragmatic knowledge boundaries when the interdepen­
dence of idea generation tasks is high [22,13]. 
Second, our research contributes to the IOS literature by examining 
how IOS tools can be used for knowledge integration. Prior research 
largely focuses on the role of IOS tools in improving operational per­
formance [10]. We examine IOS tools in knowledge-intensive activity i. 
e., enabling digital innovation, and suggest that the effects of IOS tools 
on knowledge integration are augmented when the information is pre­
sented in an explicit and codified format. 
Third, our research contributes to the digital innovation literature by 
examining the enablement of digital product innovation at the inter- 
organizational level. Prior research largely focuses on the financial 
performance of digital innovation at a single firm [6]. Our research 
explains how to improve the quality of digital product innovation at the 
inter-firm level from a knowledge integration perspective. 
2. Theoretical foundation 
Because our research focuses on IOS-enabled value co-creation at the 
inter-firm level, we review the literature on IT-based value co-creation 
to highlight key issues to be addressed in this study. We then present 
boundary-spanning theory and explain how this research applies the 
theory to examine the inter-firm knowledge integration for digital 
innovation. 
2.1. IT-based value co-creation 
In the changing IT landscape featured by big data and ubiquitous 
technologies, developing digital products often requires collective in­
telligence across organizations. Firms, therefore, use IT to facilitate 
communication, accelerate innovation speed, and generate innovative 
solutions [2,23]. IT-based value co-creation between firms emerges as 
an important research stream in the IS research field. This research 
stream, however, receives relatively less attention compared to the 
research that examines value creation at a single-firm level [7,15]. 
Specifically, IT-based value co-creation refers to using IT to generate 
business value at the inter-firm level [21,15]. 
We identify three issues in the literature on IT value co-creation and 
explain how our research addresses these issues. First, knowledge inte­
gration is an important aspect of value co-creation because collaborating 
firms need to share information and absorb external information to 
generate new knowledge at the interorganizational level [21,24]. This is 
especially important for digital innovation projects as it involves 
knowledge-intensive activities where participants generate innovative 
ideas and convert ideas into technical solutions [25,17]. Knowledge 
integration at the inter-firm level is challenging because innovation 
participants possess domain-specific knowledge and are bounded by 
their organizational routines and norms [13,26]. 
To effectively integrate domain-specific knowledge across organi­
zational boundaries, firms need to not only overcome the difficulties of 
sharing information but also address the challenges of developing a 
shared vision and beliefs in developing new knowledge [27]. Prior 
research has not sufficiently addressed how IT and organizational re­
sources work together to help overcome various knowledge boundaries 
(i.e., syntax, semantic, pragmatic) for innovation [13]. Furthermore, 
existing literature mainly examines the role of IOS in coordinating 
communication [9,28]. We lack the understanding of how IOS tools 
contribute to knowledge-driven value co-creation in interorganizational 
projects. 
Second, existing literature lacks the investigation of the interde­
pendence of tasks for value co-creation [15,16]. Interdependent tasks 
are channels where collaborating firms share information and resources 
to jointly integrate knowledge [16,29]. Prior research focuses on the 
interdependence of companies’ business processes in supply chain 
management such as decision-making [30] and buyer-supplier re­
lationships [31]. Unlike supply chain management, digital innovation 
relies on generating innovative ideas and converting these ideas into 
technical solutions [17,18]. As a result, the interdependence of idea 
generation and implementation tasks influence IOS tools and humans to 
co-create value. For example, idea generation tasks often deal with tacit 
knowledge. Because this knowledge resides in innovation actors’ minds, 
the integration of such knowledge relies on human-to-human in­
teractions [27]. Idea implementation tasks deal with explicit knowledge, 
which relies on IOS tools to share information [9]. However, there is 
limited research that sheds light on how IOS tools and project co­
ordinators are used to engage in ideation tasks and how their roles may 
differ in different types of boundary-spanning tasks for innovation. 
Finally, prior research mainly focuses on financial performance in 
general (e.g., sales; profits) or relationship-specific performance (e.g., 
shared trust; operational excellence) in supply chain management [7]. 
Few studies have examined IT-based value co-creation for digital 
products. Driven by the increasing importance of digital products, IS 
researchers have called for studies to examine how to enable digital 
product innovation [6]. Because emerging technologies such as artificial 
intelligence and the Internet of Things are highly distributed, knowledge 
to enable digital innovation is often derived from collaboration across 
multiple firms [11]. Therefore, it is useful to examine how to enable 
digital product innovation at the inter-firm level. 
2.2. Boundary-spanning theory 
Boundary-spanning theory suggests that organizations develop 
boundary objects, boundary spanners, and boundary-spanning tasks to 
achieve inter-functional collaboration within or across organizations 
[13,26,16]. Specifically, boundary objects refer to the artifacts that “are 
plastic enough to adapt to local needs and constraints of the several 
parties employing them, yet robust enough to maintain a common

identity across sites” ([32], p. 393). Boundary objects of IOS tools are 
exemplified by prototyping and computer-aided design systems, 
knowledge management systems, and collaboration systems [33,34]. 
Boundary spanners facilitate collaboration across different groups to 
overcome functional, geographical, or professional boundaries [26,11]. 
Boundary spanners are designated project coordinators who facilitate 
the collaboration between firms [13]. Boundary-spanning tasks involve 
interdependent activities that are collectively performed by collabo­
rating organizations [16]. 
The IS research has examined the interactions of boundary spanners 
and boundary objects in shaping boundary-spanning activities within a 
firm or across firms. For example, boundary spanners facilitate the 
emergence of organizational boundary-spanning competence by coor­
dinating communication, strengthening reciprocal relationships, and 
brokering knowledge [26,35]. Boundary objects such as collaboration 
tools help overcome knowledge boundaries by transferring, translating, 
and transforming information [13,22]. Ubiquitous technology provides 
opportunities for delivering new services by facilitating knowledge 
creation across organizational boundaries [36,11]. 
Prior research indicates that the roles of boundary spanners are 
contingent on the nature of the tasks [16,19]. Furthermore, researchers 
suggest that high-level task interdependence implies high knowledge 
barriers, which moderates the relationship between organizational ca­
pabilities (e.g., training, management support) and performance out­
comes [29,37]. In line with these thoughts, boundary objects and 
spanners play different roles in shaping boundary-spanning activities, so 
their effects on knowledge integration are contingent on the interde­
pendence of the tasks to be conducted [16]. 
In our research, we focus on the interdependence of ideation tasks 
because firms must generate ideas and convert these ideas into technical 
solutions to enable digital innovation. We examine idea generation and 
implementation tasks because they are two important stages of enabling 
digital product innovation [17,38]. Through interdependent idea gen­
eration and implementation tasks, collaborating firms jointly develop 
innovation. When the interdependence of idea generation tasks is high, 
boundary-spanning activities are characterized by tacit knowledge ac­
tivities such as articulating ideas and developing a shared version of 
generating digital innovation. When the interdependence of idea 
implementation tasks is high, boundary-spanning activities are charac­
terized by explicit knowledge activities such as sharing codified, explicit 
information across organizations. The interdependence of different 
types of ideation tasks, therefore, implies different knowledge barriers 
for boundary objects and spanners to engage in the process of inte­
grating knowledge. 
3. Research model and hypotheses development 
Focusing on knowledge-driven value co-creation for digital innova­
tion, we develop an integrative model to examine how IOS, project co­
ordinators, and ideation task interdependence interact to integrate 
knowledge at the inter-firm level. Our model, as shown in Fig. 1, draws 
on the nomological framework of IT-based business value creation [15, 
39] and boundary-spanning theory [22,20]. IT business value creation 
literature suggests that IS and organizational resources are antecedents 
to organizational performance [15,40,39]. Therefore, we propose that 
IOS tools and project coordinators are the antecedents to digital inno­
vation quality. 
Using boundary-spanning theory [13,22], we conceptualize IOS 
tools as boundary objects and project coordinators as boundary span­
ners. Boundary objects and boundary spanners enable knowledge inte­
gration at the inter-firm level, leading to high-level digital innovation 
quality. Boundary-spanning activities happen through interdependent 
idea generation and idea implementation tasks [17,16]. Highly coupled 
boundary-spanning activities are reflected in a high-level interdepen­
dence between tasks, and vice versa. The impacts of boundary objects 
and boundary spanners on knowledge integration are moderated by 
interdependent tasks where collaborating firms share information and 
build reciprocity [29,37]. Below, we present our five hypotheses in turn. 
3.1. IOS and inter-firm knowledge integration 
Boundary objects support the integration of knowledge across 
interorganizational boundaries syntactically, semantically, and prag­
matically [13]. IOS tools are technological artifacts that are imple­
mented to operationalize the relationships between collaborating 
organizations [7,33]. From the syntactic perspective, IOS tools increase 
collaborative organizations’ information processing capability by help­
ing transfer knowledge across firms. For example, IOS tools provide a 
common platform where collaborators share the data and their under­
standing for solving problems and for discovering innovation opportu­
nities [2]. Therefore, IOS tools support transferring domain-specific 
knowledge across syntactic boundaries [13]. 
From the semantic perspective, the use of IOS creates shared 
meanings, which allows collaborative organizations to translate 
knowledge. Because collaborators often come from complementary 
domains and have domain-specific knowledge, the shared meaning of 
the information from different domains is important for integrating 
knowledge. Collaborative technologies such as online communication

From the pragmatic perspective, the use of IOS tools facilitates 
learning-by-doing processes where collaborators jointly transform their 
expertise into collective knowledge [13]. Individuals possess different 
skills that are specific to their local context, and knowledge gaps or 
boundaries may arise when collaborators apply domain-specific skills to 
developing digital innovation that involves cross-domain tasks [42]. To 
close these gaps, innovation actors need to experiment with their ideas 
and transform them into value-added output. Interorganizational pro­
totyping systems such as computer-aided design systems (CAD) allow 
innovators to run and share simulations in trialing different conceptions 
for digital prototypes [33]. Hence, we propose the following hypothesis: 
H1: The use of IOS tools is positively associated with inter-firm knowledge 
integration. 
3.2. Boundary spanners and inter-firm knowledge integration 
In collaborative innovation, coordination roles engage in meetings, 
face-to-face discussions, and seminars at different organizational levels 
[20]. Boundary-spanning coordinators help with knowledge integration 
in sharing information and coordinating collaboration activities. First, 
boundary spanners facilitate information flows across domains and 
bridge knowledge gaps from different fields. When innovators work on 
joint tasks, they face challenges in integrating local knowledge into the 
global level because these workers come from different firms and they 
may lack connections with other collaborators. Boundary spanners 
facilitate knowledge integration by establishing communication chan­
nels based on their formal and informal interactions with innovation 
participants. For example, working as knowledge brokers, boundary 
spanners identify knowledge gaps in newly joint groups and frame 
problems in the common language shared across functional teams [35]. 
Second, boundary spanners contribute to interorganizational 
knowledge integration by coordinating divergent views to reduce un­
certainty and build reciprocal relationships among innovation partici­
pants. Frontline innovators often lack a global view because they mainly 
focus on the specific tasks assigned to them in a local context. Conflict or 
dispute may emerge when cross-boundary teams face competing tasks or 
requirements [26]. Project coordinators use formal and informal in­
teractions to communicate and discuss uncertainties and articulate the 
vision of innovation development from a global perspective. These in­
teractions build reciprocity between project members, facilitating the 
synthesis of complementary knowledge for innovation [11]. Hence, we 
propose the following hypothesis: 
H2: The use of boundary spanners is positively associated with inter-firm 
knowledge integration. 
3.3. Moderating effects of task interdependence 
Task interdependence refers to the work processes that involve 
multiple actors who interact with collaborators to perform tasks shared 
across organizations [37]. In our research, we argue that the roles of 
boundary objects and boundary spanners are contingent on the inter­
dependence of two types of ideation tasks. Specifically, we propose that 
the interdependence of idea generation tasks positively moderates the 
effects of boundary spanners on knowledge integration, and the inter­
dependence of idea implementation tasks positively moderates the ef­
fects of boundary objects on knowledge integration. 
Idea generation tasks focus on tacit knowledge because firms work 
on wicked problems at the early stage of innovation and the ideas are yet 
to take shape in an explicit, codified format [17]. For example, partic­
ipants from a collaborative project interact to identify potential market 
needs, explore the requirements of potential customers, and discuss the 
shared vision of product development. The integration of the tacit 
knowledge possessed by individuals who are from different firms pre­
sents a high level of pragmatic knowledge boundaries as participants are 
bounded by their organizational norms and interests [43]. Such kinds of 
integration rely on person-to-person interactions [44]. 
When the interdependence of idea generation tasks is high, innova­
tion participants frequently interact to address the wicked problems by 
combining their knowledge to conceptualize key functionalities of dig­
ital innovation [17]. Frequent interactions strengthen the effects of 
boundary spanners on knowledge integration. When generating ideas, 
the project relies on boundary spanners to help overcome pragmatic 
boundaries by articulating a shared vision, negotiating potential con­
flict, and channeling communications [22]. 
Furthermore, project coordinators help shape the willingness of in­
dividuals to share their knowledge and increase the trust within the 
newly formed team to integrate knowledge [26]. For example, innova­
tion participants from different domains may collect conflicting 
customer requirements in growing digital innovation. Under these sit­
uations, project coordinators reinforce the product development vision 
at the inter-organizational project level and configure different view­
points in developing new knowledge to grow digital product innovation 
[23]. Hence, we propose the following hypothesis: 
H3: The interdependence of idea generation tasks positively moderates 
the effects of boundary spanners on inter-firm knowledge integration. 
During the idea implementation stage, the tacit knowledge about 
innovative ideas has been transformed into a codified format, which is 
ready to be converted to the functionalities of digital innovation. When 
the interdependence of idea implementation tasks is high, innovation 
participants frequently exchange information about realizing the func­
tionalities of digital products [17]. The integration of explicit knowledge 
relies on digital artifacts that help overcome syntactic and semantic 
boundaries by developing a common lexicon and syntax [13]. The 
interdependence of idea implementation tasks, therefore, strengthens 
the effects of IOS tools on knowledge integration. 
For example, when implementing ideas, collaborative teams use user 
case models to capture users’ views that will be used to realize digital 
innovation based on market analysis and customer feedback [34]. IOS 
tools about case models provide a medium for firms to develop shared 
vocabularies and understanding to document customer requirements 
[42]. Furthermore, when the interdependence of idea implementation 
tasks is high, collaborative organizations tend to be overloaded by 
high-volume tasks. Under these circumstances, the effects of IOS tools 
are strengthened because the use of IOS tools allows innovators to ex­
change product data and associated information regardless of time and 
space. Hence, we propose the following hypothesis: 
H4: The interdependence of idea implementation tasks positively mod­
erates the effects of IOS tools on inter-firm knowledge integration. 
3.4. Inter-firm knowledge integration and digital innovation quality 
Inter-firm knowledge integration refers to the combination of indi­
vidual know-how and expertise to achieve collaboration outcomes at the 
interorganizational level [45,46]. Interorganizational knowledge inte­
gration increases digital innovation quality in the following ways. First, 
a high level of knowledge integration implies a broad-scope synthesis of 
complementary knowledge for digital innovation [47]. Compared to 
cross-department integration for innovation within a firm, collaborative 
innovation often occurs through knowledge integration across in­
dustries. The greater the integration scope, the better the organization 
can offer appropriate and targeted product ideas [23]. For example, by 
integrating IoT technology from Samsung with a digital health platform 
from Royal Philips, these two firms developed an IoT-based platform 
where users can access, share, and analyze health data via connected 
devices [4]. 
Second, a high level of knowledge integration implies greater 
reconfiguration capabilities for generating innovation. Compared to 
traditional products, digital products offer fluid boundaries where 
changes happen frequently because technology is highly distributed and 
readily reprogrammable [48]. Superior knowledge integration between

4. Research method 
We test our hypotheses using a field survey. The unit of the analysis is 
an interorganizational, digital innovation project. In this section, we 
first present our measurement development, followed by the approaches 
that we use to validate our constructs. Finally, we present the procedures 
for collecting data. 
4.1. Measurement development 
We operationalize constructs in the survey instrument by drawing on 
validated measurement scales from the literature with a 7-point Likert 
response scale. We operationalized the dependent variable (digital 
innovation quality), independent variables (boundary spanners, inter- 
firm knowledge integration) and moderating variables (idea genera­
tion task interdependence, idea implementation task interdependence) 
as reflective constructs. 
We operationalized the independent variable IOS tools as a formative 
construct [50]. For its measurements, we listed a number of IOS tools 
used in innovation projects based on existing literature [13,33]. To 
confirm and refine our list of tools, we conducted interviews with nine 
project managers in three firms to learn about the IOS tools that have 
been adopted in their projects (see Appendix A for interview details). 
Following the suggestions from our interviewees, we replaced ‘data­
bases’ with ‘shared digital storage’ because the latter is commonly used 
in the industry. Using existing literature and the information collected 
from project managers, the items ‘shared digital storage tools’, ‘proto­
typing tools’, ‘project collaboration tools’, and ‘CAD modeling tools’ 
form the construct IOS tools. 
To exclude alternative explanations, we include control variables 
firm age, size, and R&D investment because they indicate a firm’s in­
ternal R&D characteristics and have impacts on innovation outcomes 
[51,52]. We also include industry as a control variable to account for the 
external environment where a firm operates [51]. Furthermore, we 
include prior relationships, project size, and project cost as 
project-related characteristics, which may influence collaborative 
innovation project outcomes [53]. Table 1 presents the operationaliza­
tion of our constructs. 
4.2. Conceptual validation 
We consulted several colleagues in the IS research field to identify 
and revise the framing issues of our survey instrument. We developed 
measures based on the existing literature and validated our measures 
following Moore and Benbasat’s [56] approach. Specifically, we first 
conducted a two-stage Q-sorting to verify the content validity, conver­
gent validity, and discriminant validity of our measurements. The ac­
curacy at the unlabeled stage is 90%, while the accuracy at the labeled 
stage is 92%. The Q-sorting results suggest good measurement quality. 
Based on the verification, we made minor modifications. We developed 
our instrument in English and then translated it into Chinese. To ensure 
conceptual equivalence, we used the back-translation approach [57]. To 
check face validity, we invited the top managers of three firms to 
comment on the survey. Based on their suggestions, we further phrased 
and framed the questions of the survey instrument. 
4.3. Data collection procedures 
We distributed our survey to 758 organizations that were randomly 
selected from a high-tech organization directory in China.We collected 
data from project managers who are in charge of inter-firm innovation 
projects by using the key informant approach [58]. We asked project 
managers to identify a collaborative innovation project and respond to 
our survey questions. Of the 232 returned surveys, we removed 23 
surveys because of the incompleteness of data. We used 209 surveys for 
the data analysis. The sample size was sufficient to achieve a statistical 
power higher than 0.9 [59]. 
Table 2 presents the demographic characteristics of the respondents 
that we use as our control variables. Our target population consists of 
firms that have collaborated with other firms to co-develop digital 
products using IOS tools. We followed the procedures used by prior 
research to ensure our sample is representative of the target population. 
Specifically, we selected four industries that have a broad presence in 
China’s economy and represent the most advanced high-tech develop­
ment in China [60]. Furthermore, we selected firms that are of different 
ages and sizes to ensure the diversity of our sample [3]. We assess 
nonresponse bias by comparing the earliest 52 respondents (25%) and 
the last 52 respondents (25%) on their organizational characteristics and 
their responses to the principal constructs [61]. There are no statistical 
differences between the two cohorts. Nonresponse bias, therefore, is not 
a major concern in our study. 
5. Analysis and results 
We used variance-based partial least squares (PLS-SEM) to estimate 
the structural equation model [62,63]. Compared with covariance-based 
SEM, PLS-SEM is especially suitable for theory development and for a 
model including both reflective and formative constructs [64,65]. Our 
research focuses on developing a theoretical model of value co-creation 
for digital innovation and the model includes both reflective and 
formative constructs, so PLS-SEM is a suitable method to test our model. 
In this section, we first demonstrate the reliability and validity of our 
measurement model. We then present the results for our hypotheses 
testing, followed by our mediation analysis results. Finally, we test our 
non-hypothesized relationships to investigate if 1) the interdependence 
of idea generation tasks moderates the effects of IOS on inter-firm 
knowledge integration; and 2) the interdependence of idea imple­
mentation tasks moderates the effects of boundary spanners on inter- 
firm integration.

To assess the reliability of reflective measures, we tested indicator 
reliability, construct reliability, convergent validity, and discriminant 
validity [66,65]. As shown in Table 3, all indicators achieve satisfactory 
reliability with outer loadings exceeding 0.70. The reflective measure­
ment models present reliable internal consistency by achieving com­
posite reliability of 0.908 or higher. The internal consistency is further 
evidenced by the average variance extracted (AVE) values, exceeding 
the threshold value of 0.5 [67,68]. 
To examine the discriminant validity of constructs, we used two 
measures: the indicators’ cross-loadings [62] and the criteria developed 
by Fornell and Larcker [67]. Both cross-loadings (as shown in Table 3) 
and the comparison of the square roots of AVE and inter-construct 
correlations with other constructs (as shown in Table 4) demonstrate 
discriminant validity. We further assess the discriminant validity using 
the Heterotrait-Monotrait ratio (HTMT) criterion in Table 5. All values 
were below the conservative threshold value of 0.85 [63], reconfirming 
the established discriminant validity. 
We followed the guidelines of Cenfetelli and Bassellier [69] and 
Petter et al. [50] to validate the formative construct of boundary objects: 
IOS tools. Table 6 shows the results for VIF (multicollinearity test), 
weights (the relative contribution), and loadings (the absolute impor­
tance). The assessment output suggests that multicollinearity is not an 
issue. Because we use single informants to answer our survey questions, 
we further assess the common method variance of our research in Ap­
pendix C. The assessment shows that our results are not subject to the 
issue of common method variance.

squared multiple correlations R2, structural path coefficients, the pre­

When performing the analysis, we selected the path-weighting scheme 
and a stop criterion set at 10−7, with 5000 iterations of resampling and 
the no-sign-change using SmartPLS 3. We assessed all statistical tests at 
the two-tailed 5% significance level. 
To reduce the threats of multicollinearity, we used mean centering 
before creating the interaction terms [70]. Furthermore, VIF values are 
fewer than the recommended threshold of 3.3 (the highest VIF among 
the explanatory variables was 3.188) [71]. The effect size values f2 show 
small to moderate effects, supporting the predictive relevance of the 
endogenous latent variables. Table 7 presents the results of the hy­

5.4. Post hoc analysis 
We performed a post hoc analysis to examine the non-hypothesized 
moderating relationships between boundary objects and spanners and 
task interdependence. These moderating effects are (1) idea imple­
mentation task interdependence moderates the relationship between 
boundary spanners and inter-firm knowledge integration; and (2) idea 
generation task interdependence moderates the relationship between 
boundary objects and inter-firm knowledge integration. First, the 
interaction of boundary spanners and idea implementation task inter­
dependence does not significantly influence inter-firm knowledge inte­
gration (β = −0.020, p > 0.05). Second, the interaction of boundary 
objects and idea generation task interdependence does not have a sig­
nificant impact on inter-firm knowledge integration (β = 0.009, p >
0.05). 
Because the interaction effects of boundary spanners and idea 
implementation tasks and the interaction effects of boundary objects 
and idea generation tasks are insignificant, our post hoc analysis results 
strengthen the finding that boundary objects and boundary spanners are 
co-specialized assets in integrating knowledge. In other words, the ef­
fects of IOS tools on knowledge integration are augmented only when 
the interdependence of idea implementation tasks is high, and the ef­
fects of project coordinators on knowledge integration are augmented 
only when the interdependence of idea generation tasks is high. 
Therefore, we suggest that boundary spanners are important for 
generating ideas that deal with tacit knowledge, whereas boundary 
objects are crucial for implementing ideas that deal with explicit 
knowledge. 
6. Discussion and conclusion 
In this section, we discuss the data analysis results, followed by the 
theoretical contributions of our research. We then present our research 
implications to practice, its limitations and opportunities for future 
research. 
6.1. Discussion of findings 
In this study, we develop a boundary-spanning mechanism to explain 
how IOS tools and boundary spanners lead to knowledge-driven value 
co-creation for digital product innovation. Our findings suggest that 
project coordinators can directly influence digital innovation quality, 
whereas IOS tools cannot. Prior research has suggested that humans are 
crucial for sensing innovation opportunities, formulating problems, and 
converting ideas into technical solutions [27,18]. Our research takes 
these findings further by demonstrating that human agents such as 
project coordinators have direct effects on the quality of digital 
products. 
The effects of IOS tools on digital innovation quality are fully 
mediated by knowledge integration. These findings align with the IS 
business value literature in general, suggesting that the strategic value of 
IT assets is realized via intermediated processes [39]. Our research 
specifically demonstrates that interorganizational knowledge integra­
tion capabilities are critical for IOS tools to create value for digital 
product 
innovation. 
Given 
that 
digital 
innovation 
involves 
knowledge-intensive activities, IOS tools facilitate the combination of 
domain-specific knowledge from a single firm by transferring and 
translating knowledge across organizational boundaries [13]. For 
example, online collaboration tools facilitate information sharing more 
efficiently and CAD tools enable team members to develop shared 
meanings of ideas for designing digital innovation [33]. 
Our findings also suggest that IOS tools and boundary spanners play 
co-specialized roles in the ideation tasks of enabling digital innovation. 
Specifically, the effects of IOS tools are augmented during the idea 
implementation stage because digital artifacts are suitable for trans­
ferring codified, explicit knowledge by helping collaborating firms 
overcome syntax and semantic boundaries [9]. The effects of project 
coordinators are augmented during the idea generation stage because 
transferring tacit knowledge relies on human-to-human interactions to 
help overcome pragmatic boundaries [13,27]. 
To support these insights, we performed post hoc analysis to test 1) 
the interaction effects of IOS and the interdependence of idea generation 
tasks and 2) the interaction effects of boundary coordinators and the 
interdependence of idea implementation tasks. The results show that 
these interaction effects do not hold, further strengthening our findings 
that the role of boundary objects and spanners in knowledge integration 
depends on the tasks that are associated with tacit or explicit knowledge. 
Specifically, explicit knowledge integration relies on the information 
medium that transfers and shares information between teams [25]. 
Therefore, when idea implementation tasks are present, the effects of 
IOS tools on integrating explicit knowledge are increased. Tacit 
knowledge integration relies on team members’ willingness and trust to 
collaborate and project coordinators can help build reciprocal re­
lationships within the team [26,72]. Therefore, when idea generation 
tasks are present, the effects of boundary spanners on integrating tacit 
knowledge are increased. 
6.2. Theoretical contributions 
Our research contributes to the following literature streams. First, 
this study contributes to the IT-based value co-creation literature by 
providing a boundary-spanning mechanism to explain how to use IOS 
tools and project coordinators to engage in different stages of ideation 
tasks to integrate knowledge across organizational boundaries. Prior 
research has not developed a systematic understanding of how these 
factors interact to impact value co-creation. Our boundary-spanning 
mechanism specifically contributes to the knowledge perspective of 
IT-based value co-creation by explaining the role of IOS tools and project 
coordinators in integrating knowledge at an inter-firm digital innova­
tion project [21]. 
Furthermore, a key finding of this study is the moderating effects of 
task interdependence. Prior research suggests that high-level in­
terdependencies positively moderate the effects of IT on value co- 
creation, but has not addressed how the effects may vary across 
different types of tasks [15]. Our research advances this discourse by 
examining two types of ideation tasks in digital innovation develop­
ment. Our findings demonstrate that the interdependence of idea 
implementation tasks only moderates the effects of IOS tools on 
knowledge integration, whereas the interdependence of idea generation 
tasks moderates the effects of project coordinators on knowledge inte­
gration. We, therefore, suggest that IOS tools and project coordinators 
play a co-specialized role in integrating knowledge. Specifically, IOS 
tools are important for explicit knowledge integration by sharing in­
formation to overcome syntax and semantic knowledge boundaries, 
whereas project coordinators are important for implicit knowledge 
integration by developing reciprocity among innovation actors to 
overcome pragmatic knowledge boundaries [13]. 
Second, our research contributes to the IOS literature by addressing 
how IOS tools and organizational resources enable knowledge integra­
tion for digital innovation. The literature on IOS-based business value 
predominantly focuses on the role of IOS in information sharing at the 
operational level [8,7]. Our research suggests that IOS tools alone

cannot lead to digital innovation at the interorganizational level. Their 
performance impacts are realized through knowledge integration. 
Moreover, firms need to use the IOS tools in conjunction with 
co-specialized resources such as project coordinators to realize 
inter-firm knowledge integration. Specifically, IOS tools are more 
important when the knowledge barriers in converting ideas to technical 
solutions are high, and project coordinators are more important when 
the knowledge barriers in generating ideas are high. 
Third, prior research mainly examines the enablement of digital 
product innovation at a single-firm level. Among these studies, value is 
captured using market-facing or financial metrics of digital innovation 
[6]. Our research advances the development of the digital innovation 
literature by examining how digital innovation is enabled across firms. 
Research suggests that the integration of domain-specific knowledge is 
crucial for digital innovation at the inter-firm level [11,46]. However, 
the understanding of the antecedents of cross-boundary knowledge 
integration is currently lacking. Our research addresses this issue by 
demonstrating how boundary objects, spanners, and ideation tasks 
interact to integrate cross-domain knowledge for digital innovation. 
Furthermore, different from prior research focusing on financial per­
formance for value realization, our research examines the quality of 
digital innovation in new features and technical capabilities. 
6.3. Practical implications 
Our research offers a number of insights for practitioners to partic­
ipate in collaborative innovation. First, collaborative firms need to 
invest in both technical and human resources to enable digital innova­
tion at the inter-firm level. IOS tools and project coordinators play 
different roles in facilitating knowledge integration activities across 
firms. IOS tools facilitate knowledge integration by developing infor­
mation in a common format with shared structure and meanings. Project 
coordinators facilitate knowledge integration by resolving the conflicts 
that arise from collaboration and by building reciprocity and trust be­
tween collaborating firms. 
Second, practitioners need to invest in boundary-spanning resources 
for ideation tasks. Our findings suggest that IOS and project coordinators 
are co-specialized assets during the ideation process. As human-to- 
human interactions are vital for developing tacit knowledge, collabo­
rative organizations need to encourage project coordinators to actively 
engage in the collaboration process especially when the interdepen­
dence of tasks is high. When implementing ideas, information for 
enabling digital innovation becomes codified and explicit. Collaborative 
organizations, therefore, need to effectively use IOS to transfer explicit 
knowledge. 
Our research further suggests that collaborative organizations may 
avoid a high level of investment in IOS tools at the idea generation stage 
of enabling digital innovation. Because our research findings demon­
strate that IOS tools become more important during the idea imple­
mentation stage, collaborating firms can keep a necessary level of 
investment in IOS tools at an early stage of the innovation and increase 
the investment when idea implementation tasks are dominant in the 
collaboration. By doing so, firms can spare resources to invest in project 
coordinators at the early stage as they are important for articulating the 
shared version of the project and for building reciprocity and trust 
among innovation workers from different organizations. 
6.4. Limitations and future research 
Our research has several limitations, which provide opportunities for 
future research. First, we identify R&D and product managers who are in 
charge of a collaboration project to be the most knowledgeable infor­
mant following the key informant approach. Prior research has sug­
gested that some project coordinators can emerge from work practices 
and may not be the individuals who are nominated at the start of the 
project [26]. In our research, designated boundary-spanning project 
managers oversee the entire project, so they are the most informative 
respondents to answer our survey questions. Furthermore, single in­
formants may generate response biases, although our assessment shows 
that common method variance is not a major concern of our research 
findings. Future research could use a dyadic design to collect data from 
pairs of project coordinators, one in each collaborating firm [31]. 
Second, we expect our boundary-spanning mechanisms to be 
extended to other business contexts. We consider the interdependence of 
ideation tasks to be critical in boundary-spanning processes for digital 
innovation. In other business contexts such as supply chain manage­
ment, future research may examine the interdependence of decision- 
making or other business practices as boundary-spanning tasks. 
Furthermore, the four industries we selected may not fully represent the 
firms in general that participate in interorganizational digital innovation 
projects. We encourage future research to test our model in other in­
dustries and extend the model by adding industry-specific factors. 
Third, our study used a cross-sectional research design to measure 
constructs at a point in time through the survey research method. 
Although the survey method provides a high level of generalizability, it 
offers limited in-depth, context-based explanations. For example, idea 
generation and implementation can be iterative during the process of 
enabling digital innovation. Future research could use the case study 
research method to explore how IOS tools and project coordinators are 
used in the iterative processes of ideation. 
Apart from methodological opportunities, future research can also 
use alternative theoretical perspectives to investigate how to co-create 
digital products at interorganizational level. For example, prior 
research suggests that governance is important for the management of 
inter-firm collaboration [21,73]. Future research could use IT gover­
nance theory to examine the influence of different governance mecha­

Appendix A Interview Details 
We conducted interviews with key stakeholders to validate and 
adapt the measurements of IOS tools we identified from prior literature. 
Based on our targeted industries, We contacted five firms to explore 
their use of IOS tools in collaborative innovation activities. Three firms 
agreed to participate in the project. We then conducted interviews with 
three project managers at each firm. In total, we conducted nine in­
terviews. Each interview lasted for around 45–60 minutes. We recorded 
and transcribed each interview to ensure the reliability of the informa­
tion. We asked the interviewees to identify a collaborative innovation 
project that had been recently completed and to describe the IOS tools 
they used for the project, and their roles in the project they identified. 
Appendix B Assessment of Common Method Variance 
Our research is subject to the threat of common method variance 
because we collected the survey data using a single-informant approach 
[75]. To mitigate the threats, we kept respondents uninformed about the 
constructs that we were measuring. We also offered respondents the 
choice of answering questions anonymously to reduce respondents’ 
evaluation apprehension and to minimize social desirability bias. 
We used two approaches to assess potential common method vari­
ance in our research. First, we conducted Harmon’s one-factor test by 
entering all the constructs into a principal components factor analysis 
[75]. One constrained factor accounted for 35.6% of the variance (<
