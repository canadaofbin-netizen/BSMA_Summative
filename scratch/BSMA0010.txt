With the increasing popularity of organizational sensemaking in the literature, sensemaking
capability of ﬁrms attracts many researchers and practitioners from different ﬁelds. Nev-
ertheless, sensemaking capability is rarely addressed in the new product development
(NPD) project teams in the technology and innovation management literature. Speciﬁcally,
we know little about what team sensemaking capability is, its ingredients and beneﬁts, and
how it works in NPD projects (e.g., its antecedents and consequences). By investigating 92
NPD project teams, we found that (1) team sensemaking capability, which is composed of
internal and external communication, information gathering, information classiﬁcation,
building shared mental models, and taking experimental actions, has a positive impact on
the information implementation and speed-to-market; (2) information implementation and
speed-to-market mediate the relationship between team sensemaking capability and new
product success; and (3) team sensemaking capability mediates the relationship between
team processes and information implementation and partially mediates the relationship
between team processes and speed-to-market. We also found that team autonomy, interper-
sonal trust among team members, and open-mindedness of team members positively inﬂu-
ence the development of team sensemaking capability. Theoretical and managerial
implications of the study ﬁndings are discussed.

health care (Albolino et al., 2007; Davidson, 2010;
Kitzmiller et al., 2010; Manojlovich, 2010), and battle
ﬁelds (Jensen, 2009). In those studies, researchers
indicate that sensemaking is an ongoing process of
meaning construction, which reﬂects how team
members make interpretations of actions and events,
thus making ambiguous situations more orderly, and

few studies mention about it as a speciﬁc capability
(Weick et al., 2005). Awazu (2004), for instance,
notes that sensemaking is an important capability for
organizations and groups because it integrates peo-

as constituting an ability to reconﬁgure existent
knowledge to solve current problems. Nevertheless,
we know less about how to conceptualize and then
operationalize the team sensemaking capability in
the technology innovation management (TIM) litera-
ture. Also, from a managerial point of view, the ante-
cedents and consequences of sensemaking capability
in teams warrant an empirical investigation.
In order to deal with the issues mentioned earlier,
based on the studies of Weick (1995), Weick et al.
(2005), and Neill et al. (2007), we put forward
that team sensemaking capability can be conceptua-
lized as a compendium of interrelated routines
and practices about ‘what’ the new technology and
market- and project-related information mean, ‘how’
it is assimilated and interpreted, and ‘which’ actions
are considered for a better product development
process in teams. In a sense, team sensemaking
capability indicates the practices by which team
members (1) interpret the technological-, market-,
and project-related information/knowledge through
interaction with others; (2) construct their cognitive
accounts that allow them to comprehend the product
development project as a whole; and (3) respond
to market-, technology-, and product-related events
collectively. Also, regarding the operationalization
of team sensemaking capability, based on the gene-
ric organizational/team sensemaking literature, we
argue that team sensemaking capability is a second-
order construct composed of internal/external com-
munication (Weick et al., 2005; Neill et al., 2007),
information gathering (Thomas et al., 1993; Neill
et al., 2007), information classiﬁcation (Akgün et al.,
2006b), building shared mental models (Bogner and
Barr, 2000; Neill et al., 2007), and taking experimen-
tal actions (Bogner and Barr, 2000; Weick et al.,
2005). Next, for the antecedents and consequences of
team sensemaking capability, based on the study of
Akgün et al. (2005), we used the components of team
processes, such as trust between team members, team
member familiarity, and team member proximity, as
determinants. As team sensemaking capability is a
social act that is grounded both in individual activity
and social interactions (Weick, 1995; Maitlis, 2005),
it is necessary to pay attention to psychological proc-
esses and interrelations between team members that
enable sensemaking (Reuben, 2007). Indeed, sense-
making capability is a communicative practice in
which people interpret their environment and the sur-
roundings of the project, technology/market-related
information, as well as the stimuli through the inter-
action with others. Also, as team sensemaking capa-
bility requires ﬂow of information to bring out
meaning and obtain plausible interpretations (Daft
and Weick, 1984), trust, familiarity, and proximity
enable team members to capture the information they
need. In addition to the components of interpersonal
relationships, based on the studies of Ashmos and
Nathan (2002) and Neill et al. (2007), we also incor-
porated the autonomy and open-mindedness vari-
ables as the determinants of team sensemaking
capability. In fact, Neill et al. (2007) reveal the posi-
tive relationship between open-mindedness and
sensemaking capability at the organizational level,
which warrants an empirical investigation at the team
level. As team sensemaking capability requires the
changing of the existing thought or behavior patterns
by exploring different ways of thinking and doing
things (Ashmos and Nathan, 2002), it unveils the
importance of open-mindedness that enables teams
to question long-held practices and beliefs (Sinkula
et al., 1997). Regarding the autonomy variable,
Ashmos and Nathan (2002) suggest an empirical
study for their advice to teams that they should
increase their autonomy to be more successful at
sensemaking. Finally, consistent with TIM literature,
as the consequences of team sensemaking capability,
we investigate speed-to-market, information imple-
mentation,1 and new product success to test the team
sensemaking capabilityproject outcome link. For

that ‘the greater speed-to-market, the greater the
competitive advantage over later entrants’. Informa-
tion implementation (i.e., detection and correction
of errors during the NPD process) is also identiﬁed
as an outcome in this study because knowledge is

2. Team sensemaking capability
Sensemaking capability is the team’s ability to inter-
pret, understand, and construct meaning on the
project-, technology-, and market-related informa-
tion, and then act based on that information to com-
plete the project successfully2 (Gioia and Chittipeddi,
1991). Especially, NPD teams have a better under-
standing of the uncertainties they face during the
product development processes by developing sense-
making capability (Ashmos and Nathan, 2002).
Sensemaking capability, for instance, helps teams
to identify events that recur in the environment and
make them more predictable (Weick, 1995), and
structure the information gathered to ﬁnd out mean-
ingful patterns in customer tendencies, competitor
strategies, and sectorial trends. Also, team sensemak-
ing capability provides a body of knowledge about
project-related activities, such that sensemaking
capability helps teams to share the summarized infor-
mation
with
all
team
members
and
combine
customer–competitor information, tacit knowledge,
technological competency, insights, and ideas into the
new product concept (Dougherty et al., 2000; Akgün
et al., 2006b). For example, Mohrman et al. (2003,
p. 9), in this respect, wrote that ‘Several engineers
may examine surprising test results and share their
different disciplinary perspectives and experience-
based tacit understandings about why these results
occurred. This form of collective sensemaking,
entailing personal links between people with different
knowledge, can yield new knowledge. Knowledge
can be linked across the organization if it can be
accessed through interactions, or transactions’. Next,
team sensemaking capability develops a collective
belief and constructs shared mental models, shaping
what people perceive as technological, marketing,
and organizational realities. In a sense, sensemaking
capability is instrumental in shaping product devel-
opment process regarding the nature of new informa-
tion, rendering it more comprehensible, meaningful,
or desirable, and therefore more legitimate.

three-step perspective (noticing, interpretation, and
actioning)3 (see Weick, 1995; Weick et al., 2005), we
further argue that, from an operational perspective,
team sensemaking capability has ﬁve subdimensions
in the NPD context including the internal/external
communication (Weick et al., 2005; Neill et al.,
2007), information gathering (Daft and Weick, 1984;

Barr, 2000; Weick et al., 2005). Speciﬁcally, internal
communication is the vehicle through which team
members from different functional areas share infor-

et al. (2005) suggest that ‘sensemaking is, impor-

ity requires talking, interaction, conversation, argu-
ment, and dialogue with others where teams offer a
platform to talk, argue, and interpret (Ashmos and
Nathan, 2002). In this way, team members reduce
equivocality through social negotiation of interpreta-
tions and develop an understanding of their environ-
ment (Neill et al., 2007).
External communication shows the degree of
contact with outsiders, such as customers and suppli-
ers (Brown and Eisenhardt, 1995). Team sensemak-
ing capability has an external focus as it depends on
the availability of information from the environment.
For instance, each team member accesses not only
self-contained information from his or her functional
background as intended but also knowledge from
diverse external personal networks (Edmondson and
Nembhard, 2009).
Information gathering indicates the collection of
customer- and competitor-related information by
salespeople and market research personnel, forming
a customer council, meeting with users, creating
mechanisms for integrating customer requirements
into product design, etc. (Neill et al., 2007). Here,
gathering environmental information provides a raw
material for sensemaking capability, and collected
information is progressively assimilated and focused
on team actions.
Information classiﬁcation includes labeling, index-
ing, sorting, abstracting, and coding information
(Akgün et al., 2006b). In a team setting, organizing or
coding information into meaningful clusters impacts
the team effectiveness and the rate at which teams
make sense (Akgün et al., 2006b), and allows team
members to verify ﬁndings by comparing similar
cases, the past and the present and to answer task-
speciﬁc questions. Also, when all information is
broken down into separate categories that concentrate
just on a single area, extraneous ‘noise’is ﬁltered out.
Shared mental models are knowledge structures
and shared understandings held by team members
that enable them to form accurate explanations and
expectations for the task, and in turn, to coordinate
their actions during the NPD process (Dougherty
et al., 2000).As Neill et al. (2007) suggest for organi-
zations, through these shared mental models, NPD
teams make sense of the situation by encoding and
assigning meaning to environmental cues. Shared
mental models also help the teams distill the
voluminous data and perceptions they gather from a
complex and turbulent market environment in a more
manageable set of cognitive categories.
As the last component of the sensemaking capabil-
ity, experimental action is an improvisational process
to test customers’ reactions to a new product’s pro-
totype on a trial basis (Jeong and Brower, 2008). The
literature indicates that although interpretation is a
component of sensemaking, actual sensemaking can
only occur as action is taken. For instance, under
conditions of ambiguity and uncertainty, teams are
counseled to undertake experimental actions in an
attempt to collect information about the environment
(Bogner and Barr, 2000). Bogner and Barr (2000,
p. 220), for instance, wrote that ‘These actions are
consistent with action-based sensemaking in that they
are undertaken with little knowledge ex ante about
whether the action will be successful. Thus, while the
intent is to gain advantage, the actions are experimen-
tal’. Brown and Eisenhardt (1995) also reveal that
product teams who engage in more experiential or
improvisational product design through frequent
iterations develop products more quickly, such that
actions create the necessary conditions for further
action (Weick, 1995) and produce an essential part of
the environment in which they occur.
Having established the characteristics of team
sensemaking capability, we now develop arguments
regarding the role of team processes on the team
sensemaking capability and how team sensemaking
capability impacts a project’s and a product’s success.
3. Hypothesis development
3.1. Antecedents of team
sensemaking capacity
In this study, we investigate the interpersonal trust,
team autonomy, team member familiarity and prox-
imity, and open-mindedness as the antecedents or
drivers of the team sensemaking capability in NPD
projects.
Interpersonal trust is the extent to which a person
is conﬁdent in and willing to act on the basis of the
words, actions, and decisions of another (McAllister,
1995). We discuss that interpersonal trust fosters
sensemaking capability by dissolving psychological
barriers that prevent both explicit and tacit knowl-
edge sharing between team members, and by improv-

Interpersonal trust also strengthens the combination

H1: Interpersonal trust is positively related to NPD
team sensemaking capability.
Team autonomy indicates the extent to which the
team is responsible for its own work activities
(Gerwin and Moffat, 1997).An effective sensemaking
capability necessitates the NPD team members to be
autonomous. In fact, sensemaking capability breeds a
questioning process of organizational routines and
processes (Klein et al., 2010), which can only be
achieved by autonomy. For example, autonomy helps
team members to keep the team from performing
outlived applications that lost their usefulness as they
have enough opportunity and authority for free think-
ing (Ashmos and Nathan, 2002). Also, sensemaking
capability necessitates fast decisions and quick
actions that can only be managed by autonomous
teams that have the authority and responsibility to
make fast decisions based on the real-time informa-
tion (Moorman and Miner, 1998). Therefore:
H2: Team autonomy is positively related to NPD
team sensemaking capability.
Team member familiarity refers to interactions
between the group members (Harrison et al., 2003).
Familiar team members possess more knowledge
about each others’ skills, perspectives, and interper-
sonal styles, which can facilitate coordination of
efforts and improve the use of specialized informa-
tion as each one of them is aware of who knows what
(Akgün et al., 2006a). This high-level involvement
in information sharing fosters sensemaking capabil-
ity by establishing shared mental models that help
team members to develop cognitive maps of their
environment (Weick, 1995). In addition, familiar
people feel more comfortable about expressing their
disagreement, alternative perspectives, and judg-
ments that improve the sensemaking capability
(Ashmos and Nathan, 2002). Further, as prior inter-
actions between team members reduce uncertainty
and anxiety about social acceptance during the NPD
project, and promote interpersonal attraction and
cohesiveness, it results in higher level of information
sharing between team members, which facilitates
sensemaking capability. Therefore:
H3: Team member familiarity is positively related to
NPD team sensemaking capability.
Team member proximity means bringing people
from different departments together into closer physi-
cal locations that enable easier and more frequent
interactions between team members (Kahn and
McDonough, 1997). Proximity enhances team sense-
making capability by facilitating face-to-face interac-
tions, which are considered the richest medium of
information sharing as they allow immediate feed-
back so that understanding can be checked and inter-
pretations can be corrected (Koskinen et al., 2003).
Proximity that allows simultaneous interaction of
multiple cues, including body language, facial expres-
sion, and tone of voice, which convey knowledge
beyond the spoken message, also improves team
sensemaking capability by shaping and negotiating
the meanings (Koskinen et al., 2003). Therefore:
H4: Team member proximity is positively related to
NPD team sensemaking capability.
Open-mindedness indicates the team members’
receptivity to new and possibly different ideas (Neill
et al., 2007). As open-minded teams have the will-
ingness to critically evaluate the operational routines
in order to accept new ideas (Calantone et al., 2002)
and revise interpretations based on new information
(Maitlis and Sonenshein, 2010), they enhance their
sensemaking capability. Indeed, mental models hold
images of how the world works are formed by the
successes and failures of the past (Jeong and Brower,
2008). As time passes, these models may no longer
hold true and thus can be manipulated and changed
when they represent a surrogate reality for the per-
ceived events (Sinkula et al., 1997). Open-minded
team members who are comfortable with extraordi-
nary information and situations also notice more cues
that help in anticipating environmental changes and
events that facilitate sensemaking capability (Weick,
1995). Therefore:
H5: Open-mindedness is positively related to NPD
team sensemaking capability.

literature. As product development process is an
uncertain path through shifting markets and tech-

to cope with the unclear and changing environment
(Eisenhardt and Tabrizi, 1995). Here, sensemaking
capability helps team members to develop cognitive
maps of their environment (Weick, 1995) by display-
ing shared interpretations of the environmental situ-

cognitive perspectives (Bogner and Barr, 2000) and
timely integration of critical information (Edmond-
son and Nembhard, 2009) that leads to the discovery
of alternative technological solutions. Sensemaking
capability also helps team members to act as a single
unit to speed up product development efforts. For
example, the marketing personnel contributes to the
project by interpreting users’ requests; in turn, the
designers aid the project by drawing technical proto-
type details, and the engineering department assists
the project by testing prototypes (Akgün et al.,
2006a). Here, the higher the sensemaking capability
is, the higher the interrelationships between team
members would be. Therefore, it is hypothesized
that:
H6: Team sensemaking capability is positively
related to (1) information implementation and (2)
speed-to-market.
New product success refers to the extent to which
a project meets its expected commercial objectives
(Cooper and Kleinschmidt, 1987). Many scholars
discuss the critical factors that have positive inﬂu-
ence on new product success, such as interpersonal
conditions for teamwork, speed-to-market, and infor-
mation implementation (Eisenhardt and Tabrizi,
1995; Edmondson and Nembhard, 2009). Based on
the study of Akgün et al. (2006a), new product
success depends on the process effectiveness ingre-
dients, namely speed-to-market and information
implementation, such that process effectiveness
mediates the relationship between the sensemaking
capability and product effectiveness. Indeed, sense-
making capability does not guarantee the commercial
success of a new product. New product success
depends on how team members proﬁciently solve the
customer- and product-related issues and whether the
new product enters the market on time (Akgün et al.,
2006a). For instance, NPD teams can only reach the
best ﬁt solutions according to the constraints and
conditions of a speciﬁc environment in which there is
no single deﬁnition and solution for every strategic
situation by revising prototypes according to cus-
tomer preferences (Neill et al., 2007). As customers
not only demand higher levels of quality in new prod-
ucts but also expect the latest innovations, translating
customer requirements into product speciﬁcations is
a prerequisite for a new product success. Also, rapid
product development is usually more productive and
lowers cost, as lengthy time in product development
tends to waste resources on peripheral activities,
changes, and mistakes (Eisenhardt and Tabrizi,
1995). Therefore, it is hypothesized that:
H7: Process effectiveness, such as (1) information
implementation and (2) speed-to-market, mediates
the relationship between the team sensemaking capa-
bility and product effectiveness.
3.3. Mediating effect of sensemaking
capability between team processes
and process effectiveness
Finally, in NPD literature, there are also pieces of
evidence that team process variables affect the
process effectiveness variables. Zirger and Hartley
(1996), for instance, reveal that ‘Face-to-face com-
munication that occurs when team members are
located together helps to accelerate development by
increasing mutual understanding of constraints, limi-
tations and potential problems’. Kessler and Chakra-
barti (1996) suggest that without team autonomy, too
many formal reviews and approval processes inevi-
tably result in a longer product development time.
However, as we mentioned earlier in H6, sensemak-
ing capability also impacts the process effectiveness.
Taking into account these contributions, we propose
that sensemaking capability mediates the relationship
between the antecedent variables and process effec-
tiveness. Indeed, if there is not any high-level com-
munication among team members, which is one of
the main dimensions of sensemaking capability, ante-
cedents like trust, autonomy, or open-mindedness

4. Research method
4.1. Sampling
To test the hypotheses, multi-item scales adopted
from prior studies for the measurement of constructs
were used. All constructs were measured using ﬁve-
point Likert scales ranging from ‘strongly disagree’
(1) to ‘strongly agree’(5). By using the parallel trans-
lation method, items were ﬁrst translated into Turkish
by one person and then retranslated into English by a
second person. The two translators then jointly rec-
onciled all the differences. The suitability of the
Turkish version of the questionnaires was then pre-
tested by 10 part-time graduate students working in
industry and involved in at least one NPD project.
After reﬁning the questionnaire, based on interviews
with the pretest subjects, the questionnaires were dis-
tributed and collected by one of the authors, applying
the ‘personally administrated questionnaire’ method.
The initial sample consisted of 180 ﬁrms in the
industrial zone of Turkey, near Istanbul. These ﬁrms
were selected because they develop new products and
export them to other countries, such as the European
Union, Arabic countries, Central Asia, and Russia, as
identiﬁed by the Istanbul Chamber of Industry. In
addition, these ﬁrms are organized and managed
based on the Western management style; that is, they
operate in accordance with International Standardi-
zation Organization and European quality standards.
First, the ﬁrms’ managers were contacted by tel-
ephone and the aim of our study was explained to
them. Among the 180 ﬁrms contacted, we received
92 usable returns concerning NPD projects. During
the sampling, to assess the product performance
more accurately, we set the condition that the prod-
ucts used in this study must have been commercial-
ized and launched into the marketplace for at least six
months (Lynn et al., 2000). Also, the team size was
an important constraint such that it should contain
more than three members. Furthermore, to reduce the
possible problems associated with single sourcing,
we targeted the product or project manager to com-
plete the survey. These managers were chosen
because (1) product/project managers are likely to
have a ‘bigger-picture view’ of NPD projects than
other team members; (2) project managers have a
broader view of each member’s behaviors than other
team members; and (3) it is possible that some team
members in the study had preconceived opinions and
expectations regarding differences in work behaviors
for other team members, such as stereotypes; thus,
product/project managers were expected to provide
more reliable and objective data. After the respond-
ents were selected, each respondent was informed
that his or her responses would remain anonymous
and would not be linked to them individually, or to
their companies, or products. This was done to assure
anonymity, increasing the motivation of informants
to cooperate without the fear of potential reprisals.
The respondents were assured that there were no
right or wrong answers and that they could answer
questions as honestly and forthrightly as possible.
Further, a cover story was developed to prevent the
respondents from ﬁlling out the questionnaire as if
the measurement of the predictor variable is con-
nected with or related to the measures of the criterion
variable (Podsakoff et al., 2003).
Several industries were included, namely telecom-
munication, software, ﬁnancial services, manufactur-
ing and machinery, chemical, food, and material. The
products were software (44.7%), consumer services
(25.3%), and industrial materials, parts, and services
(29.8%).

with a view to assessing whether teams were meeting
or exceeding proﬁt and sales expectations. This
operationalization was similar to Hackman’s (1987)

4.3. Measure validity and reliability
To assess the construct validity of the measurement
instruments, a similar procedure to that of Kleijnen
et al. (2007) was performed using reﬂective indicators
for all constructs (seeAppendixA).Anull model with
no structural relationships was estimated, and then its
reliability was evaluated by means of composite scale
reliability (CR) and average variance extracted (AVE)
(Fornell and Larcker, 1981; Chin, 1998). For all meas-
ures, the partial least squares (PLS)-based CR was
well above the cutoff value of 0.70, and AVE values
were well beyond or close to the threshold levels
suggested by Fornell and Larcker (1981). In addition,
the convergent validity was evaluated by inspecting
the standardized loadings of the measures on their
respective constructs (Chin, 1998), and all measures
were found to exhibit standardized loadings that
exceed 0.40 (see Appendix A). Next, as a check for
discriminant validity, as suggested by Fornell and
Larcker (1981), the square root of AVE for each
construct is greater than the latent factor correlations
between pairs of constructs (see Table 1). Moreover,
as suggested by Chin (1998) and Kleijnen et al.
(2007), the theta matrix (Y) was inspected, and no
item was found to cross-load higher on another con-
struct than it did on its associated construct. In addi-
tion, the correlations of the residual terms across
blocks did not exceed |0.2| (Kleijnen et al., 2007).
Consequently, it was determined that all constructs
exhibit satisfactory discriminant validity.
4.4. Hypothesis testing
We used PLS technique to test our model. The choice
was motivated by several considerations. First,
according to Fornell and Bookstein (1982), PLS
avoids many of the restrictive assumptions underlying
maximum likelihood techniques and ensures against
improper solutions and factor indeterminacy. Second,
PLS is insensitive to sample size considerations and
handles both very small and very large samples with
more ease than does structural equation modeling
(SEM) (Hair et al., 2006). Chin and Newsted (1999,
p. 314), for example, wrote that ‘(For PLS) Power
analysis based on the portion of the model with the
largest number of predictors. Minimal recommenda-
tions range from 30 to 100 cases. (For Covariance
based SEM) Ideally based on power analysis of spe-
ciﬁc model – minimal recommendations range from
200 to 800’. Finally, PLS handles both reﬂective and
formative constructs (Hair et al., 2006).
PLS-Graph 3.0, which allows for explicit estima-
tion of latent variable scores, and the bootstrapping
resampling method were used to test the proposed
model (Chin, 1998). This procedure entailed gener-
ating 500 subsamples of cases randomly selected,
with replacement from the original data. Path coefﬁ-
cients were then generated for each randomly
selected subsample. T-statistics were calculated for
all coefﬁcients, based on their stability across the
subsamples, indicating which links were statistically
signiﬁcant.

4.5. Discussion and implications
This study empirically showed that team sensemak-
ing capability is important to enhance both informa-
tion implementation and speed-to-market. While
previous studies investigated the role of a variety of
factors such as IT usage (Barczak et al., 2008), deci-
sion making (Dayan and Elbanna, 2011), strategic
market orientation (Mu and Di Benedetto, 2011), and
NPD climate (Parry et al., 2009) on the speed-to-
market and process effectiveness, our study speciﬁ-
cally demonstrated the impact of team sensemaking
capability on the process effectiveness. We found that
team sensemaking capability helps NPD teams to
take outstanding jobs uncovering product problem
areas
with
which
customers
were
dissatisﬁed,
improve knowledge implementation, and share the
information quickly that leads to the detection and
correction of product-related errors accurately. In
addition, team sensemaking capability decreases the
time spent for NPD by helping team members to
organize the market and technical information in a
meaningful way and by reducing project-related
complexity.
This study also empirically demonstrated that
when team members believe that their teammates are
professional in their jobs, dedicated to their respon-
sibilities as well as duties, respond to the problems
constructively and with more care, and have the will-
ingness to listen to the work-related problems (i.e.,
interpersonal trust), teams can (1) communicate
internally and externally; (2) gather and classify the
information; (3) build a shared mental model; and (4)
take more effective and successful experimental
actions. These ﬁndings support the previous studies
(Dirks and Ferrin, 2001; Dreu and Vianen, 2001;
Muethel et al., 2012). As such, our ﬁndings are par-
allel with empirical studies that show that interper-
sonal trust enhances communication and information
sharing (Dirks and Ferrin, 2001), and promotes inter-
personal
relationships
and
collaborative
culture
(Kyriazis et al., 2012). However, at the same time, it
should be noted that our study compiled the frag-
mented studies on the information processing and
communication variables under the umbrella of team
sensemaking
capability.
Speciﬁcally,
our
study
empirically showed that interpersonal trust entails
individuals’ beliefs in the goodwill of their team-
mates and conﬁdence in their ability to complete a
task and succeed, and thereby inﬂuences their volun-
tary and spontaneous rather than systematic and
planned activities. In addition, the results indicated
that interpersonal trust entails a prediction about the
team members’ behaviors, reducing the perceived
risk and opportunism, and promoting cooperative
problem-solving activity in groups (Zaheer et al.,
1998). This study also showed that when top man-
agement does not interfere with the task undertaken
within a team, so that team members have a major
role in making important decisions about the product
(i.e., team autonomy), teams improve their sense-
making capabilities. Our ﬁndings are consistent with
studies ofVan den Ende and Wijnberg (2003), who
found that autonomy provides a platform for infor-
mation exchange by conducting case studies of inno-
vation teams; of Jassawalla and Sashittal (1998), who
showed that autonomy enhances cross-functional
collaboration; and of Donnellon (1993), who illus-
trated that team autonomy helps in the accomplish-
ment of team-related work by investigating 12
product development teams. However, it should be
taken into consideration that prior investigations have
not produced a thorough understanding of how team
autonomy affects team sensemaking capability con-
struct. In our study, it appears that team autonomy
increases team ownership, commonality, and coop-
erative negotiation; leads to focus on real-time plan-
ning, scheduling, and resource allocation; and opens
up a space for taking initiatives regarding the recon-
ﬁguration of project-related routines to enhance
sensemaking capability (Nakata and Im, 2010). This
study, next, empirically illustrated that when manag-
ers encourage employees to think outside the box and
develop original ideas that are highly valued in the
organization (i.e., open-mindedness), team members
can question existing assumptions, organize the tech-
nical and market-related information into meaningful
ways, and develop a better vision for the project. This
ﬁnding is similar to the organizational level of studies

and place attention disproportionately at the local
information rather than the team level, potentially
distracting them from attending to the team as a
whole – reducing sensemaking capability of teams.
Next, it should be noted that, consistent with pre-
vious studies, team member proximity can be a
moderator variable (Hoegl et al., 2007), or the rela-
tionship between team proximity and sensemaking
capability is not always straightforward and may
depend on some contextual conditions (Chong et al.,
2012). Also, we found that there is no statistical asso-
ciation between team sensemaking capability and
team member familiarity. It looks like when the team
members know each other prior to the formation of
the project team (i.e., team familiarity), the team
members utilize each others’ knowledge without
critically evaluating their reliability (Akgün et al.,
2006a). In addition, the results suggest that prior
interpersonal knowledge about other group members
may lead to larger bias toward discussing shared
information, which is consistent with the study of
Harrison et al. (2003) and Gruenfeld et al. (1996),
who suggest that during group discussion, the prob-
ability that a given piece of information will be men-
tioned increases with the number of members who
are already aware of it.
Next, this study showed that information imple-
mentation and speed-to-market mediate the relation-
ship between the team’s sensemaking capability and
new product success. When team members have a
better internal/external communication, information
gathering and classiﬁcation, build a shared mental
model, and take experimental actions, they develop
products that meet the volume expectations, overall
sales expectations, proﬁt expectations, return on
investment expectations, and senior management
expectations by the ways of uncovering and correct-
ing product problem areas with which customers
were dissatisﬁed and developing and launching the
product faster than the major competitor or in less
time than what was considered normal in the indus-
try. While previous studies investigated the mediating
role of speed-to-market/information implementation
between new product success and cross-functional
integration (McNally et al., 2011), team culture
(Akgün et al., 2010), product quality and innovative-
ness (Molina-Castillo et al., 2011), and organiza-
tional design (Blazevic and Lievens, 2004), we
speciﬁcally investigated the link between team sense-
making capability and new product success. Our
results suggest that team sensemaking capability is
actualized through actions and practices to leverage
the success of new products in the market, such that
new products’ success is incomplete if people do not
use sensemaking capability of teams envisioned in
the
information
implementation
and
speed-to-
market. In a sense, information implementation
enhances a team’s knowledge base and people’s atti-
tudes and perceptions on the project-related issues
and events and symbolizes the commitment and dedi-
cation of organizational actors to rational behavior
leveraging the link between sensemaking capability
and new product performance. Also, team sensemak-
ing capability leads to the development of new
opportunities during the project and early improve-
ment of the product development process by fasten-
ing the decision-making process, and helps the
project team to develop products quickly for improv-
ing the performance of new products in the market.
Finally, this study illustrated that team sensemak-
ing capability mediates the relationship between
team processes and information implementation
during the projects, such that the team’s sensemaking
capability, through the accounts it generated based
on team autonomy, shared mental models, and
trust, affects information utilization to solve project
and product-related problems. These ﬁndings extend

establish a sense of self-administration in the team
where needed; (2) encourage employees to think
outside the box; (3) emphasize open-mindedness;

5. Limitations and future research
There are some methodological limitations to this
study – notably, single sourcing and self-reporting.
Scholars (Aviolo et al., 1991) have argued that
studies employing single-source methodology can be
biased by artiﬁcially high intercorrelations produced
by an overall response tendency. Even though, as
noted, project managers are able to see the bigger
picture of project activities, by collecting data from
team members as respondents in each of the NPD
projects who will add new insights about these
complex constructs, such as team sensemaking capa-
bility. Also, because there was a time lag between the
times when the projects were completed and when
data were collected, there might be a recall issue in
survey questions. Next, utilizing a cross-sectional
design with questionnaires was one of the limitations
of this study. The generalizability of sampling is
another limitation of this study. The study was con-
ducted in a speciﬁc national context that involves
only Turkish ﬁrms. It is important to note that readers
should be cautious when generalizing the results to
different cultural contexts. Finally, the limitation of
our analysis technique is that PLS focuses much
more on prediction and does not provide a test of
theoretical ﬁt (Hair et al., 2006).
The sensemaking capability of teams triggers the
opportunity for future research. Here, the presented
model in this study is broad and does not capture the
possible moderating effects of environmental turbu-
lence, uncertainty, and routines of the projects. In
addition, team leader characteristics and IT usage
may affect the relationships between team sensemak-
ing capability and project outcomes. Also, the rela-
tionship between team sensemaking capability, and
proximity and familiarity may not be linear, which
warrants an empirical investigation (Uzzi and Spiro,
2005). For future researches, the determinants of
team sensemaking capability can be expanded, such
as team culture values (e.g., employee orientation,
customer
orientation,
innovativeness
orientation,
social responsibility) and types of team memory
(e.g., declarative and procedural memory) (Lynn and
Akgün, 2000).

capability can be enhanced and its effect on the
project success are missing and should be added to
the TIM literature. In this study, we tested the role of

Notes
1. From a behaviorist perspective, team learning is consid-
ered to be incorporating lessons learned during the
project and as correcting problems encountered, opera-
tionalized as information/knowledge implementation or
usage by Moorman (1995) and Lynn et al. (2000). Sarin
and McDermott (2003) also pointed out that team learn-
ing involves making use of information, which leads
to the detection and correction of errors and improves
the likelihood of effective NPD. As this aspect of team
learning, information implementation, has been used
widely in NPD literature and affords us a measurable
outcome, we adapted this deﬁnition for this study.
2. This deﬁnition is close to the well-established concept
of market orientation, which is deﬁned as organization-
wide generation, dissemination, and responsiveness
to market intelligence (Jaworski and Kohli, 1990).
However, we propose that sensemaking capability offers
several insights into the market orientation perspective.
For instance, whereas sensemaking capability perspec-
tive highlights the importance of both the internal and
the external information sources, market orientation
scholars tend to focus more on obtaining information
from external sources (Jaworski and Kohli, 1996). Also,
sensemaking literature argues the importance of build-
ing shared mental models between team members
(Ashmos and Nathan, 2002) whereas market orientation
literature suggests that the interpretation of market infor-
mation is made by the person(s) who acquire the infor-
mation prior to its dissemination (Jaworski and Kohli,
1996) and, therefore, does not emphasize the construc-
tion of collective mind. Further, although sensemaking
capability is driven by plausibility rather than accuracy
(Seligman, 2006) and therefore gives the opportunity to
deal with uncertainty (Weick et al., 2005), market orien-
tation literature focuses on collecting the existing infor-
mation about customers and competitors.
3. Scholars (e.g., Daft and Weick, 1984; Thomas et al.,
1993; Jeong and Brower, 2008) note that sensemaking
as a series of cognitive processes is usually interviewed
in three steps, such as noticing or scanning, interpreta-
tion, and actioning, interconnected through a feedback
loop in the literature. Noticing is concerned with data
collection (Daft and Weick, 1984). NPD teams are often
formed for the purpose of collecting necessary informa-
tion about both external and internal environments
(Ashmos and Nathan, 2002). Interpretation involves the
development or application of ways of comprehending
the meaning of information. It entails the ﬁtting of infor-
mation into some structure for understanding and action
(Thomas et al., 1993). Interpretation arises through the
communication between team members and leads to
actions which means new responses based on the inter-
pretation (Daft and Weick, 1984). The outcome of
action often provides a pretext that leads to new ways of
noticing and interpretation (Jeong and Brower, 2008).
